Would it be exhibiting a full isomorphism between the proofs-as-genuine-functions? Is there any paper or work that discusses this problem further? It seems like a classic thing that philosophers would ponder...

zigzag (Apr 23 2020 at 23:11):

Sim98 is probably Stephen's Simpson Subsystems of Second-Order Arithmetic, which is probably relevant to your question (at least the introduction); the basic idea is that, assume that you have a super strong theory TTT that act as headcanon for what the wolrd is like, two theorems ϕ1\phi_1ϕ1​ and ϕ2\phi_2ϕ2​ that you like which hold in TTT, you can attempt to compare their strength (let's say you want to ask "is ϕ1\phi_1ϕ1​ more powerful than ϕ2\phi_2ϕ2​ ?") by considering a super weak theory T′⊆TT' \subseteq TT′⊆T (which is probably not enough to prove either ϕ1\phi_1ϕ1​ or ϕ2\phi_2ϕ2​) and try to check whether T′T'T′ might be able to prove ϕ1⇒ϕ2\phi_1 \Rightarrow \phi_2ϕ1​⇒ϕ2​

zigzag (Apr 23 2020 at 23:15):

Reverse Mathematics tries to do this rather systematically, where a typical choice for T′T'T′ is the aforementioned RCA0\mathsf{RCA}_0RCA0​, TTT is classical second-order arithmetic (RCA0\mathsf{RCA}_0RCA0​ is a restriction where you have a very limited form of the comprehension and induction schemes), and at least one of the ϕi\phi_iϕi​ is ideally a nice axiom/axiom scheme corresponding to an intermediate T′′T''T′′.

zigzag (Apr 23 2020 at 23:33):

I will not babble too much more about the specifics of Reverse Maths (unless it is helpful :)), especially since there are a lot of ways to vary this basic setup that make perfect sense. For instance, I think intuitionistic theories are not often considered in "mainstream" Reverse Maths.

But regarding your question about computational content that you can get through those classical implications, while a proof of an implication in those systems might not mechanically induce an "effective reduction", there are strong links between recursion theory and those weak subsystems of second arithmetic, and often this can at the very least give hints. There are quite a few recursion theorists who also work in rev maths and draw on this connection.

Tarski told me the following story. He tried to publish his theorem ["for all infinite X, there is a bijection between X and X x X" -> AC] in the Comptes Rendus Acad. Sci. Paris but Fréchet and Lebesgue refused to present it. Fréchet wrote that an implication between two well known propositions is not a new result. Lebesgue wrote that an implication between two false propositions is of no interest. And Tarski said that after this misadventure he never tried to publish in the Comptes Rendus.

[Mod] Morgan Rogers (Apr 24 2020 at 10:32):

Gershom said:

So all theorems which hold in a given axiomatic setup in some sense "imply" one another. Constructivism alone doesn't suffice to resolve this problem, if we keep proofs as "mere propositions".

Making the setting more precise by specifying what fragment of logic one is working in gives meaning to statements like this. That is, there is more that can vary in a mathematical theory than the axioms one starts with, specifically the permissible principles of deduction. One can have propositions which are equivalent using classical logic but where only one or neither direction of implication is constructively valid, for example.

[Mod] Morgan Rogers (Apr 24 2020 at 10:32):

A less formal alternative is that for any statement there is a hypothetical minimal/reduced setting where it makes sense. A statement about a relationship between the elements of a group that doesn't mention inverses would also be directly applicable to monoids etc; we only need the operations which actually appear in the statement to be defined. So "these two theorems/statements are equivalent" should mean, "in a setting where both of these statements make sense (possibly with extra assumptions X,Y,Z) they are equivalent (in deduction system/fragment of logic D)".

[Mod] Morgan Rogers (Apr 24 2020 at 10:37):

In set theory there's the problem of needing the set-theoretic foundations to be specified ahead of time, but in categorical logic we can take "a setting" to mean "a suitable category", at which point things should become clearer. There may remain tautological cases (like the only setting where two statements make sense being one where both are theorems), but one can hope that these should only include genuine tautologies.
