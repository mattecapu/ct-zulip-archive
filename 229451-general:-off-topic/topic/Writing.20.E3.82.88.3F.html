Jason Erbele (Dec 08 2020 at 21:16):

Fair point about calligraphy – I have limited exposure to Japanese calligraphy. However, I wouldn't go so far as to conflate my "careful orthography" with "some computer fonts". Maybe it's not true for everyone, but I know my own handwriting differs when I am hastily writing versus when I focus on neat penmanship. It is certainly an aesthetic issue, and computerized typefaces likely have a reasonably strong influence.

Jason Erbele (Dec 08 2020 at 21:16):

More generally speaking, a "poorly formed" character (whether scribbled or written by someone unfamiliar with the character) and a "fanciful calligraphic" character can both depart radically from "standard" computerized typefaces, and even subvert expected norms for a character, while still being recognizable. But I would wager that a sizable majority of calligraphic 三s carefully make all three strokes differ in length.

Jason Erbele (Dec 08 2020 at 21:18):

If you are trying to communicate with a handwritten よ in isolation from the rest of the Japanese language, which is what seems to have spurred this digression, my opinion is that an "ugly" よ is fine. You can look up the accepted stroke order and direction for hiragana for guidance, but if you can create a recognizable よ more quickly with a different stroke order/direction, I won't tattle. But practice with the "proper" way to write it will likely make your よs "prettier" over time.

Oscar Cunningham (Dec 09 2020 at 08:51):

While on sabbatical at Harvard, he sat in on a course Mazur was giving and often criticized the notation. Eventually they decided to give him a T-shirt which said, “This notation sucks” on it. So one day Barry intentionally tried to get him to say it. He introduced a complex variable Ξ\XiΞ, took its complex conjugate, and divided by the original Ξ\XiΞ. This was written as a vertical fraction, so it looked like eight horizontal lines on the blackboard. He then did a few other similar things, but Serge kept quiet—apparently he didn’t criticize notation unless he knew what the underlying mathematics was about. Eventually Barry had to give up and just present him with the T-shirt.
