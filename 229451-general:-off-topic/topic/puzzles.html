Nathaniel Virgo (Oct 14 2020 at 04:03):

For puzzle 1: most Solar system bodies are quite dark in colour, e.g. Earth's moon is about the colour of asphalt. So basically I think we have to look for places where there's a reasonably fresh icy surface on top of a mountain. (Fresh compared to whatever processes are dumping dark-coloured gunk on top of it.)

Mars has polar ice caps - I don't know if there are mountains there. A lot of the icy moons have low-albedo (i.e. white-ish) surfaces, but I would guess they tend to be fairly flat at large scales, since they're essentially glaciers. Ceres has white patches, but they're in craters.

Iapetus could be a contender. It has a rocky surface with some dark and some very light regions, which are coated in fresh ice. It certainly has some huge mountains in the dark region, but I don't know if it also has mountains in the light region. (I guess it might depend on the definition of a mountain. Or not. It's hard to get a sense of scale just from images.)

Pluto has a low albedo and has some mountains that look quite white-ish if this "true colour" image is to be believed, though you could argue they're more of a cream colour. Other Kupier belt objects generally tend to have low albedos, which suggests there might be many, many undiscovered off-white, cream-ish mountains there.

And Earth, of course.
