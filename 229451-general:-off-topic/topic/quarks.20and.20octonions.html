Reid Barton (Jul 20 2020 at 12:52):

So this reminds me of something I only recently became aware of, and still don't fully understand.

If RRR is a commutative ring and MMM is a left RRR-module then I can make MMM a right RRR-module by defining mr=rmmr = rmmr=rm. However, for R=CR = \mathbb{C}R=C, there's another interesting way to do this, namely defining mz=z‾mmz = \overline{z}mmz=zm. In both cases this action is compatible with the original left action, producing a bimodule.

Now suppose VVV and WWW are two complex vector spaces which I've made into C\mathbb{C}C-bimodules in the "interesting" way. Then we can form their tensor product as bimodules and we have equations

z(v⊗w)=(zv)⊗w=(vz‾)⊗w=v⊗(z‾w)=v⊗(wz)=(v⊗w)zz(v \otimes w) = (zv) \otimes w = (v\overline{z}) \otimes w = v \otimes (\overline{z} w) = v \otimes (wz) = (v \otimes w)zz(v⊗w)=(zv)⊗w=(vz)⊗w=v⊗(zw)=v⊗(wz)=(v⊗w)z

from which we observe

  • The tensor product V⊗WV \otimes WV⊗W is a bimodule in the "ordinary" way.
  • C\mathbb{C}C-linear maps V⊗W→CV \otimes W \to \mathbb{C}V⊗W→C (where the target has the ordinary structure) are the same as sesquilinear forms V×W→CV \times W \to \mathbb{C}V×W→C (linear in the first argument and conjugate linear in the second argument; if we treated the right module structure as primary, then we would get the opposite convention).

So, this somehow "explains" the conjugation that appears in the definition of a sesquilinear form, and I think it should similarly "explain" the conjugations appearing in other places like the conjugate transpose, etc.

It also seems to potentially "explain" why your formula contains the part a‾b→+ba→\overline{a} \overrightarrow{b} + b \overrightarrow{a}ab+ba. (I see Layra also mentioned something similar in the comments.) I wonder if there is a sense in which tensoring the quaternions with the "ordinary" C\mathbb{C}C yields the biquaternions, while tensoring with the "interesting" C\mathbb{C}C yields the octonions.

All this is very interesting to me. If you take the octonions O\mathbb{O}O and take any subalgebra isomorphic to C\mathbb{C}C, left and right multiplication make O\mathbb{O}O into a (C,C)(\mathbb{C}, \mathbb{C})(C,C)-bimodule. This is not instantly obvious, since the octonions are not associative. But they're "associative enough" for this to have (ax)b=a(xb) (a x) b = a (x b)(ax)b=a(xb) for all x∈Ox \in \mathbb{O}x∈O and a,ba,ba,b in this copy of C\mathbb{C}C inside O\mathbb{O}O.

Today on Twitter I wrote an easy intro to building compact Lie groups as iterated sphere bundles. I did this because in my quark/octonion stuff I needed to describe G2\mathrm{G}_2G2​, the group of automorphisms of the octonions, as an bundle of 3-spheres over a bundle of 5-spheres over a 6-sphere.

The 3-sphere S³ can be seen as R³ plus a point at infinity. But here London Tsai shows the "Hopf fibration": S³ as a bundle of circles over the 2-sphere. Each point in S³ lies on one circle. The set of all these circles forms a 2-sphere. S³ is an S¹ bundle over S². (1/n) https://twitter.com/johncarlosbaez/status/1287060018374701057/photo/1

Trying to make sense of the non associativity of the Jordan product a∘b=(ab+ba)/2a\circ b = (ab+ba)/2a∘b=(ab+ba)/2 I've come to think of it as an average over possible sequential compositions giving the same weight to each alternative. In this view, the two different ways of parenthezising a∘b∘ca\circ b\circ ca∘b∘c describe two different "protocols" such that e.g. a∘(b∘c)a\circ (b\circ c)a∘(b∘c) says "I flip a (fair) coin to decide whether aaa goes first or last, then I flip another coin to decide whether bbb goes before or after ccc".

I don't know if this perspective is physically meaningful but I find it intuitive.
