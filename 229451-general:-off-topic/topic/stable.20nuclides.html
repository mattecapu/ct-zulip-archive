John Baez (Jan 31 2021 at 03:22):

Having an even number of neutrons and/or an even number of protons tends to make a nucleus more stable against radioactive decacy. I just learned there are only 5 stable nuclei with an odd number of neutrons and an odd number of protons:

  • deuterium (hydrogen-2)

  • lithium-6

  • boron-10

  • nitrogen-14

  • tantalum-180

Deuterium is rare compared to hydrogen and helium-4, and lithium-6 is rare compared to lithium-7, and tantalum-180 is rare compared to tantalum-181, so in these cases one gets the feeling that odd-odd nuclei are hard for nature to produce. But 20% of boron is boron-10 (the rest being boron-11), and 99.6% of nitrogen is nitrogen-14 (the rest being nitrogen-15). What's up with nitrogen???

David Michael Roberts (Jan 31 2021 at 03:52):

Wikipedia says "Deuterium is destroyed in the interiors of the stars faster than it is produced", so that is probably an explanation, of sorts, for its relative scarcity. It just pushes the question, though, to why the nuclear processes that produce deuterium are of lower rate than those that consume it, or otherwise what was happening in the analogous primordial nucelosynthesis process. Though, to quote WP again, at this time

the temperature was high enough that the mean energy per particle was greater than the binding energy of weakly bound deuterium; therefore any deuterium that was formed was immediately destroyed. This situation is known as the deuterium bottleneck. The bottleneck delayed formation of any helium-4 until the Universe became cool enough to form deuterium (at about a temperature equivalent to 100 keV). At this point, there was a sudden burst of element formation (first deuterium, which immediately fused to helium).

(emphasis added). So I guess it comes down to the weak binding energy of deuterium, and the relatively fast "reaction rate" of deuterium to helium (the high binding energy of Helium nuclei probably helps here). Looking around it seems there's only one reaction that leads to deuterium (n+p -> D + photon), but a number of reactions involving D leading to tritium and helium.

Anyway, that's just one element, and probably the least representative.

John Baez (Jan 31 2021 at 05:26):

Deuterium is fascinating since it shows up in the early universe before stars. One famous puzzle is why the deuteron is stable but the "dineutron" is not. It's connected to the Pauli exclusion principle: in deuterium the spins of the proton and neutron can be parallel but in the dineutron they must spin in opposite directions.

John Baez (Jan 31 2021 at 05:27):

The hard part is to understand why having the spins be antiparallel makes the energy higher, so much as to make two neutrons unable to bind into a dineutron. (I.e., so much that a dineutron has more energy than two separate neutrons.)
