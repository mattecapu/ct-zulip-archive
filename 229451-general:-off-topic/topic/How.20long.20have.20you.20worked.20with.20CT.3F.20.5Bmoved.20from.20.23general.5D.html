Aleks Kissinger (Apr 22 2020 at 14:00):

I guess I will reply to the original question, since I just got here (hi everybody!). I first got into category theory when I read Pierce's book "Category Theory for Computer Scientists" in the early 00s, thinking it might be somehow useful for formalising something called "type enforcement" in OS security. Never had much success there. :)

As far as the core concepts of CT, I keep learning them over and over, because there's lots of different ways to think about them. For example, if you like to draw string diagrams and think about functors as wires, distributive laws are "crossing wires over each other" (see e.g. https://arxiv.org/pdf/1401.7220.pdf by @Dan Marsden). This is the only way I can consistently reconstruct all of the parts of the definition from memory (particularly when it comes to distributive laws of (co)monads). But then, you can also think about them as distributivity in the sense of a(b+c) = ab + ac, or more philosophically fancy (and less understood by me) things like "the interaction between computation and observation".

I think it's more useful to think about getting some skill in using some concepts and expanding your "bag of tricks", rather than a binary "understanding" vs "not understanding". For instance, their are some people who are total kung fu masters at universal properties, taking co-ends and Kan extending stuff all over the place, etc etc. I tend to get pretty lost in that kind of thing, but I can do some pretty fancy string diagram acrobatics.

Henry Story (Apr 22 2020 at 16:20):

Hi, I think I heard about CT in the 1980ies, but it was still known as abstract nonsense at the time, so even though I liked mathematics I found it difficult to motivate myself there, and instead read a lot of modal logic (David Lewis in particular) as part of Analytic Philosophy. I later got back to computing, learnt Java, went to California and ended up work on the BabelFish machine translation service at AltaVista.
Then from 2004 onwards worked at Sun Microsystems on blogging and the semantic web, and around 2010 I started looking at Scala, where the word Monads made it's appearance, which made me wonder if they were related to Leibniz. Actually I was wondering for a while if they could not be used for logical security contexts (which I discovered later they can as shown by Martin Abadi). I discovered Free Monads and found them very useful for writing a read-write web server based on Actor architecture. So slowly I thought I should learn more about CT. I found the book Conceptual Mathematics: A First Introduction to Categories by Lawvere around 2014 but it was very slow, so I could not see the point of it. A bit later I got the book Category Theory by Awodey, that moved much faster but it looked like rocket science, and all the examples were from maths, where I had no idea how long it could take me to learn them. At a Scala conference in London around 2016, I mentioned Lawvere's book to Martin Odersky the creator of Scala and he somehow mentioned HoTT as the big revolutionary change in maths. Thinking CT was it, I was very surprised and later read the first few chapters of HoTT which I found very well written. But the modal story did not feel that good. (and having recently read Modal HoTT I know why).
In any case, I started to have questions as to how OO programming, FP, the semantic web, protocols and all that fit together, mostly because over time I was getting the impression that some very intelligent people could not see some very interesting advantages in other areas, as if there were major conceptual barriers around, some categorical structures people could not overcome, so to speak. Somehow after building an EU consortium with Oxford, Southampton, INRIA, EPFL, and business partners such as Imogen Heap, Catapult and a number of startups to build an open decentralised secure platform built on Tim Berners-Lee's Solid architecture, I ended up at Southampton, where I am writing a thesis on Web Architecture and Security, looking for mathematics to ground the work.
There I discovered that Modal Logics are Colagebraic and so is OO programming, through Bart Jacobs articles (see this Quora answer) things started to become a lot more interesting. In 2016 I found a thesis by Benjamin Braatz giving a Category Theorectic view of RDF which was also very helpful for me to understand CT. Recently I found a lot more on the subject having started a group Web Cats, to see how category theory can be applied to the web. On the whole as I discovered more and more introductions to CT from a programming perspective (e.g. Bartosz Milewski's courses and book I found it easier and easier to see the uses of it and so to learn it. So much so that I recently looked at Awodey's book at realized that I had learnt nearly all the concepts in it). Indeed I now feel like I can use CT to get into mathematics :-)
What I like the most about CT is its ability to reveal dualities. That should be the topic of the year 2020.
I also write more philosophical, non-CT related blog posts. My latest is Co-Immunology and the Web.
