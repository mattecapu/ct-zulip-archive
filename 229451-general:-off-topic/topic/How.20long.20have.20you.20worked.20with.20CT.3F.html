Jules Hedges (Apr 20 2020 at 10:14):

I took a masters course in category theory (lectured by a physicist doing topos theory!) in 2010. Pretty soon after that I started using Haskell a lot so I was mostly playing with monads and related things. I started doing a PhD in proof theory and was super into categorical semantics of linear logic. Trying to understand the notes "categorical semantics of linear logic" by Melliès was maybe my first "serious" category theory (and I had a goal to understand some stuff by Hyland, but never got around to it). Around 2014 I gradually picked up string diagrams through a bunch of linguistics seminars and thought they were very cool but had too much hype. 2015 discovered open games and so started using monoidal categories and string diagrams full time. Started to actually use "serious" category thoery in my research in 2017, I rediscovered the category of lenses with another name (extracted from the foundations of game theory) and was studying it using fibred category theory. In 2018 I was involved in the first ACT school + conference, and I think it was at the conference that I decided that I ought to start thinking of myself as a category theorist now

Fabrizio Genovese (Apr 20 2020 at 11:09):

I did exactly the opposite of Amar: I got a PhD with Bob without knowing shit about category theory. That was 2015. I never even heard the word before. So my first PhD year was totally hellish, I had to pick up with a lot of stuff and it felt like everyone was speaking in an alien language. People recommended me a lot of useless books. I found them to be useless because they tried to aid understanding by being intuitive, but my intuition was set-theoretic at that point. So I'd read the first 200 hundred pages thinking I was understanding everything and then boom, like a puch in the face I'd realise I was back to square one. Finally I decided to give MacLane a shot (even if everyome was advising me not to) and that was by far the best category theory book I've ever read. Maybe much more difficult than others, but at least with it slow progress was real progress.
I basically got exposed to string diagrams first and to category theory later. My main research focus at the time was linguistics, which did not require a super heavy knowledge of cats. Then I started working also on quantum stuff and later on, when I joined Statebox, I switched to concurrency. Since then I learnt the theory behind petri nets and other concurrent structures, and then started diving into "real" category theory, mainly by reading MacLane-Moerdijk. That was illuminating since I've never been a geometry person (zero sense of spatial reasoning for instance), but that book gave me a lot of insight about "what geometry is about".
As a last thing: During these five years of CT I've developed quite a bad attitute towards reading books and papers. I find the language used there very difficult in aiding understanding. Usually a 15 minutes convo with the author of a paper gives you more insights and knowledge than trying to read the paper yourself for 2 weeks. So my policy in these last 5 years has mainly been going around bothering people and asking stuff like "hey, can you explain this to me?" I hope to not have annoyed to many people in this process. I still think this is the best way to learn.

Daniel Geisler (Apr 20 2020 at 15:04):

To answer my own question, in the Nineties I read John Baez's This Week in Mathematical Physics and then Philippe Flajolet work with Sedgewick on Analytic Combinatorics. Before than I followed Stephen Wolfram's research and actually took over part of his agenda unifying maps and flows. About 2001 things clicked for me and I got the relevance of CT to my own research and the general idea behind umbral calculus. At the same time I put my math website up including a page on CT inspired combinatorics. See http://tetration.org/Combinatorics/index.html and http://tetration.org/Combinatorics/PartitionDiagrams.htm . Now my research is in general dynamical systems and I hope to connect that to CT.

Joe Moeller (Apr 20 2020 at 16:35):

It must have been around 2011~2012 after learning about group homomorphisms and continuous maps, I asked "what's going on with these functions between things that satisfy some condition? The response was something like "oh that's category theory, but I don't know much about it, so you'll have to ask someone else". This was at my community college, so there wasn't really anybody else to ask. Then when I got to UCR, I did an undergrad research project where my mentors were two of @John Baez's grad students, Brandon Coya and Jason Erbele. They taught me about categories, and I answered a question I had for myself for a few years about topology. Then when I started grad school, I attended a zillion seminars and classes with Julie Bergner, so I got a homotopical taste of category theory. Then I decided to work with John in like 2015~2016. So now I'm nearly done with my PhD in applied category theory.
