John Baez (Dec 08 2024 at 16:48):

I think I count as a physicist for this purposes of this conversation. So:

We never say "interact according to a probability amplitude".

In either classical or quantum field theory, we say two fields interact if there's a term in the Lagrangian that involves a product of those two fields, or their derivatives. For example in QED the photon field AAA interacts with the electron field ψ\psiψ because the Lagrangian is the sum of three terms:

  • one for the photon
  • one for the electron
  • and an 'interaction Lagrangian' γμψ‾ψAμ\gamma^\mu \overline{\psi}\psi A_\mu γμψ​ψAμ​

The details don't matter much here, though I'll say γμ\gamma^\muγμ are not fields: they're a bunch of matrices called "gamma matrices".

What matters is that when you put a term in the Lagrangian that involves both AAA and ψ\psiψ, it means that changes in the photon field cause changes in the electron field, and vice versa! Thus, we say they interact.

John Baez (Dec 08 2024 at 21:41):

Anyway, returning to the original question

What exactly do physicists mean when two fields interact...?

Let me answer in a bit more detail with an example. To keep things simple say we have two fields that only depend on time, f(t)f(t)f(t) and g(t)g(t)g(t). Say they evolve according to differential equations, as everything in classical/quantum mechanics/field theory tends to do. We could have

d2dt2f(t)=−f(t) \displaystyle { \frac{d^2 } {dt^2 } f(t) = - f(t) }dt2d2​f(t)=−f(t)

d2dt2g(t)=−g(t) \displaystyle { \frac{d^2 } {dt^2 } g(t) = - g(t) }dt2d2​g(t)=−g(t)

and then we'd say fff and ggg don't interact, because the solution of the first equation doesn't depend on the second one, and vice versa. Or we could have

d2dt2f(t)=−f(t)−λf(t)g(t) \displaystyle { \frac{d^2 } {dt^2 } f(t) = - f(t) - \lambda f(t) g(t) }dt2d2​f(t)=−f(t)−λf(t)g(t)

d2dt2g(t)=−g(t)−λf(t)g(t) \displaystyle { \frac{d^2 } {dt^2 } g(t) = - g(t) - \lambda f(t) g(t)}dt2d2​g(t)=−g(t)−λf(t)g(t)

where λ\lambdaλ is some number. Now we say the two fields interact, because the solutions affect each other: you can't solve the two equations separately. We call λ\lambdaλ a coupling constant and we also say the two fields are coupled.

John Baez (Dec 08 2024 at 21:44):

Another common feature you see here is that the original equations are linear and thus easy to solve, while the coupled equations are nonlinear and much harder to solve.

John Baez (Dec 08 2024 at 21:45):

In quantum field theory we don't usually write down the differential equations. Although we sometimes do, it's more common to write down the 'Lagrangian', which is a more compact expression that you can derive the equations from by turning a crank.
