Fodor, who was arguing for a computational theory of the mind, argued as I understand from listening to this passage that:

  1. a program is a logical structure
  2. Piaget was arguing for the importance role of change of cognitive function as the individual evolved (say from childhood to adolescence)
  3. But what kind of functional (computational) transformation could that be? Fodor argued
  4. It would have to be a move from a weaker logic to a stronger logic: Something like the move from Predicate logic to modal logic. (Predicate logic is #RDF essentially or W3C OWL) That would be a major change. (And indeed I can vouch that when I read David K. Lewis and Kripke I underwent such a change!) In predicate logic I can argue about all or some, but not about necessity or possibility, Further along the road by adding a distance relation I can speak about counterfactuals, which is needed to understand causality....
  5. What Fodor is right about, John Vervaeke says, is that all possible moves I can do in predicate logic will never get me to modal logic.
  6. so how is cognitive change possible?

Then I argue that from the CT point of view there may be less distance between Logic and Modal Logic:

First we should notice that David Lewis in Counterfactuals argues that one can move from first order logic to modal logic by adding possibilities to one's ontology: ie, by having variables vary about sets of possible worlds. Then one can change any n place relation in predicate logic to an n+1 place relation by adding a dependency on worlds. (In a way this is what RDF does when it moves from triples to quads!) That is enough to then defined modal logics (and even counterfactual logics, if we add a distance relation between worlds). In Category Theory I think one expresses this in terms of adjunctions between slice categories. Take the slice category over the set of possible worlds and and then create an adjunction between that and another slice category over 1 as described for example by David Corfield in his Modal Homotopy Type Theory book.
So does the possibility of working one's way from Predicate logic to modal logic via adjunctions actually stop Fodor's argument in its tracks? Has category theory perhaps shown structurally how such an evolution is possible?
What kind of mental transformation is happening when one moves to an adjunction?

I asked a bit more"I guess nominalism makes little sense in category theory, because in category theory relations are as important as objects." to which it answered:

Your observation is astute. Nominalism, as a philosophical position, focuses on denying the existence of abstract entities or universals, asserting that only individual, concrete things exist. In the context of category theory, the emphasis is not just on objects but also on the relations between those objects, represented by morphisms.

Category theory provides a unifying language and structure to study different mathematical objects and their relationships. The primary focus is on the morphisms, which are structure-preserving transformations between objects, and the composition of these morphisms. This emphasis on relations makes category theory a powerful tool for representing and analyzing complex structures and patterns.

Given the relational focus of category theory, it is true that nominalism, in its traditional form, might not fit neatly within this framework. However, it is important to note that category theory is a mathematical formalism and can be used to model various philosophical ideas, including nominalist or realist perspectives on universals.

One could potentially argue that category theory allows for a more nuanced understanding of nominalist and realist positions by emphasizing the relationships between objects and their properties, rather than focusing solely on the objects themselves. In this sense, category theory may offer new insights and perspectives on traditional metaphysical debates, such as the question of universals.

It is also worth noting that the application of category theory to metaphysics is still an ongoing area of research, and different philosophers have different opinions on the extent to which category theory can or should be used to analyze metaphysical concepts. The relationship between nominalism and category theory is an interesting topic for further exploration, but it remains an open question how these ideas can be reconciled or integrated within a broader metaphysical framework.
