Topic: Why do bialgebras have monoidal module categories *really*

Chris Grossack (they/them) (Sep 14 2023 at 18:12):

Here's a cute question that I could probably answer myself if I had time to look up some facts that somebody here will just ~know~.

If AAA is a kkk-algebra that's moreover a coalgebra in a compatible way then the module category Mod(A)\text{Mod}(A)Mod(A) is monoidal, basically because if V⊗WV \otimes WV⊗W is the usual tensor product of the underlying vector spaces, we can use the comultiplication Δ:A→A⊗A\Delta : A \to A \otimes AΔ:A→A⊗A in order to act on V⊗WV \otimes WV⊗W.

Now, one of the big reasons to care about co-algebraic structures generally is that homming out of a co-thing gives you a thing. For instance, the circle S1S^1S1 is a cogroup (in the pointed homotopy category) so homming out of S1S^1S1 always gives a group (and indeed, "pointed homotopy classes of maps S1→XS^1 \to XS1→X" is called the fundamental group of XXX). Moreover, the Mod(A)\mathsf{Mod}(A)Mod(A) functor from algebras to categories looks like homming out of AAA! Here's how to make that precise:

A kkk-algebra AAA is the same data as a one-object kkk-linear category, and through this lens Mod(A)\text{Mod}(A)Mod(A) is exactly the category of kkk-linear functors and natural transformations from AAA to Vect\mathsf{Vect}Vect. So Mod(A)=Hom‾(A,Vect)\text{Mod}(A) = \underline{\text{Hom}}(A, \mathsf{Vect})Mod(A)=Hom​(A,Vect).

So let's look at kLink\mathfrak{Lin}kLin, the 2-category of kkk-linear categories (read: categories enriched in Vect\mathsf{Vect}Vect). This has a tensor product, and if we identify AAA with its one object category, then A⊗AA \otimes AA⊗A as computed in Vect\mathsf{Vect}Vect agrees with A⊗AA \otimes AA⊗A as computed in kLink\mathfrak{Lin}kLin. In particular, if AAA is a bialgebra then it's a comonoid in kLink\mathfrak{Lin}kLin.

Now, if all is right with the world, this should tell us that for any other object CCC of kLink\mathfrak{Lin}kLin (like Vect\mathsf{Vect}Vect) [A,C][A, C][A,C] should be a monoid in Cat\mathfrak{Cat}Cat. That is, a (strict) monoidal category! This also explains "automatically" why a cocommutative bialgebra should have a symmetric monoidal category worth of modules (read: a commutative monoid object in Cat\mathfrak{Cat}Cat). (Again, obviously there's a much more concrete way to see this using sweedler's notation, but let's ignore that for now).

I'm sure that there are subtleties that I'm missing here. For instance, if we want to remove the "strict" we should really be working with pseudomonoids. I'm sure that these work the same way, but I've never actually had the time to learn about them. Also, I think Mod(−)=Hom‾(−,Vect)\text{Mod}(-) = \underline{\text{Hom}}(-, \mathsf{Vect})Mod(−)=Hom​(−,Vect) should probably be valued in something a bit more restrictive than Cat\mathfrak{Cat}Cat. Maybe locally presentable (in the enriched sense) kkk-linear categories? Also also, this whole game looks like a kkk-linear version of functorial semantics. Has anyone written about that?

Thanks in advance! ^_^

Simon Willerton (Sep 17 2023 at 19:33):

PS. This perhaps is clear to many, but I will mention it anyway. A commutative algebra (or "monoid"), A, in V can be considered as one-object monoidal V-category; a monoidal category is also promonoidal, thus the Day product then gives a (biclosed) monoidal structure to A's representation category [A, V]. This should be familiar, but noticeably different to the monoidal product on the representation category of a bialgebra.
