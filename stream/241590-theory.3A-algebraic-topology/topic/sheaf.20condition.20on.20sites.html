Jens Hemelaer (Oct 19 2020 at 11:04):

The condition colim Ui≃U⇒F(colim Ui)≃F(U)\mathrm{colim} \,U_i \simeq U \Rightarrow F(\mathrm{colim} \, U_i ) \simeq F(U)colimUi​≃U⇒F(colimUi​)≃F(U) does not depend on the Grothendieck topology. So, for an arbitrary Grothendieck topology, it is not equivalent to the sheaf condition.

You can modify your criterion a bit:
colim a(Ui)≃a(U)⇒lim F(Ui)≃F(U)\mathrm{colim} \, \mathbf{a}(U_i) \simeq \mathbf{a}(U) \Rightarrow \mathrm{lim} \, F(U_i ) \simeq F(U)colima(Ui​)≃a(U)⇒limF(Ui​)≃F(U).
Here a\mathbf{a}a denotes first taking Yoneda embedding and then sheafifying.

I think this is equivalent to the sheaf condition for an arbitrary Grothendieck topology. The reason is that a covering sieve SSS of UUU can be interpreted as a sub-presheaf of y(U)\mathbf{y}(U)y(U), and sheafification makes sure that the inclusion S⊆y(U)S \subseteq \mathbf{y}(U)S⊆y(U) becomes an isomorphism.

Reid Barton (Oct 19 2020 at 14:09):

By definition/Yoneda, "colim a(Ai)=a(A)\mathrm{colim}\, \mathbf{a}(A_i) = \mathbf{a}(A)colima(Ai​)=a(A)" means exactly "for every sheaf FFF, FA=lim⁡F(Ai)FA = \lim F(A_i)FA=limF(Ai​)". This doesn't use anything about sheaves other than that they are a reflexive subcategory of presheaves with reflector a\mathbf{a}a.
In particular the phenomena discussed here don't have anything to do with sheaves per se--the same happens in any localization.

Jens Hemelaer (Oct 19 2020 at 14:13):

Yes, the M&M definition says in other words that the natural transformation Hom(yU,−)→Hom(S,−)Hom(yU,-)\to Hom(S,-)Hom(yU,−)→Hom(S,−) is an isomorphism, when the second argument takes values in the subcategory of sheaves. So by the adjunction and Yoneda, you get that a(S)→a(yU)\mathbf{a}(S) \to \mathbf{a}(yU)a(S)→a(yU) is an isomorphism.

Jens Hemelaer (Oct 19 2020 at 14:16):

It is important here that you work with sieves, not just with covering families.

For example, the map Spec(C)→Spec(R)\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{C}) \to \mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{R})Spec(C)→Spec(R) is a covering map for the étale topology. But the colimit of Spec(C)\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{C})Spec(C), as a diagram with one object, is equal to Spec(C)\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{C})Spec(C) itself.

On the other hand, if you take the sieve generated by Spec(C)→Spec(R)\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{C}) \to \mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{R})Spec(C)→Spec(R), then you get that the colimit of this sieve is equal to Spec(R)\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{R})Spec(R), in the étale topos.
