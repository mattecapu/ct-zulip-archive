John Baez (Oct 14 2021 at 19:28):

Leopold Schlicht said:

What would be an example of two spaces whose fundamental groupoids coincide but which have non-isomorphic H2H_2H2​?

When you say "coincide", I hope you mean "are equivalent": that's the right notion here.

We should be able to find an example where both spaces are connected and simply connected. Then their fundamental groupoids will both be equivalent to the terminal groupoid. So, we just need to find two connected and simply connected spaces with different H2H_2H2​.

And the easiest example is: the point and the 2-sphere.

John Baez (Oct 14 2021 at 19:35):

Also, what about the other direction: what is an example of two spaces with the same first homology groups but with non-isomorphic fundamental groups?

Remember, for connected spaces the homology group is the abelianization of the fundamental group, and the fundamental group can be anything. So in a way you're just asking "what is an example of two groups that aren't isomorphic, whose abelianizations are isomorphic?"

And a nice example is free group on 2 generators, F2F_2F2​, and the free abelian group on 2 generators, Z2\mathbb{Z}^2Z2. They both have Z2\mathbb{Z}^2Z2 as their abelianization.

But what space has fundamental group F2F_2F2​? And what space has fundamental group Z2\mathbb{Z}^2Z2? People who want to do homotopy theory should know these, or learn them. (I can give away the answer if it's too hard for someone.)

John Baez (Oct 14 2021 at 19:42):

Leopold Schlicht said:

What would be an example of two spaces whose fundamental groupoids coincide (up to isomorphism) but with non-isomorphic singular H1H_1H1​?

I don't think there are any. First of all, I think you can write any space as a disjoint union of path-connected components, and its fundamental groupoid is the coproduct of the fundamental groupoids of its path-connected components, and its H1H_1H1​ is the coproduct of those of its path-connected components. (This is certainly true for the "locally nice" spaces that algebraic topologists mainly care about, like simplicial complexes or CW complexes.)

Second of all, the H1H_1H1​ of any path-connected space is the abelianization of its fundamental group.

Using these facts you can show that the fundamental groupoid determines H1H_1H1​ for any space (or at least any "locally nice" one - and I don't really care about the others when doing homotopy theory).

John Baez (Oct 18 2021 at 04:19):

Given this, the fundamental groupoid Π1(X)\Pi_1(X)Π1​(X) of a space XXX always determines H1(X)\mathrm{H}_1(X)H1​(X). Π1(X)\Pi_1(X)Π1​(X) is a coproduct of groupoids that are equivalent to groups, one for each path component. You can abelianize each one of these groups and take the coproduct of the resulting abelian groups, and that's H1(X)\mathrm{H}_1(X)H1​(X).

John Baez (Oct 18 2021 at 04:21):

But I recommend avoiding spaces that aren't "locally nice" unless you want to dabble in exotic delights. For example, for a path-connected space XXX that's not locally nice, the fundamental group π1(X)\pi_1(X)π1​(X) has a natural topology which may not be the discrete topology, and you can then go ahead and define π1(π1(X))\pi_1(\pi_1(X))π1​(π1​(X)). This is NOT what you should be thinking about if you're learning algebraic topology! :skull_and_crossbones:

Reid Barton (Oct 18 2021 at 08:59):

John Baez said:

My only confusion was whether the fundamental groupoid of a topological space is the coproduct of the fundamental groupoids of its path components, and similarly for the homology groups of this space. But I think it's true, even when the space itself isn't the coproduct of its path components. (Think of Q\mathbb{Q}Q with its usual topology, for example.)

That's right, and it's because for any simplex Δn\Delta^nΔn and space XXX, the set (not space, as you point out!) of continuous maps from Δn\Delta^nΔn into XXX is the disjoint union of the sets of maps from Δn\Delta^nΔn into the path-connected components of XXX. For the fundamental groupoid you only need the cases n=0n=0n=0, 111, 222.

More broadly, the "reason" that the fundamental groupoid and singular homology of XXX only depend on the weak homotopy type of XXX is that they both depend only on the sets of maps of simplices into XXX and their face and degeneracy relationships, that is, they depend only on the simplicial set Sing X\mathrm{Sing}\,XSingX (and in a homotopy-invariant way--this isn't meant to be a complete argument).
