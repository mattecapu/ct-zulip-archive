Amar Hadzihasanovic (Aug 19 2022 at 08:55):

I think this is false.

Consider a co-degeneracy map from the n-simplex to the 1-simplex. This is the same as an order-preserving surjective map from (0 < ... < n) to (0 < 1), which must send the first k elements to 0 and the next (n+1-k) to 1; so it is uniquely determined by a string 0k1n+1−k0^k 1^{n+1-k}0k1n+1−k.

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Aug 19 2022 at 08:56):

Now you're asking whether given two co-degeneracy maps f, g: (n) -> (1) we can always find a co-degeneracy e: (m) -> (n) which equalises them, that is e;f = e;g.

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Aug 19 2022 at 08:57):

I claim that we can't in general. Any co-degeneracy map can be factored into a sequence of “elementary collapses” (m+1) -> (m).

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Aug 19 2022 at 08:58):

The way that precomposition with such an “elementary collapse” acts on the representation of a map (n) -> (1) as a bit-string is that it “duplicates” the bit in a certain position.

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Aug 19 2022 at 09:02):

For f: (n) -> (1), you can consider the function p(f) that gives you the parity of (length of the string - number of 0s) in the bit-string representation of f.

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Aug 19 2022 at 09:03):

Now you can see that, for all elementary collapses, you have that p(e;f) is the “negation” of p(f), because e either “adds a 0” or “adds a 1”, changing the parity.

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Aug 19 2022 at 09:05):

By induction on the decomposition of a co-degeneracy into “elementary collapses”, you can see that for all co-degeneracies e: (m) -> (n), you have p(e;f) = p(e;g) if and only if p(f) = p(g).

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Aug 19 2022 at 09:06):

So you just have to start from f, g with different parity and see that they can never be “unified”. For example, have f: (2) -> (1) be 001 and g: (2) -> (1) be 011.

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Aug 19 2022 at 09:07):

So for example if in a simplicial set you have a non-degenerate 1-simplex zzz, then the degenerate 2-simplices f∗zf^*zf∗z and g∗zg^*zg∗z do not have any common degeneracy.

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Aug 20 2022 at 08:33):

Let Δd\Delta_dΔd​ be the category of simplices and co-degeneracy maps; equivalently, this is the category whose

  • objects are finite linear orders (n)=(0<…<n)(n) = (0 < \ldots < n)(n)=(0<…<n) for n∈Nn \in \mathbb{N}n∈N, and
  • morphisms are surjective order-preserving maps.

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Aug 20 2022 at 08:35):

For each nnn, we consider the poset Degn\mathrm{Deg}_nDegn​ whose

  • objects are maps f:(m)→(n)f: (m) \to (n)f:(m)→(n) in Δd\Delta_dΔd​ with target (n)(n)(n), and
  • f≤gf \leq gf≤g if and only if fff factors through ggg (that is, f=h;gf = h;gf=h;g for some co-degeneracy hhh).

This is the poset reflection of the slice category Δd/(n)\Delta_d / (n)Δd​/(n).

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Aug 20 2022 at 08:39):

Claim: for all nnn, the poset Degn\mathrm{Deg}_nDegn​ has binary meets; that is, for all f,gf, gf,g, there is a “greatest” f∧gf \land gf∧g that factors through fff and ggg, in the sense that

  • f∧gf \land gf∧g factors through fff and ggg, and
  • any hhh that factors through fff and ggg also factors through f∧gf \land gf∧g.

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Aug 20 2022 at 08:40):

(This would be implied by Δd\Delta_dΔd​ having pullbacks, which is the same as Δd/(n)\Delta_d / (n)Δd​/(n) having products for all (n)(n)(n), but it is strictly weaker, and in fact the stronger statement does not hold).

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Aug 20 2022 at 08:46):

Then f∧gf \land gf∧g factors through fff (possibly non-uniquely): take any sequence of n+1n+1n+1 surjective maps (max(fi,gi))→(fi)(max(f_i, g_i)) \to (f_i)(max(fi​,gi​))→(fi​), and concatenate them together to get a surjective map f1:(m)→(m1)f_1: (m) \to (m_1)f1​:(m)→(m1​) such that f∧g=f1;ff \land g = f_1 ; ff∧g=f1​;f.
Similarly, f∧gf \land gf∧g factors through ggg (possibly non-uniquely).

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Aug 20 2022 at 08:49):

Let h:(m′)→(n)h: (m') \to (n)h:(m′)→(n) be any other map that factors through both fff and ggg, and is represented by the sequence (h0,…,hn)(h_0, \ldots, h_n)(h0​,…,hn​).

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Aug 20 2022 at 08:53):

Then it is straightforward to see that hi≥fih_i \geq f_ihi​≥fi​ and hi≥gih_i \geq g_ihi​≥gi​ for all iii (this is essentially the fact that the size of inverse images of an element can only increase by precomposition with a surjective map)

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Aug 20 2022 at 08:54):

It follows that hi≥max(fi,gi)h_i \geq max(f_i, g_i)hi​≥max(fi​,gi​) for all iii. Then taking any sequence of n+1n+1n+1 surjective maps (hi)→(max(fi,gi))(h_i) \to (max(f_i, g_i))(hi​)→(max(fi​,gi​)) and concatenating them together, we get a map k:(m′)→(m)k: (m') \to (m)k:(m′)→(m) such that h=k;(f∧g)h = k; (f \land g)h=k;(f∧g), and that completes the proof.

So what follows is that if f∗zf^* zf∗z and g∗zg^* zg∗z are degenerate simplices coming from the same simplex, (f∧g)∗z(f \land g)^* z(f∧g)∗z is the “smallest unifier” in this sense: if h∗zh^* zh∗z is a degeneracy of both f∗zf^* zf∗z and g∗zg^* zg∗z, then it is a degeneracy of (f∧g)∗z(f \land g)^* z(f∧g)∗z.
