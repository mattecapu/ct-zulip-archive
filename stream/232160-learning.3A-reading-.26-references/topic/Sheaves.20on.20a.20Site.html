Benjamin Merlin Bumpus (he/him) (Apr 02 2024 at 15:26):

Hi everyone!
I'm quite comfortable with the definition of sheaves on a category CCC equipped with a coverage KKK. I know that, when thinking of sheaves on a topological space XXX, these can be identified with Etále spaces. Now my question is: is there a nice analogue for this construction for sites: given a sheaf F ⁣:Cop→SetF \colon C^{\mathsf{op}} \to SetF:Cop→Set on a site (C,K)(C, K)(C,K), can I always write this as a morphism of sites (E,KE)→(C,K)(E, K_E) \to (C, K)(E,KE​)→(C,K)? Something like this must exist, but I don't know what to look up, any help would be greatly appreciated :)

Morgan Rogers (he/him) (Apr 02 2024 at 15:59):

Riccard Zanfa's PhD thesis deals with this at a very high level. For a more elementary take, you should know that for a presheaf XXX on C\mathcal{C}C, PSh(C)/X\mathrm{PSh}(\mathcal{C})/XPSh(C)/X is equivalent to the category of presheaves over the category of elements of XXX, and by pulling back you can deduce that one choice of site for Sh(C,K)/X\mathrm{Sh}(\mathcal{C},K)/XSh(C,K)/X is the category of sheaves for a topology on the category of elements of XXX derived from KKK. This is made explicit in Caramello's work somewhere (possibly in her "Denseness conditions..." paper)

identified with _Etále_ spaces

Since we're in the learning stream: the word is étalé, three syllables, meaning something like "laid out flat".

Let me know if you plan a deep-delve into the resources Morgan pointed out. My PhD thesis has a section giving a nonstandard-analytic treatment of sheaves on topological spaces, relying heavily on étalé spaces. This prompted questions about whether similar concepts could extend to sheaves on a site. I couldn't pursue this line of research at the time, and one of my stumbling blocks was my lack of clarity on how to do étalé spaces without ordinary topology. It might be time to revisit this question.

Rémy Tuyéras (Apr 03 2024 at 11:32):

Just a note on the naming thing: I believe the adjective "étale" is the word that we want to use when talking about topologies (as in "mer étale", a completly flat sea). The adjective étalé is a different word with a similar meaning:
Étale = slack
Étalé = spread
