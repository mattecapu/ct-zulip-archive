Evan Washington (May 05 2024 at 17:39):

Jacob Zelko said:

It was found and proven by Monk in $1961$ that the calculus of relations did not have a complete axiomatisation and was therefore less expressive than first order logic. What does it mean for something to have a "complete axiomatisation"?

By "the calculus of relations" here, all we mean is the equational theory of relation algebras given by Tarski (and his students). By a "theory" I just mean a set of first-order sentences closed under logical consequence. For a theory TTT to have a complete axiomatization, we usually mean an "effective" axiomatization. That is, we want to give a finite or recursively enumerable set of sentences that have as their logical consequence the entire theory TTT.

Here's where the connection with first-order logic comes in. The calculus of relations is actually equivalent to first-order logic with at most three variables. But it turns out this fragment is really powerful: within it you can already express Peano arithmetic! But by Gödel's incompleteness theorem, no consistent theory that can express Peano arithmetic can have an effective complete axiomatization.

Evan Washington (May 05 2024 at 17:42):

Also note that Quine's predicate functor logic (another attempt to algebraicize first order logic) has nothing to do with functors or adjoints, but is just an unfortunate coincidence of terminology.

Zoltan A. Kocsis (Z.A.K.) (May 06 2024 at 00:10):

@Evan Washington , you write:

we want to give a finite or recursively enumerable set of sentences that have as their logical consequence the entire theory T.

Alas, I find this a bit misleading. The equational theory $T$ of relation algebras _does_ admit a finite set of sentences $\Sigma$ so that $T \vdash \varphi$ precisely if $\Sigma \vdash \varphi$ - indeed it is outright defined using such a set of sentences by Tarski.

What does not admit a finite equational axiomatization is the set of equations that hold in precisely the representable relation algebras, i.e. the ones where the underlying set is a family of binary relations on some set, and the operations are interpreted as the usual Boolean or relational operations. One can prove this using simple model-theoretic machinery without invoking Goedel.

I think this is what the authors mean when they write that "there is no finite complete axiomatisations for the whole calculus". In any case, this is the only thing established in the reference they give, which is good, since the common equational theory of representable relation algebras is recursively enumerable.

Zoltan A. Kocsis (Z.A.K.) (May 06 2024 at 00:41):

Since we're in a learning thread, let me mention a neat technique for proving the absence of finite axiomatizability.

If you already know some infinite axiomatization Σ\SigmaΣ of your theory TTT, then it's usually easy to prove that TTT admits no finite axiomatization. All you have to prove is that no finite subset of Σ\SigmaΣ itself suffices to axiomatize TTT!

Why?

Well, assume that TTT admits some finite axiomatization using axioms A1,A2,…,AnA_1, A_2, \dots, A_nA1​,A2​,…,An​, possibly unrelated to the axioms already in Σ\SigmaΣ. Then the conjunction A1,∧A2,∧⋯∧AnA_1, \wedge A_2, \wedge \dots \wedge A_nA1​,∧A2​,∧⋯∧An​ is enough to prove any sentence in the set Σ\SigmaΣ. And vice versa, there is a proof ppp of the sentence A1,∧A2,∧⋯∧AnA_1, \wedge A_2, \wedge \dots \wedge A_nA1​,∧A2​,∧⋯∧An​ starting only from the axioms of Σ\SigmaΣ. But this latter proof ppp is finite, so it uses only a finitely subset Σp\Sigma_pΣp​ of the axioms in Σ\SigmaΣ.

So we have shown that a finite set of axioms Σp⊆Σ\Sigma_p \subseteq \SigmaΣp​⊆Σ is sufficient to prove A1,∧A2,∧⋯∧AnA_1, \wedge A_2, \wedge \dots \wedge A_nA1​,∧A2​,∧⋯∧An​, and in turn that the sentence A1,∧A2,∧⋯∧AnA_1, \wedge A_2, \wedge \dots \wedge A_nA1​,∧A2​,∧⋯∧An​ lets one prove any axiom in Σ\SigmaΣ (and hence any consequence of the theory TTT). This means that whenever TTT has _some_ finite axiomatization, then some Σp\Sigma_pΣp​ is by itself sufficient to prove all the other axioms in Σ\SigmaΣ.

So all you have to prove is that no finite subset of Σ\SigmaΣ itself suffices to axiomatize TTT to conclude that TTT has no finite axiomatization.

Exercise: use this observation to prove that the theory of fields in characteristic 0 (in e.g. the language (+,−,⋅,0,1)(+,-,\cdot,0,1)(+,−,⋅,0,1)) does not admit a finite axiomatization.

The "white" structure is what most people would probably think of as the "ordinary" structure forming the bicategory or double category of relations: the composite of relations has xS∘Rzx S\circ R zxS∘Rz if and only if for some y,y,y, we have xRyx RyxRy and ySz.ySz.ySz. So you can read more about this anywhere you want to read about relations in category theory; "Categories, Allegories" is one relevant text pretty narrowly focused there, while there's also the theory of cartesian bicategories and its generalization to double categories with finite products that my colleague Evan Patterson has been writing about recently.

The "black" structure is formally dual to the "white" structure in that the "black" composite of two relations is the complement of the "white" composite of their complements, so we have xS∙Rzx S\bullet R zxS∙Rz if for all y,y,y, either xRyx R yxRy or ySz.y S z.ySz.

Generally, a "copy" map is a map A→A⊗AA\to A\otimes AA→A⊗A and a "discard" map, a map A→IA\to IA→I, in some monoidal category, together making AAA into a commutative comonoid. We can interpret copying as duplicating a variable of type AAA and of discarding as throwing that variable away (it could be nice to think about why any reasonable copying and discarding operations should satisfy the axioms of a commutative comonoid.) This interpretation is apparent in the case at hand: as you say, the white copy map simply relates any xxx to the duplicate (x,x),(x,x),(x,x), for instance. Categories admitting copy and discard maps for all objects are, imaginatively, called copy-discard categories. It's critical that the copy-discard maps are not natural transformations, or in other words not every map in a CD-category is a homomorphism of commutative comonoids. There's an important theorem of Fox that says that, in fact, a CD-category which is "homomorphic" in this sense is just a cartesian monoidal category! So the category of (white) relations is a non-homomorphic CD-category; other examples come from categorical probability theory.
