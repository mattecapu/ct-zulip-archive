Vincent Moreau (Mar 14 2025 at 18:24):

I don't have a reference for you. Instead, let me explain how it comes from very general considerations about essentially algebraic theories. I'm hoping this is interesting to you nevertheless.

Let me write LMonL_{\mathbf{Mon}}LMon​ and LCatL_{\mathbf{Cat}}LCat​ for the essentially algebraic theories of monoids and categories respectively. These are the opposite of categories of finitely presented monoids and finitely presented categories. We see a monoid MMM as a finite limit-preserving functor

M:LMon ⟶ SetM \quad:\quad L_{\mathbf{Mon}} \ \longrightarrow\ \mathbf{Set}M:LMon​ ⟶ Set

Vincent Moreau (Mar 14 2025 at 18:31):

I define a category internal to LMonL_{\mathbf{Mon}}LMon​

  • whose finitely presented monoid of objects is the terminal one 000
  • whose finitely presented monoid of morphisms is the free one on one generator N\mathbb{N}N
  • whose composition is the morphism N→N+N≅{a,b}∗\mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{N} + \mathbb{N} \cong \{a, b\}^*N→N+N≅{a,b}∗ which sends 1 on the word ababab

and the rest of the data is determined. This yields a finite limit-preserving functor

P:LCat ⟶ LMonP \quad:\quad L_{\mathbf{Cat}} \ \longrightarrow\ L_{\mathbf{Mon}}P:LCat​ ⟶ LMon​

The precomposition functor (−)∘P(-) \circ P(−)∘P takes a monoid MMM and produces a category

  • whose set of objects is Mon(0,M)\mathbf{Mon}(0, M)Mon(0,M), i.e. a singleton
  • whose set of morphisms is Mon(N,M)\mathbf{Mon}(\mathbb{N}, M)Mon(N,M), i.e. MMM
  • whose composition is the one of MMM given the identification just mentionned

Therefore, (−)∘P(-) \circ P(−)∘P is the functor Mon→Cat\mathbf{Mon} \to \mathbf{Cat}Mon→Cat that you are interested in. Now, for any such finite limit-preserving PPP, the precomposition functor (−)∘P(-) \circ P(−)∘P has a left adjoint, which is the left Kan extension along PPP -- indeed, it preserves the preservation of finite limits. This is the left adjoint you mentioned!
