The short answer is: the homotopy fiber of a functor F:A→XF: A \to XF:A→X over a point xxx is the category of ways you can get xxx (up to isomorphism) by applying the functor FFF to some object of AAA. The "up to isomorphism" part is what makes it a homotopy fiber instead of an ordinary fiber.

In homotopy theory we are often interested in a map F:A→XF: A \to XF:A→X between topological spaces, and then the homotopy fiber of a point x∈Xx \in Xx∈X consists of points a∈Aa \in Aa∈A that don't need to have F(a)=xF(a) = xF(a)=x "on the nose": instead, it's enough to have a continuous path from F(a)F(a)F(a) to xxx.

So for any functor F:A→XF:A\to XF:A→X and x∈Xx\in Xx∈X, the homotopy fiber over xxx has an action by the group AutD(x){\rm Aut}_D(x)AutD​(x). Namely, given an isomorphism F(a)≅xF(a) \cong xF(a)≅x, we can compose it with any automorphism of xxx to get another isomorphism F(a)≅xF(a) \cong xF(a)≅x. Thus, if FFF is faithful and conservative, so that this homotopy fiber is equivalent to a set, it is moreover an AutD(x){\rm Aut}_D(x)AutD​(x)-set. Hence, like any set with a group action, it is a disjoint union of orbits, each of which is a homogeneous space. And if the action happens to be transitive, then it is a single orbit, i.e. a homogeneous space.

Transitivity of the action means directly that if we have ϕ:F(a)≅x\phi : F(a) \cong xϕ:F(a)≅x and ϕ′:F(a′)≅x\phi' : F(a') \cong xϕ′:F(a′)≅x, there is ψ:a≅a′\psi : a\cong a'ψ:a≅a′ and χ:x≅x\chi : x\cong xχ:x≅x making a commutative square. But then χ\chiχ is uniquely determined by the other three data, and if this holds for all xxx then the hypothesis might as well be just that F(a)≅F(a′)F(a)\cong F(a')F(a)≅F(a′). So, if FFF is faithful and conservative, then its homotopy fibers are homogeneous spaces if it is injective on isomorphism classes.

Felix Klein massively generalized Euclidean and various known non-Euclidean geometries by saying that you get different kinds of geometry from different groups.
