Here is another puzzle on rigs. I don't know how to solve it or make a program to solve it but maybe some will be entertained to try.

Define the characteristic of a rig RRR as the smallest integer n≥1n \ge 1n≥1 such that n.1R=0n.1_{R}=0n.1R​=0 if there exists such a nnn and 000 if there doesn't exist such a nnn. The characteristic of a finite ring is always >0>0>0. But it is not the case for a finite rig. For instance B={0,1}\mathbb{B}=\{0,1\}B={0,1} with 1+1=11+1=11+1=1 is finite and of characteristic 000, the only other rig of cardinal 222 is Z2\mathbb{Z}_{2}Z2​ and it is a ring so it is of characteristic >0>0>0 (it is 222). The zero rig is also a ring of characteristic >0> 0>0 (it is 111).

Question: Do we have a rig of cardinal 333 of characteristic 000? of cardinal 444?... Can you list them?

Oscar Cunningham (Mar 15 2023 at 08:49):

Any rig has a canonical map from N\Bbb NN that sends nnn to 1+⋯+11+\dots+11+⋯+1 (nnn times). The image of this map is a quotient rig of N\Bbb NN. The possible quotients of N\Bbb NN are all of the form 'quotient together nnn and n+pn+pn+p for all n≥Nn\geq Nn≥N' for some ppp and NNN. I think it makes more sense to define the characteristic to be this ppp, rather than setting it to be 000 whenever N>0N>0N>0.

In other words, take 000 in your rig and start repeatedly adding 111. If the values you get start to repeat then the characteristic is the period with which they repeat, and otherwise it's 0.

Oscar Cunningham (Mar 15 2023 at 08:51):

For example, this would make B\Bbb BB have characteristic 111 rather than 000.

Morgan Rogers (he/him) (Mar 15 2023 at 18:11):

I have only just seen this, but I wish I had gotten here sooner: in my opinion the correct generalization of characteristic for rigs is the pair of numbers determining the subrig generated by 1, which is necessarily a quotient of N\NN. A nice indexing is for characteristic (a,b)(a,b)(a,b) with a≥1a\geq 1a≥1 and b≥0b\geq 0b≥0 to denote {0,...,a+b−1}\{0,...,a+b-1\}{0,...,a+b−1}, with addition and multiplication reduced modulo aaa into the range {b,...,a+b−1}\{b,...,a+b-1\}{b,...,a+b−1}. This is nice because it means that the existence of a rig homomorphism from a rig of characteristic (a,b)(a,b)(a,b) to one of characteristic (a′,b′)(a',b')(a′,b′) forces a′∣aa' | aa′∣a and b′≤bb' \leq bb′≤b. We can extend this with characteristic (1,∞)(1,\infty)(1,∞) for the case of the natural numbers embedding into the rig.
