Beware of your intuition here. There is at most one ring homomorphism from Q\mathbb{Q}Q, and since this is dense in R\RR, a continuous ring homomorphism from R\RR is unique if it exists. John Baez (Dec 12 2022 at 14:34):

Here's something vaguely, not precisely, related to the question of whether there can be more than one ring homomorphism f:R→Rf: \mathbb{R} \to Rf:R→R for some ring RRR.

John Baez (Dec 12 2022 at 14:37):

There's a concept of [[real closed field]]. Besides the real numbers R\mathbb{R}R we can find another real closed field R′\mathbb{R}'R′, not isomorphic to R\mathbb{R}R, with the same cardinality. We can build the complex numbers C\mathbb{C}C using pairs of real numbers. We can use the exact same formula to build a field C′\mathbb{C}'C′ using pairs of guys in R′\mathbb{R}'R′. But it’s easy to check that this funny field C′\mathbb{C}'C′ is algebraically closed and of characteristic zero. Since it has the same cardinality as C\mathbb{C}C, it must be isomorphic to C\mathbb{C}C.

John Baez (Dec 12 2022 at 14:38):

So, C\mathbb{C}C contains real closed fields other than R\mathbb{R}R.

Jean-Baptiste Vienney (Dec 12 2022 at 15:28):

And what is this R′\mathbb{R}'R′? Does it have a name?

John Baez (Dec 12 2022 at 21:17):

I believe there are many real closed fields with same cardinality as the reals. You can use any one as R′\mathbb{R}'R′. For example, you can use the field of Puiseaux series with real coefficients. You can also use the field of hyperreal numbers.
