In Mike Shulman's expository paper about linear logic, he defines 'affine triposes' as follows:
affine-tripos.png
I have two questions regarding this

  1. Why wasn't 'cartesian' replaced with 'monoidal' throughout?
  2. Is there an equivalent to the Pitts construction for these triposes, i.e. a construction of some category whose internal theory coincides with the starting tripos? Mike Shulman (Jun 09 2021 at 13:07):

Re 1, there's only one remaining occurrence of "cartesian", in the assumption that T\mathbf{T}T is cartesian closed. If T\mathbf{T}T were only monoidal closed, there would be no "product projections" with respect to which to formulate the third bullet point. One could imagine taking T\mathbf{T}T to be only semicartesian, but I didn't feel like going down that rabbit hole. In particular, usually in first-order linear/affine logic the quantifiers are regarded as wholly additive, which suggests that the base category should be cartesian monoidal.

Mike Shulman (Jun 09 2021 at 13:09):

Re 2, I thought about that a bit, but wasn't able to make sense of it. There is a construction of a category of what you might call "affine sets", but it doesn't carry all the information of the original affine tripos, and in particular there's no obvious sense in which the affine predicates are incarnated as "subobjects" in it.

Mike Shulman (Jun 09 2021 at 13:43):

BTW, I'm actually revising this paper at the very moment, so if there's something interesting to say here I might incorporate it. In particular, in response to referee comments I'm generalizing the semantic context to deal with intuitionistic and affine hyperdoctrines rather than just triposes, where the fiber categories may not be posets and there may not be a generic predicate. Also, the "standard interpretation" is being renamed the "antithesis translation".

Mike Shulman (Jun 09 2021 at 13:49):

For the purposes of this paper I'm not hugely interested in generalizing to semicartesian T\mathbf{T}T, since the main point is to study the antithesis translation applied to intuitionistic logic, and this always starts with a cartesian T\mathbf{T}T and preserves it. However, if there's something interesting and not too difficult to say, it might be worth mentioning.

Mike Shulman (Jun 09 2021 at 14:54):

On the other hand, (3) could be replaced with a more agnostic requirement that every map f∗=T(f,Ω)f^* = T(f, \Omega)f∗=T(f,Ω) has both adjoints.

A problem with this is how do you formulate a Beck-Chevalley condition? In the cartesian case, the BC condition for adjoints of an arbitrary f∗f^*f∗ refers to arbitrary pullback squares. When restricted to product projections this is sensible, since the pullback of a product projection is a product projection. And the product projection version generalizes to the semicartesian case directly, but the squares in question are no longer pullbacks. But in a noncartesian case with adjoints for non-projections, which squares should satisfy the BC condition?
