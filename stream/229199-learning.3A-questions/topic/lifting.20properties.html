Reid Barton (Aug 25 2022 at 15:19):

This was already basically answered on Twitter, but the simplest example from topology would be that we have a map p:X→Yp : X \to Yp:X→Y, and we want to lift a path γ\gammaγ in YYY to a path in XXX that starts at a specified lift of the initial point of γ\gammaγ. Diagrammatically, that is a condition involving a square with {0}→[0,1]\{0\} \to [0, 1]{0}→[0,1] and p:X→Yp : X \to Yp:X→Y as its two sides.

Reid Barton (Aug 25 2022 at 15:35):

Note that the condition is about iii and ppp, and quantifies over all fff and ggg. If AAA is the initial object, so that the diagram reduces to the one you drew, it would still be a condition about ppp that quantifies over all ggg, not a condition about all ppp and ggg.

John Baez (Aug 25 2022 at 21:01):

However as Barton pointed out, in topology (and elsewhere) we also often want to look at a combination of lifting and extension. His example is the fundamental one: we are trying to lift a path in YYY to a path in XXX, but we have already chosen how to lift the starting point of that path, so we are extending that lift to a lift of the whole path.

Mike Shulman (Aug 26 2022 at 15:13):

A bimodule (endo- or not) between monads M, N in Span(Set)\rm Span(Set)Span(Set) is a [[profunctor]] Mop×N→SetM^{op}\times N\to \rm SetMop×N→Set. A bimodule in Span(C){\rm Span}(C)Span(C) is an [[internal profunctor]] in CCC. A one-sided module is the same as a bimodule with a trivial monad on one side.
