Agreed, it's definitely not symmetric in UUU and TTT!

If TTT and UUU are both the free monoid monad Set→Set\mathbf{Set} \to \mathbf{Set}Set→Set, then I think the pullback should be the identity monad - an element would have to be a pair of lists (l1:List(X),l2:List(X))(l_1 : \text{List}(X), l_2 : \text{List}(X))(l1​:List(X),l2​:List(X)) such that [l1]=List([−])(l2)[l_1] = \text{List}([-])(l_2)[l1​]=List([−])(l2​), which I believe is equivalent to requiring that both l1l_1l1​ and l2l_2l2​ are equal singleton lists (with a single element of XXX) by injectivity of [−][-][−]. I think the free group monad should be the same story?

If TTT is the free group monad and UUU is some open modality on a topos (cf RSS), though, the story is more interesting - the pullback classifies group syntax that collapses in the open subtopos to a generator, eg strings like x1⋅x2−1⋅yx_1 \cdot x_2^{-1} \cdot yx1​⋅x2−1​⋅y that cancel to just yyy in the open subtopos (assuming x1=x2x_1 = x_2x1​=x2​ in the open subtopos).

For convenience denote by SSS the pullback of idT⊗ηU\mathrm{id}_T \otimes \eta_UidT​⊗ηU​ and ηT⊗idU\eta_T \otimes \mathrm{id}_UηT​⊗idU​, and denote by pT:S→Tp_T : S \to TpT​:S→T and pU:S→Up_U: S \to UpU​:S→U the projections.

For each nnn, we have an nnn-ary map tn:S⊗…⊗S→Tt_n : S \otimes \ldots \otimes S \to Ttn​:S⊗…⊗S→T obtained by composing pT⊗…⊗pTp_T \otimes \ldots \otimes p_TpT​⊗…⊗pT​ with the nnn-ary multiplication for TTT. Similarly, we have an nnn-ary map un:S⊗…⊗S→Uu_n : S \otimes \ldots \otimes S \to Uun​:S⊗…⊗S→U.

Hence we can form maps tn⊗um:S⊗…⊗S→T⊗Ut_n \otimes u_m: S \otimes \ldots \otimes S \to T \otimes Utn​⊗um​:S⊗…⊗S→T⊗U. Observe tn⊗um=tn′⊗um′t_n \otimes u_m = t_{n'} \otimes u_{m'}tn​⊗um​=tn′​⊗um′​ whenever n+m=n′+m′n + m = n' + m'n+m=n′+m′, by repeatedly introducing units and applying the defining equation for the pullback.

In particular, for each nnn,
tn;(idT⊗ηU)=tn⊗ηU=tn⊗u0=t0⊗un=ηT⊗un=un;(ηT⊗idU)t_n ; (\mathrm{id}_T \otimes \eta_U) = t_n \otimes \eta_U = t_n \otimes u_0 = t_0 \otimes u_n = \eta_T \otimes u_n = u_n ; (\eta_T \otimes \mathrm{id}_U)tn​;(idT​⊗ηU​)=tn​⊗ηU​=tn​⊗u0​=t0​⊗un​=ηT​⊗un​=un​;(ηT​⊗idU​).
By the universal property of the pullback, we thus obtain a unique map μn:S⊗…⊗S→S\mu_n: S \otimes \ldots \otimes S \to Sμn​:S⊗…⊗S→S such that μn;pT=tn\mu_n ; p_T = t_nμn​;pT​=tn​ and μn;pU=un\mu_n ; p_U = u_nμn​;pU​=un​. This will be the nnn-ary multiplication for SSS.

The nnn-ary associativity/unit laws say that every nnn-ary map S⊗…⊗S→SS \otimes \ldots \otimes S \to SS⊗…⊗S→S obtained as a tree composed of μi\mu_iμi​ maps is itself μn\mu_nμn​. Indeed, any such tree μn′\mu'_nμn′​ composed of μi\mu_iμi​ maps satisfies μn′;pT=tn\mu'_n ; p_T = t_nμn′​;pT​=tn​ and μn′;pU=un\mu'_n ; p_U = u_nμn′​;pU​=un​, using the associativity/unit laws for TTT and UUU, and so μn′=μn\mu'_n = \mu_nμn′​=μn​ by uniqueness of the pullback factoring map.

The reason I was interested in this originally was from a conversation with Peter Selinger where we were just looking at the case of monads on Set and where T=1T = 1T=1, the terminal monad. So, we were looking at the pullback of η:1→U(1)\eta: 1 \to U(1)η:1→U(1) and U(!):U(X)→U(1)U(!): U(X) \to U(1)U(!):U(X)→U(1).

From the powerset monad we get the "nonempty powerset" monad sending a set to the set of its nonempty subsets. As I think is being discussed by @Harrison Grodin and @Mike Shulman in greater generality (but I don't yet understand that level of generality), from the free group monad we get a monad corresponding to an algebraic theory where the operations are group words such that the number of inverted letters is one less than the number of non-inverted letters.
