Matteo Capucci (he/him) (Oct 14 2020 at 11:08):

For every field KKK, there's an adjunction F:Set⇆KVect:UF : \mathbf{Set} \leftrightarrows K\mathbf{Vect} : UF:Set⇆KVect:U where

  • UUU is the forgetful functors: it sends a vector space to its underlying set (it 'forgets' the additional structure) and a linear map to its underlying set function
  • FFF is the 'free vector space' functor: it sends a set XXX to the vector space KXK^XKX of KKK-linear combinations of elements of XXX.

The adjunction leans on the fact that a KKK-linear combination of elements of XXX is really a map of sets X→KX \to KX→K. This has the consequence that to give a map X→U(V)X \to U(V)X→U(V) is equivalent to give a map F(X)→VF(X) \to VF(X)→V, and viceversa (one says the two maps are transposes of each other), so F⊣UF \dashv UF⊣U.
Now, every adjunction gives rise to a monad on the 'left' (= the domain of the left adjoint) and a comonad on the 'right' (= the domain of the right adjoint).

To describe them, we need to introduce the unit and counit of an adjunction. The first, η:1→UF\eta : 1 \to UFη:1→UF, is gotten by choosing ηX:X→UF(X)\eta_X : X \to UF(X)ηX​:X→UF(X) to be the transpose of 1F(X):F(X)→F(X)1_{F(X)} : F(X) \to F(X)1F(X)​:F(X)→F(X), for each X:SetX : \mathbf{Set}X:Set. The second, ε:FU→1\varepsilon : FU \to 1ε:FU→1, is just the dual construction: pick εV:FU(V)→V\varepsilon_V : FU(V) \to VεV​:FU(V)→V to be the transpose of 1U(V):U(V)→U(V)1_{U(V)} : U(V) \to U(V)1U(V)​:U(V)→U(V).
So then the monad and comonad you get are (UF,η,UεF)(UF, \eta, U\varepsilon F)(UF,η,UεF) on Set\mathbf{Set}Set and (FU,ε,FηU)(FU, \varepsilon, F \eta U)(FU,ε,FηU) on KVectK\mathbf{Vect}KVect. They satisfy the required laws by virtue of the so-called 'triangle identities', which are identities which characterize the unit and counit of an adjunction.

John Baez (Oct 14 2020 at 16:04):

Matteo Capucci said:

  • FFF is the 'free vector space' functor: it sends a set XXX to the vector space KXK^XKX of KKK-linear combinations of elements of XXX.

[...] a KKK-linear combination of elements of XXX is really a map of sets X→KX \to KX→K.

By the way, KXK^XKX is only the free vector space on XXX when XXX is a finite set. In algebra, "linear combination" means finite linear combination, so only when XXX is finite can we treat every map of sets X→KX \to KX→K as a KKK-linear combination of elements of XXX.

In the general case the free vector space on XXX , which I call K[X]K[X]K[X], can be identified with the vector space of functions X→KX \to KX→K that vanish except at finitely many points.

Any function X→X′X \to X'X→X′ gives a linear map K[X]→K[X′]K[X] \to K[X']K[X]→K[X′] and also a linear map KX′→KXK^{X'} \to K^{X}KX′→KX. So we have a covariant and a contravariant way to turn a set into a vector space, but for finite vector spaces these can be identified, so we get covariant and contravariant functors FinSet→FinVect\mathsf{FinSet} \to \mathsf{FinVect}FinSet→FinVect that agree on objects... very handy!

Morgan Rogers (he/him) (Oct 15 2020 at 09:09):

Succinctly, no. Specifying the data as you have is equivalent to giving a function X→U(Rm)X \to U(\mathbb{R}^m)X→U(Rm), where UUU is the forgetful functor Vect→Set\mathbf{Vect} \to \mathbf{Set}Vect→Set as above. The fact that this functor is the right adjoint in an adjunction means that this function must factor through the unit map X→UF(X)X \to UF(X)X→UF(X) along (the underlying function of) a linear map UF(X)→U(Rm)UF(X) \to U(\mathbb{R}^m)UF(X)→U(Rm); specifically, this is the function which sends ∑i=1naixi\sum_{i=1}^n a_i x_i∑i=1n​ai​xi​ to that same sum viewed as an element of Rm\mathbb{R}^mRm. This is purely linear algebra; all of the functions involved completely ignore the dynamic structure of the time series XXX.

Takens' theorem uses ideas from Whitney's embedding theorem for embedding finite-dimensional real manifolds into higher dimensional Euclidean space. This result lies in differential geometry, and the linear structure of Rk\mathbb{R}^kRk is not really relevant here.

David Michael Roberts (Oct 15 2020 at 10:08):

Functorial wrt what maps? A cheap way to get an embedding of a compact manifold in a Euclidean space is to take a partition of unity subordinate to a good (say) open cover by charts. A good open cover by charts is more-or-less equivalent to a groupoid internal to the coproduct completion of the category Cart of cartesian spaces. The category of manifolds is some kind of colimit completion of this (eg inside the category of diffeological spaces = concrete sheaves on Cart). Alternatively, one can think of maps between manifolds as being certain anafunctors between these groupoids. It's presumably possible to think of some way of getting data including partitions of unity into this picture. Then the embedding of the manifold represented by one of these groupoids with a partition of unity could be constructed directly from the groupoid. The naive thing, assuming the partitions of unity are respected on the nose by anafunctors presumably loses too much. But I can imagine having some modulating data that allows for more flexibility.
Just some random thoughts.

Morgan Rogers (he/him) (Oct 18 2020 at 09:32):

Step 1: Find the right categorical setting.

  • What category/categories do the objects your working with live in? This will be informed by the class of objects involved, but also:
  • What transformations are involved in your formalism? Are they morphisms within the categories, or transformations from one category to another? In the latter case, are they functorial (transformations not just of objects but also of morphisms)?
  • It may be that you need not just categories but enriched categories, depending on whether you rely on any geometric or algebraic structure in your morphisms.

Step 2: Express the process categorically.

  • Most constructions in classical maths involve sets, elements, points etc. Working out what morphisms or categorical constructions these correspond to can take some creativity.
  • Steps 1 and 2 may be iterative; if you come to some obstacle to expressing a step categorically, you may need to adjust the setting to compensate.

Step 3: Identify the key ingredients and distil them.

  • At this point, set aside the specific categories you're working with and see what categorical features you actually used in your construction. Is one of the functors part of an adjunction? Is one of the morphisms you use a unit or counit for some adjunction (this could be where you find a monad, or indeed a comonad)? Do you use some canonical maps to limits or from colimits? Is there some kind of tensor product involved? Do you assume the commutativity of some diagram when performing the operations involved? Does it rely on certain morphisms being monomorphisms or epimorphisms?
  • With answers to these questions in hand, you should be able to throw away all of the earlier stuff, because in any categorical setting having all of the ingredients you've just extracted, your original argument will be valid.

Step 4: Find the relevant literature.

  • People have already performed this process for a lot of different starting situations. Once you've gotten to Step 3, you can identify whether there is an existing categorical framework into which your argument fits. If you needed any kind of tensor product, you might be looking for some class of monoidal categories. If you needed to work with enriched categories, maybe someone has studied the same (or a comparable) enrichment before.
  • You cannot skip steps 1, 2 and 3 to get straight to Step 4 unless you happen to find someone who has applied category theory to exactly the same situation as you. You need to know exactly how much categorical structure you need or you'll either be carrying around useless baggage or won't have enough structure to perform your argument.
  • The categorical setting will not be unique: there might be several ways of expressing your argument categorically; but making any progress requires finding at least one.

There are steps beyond Step 4, regarding what you can do once you have a categorified version of your argument or construction in hand, but there's no point getting too far ahead. To give you fair warning: it's entirely possible that you could do the work of Steps 1 to 3 and find at Step 4 that from a categorical perspective, what's happening is a common phenomenon. This might seem disappointing at first, but:

  1. Congratulations! You've consolidated some mathematics, showing that a particular situation is a special case of something already well-known. You immediately have a wealth of examples which you can use your original situation to help you understand.
  2. Congratulations! You've put in the effort and learned some category theory relevant to some mathematics you are interested in.
  3. Congratulations! You have a broader perspective on your original situation, and the limitations of it. It will be much easier to see what extra ingredients you need to extend the categorical framework to perform further constructions or calculations.

John Baez (Oct 18 2020 at 15:53):

Yes, this sort of process often takes time. It's fun to recall examples:

When @Brendan Fong and I came up with a category-theoretic description of electrical circuits, I thought it would be easy since circuit diagrams look like string diagrams, a well-known tool in category theory. I worked out the basic idea in May 2010 here. But there were some "details" that seemed hard to check, so I gave this as a thesis project to Brendan. He developed a new technique, the theory of decorated cospans and decorated corelations, to fill in these "details". By February 2015 he finished a paper on decorated cospans and by April 2015 we came out with a paper solving the problem: A compositional framework for passive linear networks.

However, Brendan later realized that more use of decorated corelations would help explain some mysterious ad hoc steps in our construction. Our paper took a long time to referee, and when we finally got the referee's report, which demanded extensive changes, we rewrote the paper to include a lot more material on decorated corelations. The result was a much deeper paper, which we finished in November 2018. Also in 2018 Brendan came out with a a paper on decorated corelations.

It later turned out that there were some problems with the decorated cospan framework, which can be fixed in a number of ways. My favorite way is the theory of structured cospans, which @Kenny Courser did his thesis on. He finished his thesis in August 2020, and we wrote a paper on structured cospans based on this material, which is close to being published - we finished the first version in November 2019 but we're almost done dealing with the referee's corrections now, and with luck the editor will accept this new version!

This seems like a lot of work to figure out what's going on with electrical circuits - over a decade! Was it really worthwhile? I think so. Electrical circuits are interesting and it's good to understand them clearly. Further, the ideas apply to many other subjects: Brendan, @Blake Pollard and I wrote a paper applying them to Markov processes, Blake and I wrote a paper applying them to chemical reaction networks, @Jade Master and I wrote a paper applying them to Petri nets, and there are many more applications waiting to be developed.
