Topic: Monoidal category question

John Baez (Jul 24 2024 at 22:52):

@Jean-Baptiste Vienney raised a fun question which I posted to MathOverflow. Check it out:

  • Are there noncartesian monoidal categories with A⊗B=A×B?

John Baez (Jul 24 2024 at 22:54):

I think I've answered it: yes. Let me test out the answer on you folks.

First think about the category of affine spaces over some field. This famously has both a cartesian monoidal structure ×\times× and a semicartesian monoidal structure ⊗\otimes⊗: the latter is explained here.

John Baez (Jul 24 2024 at 22:57):

Let's look at the full subcategory C\mathsf{C}C of affine spaces that are either terminal (the one-element affine space) or of countably infinite dimension, by which I mean they're a coproduct of a countably infinite number of copies of the terminal affine space. I claim that for all objects A,B∈CA, B \in \mathsf{C}A,B∈C we have

A⊗B≅A×B A \otimes B \cong A \times BA⊗B≅A×B

John Baez (Jul 24 2024 at 22:58):

I also claim C\mathsf{C}C is a monoidal subcategory with respect to either ×\times× or ⊗\otimes⊗: for example, the cartesian or tensor product of two affine spaces of countably infinite dimension again has countably infinite dimension.

John Baez (Jul 24 2024 at 23:01):

Now take a skeleton S\mathsf{S}S of C\mathsf{C}C. We can transfer both the ×\times× and ⊗\otimes⊗ monoidal structures from C\mathsf{C}C to this skeleton, and since we had

A⊗B≅A×BA \otimes B \cong A \times BA⊗B≅A×B in C\mathsf{C}C

we have

A⊗B=A×B A \otimes B = A \times BA⊗B=A×B in S\mathsf{S}S

Concretely, C\mathsf{C}C has just two objects, the terminal object 111 and the affine space of countably infinite dimension, xxx. These have

1×x=x×1=x,x×x=x1 \times x = x \times 1 = x, x \times x = x1×x=x×1=x,x×x=x

and also

1⊗x=x⊗1=x,x⊗x=x 1 \otimes x = x \otimes 1 = x, x \otimes x = x1⊗x=x⊗1=x,x⊗x=x

John Baez (Jul 24 2024 at 23:05):

John Baez (Jul 25 2024 at 06:32):

The nLab link I gave describes the tensor product of two finite-dimensional affine spaces quite explicitly, and the same basic idea works in general. We get this description using the fact that

1) every affine space is free on some set XXX, so call it F(X)F(X)F(X)
2) F(X)⊗F(Y)≅F(X×Y)F(X) \otimes F(Y) \cong F(X \times Y) F(X)⊗F(Y)≅F(X×Y)

This should remind you immensely of how vector spaces work. But you have to adjust your intuitions a bit, remembering that in an affine space we can only form affine combinations, not more general linear combinations, and in particular an affine space doesn't have an element called 000.

Thus, the free vector space on ∅\emptyset∅ has one point, while the free affine space on ∅\emptyset∅ is empty. Similarly the free vector space on 111 is kkk (your chosen field), while the free affine space on 111 is a one-element set. The free vector space on 222 is k2k^2k2, while the free affine space on 222 is a line containing the two points of 222. The free vector space on 333 is k3k^3k3, while the free affine space on 333 is a plane containing the three points of 333, which form the vertices of a triangle. And so on.

John Baez (Jul 25 2024 at 08:25):

Using this idea (which extends to the free affine space on an infinite set) and 2), we can understand a lot about the tensor product of affine spaces.

John Baez (Jul 25 2024 at 11:18):

Affine spaces are known to have a semicartesian tensor product ⊗\otimes⊗ that's not cartesian, so the unit object for ⊗\otimes⊗ is the terminal object, so it's same as the unit object for ×\times×.

Vector spaces don't have any semicartesian tensor product that I know of: in particular, the unit object for the usual ⊗\otimes⊗ of vector spaces is not the terminal vector space.

John Baez (Jul 25 2024 at 11:27):

So, my plan was this: start with the category of affine spaces with its two tensor products ⊗\otimes⊗ and ×\times×. They share the same unit object. Next, try to make them equal on all objects. The two different tensor products of finite-dimensional affine spaces aren't isomorphic since they have different dimension. But:

  • if AAA and BBB have countably infinite dimension then A×B≅A⊗BA \times B \cong A \otimes BA×B≅A⊗B since they have the same dimension.

  • if 111 is the terminal affine space we have 1×A≅1⊗A1 \times A \cong 1 \otimes A1×A≅1⊗A since ⊗\otimes⊗ is semicartesian.

  • also 1×1≅1⊗11 \times 1 \cong 1 \otimes 11×1≅1⊗1

John Baez (Jul 25 2024 at 11:29):

So let's consider the full subcategory C\mathsf{C}C of the category of affine spaces consisting only of terminal and countably-infinite-dimensional affine spaces! For any objects A,B∈CA, B \in \mathsf{C}A,B∈C we have

A×B≅A⊗BA \times B \cong A \otimes BA×B≅A⊗B

Further, C\mathsf{C}C is a monoidal subcategory of Aff\mathrm{Aff}Aff under either ⊗\otimes⊗ or ×\times×, since the product or tensor product of two affine spaces of countably infinite dimension again has countably infinite dimension.

(This is easy to see in terms of bases. To get a basis of A×BA \times BA×B we take the disjoint union of a basis of AAA and a basis of BBB. To get a basis of A⊗BA \otimes BA⊗B we take the cartesian product of a basis of AAA and a basis of BBB.)

Now let S\mathsf{S}S be a skeleton of C\mathsf{C}C. Now isomorphic objects become equal, so we get

A×B=A⊗BA \times B = A \otimes BA×B=A⊗B

for all objects of S\mathsf{S}S.
