Ralph Sarkis (Jul 26 2023 at 14:24):

The free category on a directed graph G=(V,E)G= (V,E)G=(V,E) with vertices VVV and edges EEE is the category whose objects are VVV and morphisms u→vu \to vu→v are path in GGG, that is, a finite sequence of edges such that the target of one edge is the source of the next one in the sequence, and the source (resp. target) of the first (resp. last) edge in the path is uuu (resp. vvv). Identities are empty paths, and composition is done by concatenating paths (sequences of edges). This should look a lot like taking the free monoid on the set of edges but taking care that edges can only be connected when the source and targets match.

Ralph Sarkis (Jul 26 2023 at 14:24):

Taking the free thin category on GGG would mean doing the exact same process, but quotienting by saying morphisms (paths) are equal when they have the same source and target. More concretely, the objects are still VVV, but now Hom(u,v)\mathrm{Hom}(u,v)Hom(u,v) is either empty or a singleton, it contains a morphism if and only if there is a path from uuu to vvv in GGG. Identities and composition are completely determined.

Ralph Sarkis (Jul 26 2023 at 14:25):

Taking as an example the graph representing a square (drawn below), the free category will have two diagonal morphisms (two different paths) but the free thing category will only have one.
image.png

Ralph Sarkis (Jul 26 2023 at 14:25):

Now, there is an evident functor F:FreeCat(G)→FreeThinCat(G)F: \mathbf{FreeCat}(G) \to \mathbf{FreeThinCat}(G)F:FreeCat(G)→FreeThinCat(G) that sends a path u→vu \to vu→v in the domain to the unique morphism u→vu \to vu→v in the codomain (that witnesses the existence of at least one path from uuu to vvv).

Ralph Sarkis (Jul 26 2023 at 14:25):

If you label the nodes and arrows of GGG with objects and morphisms from a category C\mathbf{C}C, this yields a functor D:FreeCat(G)→CD: \mathbf{FreeCat}(G) \to \mathbf{C}D:FreeCat(G)→C. By saying that this factors through the free thin category on GGG, I mean that there is a diagram D′:FreeThinCat(G)→CD': \mathbf{FreeThinCat}(G) \to \mathbf{C}D′:FreeThinCat(G)→C such that D′∘F=DD' \circ F = DD′∘F=D. You can check that this means DDD sends any two paths with the same source and target to the same morphism in C\mathbf{C}C. Indeed these two paths are equal after applying FFF, so applying D=D′∘FD = D' \circ FD=D′∘F will also make them equal.

Morgan Rogers (he/him) (Jul 27 2023 at 09:06):

I feel that the context in which we actually use commutative diagrams is being buried in this discussion.
When we draw a diagram (commutative or not), it's a directed graph whose vertices are labelled with objects of a category and edges labelled with morphisms. Saying that the diagram is commutative is asserting some equations between those morphisms. If we have a way of asserting which equations externally, like Riehl's functoriality condition, then great, but I fear this is circular since we still need a way of defining the equations in the indexing categories.

There are two reasons we would assert equations that I can think of: as part of a definition (a hypothesis/axiom), or in a diagram-chasing proof when verifying such an equation holds.
In the former case, we would want to make the equation as explicit as possible: if your equations are not identifying maximal paths in the diagram you draw, that's a recipe for confusion (or you should probably say "this diagram and all its subdiagrams commute").
In the second case, the proof may require expanding or decomposing the diagram that you actually want to show is commutative, and in practice one refers to commutativity of subdiagrams of a composite diagram to save space. For example...

John Baez (Jul 27 2023 at 13:55):

I guess everyone here has already noticed, but I think there are at least three things category theorists mean by "diagram":

1) The original meaning, probably, is a diagram that you actually draw on the page - thus, a graph in the category theorist's sense, also called a [[quiver]], with vertices labelled by objects in some category CCC and edges labelled by morphisms in CCC. I'll use the word "quiver" since graph theorists have another meaning of the word "graph".

2) At least nine times out of ten, when someone actually draws such a diagram, the edges don't cross. Then they're taking a graph and embedding it into the plane - at least up to isotopy: i.e. if they draw it on rubber and you bend the rubber, the diagram shouldn't change meaning. This lets you unambiguously speak of 2-cells bounded by edges, and ask whether each one of these commutes.

3) But sometime around when people started trying to formalize the concept of limit and colimit of a diagram, they decided it was good to define a diagram in a category CCC to be a functor F:J→CF: J \to CF:J→C where JJJ is some category describing the diagram's 'shape'. This concept of diagram reduces to the first definition if we let JJJ be the free category on some quiver.

John Baez (Jul 27 2023 at 13:55):

Meanings 1) and 3) are the most important.

John Baez (Jul 27 2023 at 13:58):

If you want to "ask if a diagram commutes", you can do this. Given a quiver QQQ there is not only the free category on QQQ, say F(Q)F(Q)F(Q), but also the free preorder on QQQ, say G(Q)G(Q)G(Q).

John Baez (Jul 27 2023 at 13:59):

There's a natural transformation F⇒GF \Rightarrow GF⇒G so we get a functor F(Q)→G(Q)F(Q) \to G(Q)F(Q)→G(Q) which forces all parallel pairs of arrows in F(Q)F(Q)F(Q) to become equal.

Then a "not necessarily commutative diagram in CCC shaped like the quiver QQQ" is a functor ϕ:F(Q)→C\phi: F(Q) \to Cϕ:F(Q)→C. It "commutes" iff ϕ\phiϕ factors through the functor F(Q)→G(Q)F(Q) \to G(Q)F(Q)→G(Q).
