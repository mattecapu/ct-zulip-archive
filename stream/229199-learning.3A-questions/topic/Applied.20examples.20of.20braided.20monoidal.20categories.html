John Baez (Mar 27 2026 at 22:01):

There's a weird connection between braidings and large cardinals, which I described here:

  • Shelves and the infinite.

A [[shelf]] is a set with a binary operation that distributes over itself, usually written ▹\triangleright▹. Any shelf SSS gives a lax-braided monoidal category where the monoidal structure is the cartesian product of sets and the lax braiding is determined by

B:S×S→S×S B: S \times S \to S \times SB:S×S→S×S

B:(x,y)↦(x▹y,x) B: (x, y) \mapsto (x \triangleright y, x) B:(x,y)↦(x▹y,x)

I call this a 'lax braiding' because BBB may not be invertible. If BBB is invertible we call our shelf a [[quandle]].

Here's the weird part: there's a simple-sounding question about quotients of the free shelf on one generator whose answer is only known if we assume the existence of a certain extremely large cardinal. The Ackermann function is also relevant.

IIRC, you get examples of braided monoidal structures by starting with $\mathbb{N}$-graded vector spaces with its standard signed symmetric monoidal structure, but replacing the signs (−1)mn(-1)^{mn}(−1)mn by qmnq^{mn}qmn for parameters qqq.
