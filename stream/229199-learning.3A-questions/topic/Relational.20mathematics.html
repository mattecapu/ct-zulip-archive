That illuminates the exercise of "Galileo and the flight of a bird" in session 1 of the book Conceptual Mathematics by William Lawvere and the quantum logic of Joseph-Maria Jauch. Using relations seems like another way of reasoning in this exercise.

Rémy Tuyéras (Jul 19 2024 at 18:19):

Peiyuan Zhu said:

Rémy Tuyéras said:

And you can also make a semiring with sets using unions and products (I think), so that you can do something similar with homsets for the categories

Can you elaborate? Not sure what you mean by this.

Take two sets AAA and BBB and define:

  • A+B={x ∣ x∈A or x∈B}A+B = \{x~|~x \in A \textrm{ or }x\in B\}A+B={x ∣ x∈A or x∈B}
  • A×B={(a,b) ∣ a∈A and b∈B}A \times B = \{(a,b)~|~a \in A\textrm{ and }b \in B\}A×B={(a,b) ∣ a∈A and b∈B}

I believe this defines a semiring structure on the objects of Set\mathbf{Set}Set. Now take a finite set III and take a matrix M:I×I→SetM:I \times I \to \mathbf{Set}M:I×I→Set whose coefficients are sets. Therefore, M(a,b)M(a,b)M(a,b) is a set.

You can use the semiring structure (above) to multiply matrices such as MMM. In particular, you have:

M2(a,b)=∑c∈IM(a,c)×M(c,b)={(f,g) ∣ ∃c: f∈M(a,c) and g∈M(c,b)}M^2(a,b) = \sum_{c \in I} M(a,c) \times M(c,b) = \{(f,g)~|~\exists c:\,f \in M(a,c)\textrm{ and }g \in M(c,b)\}M2(a,b)=∑c∈I​M(a,c)×M(c,b)={(f,g) ∣ ∃c:f∈M(a,c) and g∈M(c,b)}

If you now have functions M2(a,b)→M(a,b)M^2(a,b) \to M(a,b)M2(a,b)→M(a,b), then you actually have a composition operation (up to associativity and unity axioms).

Eric M Downes (Jul 20 2024 at 19:08):

Yes, Lawvere is definitely suggesting a morphism.

The term "Morphism" comes from"homomorphism" from group theory etc; in particular monoid homomorphisms preserve multiplication and the identity. Here I'll just emphasize the former
ϕ:X→Y\phi:X\to Yϕ:X→Y
ϕ(xy)=ϕ(x)ϕ(y)\phi(xy)=\phi(x)\phi(y)ϕ(xy)=ϕ(x)ϕ(y)
This can be written in purely functional form, given multiplications μ:XX→X\mu:XX\to Xμ:XX→X and ν:YY→Y\nu:YY\to Yν:YY→Y we have
(1)     ϕ∘μ=ν∘(ϕ×ϕ)(1)~~~~~\phi\circ\mu=\nu\circ(\phi\times\phi)(1)     ϕ∘μ=ν∘(ϕ×ϕ)
where I have used the universal property of the product to allows us to express the function ϕ×ϕ:XX→YY\phi\times\phi:XX\to YYϕ×ϕ:XX→YY that acts "elementwise". This corresponds to the diagram

The first page you posted shows a morphism that preserves a relation called a preorder ≤\leq≤, which must be at least transitive
x≤y & y≤z  ⟹  x≤zx\leq y~ \& ~y\leq z\implies x\leq zx≤y & y≤z⟹x≤z
and reflexive x≤xx\leq xx≤x.
These morphisms ϕ\phiϕ, as the author notes are called monotonic maps:
∀x,y∈X; x≤Xy  ⟹  ϕ(x)≤Yϕ(y)\forall x,y\in X; ~x\leq_X y\implies \phi(x)\leq_Y\phi(y)∀x,y∈X; x≤X​y⟹ϕ(x)≤Y​ϕ(y).
This can be phrased to look more like (1) above.

Lets define ≤S:SS→2\leq_S:SS\to 2≤S​:SS→2 where 2={ 0,1 }2=\set{0,1}2={0,1}. So ≤(x,y)=1\leq(x,y)=1≤(x,y)=1 just when xxx is less than or equal to yyy.

Then a monotonic function ϕ\phiϕ almost satisfies
" ≤Y∘(ϕ×ϕ)=≤X\leq_Y\circ(\phi\times\phi)=\leq_X≤Y​∘(ϕ×ϕ)=≤X​ "
except we can't quite because we do not know for certain that ϕ\phiϕ is strict
ϕ(y)≤Yϕ(x)  ⟹  ?y≤Xx\phi(y)\leq_Y\phi(x)\overset{?}{\implies}y\leq_X xϕ(y)≤Y​ϕ(x)⟹?​y≤X​x.
Above that reads implies as in the logical operation.

So we can instead use the order on 222 (where 1 corresponds to true and 0 corresponds to false) and recall some facts about implication;
0  ⟹  x0\implies x0⟹x is vacuously true for all xxx
1  ⟹  11\implies 11⟹1 is true, but 1̸  ⟹  01\not\implies01⟹0.
So we are only concerned with avoiding the case where 1  ⟹  01\implies01⟹0, which we can capture with a function mapping 0↦1,1↦10\mapsto1, 1\mapsto10↦1,1↦1. That way, if ϕ(x)≤ϕ(y)\phi(x)\leq\phi(y)ϕ(x)≤ϕ(y) is false, while x≤yx\leq yx≤y is true the diagram fails to commute (as it should) but otherwise it succeeds.

This can be captured by taking the constant map !:2→1!:2\to1!:2→1 followed by the truth map ⊤:1→2\top:1\to2⊤:1→2; here 1={ ∗ }1=\set*1={∗} and ⊤(∗)=1\top(*)=1⊤(∗)=1. (Is this composite a monotonic map on 222?)

So we have another diagram

Hopefully that diagram makes clear the essential similarity between the two concepts, of a monoid homomorphism, and a montonic map, both of which are morphisms in their own categories. We can do this because logical implies is a preorder!

Reflect this is really just a "functionalization" of the concept of inequality: it encodes the ideas as functions in the category Set\sf SetSet instead of leaving them in logic or undefined. More sophisticated approaches will specify categories of morphisms and objects, etc. but the essential insight is that equalities and inequalities can be expressed using diagrams. If you like the above, you might like Lawvere's other intro-level book Sets for Mathematics, which also teaches the set theory etc one needs for mathematics. It is also much more accessible than the book by Freyd I mentioned.
