Topic: What is a D-filtered category?

Julius Hamilton (May 19 2024 at 15:10):

(∗)(*)(∗) Let D\mathbb DD be a collection of (small) categories. A (small) category C\mathcal CC is called D\mathbb DD-filtered when C\mathcal CC-colimits commute with D\mathbb DD-limits in the category of sets.

A limit/colomit is a universal cone/cocone. A cone/cocone is a family of morphisms (with some more details).

How can a colimit commute with a limit? Since the limit of a diagram can be thought of as an object in a category with morphisms to all the objects in the diagram, it could make sense to say those morphisms commute with certain other morphisms. However, C\mathcal CC is a separate category from any category DDD in D\mathbb DD. In order to commute, there has to be a way to compose morphisms from these two categories. Since they say “in the category of sets”, it sounds like there is an assumed functor into Set\mathbf SetSet. Does this mean any functors?

Kevin Carlson (Jun 08 2024 at 17:29):

No, functors don’t actually generally induce a mapping on endofunctors! If f:x→yf:x\to yf:x→y is a morphism in an arbitrary category, there’s generally no way to use it to turn endomorphisms of xxx into endomorphisms of y.y.y. In other words, mapping an object to its endomorphism monoid is not a functor. This is for whatever reason a very tempting misconception, so you’re in good company!

Kevin Carlson (Jun 08 2024 at 17:32):

Taking the limit also isn’t really an endofunctor. A JJJ-shaped diagram in CCC is not an object of C,C,C, after all; it’s an object of the functor category CJ.C^J.CJ. For myself, I like metaphors that relate to imaging or observation. I sometimes think of a cone over a diagram with apex AAA as a collection of related observations of AAA. Then, if that diagram has a limit, each morphism from AAA to the corresponding limit object induces (and is induced by) one such collection of related observations of AAA. So, in a way, the limit "condenses" the multiple related "perspectives" provided by the diagram: roughly speaking, the limiting object "sees the world" in a way equivalent to how our original collection of related objects (our diagram) "sees the world".

This intuition corresponds to the fact that we have a bijection: C(A,limF)≅Cone(A,F)C(A, \mathrm{lim} F) \cong \mathrm{Cone}(A, F)C(A,limF)≅Cone(A,F). Here CCC is a category, FFF is a diagram in CCC, by C(A,limF)C(A, \mathrm{lim} F)C(A,limF) we mean the set of morphisms in CCC from AAA to the object corresponding to a limit of FFF, and Cone(A,F)\mathrm{Cone}(A,F)Cone(A,F) is the set of cones over the diagram FFF with apex AAA.
