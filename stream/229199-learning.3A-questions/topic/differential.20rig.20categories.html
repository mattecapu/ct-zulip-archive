So, the idea is really simple, and I will lazily summarise it in the abstract of the draft-of-a-paper I am writing. (Jokes apart, I find it quite self-explanatory of the general motivation I had, when this project began)

image.png

Now, if C\mathcal CC is a rig category, in the sense that a monoidal structure ⊗\otimes⊗ distributes over coproducts, a derivation is just an endofunctor ∂:C→C\partial : \mathcal C \to \mathcal C∂:C→C with the property that

  • ∂(A∪B)≅∂A∪∂B\partial(A\cup B) \cong \partial A \cup \partial B∂(A∪B)≅∂A∪∂B

  • ∂(A⊗B)≅∂A⊗B∪A⊗∂B\partial(A\otimes B) \cong \partial A \otimes B \cup A\otimes\partial B∂(A⊗B)≅∂A⊗B∪A⊗∂B.

As a general rule, one knows to be after some deep Mathematics when a seemingly innocuous assumption yields unexpected or counterintuitive results . This is the case here, because from these very natural assumptions most of the theory of differential rings carries over, with one, eminent, painful exception: there is absolutely no reason why the derivative of the monoidal unit is zero (=the initial object of the rig).

Let me offend your intelligence by recalling that if if RRR is a differential ring, with derivation ddd, then d1=d(1⋅1)=d1+d1d1=d(1\cdot 1)= d1 +d1d1=d(1⋅1)=d1+d1; now, since the additive monoid of RRR is cancellative, we get d1=0d1=0d1=0. In fact, in differential semiring theory, the most you can get is that the derivative of 111 is an additive idempotent. Now, some authors require d1=0d1=0d1=0 as an axiom of a differential semiring, essentially because if this is not required, ddd is not well-defined: d(a)=d(a⋅1)=da+a⋅d1d(a)=d(a\cdot 1) = da + a \cdot d1d(a)=d(a⋅1)=da+a⋅d1, which in its own right is da+a⋅d1+a⋅d1da + a\cdot d1 + a\cdot d1da+a⋅d1+a⋅d1, which... on the other hand, there are examples of semirings where every object is an additive idempotent (posets with ∨\vee∨, an eminent instance of which are co-Heyting algebras, that carry interesting operations that satisfy the axioms of a derivation.

When one moves to true categories things get way hairier, essentially because ∂1=∂1+∂1\partial 1 = \partial 1 + \partial 1∂1=∂1+∂1 entails a whole lot of counterintuitive properties: I will list the ones that I found until now, in discussions with @Nathanael Arkor @Fabrizio Genovese (with which this ended up being a joint work) @Daniele Palombi and others (I get pretty pushy when there's mathematics I like and I can't get it right :grinning: )

  • First, ∂1\partial 1∂1 must be "empty or big", in the sense that -for example in the category of sets- a set AAA such that A+A≅AA+A\cong AA+A≅A is either empty or infinite.

  • On the other hand, in the category of _finite_ sets, AAA can only be empty; same in the category of finite dimensional vector spaces, where d=dim⁡V=2dd=\dim V = 2dd=dimV=2d has 0 (thus the zero space) as unique solution. Same, in every category with a decent choice of dimension C→N\mathcal C \to \mathbb NC→N. (What are examples of these categories? Do they have a name)?

  • On another hand, sometimes ∂1=∂1+∂1\partial 1 = \partial 1 + \partial 1∂1=∂1+∂1 is forced to have just trivial solutions due to the request that this equality is in some sense natural; for example, let ∂\partial∂ be a derivation in a category of functors A→Set\mathcal A \to SetA→Set; then, ∂1\partial 1∂1 is a functor A→Set\mathcal A \to SetA→Set such that F≅F+FF \cong F + FF≅F+F, and naturally so; such functors are few, because no bijection FA≅FA+FAFA\cong FA + FAFA≅FA+FA can be natural if the domain A\mathcal AA is just a little bit nontrivial. I have asked this question for the exact purpose of establishing this obstruction result.

  • In an abstract category of course you can't uniquely attach to an object information about how big it is; but you still have morphisms and the Yoneda lemma! So, let's stick ∂1\partial 1∂1 in the hom-set hom⁡(∂1,Z)\hom(\partial 1,Z)hom(∂1,Z) for a generic object ZZZ; the fact that ∂1≅∂1+∂1\partial 1\cong \partial 1 + \partial 1∂1≅∂1+∂1 yields hom⁡(∂1,Z)≅hom⁡(∂1+∂1,Z)≅hom⁡(∂1,Z)×hom⁡(∂1,Z)\hom(\partial 1, Z) \cong \hom(\partial 1+\partial 1, Z)\cong \hom(\partial 1,Z) \times \hom(\partial 1, Z)hom(∂1,Z)≅hom(∂1+∂1,Z)≅hom(∂1,Z)×hom(∂1,Z). Now _this_ is interesting, because hom⁡(∂1,Z)\hom(\partial 1,Z)hom(∂1,Z) can only be empty, a singleton or infinite. Let me sum up: a property of C\mathcal CC that -at least a priori- has nothing to do with its size, the presence of a differential structure, imposes some conditions on the numerosity of some of its hom-sets; if a category is finite, thus, hom⁡(∂1,Z)\hom(\partial 1, Z)hom(∂1,Z) is either empty or a singleton, and in particular it must be a singleton when Z=∂1Z=\partial 1 Z=∂1. Again, having a differential structure and being finite entails that ∂1\partial 1∂1 is a "rigid" object, in the sense that the monoid of endomorphisms of ∂1\partial 1∂1 is trivial.

  • More on this: whenever there is an extension ∂1↪A\partial 1 \hookrightarrow A∂1↪A in C\mathcal CC, there must be a monomorphism hom⁡(∂1,∂1)↪hom⁡(∂1,A)\hom(\partial 1, \partial 1) \hookrightarrow \hom(\partial 1, A)hom(∂1,∂1)↪hom(∂1,A), so that if the domain, endomorphisms of ∂1\partial 1∂1, is infinite, the codomain, arrows ∂1→A\partial 1 \to A∂1→A, can only be bigger. The endomorphisms of ∂1\partial 1∂1 clearly are "something special"! And this is why I would like to get to know them better: let M=hom⁡(∂1,∂1)M=\hom(\partial 1,\partial 1)M=hom(∂1,∂1) for short; then, from M≅M2=M×MM\cong M^2 = M\times MM≅M2=M×M one easily gets, by induction, that M≅MnM\cong M^nM≅Mn for every finite nnn. How many monoids with this property do you know, apart the terminal one?

  • Let's go deeper in the white rabbit's hole: if ∂1≅∂1+∂1\partial 1 \cong \partial 1 + \partial 1∂1≅∂1+∂1, this means that ∂1\partial 1∂1 is naturally a coalgebra for the "leave it or double it" functor S:A↦A+AS : A\mapsto A +AS:A↦A+A, in such a way that there is a unique map

    ∂1≅∂1+∂1↓↓C≅C+C\begin{array}{ccc} \partial 1 &\cong& \partial 1 + \partial 1 \\ \downarrow && \downarrow \\ C &\cong& C + C \end{array}∂1↓C​≅≅​∂1+∂1↓C+C​

    between ∂1\partial 1∂1 and the terminal coalgebra of SSS; but wait, in the category of topological space CCC is the Cantor set! What just happened here? Is the map ∂1→C\partial 1 \to C∂1→C an epi, a mono in general?

So. All these questions are vague and somewhat alluding to the fact that I do not have a clear picture of what's going on: indeed, I have no idea which wa to turn: what am I after? Another interesting idea related to the perks and shortcomings of being ∂1\partial 1∂1 is the following: as an endofunctor, ∂\partial∂ might have interesting fixed points, and there is a standard procedure to build its initial algebra and terminal coalgebra. One could legitimately call such an object "Eulerian" or "Neperian", for obvious reasons (the most reasonable is that "exponential object" already means something else).

Initial algebras are trivial, in that ∂0=0\partial 0=0∂0=0 by using the Leibniz rule. On the other hand, the triviality of terminal coalgebras is governed by ∂1\partial 1∂1, in the sense that (assuming the terminal object is the monoidal unit, that's what is called a semicartesian monoidal category)

1←∂1←∂∂1←∂∂∂1←… 1\leftarrow \partial 1 \leftarrow \partial\partial 1 \leftarrow \partial\partial\partial 1 \leftarrow \dots 1←∂1←∂∂1←∂∂∂1←…

and the first ordinal λ\lambdaλ for which the transition morphism v:∂λ1→∂λ+11v : \partial^\lambda 1 \to \partial^{\lambda + 1} 1v:∂λ1→∂λ+11 is invertible realises the terminal coalgebra. Now, what is this map? What is the result of applying ∂\partial∂ many times to 111? Well, since ∂1=∂1+∂1\partial 1 = \partial 1 + \partial 1∂1=∂1+∂1, and ∂\partial∂ is linear, ∂∂1=∂(∂1+∂1)=∂∂1+∂∂1\partial\partial 1 = \partial (\partial 1 + \partial 1) = \partial \partial 1 + \partial \partial 1∂∂1=∂(∂1+∂1)=∂∂1+∂∂1, thus the entire cochain is made by coalgebras for the functor _+_\_ + \__+_ (even more, by coalgebras whose coalgebra map is invertible: how many such algebras are there? What's their shape?).

Fabrizio Genovese (Jan 23 2021 at 13:36):

Yeah, I feel like ∂1\partial 1∂1 sometimes gives us a very coarse way of representing cardinality within C\mathcal{C}C. In Set theory, we "measure:" size of sets by using injections. This is due to Hartog's lemma, which basicaly says that for any two sets X,YX,YX,Y you have either an injection X→YX \to YX→Y or the other way around. Bernstein's theorem instead says that if you have monos both ways, then X,YX,YX,Y are in bijection. This is what allows us to order sets using cardinality.

In here, if ∂1\partial 1∂1 is big, meaning that Hom(∂1,∂1)Hom(\partial 1, \partial 1)Hom(∂1,∂1) is infinite, then a monomorphism ∂1→∂Z\partial 1 \to \partial Z∂1→∂Z guarantees that there is a mono Hom(∂1,∂1)→Hom(∂1,Z)Hom(\partial 1, \partial 1) \to Hom (\partial 1, Z)Hom(∂1,∂1)→Hom(∂1,Z). Hence, the generalized elements of ZZZ of type ∂1→Z\partial 1 \to Z∂1→Z are infinite, and ZZZ is in some sense "big". I feel there is much more to say about this if one looks deep enough...
