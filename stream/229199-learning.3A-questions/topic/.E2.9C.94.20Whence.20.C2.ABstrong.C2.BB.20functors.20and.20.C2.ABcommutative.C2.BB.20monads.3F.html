If MMM is a commutative monoid, then it satisfies m(x,y)=m(y,x)m(x,y)=m(y,x)m(x,y)=m(y,x), which we draw as an axiom saying that you can swap the two wires:

Note that we need to draw a 'braiding' to swap the legs representing xxx and yyy. This makes sense for monoids in Set\mathbf{Set}Set because for any sets XXX and YYY there is a natural transformation X×Y→Y×XX\times Y \to Y\times XX×Y→Y×X sending (x,y)(x,y)(x,y) to (y,x)(y,x)(y,x).

Now suppose we have a monad (T,μ,η)(T,\mu,\eta)(T,μ,η) on some category C\mathbf{C}C. We can draw the natural transformation μ\muμ like so:
(6)

The red areas represent the category C\mathbf{C}C. The top edge represents the functor T∘TT\circ TT∘T. The bottom edge represents the functor TTT, and the interior of the diagram represents the natural transformation μ:T2→T\mu:T^2\to Tμ:T2→T. We can represent η:IdC→T\eta:\mathrm{Id}_\mathbf{C}\to Tη:IdC​→T similarly:
(7)

These natural transformations obey associativity and identity axioms that look just the same as (3) and (4) except in red boxes. This is what is meant by 'a monad is just a monoid in the category of endofunctors'.

Now, can we define what it means for TTT to be commutative? We would want to draw a diagram like (5). But there is no way to ascribe a meaning to the 'braiding' in that diagram. If XXX and YYY are endofunctors of C\mathbf{C}C there is no canonical natural transformation X∘Y→Y∘XX\circ Y\to Y\circ XX∘Y→Y∘X. So we can't define 'commutative monad' as simply a commutative monoid in the category of endofunctors.

Now suppose that we are given a monoidal structure on the category C\mathbf{C}C. This has a multiplication map ⊗:C×C→C\otimes:\mathbf{C}\times\mathbf{C}\to\mathbf{C}⊗:C×C→C. We can draw a diagram for this in three dimensions. It gives a way to take two of the red sheets representing C\mathbf{C}C and join them together.
(8)

A strong monad is a monad TTT equipped with a right-strength and a left-strength. These strengths are natural transformations that allow us to move the wire representing TTT through ⊗\otimes⊗ onto either of the two sheets.
(9)

(The strengths also have to obey some axioms which I can't be bothered to draw.) A commutative monad is then defined as a strong monad obeying the following axiom:
(10)

Note that on the left-hand-side of this axiom the wire from the left side of μ\muμ ends up on the front sheet, and the wire from the right side of μ\muμ ends up on the rear sheet. Whereas on the right-hand-side of this axiom the wire from the left side of μ\muμ ends up on the rear sheet, and the wire from the right side of μ\muμ ends up on the front sheet.

So this axiom says that a commutative monad is precisely a monad where you can swap the two wires!
