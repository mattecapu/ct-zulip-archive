Joshua Meyers (Aug 30 2024 at 23:00):

Julius Hamilton said:

How do we know that a morphism always exists in D from F(X) to G(X)?

We don't. The claim that η:F⇒G\eta: F\Rightarrow Gη:F⇒G is a natural transformation implies the claim that for any object XXX of CCC, a morphism exists in DDD from F(X)F(X)F(X) to G(X)G(X)G(X).

Joshua Meyers (Aug 30 2024 at 23:02):

Julius Hamilton said:

It seems like the definitions of natural transformation I have seen do not choose to express it as a collection of morphisms from the image of FFF to the image of GGG. Is there a reason people would avoid expressing it this way?

Suppose F(X)=F(Y)F(X)=F(Y)F(X)=F(Y), and G(X)=G(Y)G(X)=G(Y)G(X)=G(Y). Then we would still need two morphisms ηX\eta_XηX​ and ηY\eta_YηY​, and they might even have to be distinct in order for the naturality condition to be true. But if we thought in terms of the images of FFF and GGG, we wouldn't notice that these points have to be counted twice because they just occur once in the images.
