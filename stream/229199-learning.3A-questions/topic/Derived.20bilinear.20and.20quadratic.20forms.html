So that's a very good question, and IMHO a good rule of thumb to have an intuition about the difference between line bundles and polarisations is that it is exactly (a derived version of) the difference between a (normalised) quadratic form and a symmetric bilinear form.

To a (normalised) quadratic form q:X→Zq: X \to Zq:X→Z (where X,ZX, ZX,Z are say some abelian groups) we can associate a symmetric bilinear form b:X×X→Zb: X \times X \to Zb:X×X→Z via b(x,y)=q(x+y)−q(x)−q(y)b(x,y)=q(x+y)-q(x)-q(y)b(x,y)=q(x+y)−q(x)−q(y). Conversely to a bilinear form bbb, we can associate a quadratic form Q(x)=b(x,x)Q(x)=b(x,x)Q(x)=b(x,x). Crucially, these are not inverse operations!

A few remarks on this:

  • Two (normalised) quadratic forms q,q′q, q'q,q′ induce the same bbb precisely when they differ by a linear form, i.e. by an element of the "dual" X∨=Hom(X,Z)X^{\vee}=Hom(X,Z)X∨=Hom(X,Z)
  • When qqq is homogeneous of degree 2 (or equivalently it is symmetric, i.e. [−1]∗q=q[-1]^{\ast} q=q[−1]∗q=q, i.e. q(−x)=q(x)q(-x)=q(x)q(−x)=q(x)), then Q=2qQ=2qQ=2q. So as usual with quadratic forms we have problems with 222, notably if 222 is not invertible, we cannot recover QQQ from qqq.
  • If we encode bbb as the associated "polarisation" ϕ:X→X∨\phi: X \to X^{\vee}ϕ:X→X∨, then QQQ is the pullback by Id×ϕId \times \phiId×ϕ of the universal quadratic form on X×X∨X \times X^{\vee}X×X∨ given by (x,ψ)↦ψ(x)(x,\psi) \mapsto \psi(x)(x,ψ)↦ψ(x). Notice also that ϕ\phiϕ is given by ϕ:x↦tx∗q−q\phi: x \mapsto t_x^{\ast} q - qϕ:x↦tx∗​q−q (up to renormalisation to have a linear form).

All of this generalizes to abelian varieties, replacing XXX by AAA and ZZZ by Gm\mathbb{G}_mGm​, or rather by BGmB\mathbb{G}_mBGm​:

  • to a line bundle LLL over AAA we can associate a biextension over A×AA\times AA×A, encoded via the polarisation ϕL:A→A∨,P↦tP∗L⊗L−1\phi_L: A \to A^{\vee}, P \mapsto t_P^{\ast} L \otimes L^{-1}ϕL​:A→A∨,P↦tP∗​L⊗L−1.

There is a famous article by Poonen and Stoll about this situation. When A/kA/kA/k is a principally polarised abelian variety over a number field kkk, the Cassel-Tate pairing ш(A)×ш(A)→Q/Zш(A) \times ш(A) \to \mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}ш(A)×ш(A)→Q/Z is non degenerate and antisymmetric (assuming the Tate-Shafarevich group is finite). If furthermore λ\lambdaλ is represented by a rational line bundle, then the Cassel-Tate pairing is even alternate, so the Tate-Shafarevich group has cardinal a square. So that's a very good question, and IMHO a good rule of thumb to have an intuition about the difference between line bundles and polarisations is that it is exactly (a derived version of) the difference between a (normalised) quadratic form and a symmetric bilinear form.

To a (normalised) quadratic form q:X→Zq: X \to Zq:X→Z (where X,ZX, ZX,Z are say some abelian groups) we can associate a symmetric bilinear form b:X×X→Zb: X \times X \to Zb:X×X→Z via b(x,y)=q(x+y)−q(x)−q(y)b(x,y)=q(x+y)-q(x)-q(y)b(x,y)=q(x+y)−q(x)−q(y). I've mainly thought about this in two contexts, which you might be interested in. Maybe you know all about this, and I'm not really an expert on it, but anyway:

  1. The classification of finite simple groups includes those of 'Lie type' like the special orthogonal groups SO(n,F) and symplectic groups Sp(n,F) for finite fields F, but this becomes tricky when F has characteristic two, because the difference between symmetric and antisymmetric bilinear forms evaporates, but also the difference between bilinear forms and quadratic forms becomes very significant. (There's also the added complication that the classification of nondegenerate quadratic forms over finite fields is unfamiliar to physicists such as myself who are used to working over R\mathbb{R}R or C\mathbb{C}C.)

  2. Mac Lane classified homotopy 2-types of double loop spaces in terms of 'quadratic functions' from an abelian group GGG to an abelian group AAA. Joyal and Street realized that Mac Lane was secretly classifying braided 2-groups XXX: braided monoidal groupoids where every object has an inverse under tensor product. GGG is the group of isomorphism classes of objects of XXX (aka π0\pi_0π0​) while AAA is the group of automorphisms of the unit object (aka π1)\pi_1)π1​)). The quadratic function comes from the 'self-braiding' of objects xxx, namely Bx,x:x⊗x→x⊗x B_{x,x} : x \otimes x \to x \otimes x Bx,x​:x⊗x→x⊗x, together with a standard trick for turning an endomorphism of x⊗xx \otimes xx⊗x into an endomorphism of the unit object.
