John Baez (Dec 20 2024 at 00:48):

Getting your toes wet in algebraic geometry by thinking about the field with one element is kind of like starting to study music by listening to Cage's 4'33''. How can we answer a question like "if F1\mathbb{F}_1F1​ existed, it would have a single prime ideal?" It's like a koan.

The idea is that if you have some variety systematically defined over all fields, you can count the number of points over Fq\mathbb{F}_qFq​ where qqq is a prime power, and sometimes you get a polynomial function of qqq. Then if you set q=1q = 1q=1 you get the number of points of a very interesting set, which we pretend is that variety defined over F1\mathbb{F}_1F1​, because it's actually defined in a way that closely resembles the variety were talking about. You have to look at examples, like the projective line or projective plane. I do some here.

Damiano Mazza (Dec 20 2024 at 13:05):

Yes F1\mathbb F_1F1​ morally has to have only one prime ideal, but this is because all fields have exactly one prime ideal (namely the zero ideal, which is a prime ideal in any integral domain). So the relationship between sets and F1\mathbb F_1F1​ is not due to it having only one prime ideal, otherwise this would be true for all fields. The reason is more related to what John explained (and I agree with his analogy F1\mathbb F_1F1​/John Cage's 4'33'' :big_smile:)

For a bit more about geometry over F1\mathbb F_1F1​, you can start by thinking that, if kkk is a field, then Spec k\mathrm{Spec}\,kSpeck is a one-point space, but "colored" with kkk. So in algebraic geometry there are many "one-point spaces", but none of them is the terminal object (like the usual one-point space), because no field is initial. Relativizing over any of them gives you geometry over the corresponding field: the category of kkk-schemes is literally the slice over Spec k\mathrm{Spec}\,kSpeck, in which Spec k\mathrm{Spec}\,kSpeck is terminal, that is, it really behaves like the one-point space.

Since there is no initial field, there is no "absolute point", that is, a scheme ∗\ast∗ whose underlying space is a point and such that there is a unique arrow S→∗S\to\astS→∗ for every scheme SSS. The idea is that Spec F1\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb F_1SpecF1​ should play the role of such an "absolute point". In fact, as far as I know (but I have an extremely limited understanding of these things), much of F1\mathbb F_1F1​-geometry is about seeing Spec Z\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb ZSpecZ (the spectrum of the integers) as a "curve" over Spec F1\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb F_1SpecF1​, which you can't do in usual algebraic geometry because Z\mathbb ZZ is initial in rings, so there's nothing "below" Spec Z\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb ZSpecZ.

This blog post tells you more about how the points of the underlying topological space of Spec Z[X]\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb Z[X]SpecZ[X], which is the affine integer line, actually look more like the points of a product space, that is, more like a plane, and that this hints to the fact that it should be seen as the product of Spec Z\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb ZSpecZ and Spec F1[X]\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb F_1[X]SpecF1​[X]. But I am not sure you are going to learn much algebraic geometry by looking at this stuff!

John Baez (Dec 20 2024 at 23:17):

Well, but that's if we stick to working with schemes over a fixed field kkk: Spec(k)\mathrm{Spec}(k)Spec(k) is terminal in the category of schemes over kkk, and since we've chosen a field, there's no pressure to get interested in the field with one element yet. The problem starts when we go beyond this, e.g. when thinking about the Riemann Hypothesis. There's a Riemann hypothesis for smooth varieties over a finite field kkk, called the Weil Conjectures, and those have been proven, but the actual Riemann Hypothesis is about Z\mathbb{Z}Z, and then we get very stuck, and people start desperately searching for the field with one element.

John Baez (Dec 21 2024 at 22:16):

Good question! For prime powers we know what Fq\mathbb{F}_qFq​ - the unique field of that cardinality - and that's what we need to get all the combinatorics of various linear algebra puzzles to give formulas that reduce to familiar set-theoretic combinatorics when q=1q = 1q=1.

So what if qqq is not a prime power? Is there a commutative ring that works in this case? Or do we need something weirder? That's the question I think you're really asking. And that's a great question.

There is more than one finite semisimple commutative ring (= products of finite fields) with qqq elements when qqq is composite. E.g. for q=24q = 24q=24 we have three

F8×F3,F2×F4×F3,F2×F2×F2×F3\mathbb{F}_8 \times \mathbb{F}_3, \mathbb{F}_2 \times \mathbb{F}_4 \times \mathbb{F}_3, \mathbb{F}_2 \times \mathbb{F}_2 \times \mathbb{F}_2 \times \mathbb{F}_3F8​×F3​,F2​×F4​×F3​,F2​×F2​×F2​×F3​

There's a canonical "best" one that's a product of just one field for each prime in the prime factorization of qqq. Here it's F8×F3\mathbb{F}_8 \times \mathbb{F}_3F8​×F3​. Is that the commutative ring we should use to get the combinatorial formulas to work just like when qqq is a prime power?

Sometime I'll check with a simple example.

There's another canonical choice of semisimple commutative ring with qqq elements that's the product of as many field as possible, but that wouldn't give the usual field Fq\mathbb{F}_qFq​ when qqq is a prime power, so that doesn't sound good.

John Baez (Dec 21 2024 at 22:21):

This stuff about semisimple commutative rings as generalizations of fields may sound weird, but it does show up naturally when you think about LLL-functions. For example James Dolan noticed that the Riemann zeta function shows up naturally when you decategorify this species (i.e. functor from the groupoid of finite sets to Set\mathsf{Set}Set):

The Riemann species assigns to any finite set the collection of ways of making that set into a product of finite fields.

Later we generalized the heck out of this fact.
