Topic: A converse to Lambek theorem

fosco (Feb 02 2021 at 16:41):

A famous theorem by Lambek asserts that if GGG is an endofunctor of a category C\mathcal CC, and if ξ:X→GX\xi : X\to GXξ:X→GX is its terminal coalgebra, then ξ\xiξ is an isomorphism.

Until today, I stupidly believed that this was an if and only if! "By the universal property of ξ\xiξ, any other coalgebra (A,α)(A,\alpha)(A,α) where α\alphaα is an isomorphism, is the terminal coalgebra." However, it takes just the time to draw the right diagram to understand that this is false.

So, I have two questions:

  • What are the endofunctors GGG for which it is true that if a coalgebra (A,α)(A,\alpha)(A,α) has α\alphaα invertible, then (A,α)(A,\alpha)(A,α) is terminal? I expect there are very few.
  • Given G, is there a way to "count" how many GGG-coalgebras (A,α)(A,\alpha)(A,α) have α\alphaα invertible? I expect there are probably "as much as the object GXGXGX is big", where XXX is the carrier of the terminal coalgebra.

Feel free to assume whatever you want on G and on its domain!

Tom Hirschowitz (Feb 02 2021 at 17:27):

Not quite answering your question, but are you aware of work by Stefan Milius and colleagues about characterising various kinds of coalgebras with invertible α\alphaα?

Nathanael Arkor (Feb 02 2021 at 17:34):

However, it takes just the time to draw the right diagram to understand that this is false.

Even more simply, this applies to both terminal coalgebras and initial algebras, so, unless they coincide, this is immediately seen to be false.

Tom Hirschowitz (Feb 02 2021 at 21:46):

IIRC, one of their contributions was to characterise a kind of "regular" fixed point. In lots of examples, the initial algebra intuitively consists of finite trees, while the final coalgebra consists of all, potentially wildly infinite trees. And their regular fixed point would catch something like regular trees, in the sense of finite trees with "loops".
