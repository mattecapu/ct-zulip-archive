Topic: Exponential is Automatically Contravariant in Exponent?

Ignat Insarov (Dec 22 2024 at 09:58):

Page 18 of Sheaves in Geometry and Logic says:

C→BAA×C→B(16){C → B^A \over A × C → B} (16)A×C→BC→BA​(16)

… It follows from the various uniqueness properties that BAB^ABA is also a (contravariant!) functor of AAA (at least on those AAA which are exponentiable), and that (16)(16)(16) is also natural in AAA. …

This shocked me. I looked at this adjunction many times times, but it never occurred to me that the exponential is automatically contravariant in the exponent — I always assumed this needs to be postulated on top of the adjunction. Is there a way I could have foreseen this — is there a way this result is to be expected?

I took this as an exercise and figured that we have as the function on arrows f↦Aff ↦ A^ff↦Af the operation:

f:x→y↦εx∘(f×Ay)x∘η:Ay→Axf: x → y \quad ↦ \quad ε^x ∘ (f × A^y)^x ∘ η: A^y → A^xf:x→y↦εx∘(f×Ay)x∘η:Ay→Ax

It takes about a hundred arrows to show functoriality and naturality! «It follows from the various uniqueness properties…»

Right from the start, I'd expect BAB^ABA is either covariant or contravariant in AAA, because it's defined using a universal property. That's just my expectation.

I believe that using the Yoneda lemma we can show BAB^ABA is covariant (resp. covariant) in AAA iff hom(C,BA)\text{hom}(C,B^A)hom(C,BA) is covariant (resp. contravariant) in AAA for each CCC.

But hom(C,BA)\text{hom}(C,B^A)hom(C,BA) is naturally isomorphic to hom(A×C,B)\text{hom}(A \times C, B)hom(A×C,B), which is clearly contravariant in AAA.

Mike Shulman (Jan 03 2025 at 17:13):

But we aren't even given that BAB^ABA is functorial in AAA at all. So we can (and do) define that functorial action so as to make that square commute, which is (uniquely) possible by the Yoneda lemma and since the natural components are isomorphisms.

Mike Shulman (Jan 03 2025 at 17:17):

The naturality of those isomorphisms in xxx is what tells us that when we transport the functoriality of A×xA\times xA×x in AAA across those isomorphisms, we get a natural transformation and hence can apply the Yoneda lemma.

Their naturality in yyy then tells us that the resulting functoriality of BAB^ABA in AAA commutes with its given functoriality in BBB. (Assuming that we were given the latter, which is necessary to talk about naturality in yyy. Alternatively we could just be given the BAB^ABA as objects and define their functorial action on BBB in the same way.)
