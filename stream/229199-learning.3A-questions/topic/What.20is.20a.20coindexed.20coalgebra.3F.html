Topic: What is a coindexed coalgebra?

Antoine Van Muylder (Apr 17 2024 at 16:38):

I'm pretty sure this has been asked before but had trouble finding the information.

Let FFF be a functor and let α\alphaα be a functor algebra. From what I know there are 2 ways to define what "an algebra depending on α\alphaα" should be (in what follows ∣α∣|\alpha|∣α∣ is the carrier of α\alphaα).

  • A fibered algebra over α\alphaα is simply an algebra morphism into α\alphaα
  • (edit) In the case of N\mathbb{N}N which is the intial algebra of X↦1+XX\mapsto 1+XX↦1+X, N\mathbb{N}N-indexed algebras are predicates P:N→SetP : \mathbb{N} \to \mathrm{Set}P:N→Set that have P0P0P0 and ∀n. Pn→P(n+1)\forall n.\, Pn\to P(n+1)∀n.Pn→P(n+1). More generally, assuming that FFF has some sort of action on relations/predicates, we can define a α\alphaα-indexed F-algebra to be a logical predicate (i.e. unary logical relation, unary bisimulation, structured predicate) P:∣α∣→SetP : |\alpha| \to \mathrm{Set}P:∣α∣→Set. These are the predicates one need to provide to make use of induction in proof assistants.

To get an indexed algebra out of a fibered one you use the fibers predicate. In the other direction you "sigma up" your indexed algebra.

Induction is then the fact that each μ F\mu\,FμF fibered algebra has a section or that each indexed algebra is "inhabited".

My question is what happens for coalgebras?

  • An α\alphaα-cofibered coalgebra is a coalgebra morphism from α\alphaα
  • But then what is a coindexed coalgebra?

On your example on PPP: If we let your PPP be a mono p:P↣Np : P \rightarrowtail \mathbb{N}p:P↣N into the natural numbers, then what you presented as an N\mathbb{N}N-indexed algebra is

  • The existence of a subalgebra [p0,pind]:1+P→P[p_{0},p_{\text{ind}}] : 1 + P \to P[p0​,pind​]:1+P→P into the initial 1+(−)1 + (-)1+(−) algebra N\mathbb{N}N, such that
  • ppp is an algebra homomorphism.

By initiality, P≅NP \cong \mathbb{N}P≅N, hence PnPnPn for all nnn. Like you said, this is what a proof by induction looks like categorically (i.e. showing the existence of such an algebra 1+P→P1 + P \to P1+P→P), I just found your notation and viewpoint a bit unfamiliar to me. Also, I don't get why you would call this a logical predicate. Is it simply because it is a subcoalgebra? I disagree with this terminology, because to me a logical predicate (or relation) is a certain predicate on an initial algebra, where the carrier of said initial algebra exhibits some higher-order "applicative" behavior and said predicate maps inputs in the predicate to output in the predicate. I gave a (lousy?) presentation (edit: better slides here) on precisely this topic in your university two weeks ago (see also relevant paper) ;).

Stelios Tsampas (Apr 18 2024 at 19:23):

So, to answer your question, I will simply consider the dual of a subalgebra, a subcoalgebra. A predicate p:P↣Xp : P \rightarrowtail Xp:P↣X to the state space of a coalgebra X→FXX \to FXX→FX is what is known as a (coalgebraic) invariant, i.e. in Set this corresponds to a subset on states which is preserved through computation.

Stelios Tsampas (Apr 18 2024 at 19:41):

If FFF has, as you mention, an action on relations/predicates, e.g. F‾\overline{F}F, the correct thing to say would be that a PPP is an F‾\overline{F}F-algebra (dually, an F‾\overline{F}F-coalgebra). Furthermore, in his 2016 book, Jacobs calls the relational counterpart of the above a logical bisimulation, which justifies the terminology you used :grinning_face_with_smiling_eyes:.

Awesome! Yeah, there's quite a lot of moving parts, but only because the concrete setting has a lot of moving parts. I would be very happy to help you with specific questions, but some things to keep in mind:

  • The outer layer of proving that a logical predicate □P↣μF\square P \rightarrowtail \mu F□P↣μF holds is structural induction, as expected.
  • The "inner" layer proof terms, one for each constructor, are based on coinduction (so we're talking about mixed induction and coinduction, also keep in mind that in the literature coinduction is quite degenerate, but it's there).
