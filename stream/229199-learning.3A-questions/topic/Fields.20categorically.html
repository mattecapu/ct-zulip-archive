Consider the (countably infinite) meadow MMM of finite-support functions from N\mathbb{N}N to Z/2Z\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}Z/2Z. This is a commutative ring, and every element is idempotent and hence its own generalized inverse. Here's something that might be useful:

Oscar Cunningham (Apr 08 2020 at 09:57):

Consider the monoidal category of pointed sets with the smash product. A monoid internal to this category is just an ordinary monoid equipped with a 000 (a two-sided absorbing element).

Oscar Cunningham (Apr 08 2020 at 09:59):

The Hopf-algebras internal to this category are groups with a 000 adjoined to them; which are precisely the multiplicative structures we want fields to have.

Oscar Cunningham (Apr 08 2020 at 10:00):

Joe Moeller (Apr 12 2020 at 16:24):

Right, many people actually do consider that additive structure at times. I think a good example of what he's referring to can be found in this paper: https://arxiv.org/pdf/1308.0042.pdf Equations of tropical varieties by Giansiracusa and Giansiracusa.

Rather than defining an object F1\mathbb F_1F1​, one starts by defining the category of modules, F1\mathbb F_1F1​-Mod, to be the category of pointed sets. The basepoint of an F1\mathbb F_1F1​-module M is denoted 0M and is called the zero element of M. This category has a closed symmetric monoidal tensor product ⊗ given by the smash product of pointed sets (take the cartesian product and then collapse the subset M × {0N} ∪ {0M} ×N to the basepoint). The two-point set {0,1} is a unit for this tensor product. An F1\mathbb F_1F1​-algebra is an F1\mathbb F_1F1​-module A equipped with a commutative and unital product map A⊗A→ A (i.e., it is a commutative monoid in F1\mathbb F_1F1​-Mod). Concretely, an F1\mathbb F_1F1​-algebra is a commutative and unital monoid with a (necessarily unique) element 0A such that 0A · x = 0A for all x; thus F1\mathbb F_1F1​-algebras, as defined here, are sometimes called monoids-with-zero. The two-point set {0,1} admits a multiplication making it an F1\mathbb F_1F1​-algebra, and it is clearly an initial object, so we can denote it by F1\mathbb F_1F1​ and speak of F1\mathbb F_1F1​-algebras without ambiguity.
