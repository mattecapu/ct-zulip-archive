Peva Blanchard (Apr 13 2024 at 22:07):

I'll try to answer, although I guess there are many ways to respond.

In my case, "classify" really means "representing" a functor. More precisely, let's say we have a contravariant functor FFF that associates with every XXX a specific set FXFXFX. We can picture an element of FXFXFX as a sort of data structure related to XXX, e.g., sub-object, or epic arrows, or etc. Let's call an element of FXFXFX a FFF-structure over XXX.

The functor FFF is representable when there exists an object Ω\OmegaΩ such that we have a Set bijection

FX≅Hom(X,Ω)FX \cong Hom(X, \Omega)FX≅Hom(X,Ω)

natural in the variable XXX. Intuitively, this means that specifying a FFF-structure over XXX is the same as specifying a morphism from XXX to the special object Ω\OmegaΩ. In other words, the FFF-structures are classified by the morphisms with codomain Ω\OmegaΩ.

Peva Blanchard (Apr 13 2024 at 22:15):

The advantage is that Hom(X,Ω)Hom(X, \Omega)Hom(X,Ω) is a HomHomHom set, so it can be acted upon by pre/post-composing with other morphisms! In particular, when X=ΩX = \OmegaX=Ω, we have FΩ≅Hom(Ω,Ω)F\Omega \cong Hom(\Omega,\Omega)FΩ≅Hom(Ω,Ω).

The element S∈FΩS \in F\OmegaS∈FΩ that corresponds to id∈Hom(Ω,Ω)id \in Hom(\Omega,\Omega)id∈Hom(Ω,Ω) is quite special, as witnessed by the diagram below.

This says that any FFF-structure on an arbitrary object XXX can be obtained by transforming (pulling-back) the FFF-structure SSS on Ω\OmegaΩ along a characteristic morphism X→ΩX \rightarrow \OmegaX→Ω. The FFF-structure SSS is a sort of "universal FFF-structure".

Kevin Carlson (Arlin) (Apr 13 2024 at 23:50):

That all looks pretty much right to me. The one refinement I'd point out is that while the functors Sub\mathrm{Sub}Sub and (therefore) X↦Hom(X,Ω)X\mapsto \mathrm{Hom}(X,\Omega)X↦Hom(X,Ω) are presheaves, i.e. contravariant functors into Set,\mathbf{Set},Set, the functor X↦Hom(A,X)X\mapsto \mathrm{Hom}(A,X)X↦Hom(A,X) is covariant and thus not a presheaf. However since covariant hom-functors do exist you can certainly still ask the representability question for covariant functors C→Set.\mathcal C\to \mathbf{Set}.C→Set. For complicated reasons, in practice it's often easier to prove that a presheaf is representable than that a covariant functor is, but both problems are very important.

Here is another example of representable functor.

Fix two objects AAA and BBB in a category C\mathcal{C}C. Let FFF be the functor that maps any object XXX to the set Hom(X,A)×Hom(X,B)Hom(X, A) \times Hom(X, B)Hom(X,A)×Hom(X,B), i.e., pairs of arrows from XXX to AAA and BBB respectively. It is a contravariant functor: for any f:X→Yf : X \rightarrow Yf:X→Y, we obtain a function FX←FYFX \leftarrow FYFX←FY as described in this diagram:
image.png

We just pre-compose the two arrows out of YYY with fff.

Then we can wonder if FFF is representable: is there an object PPP of C\mathcal{C}C such that

Hom(X,A)×Hom(X,B)≅Hom(X,P) (?) Hom(X, A) \times Hom(X, B) \cong Hom(X, P)~(?)Hom(X,A)×Hom(X,B)≅Hom(X,P) (?)

It turns out that a representing object for FFF (if it exists) is exactly the cartesian product A×BA \times BA×B of AAA and BBB.
