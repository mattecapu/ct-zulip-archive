Paolo Perrone (Apr 11 2024 at 09:25):

Hello all.
A coseparator or cogenerator in a category is an object CCC which "has enough resolution to tell things apart".
In rigor, for all objects XXX and YYY, given two morphisms f,g:X→Yf,g:X\to Yf,g:X→Y, if for all morphisms c:Y→Cc:Y\to Cc:Y→C we have that c∘f=c∘gc\circ f=c\circ gc∘f=c∘g, then f=gf=gf=g.

I'm now wondering if anyone has come across a sort of stronger, "monoidal" version of it.
That is, in a monoidal category, a monoid object (C,m,e)(C,m,e)(C,m,e) such that for all objects X,Y1,Y2X,Y_1,Y_2X,Y1​,Y2​, given two morphisms f,g:X→Y1⊗Y2f,g:X\to Y_1\otimes Y_2f,g:X→Y1​⊗Y2​, if for all morphisms h1:Y1→Ch_1:Y_1\to Ch1​:Y1​→C and h2:Y2→Ch_2:Y_2\to Ch2​:Y2​→C we have that m∘(h1⊗h2)∘f=m∘(h1⊗h2)∘gm\circ (h_1\otimes h_2)\circ f=m\circ (h_1\otimes h_2)\circ gm∘(h1​⊗h2​)∘f=m∘(h1​⊗h2​)∘g, as morphisms X→CX\to CX→C, then f=gf=gf=g.
(And similarly for higher arity, using the n-ary multiplication.)

Has anyone seen anything like this? I believe that an example is the two-element set in (Set,×,1)(\mathrm{Set},\times,1)(Set,×,1).

Morgan Rogers (he/him) (Apr 12 2024 at 08:58):

What Paolo asked for is a strictly stronger property, where morphisms into a monoidal product Y1⊗Y2Y_1 \otimes Y_2Y1​⊗Y2​ are distinguished by morphisms of the form m(h1⊗h2)m(h_1 \otimes h_2)m(h1​⊗h2​). Or to put it another way, CCC being a coseparator requires that the collection of all morphisms X→CX \to CX→C are jointly monic, whereas Paolo is asking for the stronger property that the collection of morphisms of the given form (as h1,h2h_1,h_2h1​,h2​ vary) are jointly monic.

Morgan Rogers (he/him) (Apr 12 2024 at 09:52):

The point is that they're jointly monic, not individually so. There is no reason we can't have m(h1⊗h2)=m(h1⊗h2′)m (h_1 \otimes h_2) = m (h_1 \otimes h_2')m(h1​⊗h2​)=m(h1​⊗h2′​). Or to be more specific, in Set\mathrm{Set}Set the multiplication monoid structure on {0,1}\{0,1\}{0,1} does the trick, since squaring is the identity (given a map h:B×C→2h:B \times C \to 2h:B×C→2 distinguishing f,g:A⇉B×Cf,g:A\rightrightarrows B \times Cf,g:A⇉B×C we have h=m(h×h)h = m(h \times h)h=m(h×h) being a distinguishing map of the required form). This monoid is not cancellative. Moreover, any monoid containing a submonoid with the property that Paolo mentioned will also have this property!
