David Egolf (Dec 10 2023 at 23:43):

That looks alright to me, as far as I can tell after looking through it quickly!

To make things less laborious, you might find it interesting to prove these two things, which can help enable a quicker proof (I believe):

  • composing any two isomorphism yields an isomorphism
  • the inverse of any isomorphism is an isomorphism

If we accept (or prove!) these two things, here's how one way to quickly prove that ggg is an isomorphism if fff and hhh are:

  • We know that h=g∘fh = g \circ fh=g∘f
  • Since fff is an isomorphism it has an inverse f−1f^{-1}f−1. Composing on the right with f−1f^{-1}f−1 we get: h∘f−1=gh \circ f^{-1} = gh∘f−1=g
  • Since the inverse of an isomorphism is an isomorphism, f−1f^{-1}f−1 is an isomorphism. And hhh is an isomorphism by assumption. So h∘f−1=gh \circ f^{-1}=gh∘f−1=g is given by composing two isomorphisms and hence ggg is an isomorphism.

David Egolf (Dec 11 2023 at 00:15):

You are welcome!

Yes, I think you showed above that if fff and ggg are isomorphisms, then f∘gf \circ gf∘g is an isomorphism. So, composing two isomorphisms gives us an isomorphism.

Regarding left and right inverses, for fff to be an isomorphism, it has to have a two-sided inverse. For example, in "Category Theory in Context", Riehl defines an isomorphism like this "An isomorphism in a category is a morphism f:X→Yf: X \to Yf:X→Y for which there exists a morphism g:Y→Xg: Y \to Xg:Y→X so that g∘f=1Xg \circ f = 1_Xg∘f=1X​ and f∘g=1Yf \circ g = 1_Yf∘g=1Y​."

In addition, if an isomorphism fff has a left (or right) inverse kkk, that one-sided inverse is actually a two sided inverse! (So k=f−1k = f^{-1}k=f−1). A proof is given on the nLab: here. You might also find it interesting to prove this.

Oh John, you are a man after my own heart.
Even right now, in my 'intro to proof-based math' course for first year undergrads, I use almost exactly this language when exploring new topics with students.
First we make a general claim, then try to prove it wrong (produce a counterexample).
The counterexample reveals what further assumptions might be necessary to make the theorem follow in generality
To use a very basic example;
Claim; If n is divisible by 4 then it is divisible by 12.
Counterexample; If n=16 then 4|n but not 12|n
... what went wrong with 16?
Ah, it isn't divisible by 3!
Ok so THEOREM: If 4|n AND 3|n then 12|n
