Topic: Should we replace two objects with their product?

Julius Hamilton (Sep 21 2024 at 18:42):

If it turns out that for two fixed objects X,Y∈Ob(C)X, Y \in Ob(C)X,Y∈Ob(C), X×YX \times YX×Y exists, does that mean we should just “forget about XXX and YYY”? It sounds like we can make our category simpler by getting rid of XXX and YYY.

John Baez (Sep 21 2024 at 20:33):

No, you should not forget about XXX and YYY. For example, the 6-element set {1,2,3,4,5,6}\{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6 \}{1,2,3,4,5,6} is the product of {1,2}\{1, 2\}{1,2} and {1,2,3}\{1,2,3\}{1,2,3}. But this does not mean we should forget about those other sets.

All this is a fancy way of saying "just because 6=2×36 = 2 \times 36=2×3, that doesn't mean you should get rid of 222 and 333". For one thing, if you did, you couldn't say 6=2×36 = 2 \times 36=2×3 anymore! For another, I really like the numbers 222 and 333.

Madeleine Birchfield (Sep 22 2024 at 03:52):

Part of the definition of a category theoretic product X×YX \times YX×Y is the data of XXX and YYY, since one needs them to define the morphisms from X×YX \times YX×Y to XXX and YYY and then to assert the universal property that this span is the terminal span to XXX and YYY.

David Michael Roberts (Sep 22 2024 at 08:38):

I think John's example doesn't convey the sense of violence of removing sets from the category. If one removed _all_ two-element sets and all three-element sets (which is the only thing that would make a material difference, not just removing one such pair), then your category suddenly is much poorer for structure. Certain colimits would fail to exist, I think. Moreover, if you did this with one-element sets, since every set is isomorphic to the product of itself with a one-element set, you would lose terminal objects, and in fact you could accidentally throw out all the objects!
