Soichiro Fujii (Apr 30 2020 at 12:10):

Hi, I just noticed that the following (apparently common) conventions are incompatible with each other, if one works in a sufficiently general setting.

  • In a category C\mathcal{C}C, the composition operation is of type C(y,z)×C(x,y)⟶C(x,z)\mathcal{C}(y,z)\times\mathcal{C}(x,y)\longrightarrow\mathcal{C}(x,z)C(y,z)×C(x,y)⟶C(x,z) (as opposed to C(x,y)×C(y,z)⟶C(x,z)\mathcal{C}(x,y)\times\mathcal{C}(y,z)\longrightarrow\mathcal{C}(x,z)C(x,y)×C(y,z)⟶C(x,z)).

  • A profunctor H ⁣:A⟶BH\colon\mathcal{A}\longrightarrow\mathcal{B}H:A⟶B is defined as a functor H ⁣:Bop×A⟶SetH\colon\mathcal{B}^\mathrm{op}\times\mathcal{A}\longrightarrow\mathbf{Set}H:Bop×A⟶Set (as opposed to H ⁣:Aop×B⟶SetH\colon\mathcal{A}^\mathrm{op}\times\mathcal{B}\longrightarrow\mathbf{Set}H:Aop×B⟶Set).

The former convention may be defended on the basis of the (very common) Leibniz convention for composition. The latter can be justified because this lets us view the bicategory Prof\mathscr{P}rofProf of profunctors as an extension of Cat\mathscr{C}atCat (as well as of Catcoop\mathscr{C}at^\mathrm{coop}Catcoop), rather than an extension of Catop\mathscr{C}at^\mathrm{op}Catop or Catco\mathscr{C}at^\mathrm{co}Catco.

However, if one works in the enriched setting where the base monoidal category is not symmetric (or even where the base is a bicategory), then unfortunately the above conventions are incompatible. More precisely, one cannot view the representable presheaf C(−,x)\mathcal{C}(-,x)C(−,x) as a profunctor (between C\mathcal{C}C and the unit category I\mathcal{I}I). (In this general setting, one defines profunctor as a family of objects in the base monoidal category equipped with a suitable two-sided action; see e.g., Section 3 of Enriched categories and cohomology by Street.)

So how do you deal with this difficult situation?

John Baez (Apr 30 2020 at 15:22):

So how do you deal with this difficult situation?

I complain about it, try various conventions, get annoyed, and ultimately shrug my shoulders and decide to wait for civilization to collapse, hoping the next civilization adopts more consistent notation.

The ultimate problem is that we like to write f∘gf \circ gf∘g to mean "do ggg first then ggg" as if we were reading from right to left, yet we write f ⁣:x→yf \colon x \to yf:x→y, with the arrow going from left to right.

So, one solution is to consistently write from left to right. This means we should use f;gf ; gf;g to mean "do fff first, then ggg. This is not too hard for people to accept until we have to apply functions to arguments; then we should say

(x)(f;g)=((x)f)g (x) (f; g) = ((x)f)g (x)(f;g)=((x)f)g

and most people find this upsetting, since they've been trained in the opposite convention (except when using calculators with reverse Polish notation). It's fun to think about how this convention would work:

(x)sin⁡2dxd=(x)sin⁡ (x)cos⁡2 \displaystyle{ (x)\sin^2 \frac{d}{xd} = (x)\sin \, (x)\cos 2 }(x)sin2xdd​=(x)sin(x)cos2

The other convention is to systematically write from right to left, which also upsets people:

y←x:f y \leftarrow x : f y←x:f

Sam Tenka (Apr 30 2020 at 17:10):

@Fabrizio Genovese

Could f:Y←Xf:Y \leftarrow Xf:Y←X be less upsetting than the other possibilities? (My friends have teased me about this notation, so perhaps the answer is no.)

Here's my reasoning. There seem to be 4 possibilities (when writing in plain text on a computer):
(a) f:Y←Xf:Y \leftarrow Xf:Y←X
(b) f:X→Yf:X \to Yf:X→Y
(c) Y←X:fY \leftarrow X:fY←X:f
(d) X→Y:fX \to Y:fX→Y:f,

I often use (a). The styles (a) and (c) have the advantage that they align with conventions for function composition, matrix multiplication, and so on. The styles (a) and (b) have the advantage that they align with conventions for judgements in type theory. The styles (a) and (d) have the advantage that the target is written more closely to f. This is nice because f's type is more like the type of its target than of its source. More precisely, only one of the two hom functors is covariant! This corresponds to a common goal-oriented approach to interactive proving and to vanilla functional programming. So, for all three matchings on the tetrahedral graph, (a)'s pair wins!
