Then y(X) y(X) y(X) is exactly such a family generated by one element at X X X, i.e. a free presheaf. Now what do we expect from morphisms out of something freely generated? John Baez (Jul 29 2025 at 11:50):

At a very raw intuitive level I think of the presheaf category on a category as analogous to the free vector space on a set, with colimits taking the place of linear combinations, and the Yoneda embedding as analogous to the map sending elements of the set to the corresponding basis vectors. This can be elaborated, e.g. profunctors are like matrices, etc. I think of this whole body of math as the "rediscovery of linear algebra, one level up". It's very worth pondering why linear algebra was useful in the first place, and why this reincarnation is useful.

John Baez (Jul 29 2025 at 12:43):

The analogue of the Yoneda isomorphism

Hom(y(X),P)≃P(X)\mathrm{Hom}(y(X), P) ≃ P(X)Hom(y(X),P)≃P(X)

is the equation

⟨y(i),v⟩=vi \langle y(i), v \rangle = v_i ⟨y(i),v⟩=vi​

Here

  • SSS is some set,
  • VVV is the free vector space on SSS,
  • i∈Si \in Si∈S,
  • y(i)y(i)y(i) is the corresponding basis vector in VVV,
  • vvv is any vector in VVV,
  • viv_ivi​ is the ith component of vvv,
  • and the angle brackets are the inner product where the basis vectors y(i)y(i)y(i) are orthonormal.

Inner products are like homsets, especially in quantum physics! Adjoint operators between inner product spaces are like adjoint functors.

(I don't usually write the ith basis vector as y(i)y(i)y(i); I usually write it as eie_iei​, but that could be mistaken for the ith component of some vector called eee, which would be very bad right now.)

Peva Blanchard (Jul 29 2025 at 12:47):

Another point of view, that I learned in this forum here, is as follows. Take for instance the category CCC with two objects V,EV,EV,E and two arrows s,t:V→Es, t : V \to Es,t:V→E.

A presheaf F:Cop→SetF : C^{op} \to \text{Set}F:Cop→Set is defined by

  • a set FVFVFV (of vertices)
  • a set FEFEFE (of edges)
  • and source and target map Fs,Ft:FE→FVFs, Ft : FE \to FVFs,Ft:FE→FV
    I.e., FFF is a graph (oriented, possibly with multiple edges between vertices, and self-loops).

I find useful to think of CCC as the kinds of "shapes", and FFF as a kind of gluing of instances of those shapes.

One can check that

  • The representable functor y(V)=C(_,V)y(V) = C(\_, V)y(V)=C(_,V) is a graph with a single vertex
  • The representable functor y(E)=C(_,E)y(E) = C(\_, E)y(E)=C(_,E) is a graph with a single edge and two vertices

Given two presheaves (i.e. graphs) F,GF, GF,G, a natural transformation F⇒GF \Rightarrow GF⇒G can be seen as morphism of graph (it maps vertices to vertices, edges to edges, while respecting the source and target maps).

Now, a natural transformation y(V)⇒Fy(V) \Rightarrow Fy(V)⇒F is a morphism from the graph with a single vertex to the graph FFF. Hence, there are as many such natural transformations as there are vertices in the graph:

Nat(y(V),F)≃FVNat(y(V), F) \simeq FVNat(y(V),F)≃FV

And similarly for edges.

Alex Kreitzberg (Jul 29 2025 at 18:56):

I think the number of footholds folks use to get an understanding speaks to how deep Yoneda is.

The nLab page [[Yoneda Lemma]] lists three corollaries with three corresponding intuitions. As a beginner, I notice the examples folks give of "what the Yoneda lemma is" are heavily skewed in favor of "corollary 1" (that the Yoneda embedding is an embedding). Imo this is hard for a beginner to appreciate because, as a beginner, I don't fully understand the presheaf category, because I don't fully understand free cocompletions, because I don't fully understand colimits. (I'm overselling my confusion a touch)

Despite its superficial complexity, I'm partial to "corollary 3" as the first one to learn, because it gives more context for universal properties.

Here's a simple example that really impressed me:

"a generalized element of a pair, is a pair of generalized elements", corresponds to the potential natural isomorphism

C(X,A×B)≅C(X,A)×C(X,B)C(X, A\times B) \cong C(X, A) \times C(X, B)C(X,A×B)≅C(X,A)×C(X,B)

The Yoneda lemma tells you what your category needs to have to implement this isomorphism, by spelling out the way in which A×BA\times BA×B is a universal object of a certain category. The projection functions of the pair end up being a "universal element", or what I think of as a universal interface.

That is you can specify the arrows going into some object with a set valued functor, and the Yoneda lemma can then tell you what resulting object gets defined (if possible). Or in other words, you can specify what the generalized elements of an object is, and the Yoneda lemma then tells you what you need to define the object.

I'm not a pro, but considering the category C=V→s,tEC = V \xrightarrow{s,t} EC=Vs,t​E, you obtain a relatively low-brow version of this. A presheaf FFF on CCC is just a graph (see e.g. my previous comment)

The embedding of VVV (resp. EEE) into the presheaf category PSh(C)PSh(C)PSh(C), i.e. the category of graphs, is the graph y(V)y(V)y(V) with a single vertex (resp. the graph y(E)y(E)y(E) with a single edge and the two corresponding vertices).

The set FVFVFV (resp. FEFEFE) of vertices (resp. edges) of the graph FFF is in bijection with the set of graph morphisms from y(V)y(V)y(V) (resp. y(E)y(E)y(E)) to FFF. This is an instance of the Yoneda lemma.
