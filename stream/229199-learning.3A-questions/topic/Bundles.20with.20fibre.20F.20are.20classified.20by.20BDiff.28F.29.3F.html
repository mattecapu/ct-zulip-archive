John Baez (Oct 03 2024 at 23:52):

It may seem trivial that Diff+(S1)≃SO(2)\mathrm{Diff}^+(S^1) \simeq \text{SO}(2)Diff+(S1)≃SO(2), but this is a bit stronger than just saying every orientation-preserving diffeomorphism of the circle is smoothly homotopic to a rotation: it's saying we can do that homotopy in a kind of 'uniform' way.

It's a lot harder to show Diff+(S2)≃SO(3)\mathrm{Diff}^+(S^2) \simeq \text{SO}(3)Diff+(S2)≃SO(3). For some reason I was interested in these things once, and a proof is section 7.3 of Kuipers' Lectures on Diffeomorphism Groups of Manifolds

But then things get really hard. There are 'weird' diffeomorphisms of the nnn-sphere, and gluing together two (n+1)(n+1)(n+1)-balls along their boundary using such a weird diffeomorphism you can get an exotic (n+1)(n+1)(n+1)-sphere: one that's homeomorphic but not diffeomorphic to the usual (n+1)(n+1)(n+1)-sphere. For example, Diff+(S6)\mathsf{Diff}^+(S^6)Diff+(S6) has 28 connected components, corresponding to the 28 exotic 7-spheres. So we can't have Diff+(S6)≃SO(7)\mathsf{Diff}^+(S^6) \simeq \mathrm{SO}(7)Diff+(S6)≃SO(7), since SO(n)\mathrm{SO}(n)SO(n) always has just one connected component!

Kevin Carlson (Oct 04 2024 at 00:35):

An even more core way of stating the vibe is that BDiff(F)B\mathrm{Diff}(F)BDiff(F) is "the space of all possible copies of FFF, up to diffeomorphisms." It's just that there's no actual universal space in which all copies of FFF live, so we pick R∞\mathbb R^\inftyR∞ as a sufficiently-universal space if FFF is a smooth manifold insofar exactly as we get the contractible embedding space; any other universal space with that property also works.

If we want the diffeomorphisms to be smooth cocycles (as is natural in differential topology) there may be extra things to check.
