Chris Grossack (they/them) (Feb 04 2024 at 17:29):

Here's an approach that seems closer to what you want (though it's possible you can get this from Morgan's comments, I haven't checked).

Let C\mathcal{C}C be a cartesian closed category (as you were expecting) and GGG a group object in C\mathcal{C}C. Then there's an associated one-object C\mathcal{C}C-enriched category BG\mathrm{B}GBG, where we take a single object ⋆\star⋆ and define Hom(⋆,⋆)=G\text{Hom}(\star,\star) = GHom(⋆,⋆)=G.

Chris Grossack (they/them) (Feb 04 2024 at 17:30):

Now we have two ways of defining an action of GGG on an object X∈CX \in \mathcal{C}X∈C.

  1. We could look at an arrow α:G×X→X\alpha : G \times X \to Xα:G×X→X in C\mathcal{C}C satisfying the usual axioms
  2. We could look at a C\mathcal{C}C-enriched functor F:BG→CF : \mathsf{B}G \to \mathcal{C}F:BG→C sending ⋆↦X\star \mapsto X⋆↦X.

In definition (2), we're crucially using the cartesian closed-ness in order to view C\mathcal{C}C as being enriched over itself.

Chris Grossack (they/them) (Feb 04 2024 at 17:35):

The key way of translating back and forth between these two perspectives is to remember what a "C\mathcal{C}C-enriched functor" is. It has to send objects to objects (for us we send ⋆↦X\star \mapsto X⋆↦X) and hom-objects to hom-objects. That is, there had better be a C\mathcal{C}C-arrow Hom(⋆,⋆)→Hom(F⋆,F⋆)\text{Hom}(\star, \star) \to \text{Hom}(F\star, F\star)Hom(⋆,⋆)→Hom(F⋆,F⋆).

But Hom(X,X)=Hom(F⋆,F⋆)\text{Hom}(X,X) = \text{Hom}(F\star, F\star)Hom(X,X)=Hom(F⋆,F⋆) is the C\mathcal{C}C-object given by the internal hom [X,X][X,X][X,X]. So taken together a C\mathcal{C}C-enriched functor BG→C\mathsf{B}G \to \mathcal{C}BG→C is given by the data of an object X∈CX \in \mathcal{C}X∈C and a C\mathcal{C}C-arrow G→[X,X]G \to [X,X]G→[X,X].

Chris Grossack (they/them) (Feb 04 2024 at 17:36):

As you intuited, this is exactly the same story as the Set\mathsf{Set}Set-enriched case, since the data of such an enriched functor uncurries to exactly the data of an internal group action G×X→XG \times X \to XG×X→X by CCC-ness of C\mathcal{C}C.

Now you can work in your favorite convenient category of topological spaces in order to play this game with continuous group actions. ^_^
