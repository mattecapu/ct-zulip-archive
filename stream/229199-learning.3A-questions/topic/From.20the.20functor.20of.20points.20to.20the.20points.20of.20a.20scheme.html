Topic: From the functor of points to the points of a scheme

Damiano Mazza (Jan 21 2024 at 16:58):

In the "functor of points" approach one sees a scheme as a functor

X:CRing⟶Set.X:\mathbf{CRing}\longrightarrow\mathbf{Set}.X:CRing⟶Set.

In the introduction to the second edition of EGA I, Grothendieck shows how, in the affine case, one may recover the points of the underlying scheme. The idea is that the points of XXX should morally be given by

⋃k fieldX(k)\bigcup_{k\text{ field}} X(k)⋃k field​X(k)

(see also this Mathoverflow question). However, the above is a proper class, so one considers the following equivalence relation: given f∈X(k)f\in X(k)f∈X(k) and f′∈X(k′)f'\in X(k')f′∈X(k′), we set f∼f′f\sim f'f∼f′ iff there exists a common field extension i:k→Ki:k\to Ki:k→K, i′:k′→Ki':k'\to Ki′:k′→K such that X(i)(f)=X(i′)(f′)X(i)(f)=X(i')(f')X(i)(f)=X(i′)(f′). The intuition is that X(k)X(k)X(k) is the set of solutions of some kind of system of polynomial equations in the field kkk, and we shouldn't distinguish, say, the solution 333 as a rational number from the solution 333 as a real or complex number (but we should distinguish it from the solution we would usually denote by 333 in the field F7\mathbb F_7F7​, for example).

Damiano Mazza (Jan 21 2024 at 16:59):

Now, if X=CRing(R,−)X=\mathbf{CRing}(R,-)X=CRing(R,−), i.e., if it is the functor of points of SpecR\mathrm{Spec} RSpecR for some commutative ring RRR, then f∈X(k)f\in X(k)f∈X(k) means that fff is a homomorphism R→kR\to kR→k, and it turns out that

Lemma. f∼f′f\sim f'f∼f′ iff ker⁡f=ker⁡f′.\ker f=\ker f'.kerf=kerf′.

It is immediate to check that ker⁡f\ker fkerf is a prime ideal of RRR because the target is a field. It is a bit less immediate (at least for me) but it is also true that any prime ideal of RRR is of the form ker⁡f\ker fkerf for some f:R→kf:R\to kf:R→k with target a field. So we get exactly the points of the underlying topological space of XXX qua locally ringed space. Cool!

Damiano Mazza (Jan 21 2024 at 22:01):

We start by letting p:=ker⁡f=ker⁡f′\mathfrak p:=\ker f=\ker f'p:=kerf=kerf′, which is a prime ideal of RRR. As both Todd and Mike suggested, both fff and f′f'f′ factor through R/pR/\mathfrak pR/p, which is an integral domain, via monos mmm and m′m'm′ into kkk and k′k'k′, respectively. Let K:=Frac(R/p)K:=\mathrm{Frac}(R/\mathfrak p)K:=Frac(R/p) be the field of fractions of R/pR/\mathfrak pR/p. By its universal property, we have that mmm and m′m'm′ factor through KKK via monos K→kK\to kK→k and K→k′K\to k'K→k′.

Damiano Mazza (Jan 21 2024 at 22:01):

Now, these exhibit kkk and k′k'k′ as KKK-algebras. In particular, they are non-zero KKK-vector spaces, and their tensor product S:=k⊗Kk′S:=k\otimes_K k'S:=k⊗K​k′ as KKK-algebras is the same as their tensor product as KKK-vector spaces. In particular, it is non-zero, so we found our SSS!

I guess the lesson I learned here is that "every span in the category of fields may be closed", in the sense that two extensions k,k′′k,k''k,k′′ of a field KKK always have a common extension K0K_0K0​. There's nothing canonical about K0K_0K0​ because it depends on a choice of a maximal ideal of the algebra k⊗Kk′k\otimes_K k'k⊗K​k′, but it exists. This is the "union" Mike was hinting at.
