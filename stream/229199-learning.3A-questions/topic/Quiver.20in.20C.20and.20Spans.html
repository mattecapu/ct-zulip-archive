John Baez (Dec 10 2020 at 17:24):

A quiver in C is the same as an "endospan" in C. A general span looks like

John Baez (Dec 10 2020 at 17:24):

x←a→yx \leftarrow a \rightarrow yx←a→y

John Baez (Dec 10 2020 at 17:25):

and we call this a span from xxx to yyy . So, by "endospan" I mean a span from some object to itself, like this:

John Baez (Dec 10 2020 at 17:25):

x←a→x x \leftarrow a \rightarrow x x←a→x

John Baez (Dec 10 2020 at 17:26):

But this is just a quiver in C.

John Baez (Dec 10 2020 at 17:27):

Btw, don't say "endospan" unless you're talking to someone who deeply understands category theory: it's not a standard term.

John Baez (Dec 10 2020 at 17:28):

John Baez (Dec 10 2020 at 17:31):

Well, in my article I explain how every object in Setop\mathsf{Set}^{op}Setop is a commutative monoid in Setop\mathsf{Set}^{op}Setop. "Everyone" knows this, but I go a bit further:

John Baez (Dec 10 2020 at 17:32):

We can define bimodules not just for algebras (which are modules in Vect) but for monoids in any category... any category that can have monoids in it. And then a span of sets is the exact same thing as a bimodule in Setop\mathsf{Set}^{op}Setop.

John Baez (Dec 10 2020 at 17:34):

So, in brief: the concept of bimodule makes sense in a large class of categories, called "monoidal categories". And this generalization of bimodule has spans of sets as a special case.

John Baez (Dec 10 2020 at 17:35):

So in particular a quiver of sets (better known to category theorists as a "graph"), which is the same as an endospan of sets, is a special case of an endo-bimodule.

John Baez (Dec 10 2020 at 17:36):

Again, "endo-bimodule" is not a standard term, but I mean an (A,B)(A,B)(A,B)-bimodule where A=BA = BA=B.

John Baez (Dec 10 2020 at 17:36):

I'll just say a couple of things. A category is a quiver in Set with extra structure, namely "identity" arrows and the ability to compose arrows.

John Baez (Dec 10 2020 at 17:41):
