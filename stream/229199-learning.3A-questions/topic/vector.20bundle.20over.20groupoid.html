Hi,

I’m starting to learn category theory in an attempt to find more elegant specifications of machine learning models. I’m trying to model vector fields over groupoids and have the following questions. I’d also be very happy with pointers to reference materials.

Consider a groupoid G and groupoid representation functor ρ:G→Vec \rho : G \to Vec ρ:G→Vec. For simplicity, the objects of G are a finite discrete set. Such a functor appears to also describe a generalization of a vector bundle over the objects of G, in which only the fibers of the connected components of G are required to be isomorphic. A morphism ϕ:x→y \phi : x \to y ϕ:x→y in G is lifted to a linear map between the vector bundle fibers ρϕ:Vx→Vy \rho \phi: V_x \to V_y ρϕ:Vx​→Vy​. The functor ρ \rho ρ here plays a similar role as the transport functor in Schreiber and Waldorf “Parallel Transport and Functors”.

Is there an elegant way to describe how the vector bundle V→Obj(G) V \to Obj(G) V→Obj(G) is induced by ρ \rho ρ? And how the space of sections of the vector bundle comes about? The space of sections is obviously just isomorphic to the direct sum of the fibers, but I wonder if there’s a more categorical way of think about it.

I was thinking of the vector bundle simply being the co-limit of the functor ρ \rho ρ, but that results in a vector space that transforms trivially under all groupoid morphisms, so that’s not it.

fosco (Sep 21 2020 at 21:23):

Hi, I think the keyword here is "geometric realization": up to homotopy, a groupoid "is" exactly a topological space with trivial homotopy groups in degree n≥2n\ge 2n≥2. Now, thanks to the Grothendieck construction, a functor ϱ:G→Vec\varrho : G \to \sf Vecϱ:G→Vec can be turned into a discrete opfibration E(G)→GE(G) \to GE(G)→G, whose fiber over x∈Gx\in Gx∈G is exactly ϱ(x)\varrho(x)ϱ(x). So, you don't just get a vector bundle over Ob(G)Ob(G)Ob(G), but a whole fibration over (the geometric realization of) GGG.

The link between group(oid) actions and fibrations over group(oid)s is quite deep, and even the case where the groupoid GGG is a group is enough to keep you busy for a long time. I first learned something on the topic in Chapter 3 of May's "Concise course in Algebraic Topology"; see also John Baez's "Lectures" here https://math.ucr.edu/home/baez/cohomology.pdf

David Michael Roberts (Sep 22 2020 at 00:29):

There is a canonical vector bundle of the groupoid Core(Vect)Core(Vect)Core(Vect) of vector spaces and linear isomorphisms, namely that over the object VVV, we place the vector space... VVV! The linear maps act exactly as they should. This canonical vector bundle is exactly analogous to the "universal covering space" over the groupoid of sets and bijections, which is the core of the category of pointed sets and pointed functions, mapping down to SetSetSet by forgetting the chosen point. This is what is used in the Grothendieck construction.

Given a groupoid GGG and a functor to VectVectVect, it factors through Core(Vect)Core(Vect)Core(Vect), and then one can pull the canonical vector bundle back, much as the category of elements over a groupoid arises as pullback of Core(Set∗)→Core(Set)Core(Set_*) \to Core(Set)Core(Set∗​)→Core(Set).

All this works for arbitrary categories in place of GGG, in which case we forget taking the cores, and in fact the universal vector bundle exists over all of VectVectVect, not just Core(Vect)Core(Vect)Core(Vect).

Before diving into the Grothendieck construction, it might help to check out the "category of elements" first, see for example the Wikipedia page here.

Starting from ρ:G→Vec\rho : G \to Vecρ:G→Vec, you can compose with the forgetful functor Vec→SetVec \to SetVec→Set to get a functor F:G→SetF : G \to SetF:G→Set. The category of elements of FFF is then maybe what you are looking for.

David Michael Roberts (Sep 23 2020 at 00:26):

There is a category over VectVectVect with class of objects the disjoint union of all objects in VectVectVect, and a morphism from v∈Vv\in Vv∈V to w∈Ww\in Ww∈W is a linear map L ⁣:V→WL\colon V\to WL:V→W with L(v)=wL(v)=wL(v)=w. The functor to VectVectVect sends v∈Vv\in Vv∈V to VVV and the obvious thing on arrows. The pullback of this functor with G→VectG\to VectG→Vect is what you want. No need to go to the category of sets.
