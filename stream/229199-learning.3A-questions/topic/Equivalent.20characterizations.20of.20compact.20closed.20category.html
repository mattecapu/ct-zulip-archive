John Baez (Aug 13 2024 at 08:32):

You can also define compact closed categories this way.

A symmetric monoidal category C\mathsf{C}C is closed if for each object A∈CA \in \mathsf{C}A∈C the functor −⊗A:C→C- \otimes A : \mathsf{C} \to \mathsf{C}−⊗A:C→C has a right adjoint. That means there's an isomorphism, natural in B∈Cop,C∈CB \in \mathsf{C}^{\rm op}, C \in \mathsf{C}B∈Cop,C∈C, like this:

C(B⊗A,C)≅C(B,[A,C]) \mathsf{C}(B \otimes A, C) \cong \mathsf{C}(B, [A,C]) C(B⊗A,C)≅C(B,[A,C])

You can say a symmetric monoidal category C\mathsf{C}C is compact closed if for every object AAA there is an object A∗A^\astA∗ such that there's an isomorphism, natural in B∈Cop,C∈CB \in \mathsf{C}^{\rm op}, C \in \mathsf{C}B∈Cop,C∈C, like this:

C(B⊗A,C)≅C(B,C⊗A∗) \mathsf{C}(B \otimes A, C) \cong \mathsf{C}(B, C \otimes A^\ast) C(B⊗A,C)≅C(B,C⊗A∗)

Todd Trimble (Aug 15 2024 at 01:01):

We once had a long conversation (including me and Robin Houston and Bruce Bartlett and Jamie Vicary and John Baez) about related matters at the n-Category Cafe. One may define a compact closed category as a symmetric monoidal category in which every object CCC has a monoidal dual C∗C^\astC∗ (left or right dual, take your pick; since the monoidal product is symmetric monoidal, it doesn't matter which). Now, speaking from personal experience, it can be tempting to say that C∗C^\astC∗ is a monoidal dual of CCC if C∗⊗−C^\ast \otimes -C∗⊗− is right adjoint to C⊗−C \otimes -C⊗−, i.e., if there is a natural isomorphism

C(A,C∗⊗B)≅C(C⊗A,B).\mathcal{C}(A, C^\ast \otimes B) \cong \mathcal{C}(C \otimes A, B).C(A,C∗⊗B)≅C(C⊗A,B).

But this is not enough! You need an extra condition to get the usual notion of monoidal dual. If cX:C⊗C∗⊗X→Xc_X: C \otimes C^\ast \otimes X \to XcX​:C⊗C∗⊗X→X denotes a typical component of the counit of C⊗−⊣C∗⊗−C \otimes - \dashv C^\ast \otimes -C⊗−⊣C∗⊗−, then you need also

cI⊗X=cI⊗1Xc_{I \otimes X} = c_I \otimes 1_XcI⊗X​=cI​⊗1X​

(see the comment here).

Eventually it was Bruce Bartlett who explained that this condition really doesn't come for free, in a comment here.

It's a fairly subtle issue. See also Remark 2.16 here.

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Aug 15 2024 at 07:22):

One conceptual way of understanding the characterisation at the beginning is to consider it a special case of a result in formal 2-category theory, by translating facts about a symmetric monoidal category C\mathcal{C}C into facts about its delooping BC\mathrm{B}\mathcal{C}BC, a 2-category with a single object.
In the latter, the 1-morphisms - which are all endomorphisms of the unique object - are the objects of C\mathcal{C}C and the 2-morphisms are the morphisms of C\mathcal{C}C.

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Aug 15 2024 at 07:27):

Then, an internal hom [A,B][A, B][A,B] in C\mathcal{C}C with its evaluation map evA,B:A⊗[A,B]→B\mathrm{ev}_{A,B}: A \otimes [A, B] \to BevA,B​:A⊗[A,B]→B is the same as a right Kan extension of BBB along AAA in BC\mathrm{B}\mathcal{C}BC.

So the following are equivalent:

  • C\mathcal{C}C is closed,
  • BC\mathrm{B}\mathcal{C}BC has right Kan extensions of all morphisms along all morphisms.

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Aug 15 2024 at 07:32):

Now, in a 2-category, a right Kan extension ρ:Ranpf∘p⇒f\rho: \mathrm{Ran}_p f \circ p \Rightarrow fρ:Ranp​f∘p⇒f of fff along ppp is preserved by a morphism ggg if g∘ρg \circ \rhog∘ρ is a right Kan extension of g∘fg \circ fg∘f along ppp.

A right Kan extension is called absolute if it is preserved by every morphism.

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Aug 15 2024 at 07:36):

So in BC\mathrm{B}\mathcal{C}BC, an absolute right Kan extension is the same thing as an evaluation map evA,B\mathrm{ev}_{A, B}evA,B​ with the property that, for all CCC, evA,B⊗C\mathrm{ev}_{A, B} \otimes CevA,B​⊗C is an evaluation map itself.

Which translates to the fact that the canonical morphism [A,B]⊗C→[A,B⊗C][A, B] \otimes C \to [A, B \otimes C][A,B]⊗C→[A,B⊗C] is an isomorphism.

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Aug 15 2024 at 07:39):

Now, AAA has a dual in C\mathcal{C}C if and only if AAA is (let's say right) adjoint as a 1-morphism in BC\mathrm{B}\mathcal{C}BC.

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Aug 15 2024 at 07:41):

There is a general result in formal 2-category theory which says that, for a 1-morphism f:x→yf: x \to yf:x→y, the following are all equivalent:

  • fff is a right adjoint,
  • a right Kan extension of idx\mathrm{id}_xidx​ along fff exists and is absolute,
  • a right Kan extension of idx\mathrm{id}_xidx​ along fff exists and is preserved by fff itself.

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Aug 15 2024 at 07:45):

It is clear, moreover, that if an absolute right Kan extension of idx\mathrm{id}_xidx​ along fff exists, then an absolute right Kan extension of any morphism along fff exists (just whisker the first one with that morphism!).

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Aug 15 2024 at 07:46):

So the following are equivalent:

  • C\mathcal{C}C is compact closed,
  • BC\mathrm{B}\mathcal{C}BC has absolute right Kan extensions of all morphisms along all morphisms.

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Aug 15 2024 at 07:48):

But by the result above, the latter is equivalent to

  • for all objects AAA of C\mathcal{C}C, BC\mathrm{B}\mathcal{C}BC has a right Kan extension of III along AAA, which is preserved by AAA.

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Aug 15 2024 at 07:50):

So if we assume that C\mathcal{C}C is closed to begin with, then to prove that it is compact closed, it suffices to show that evA,I⊗A\mathrm{ev}_{A, I} \otimes AevA,I​⊗A is an evaluation map for all AAA, which, as we saw above, is equivalent to the canonical map [A,I]⊗A→[A,A⊗I][A, I] \otimes A \to [A, A \otimes I][A,I]⊗A→[A,A⊗I] being an isomorphism.

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Aug 15 2024 at 07:50):

Which is the Kelly-Laplaza characterisation mentioned at the beginning!

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Aug 15 2024 at 07:56):

(By the way, all of this works in the non-symmetric case as well, giving a characterisation of the existence of one-sided duals; one has to dualise from right Kan extensions to right Kan lifts to get the other dual.)
