Topic: adjunction puzzle

John Baez (Aug 16 2022 at 15:58):

Yesterday James Dolan asked me this puzzle, and then told me the answer:

For any bicategory BBB there's a bicategory Adj(B)\mathrm{Adj}(B)Adj(B) with the same objects as BBB, where the morphisms are adjunctions in BBB, and the 2-morphisms are some well-known maps between adjunctions.

John Baez (Aug 16 2022 at 15:59):

For example if B=CatB = \mathbf{Cat}B=Cat, Adj(B)\mathrm{Adj}(B)Adj(B) is the usual 2-category where morphisms are adjoint functors.

Joe Moeller (Aug 16 2022 at 16:52):

You have four functors, two adjunctions. The internal-internal unit and counit actually pan out to give an internal unit and counit giving an adjunction between the left of the left and the left of the right, which of course forces the left of the right to be the same as the right of the left. So an adjoint triple.

Tobias Schmude (Aug 16 2022 at 17:09):

Mike Shulman (Aug 18 2022 at 17:50):

Adj(Adj(B)\rm Adj(Adj(B)Adj(Adj(B) plays an important role in the specification of modal type theories for adjoint strings, particularly cohesion. When you specify a 2-category M\cal MM as the "mode theory" for a type theory, then every morphism in M\cal MM gives rise to an adjoint pair of modal operators, a "positive" one (with a left universal property) and a "negative" one (with a right universal property). Thus, if you want an adjoint triple of modal operators, you can achieve that by specifying an adjoint pair of morphisms in M\cal MM.

A more category-theoretic way of saying something similar is that if p:C→Bp:C\to Bp:C→B is a bifibration (i.e. both a fibration and an opfibration), then every morphism in BBB gives rise to an adjoint pair of functors between the fibers over its domain and codomain. Therefore, an adjoint pair of morphisms in BBB gives rise to an adjoint triple of functors between these fibers.

Kenji Maillard (Aug 22 2022 at 09:07):

A similar argument also shows that Adj\mathbf{Adj}Adj cannot be the underlying functor of a monad or a comonad.
If adj\mathbf{adj}adj is the walking adjunction (the 222-category with two objects and a single adjunction between these), then Adj(adj)=→\mathbf{Adj}(\mathbf{adj}) = \toAdj(adj)=→ the walking arrow 222-category and Adj(→)=2\mathbf{Adj}(\to) = \mathbb{2}Adj(→)=2 the discrete 222-category with two objects. There is no functor from →\to→ to 2\mathbb{2}2 and no functor from adj\mathbf{adj}adj to →\to→, so the former shows that Adj\mathbf{Adj}Adj cannot be equipped with a comultiplication, and the latter that it cannot be equipped with a unit.
