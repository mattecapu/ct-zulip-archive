Mike Shulman (Feb 18 2021 at 01:40):

I'm not sure what "defined over a dependent type" means. Inside of a 2-category such as Cat, you can define fibrations, and a C-indexed category is equivalent to a fibration over C. Of course an internal category in Cat would be a double category, and an internal category in the 2-category of fibrations over C would be a double indexed category.

Mike Shulman (Feb 18 2021 at 15:32):

Matteo Capucci (he/him) said:

In my head it corresponds to give the definition of category in the TT but every context has some c:Cc : Cc:C in it.

Sure, that's just a category in context CCC, or equivalently a function from CCC to the type Cat\mathsf{Cat}Cat of categories. If it's interpreted in the internal logic of Set, you get literally a set CCC and a CCC-indexed family of categories. To interpret it in the "internal logic of Cat", you have to say what you mean by the latter: are the dependent types arbitrary categories over categories? Fibrations? Opfibrations? If they're fibrations, then just a simple dependent type c:C⊢D typec:C \vdash D\,\mathsf{type}c:C⊢Dtype is already (equivalent to) an indexed category, and a CCC-indexed family of categories as you describe is a category internal to indexed categories, i.e. an indexed double category. One thing you can say, I guess, is that if you take the dependent types to be discrete fibrations (but allow the base type CCC to be an arbitrary category, i.e. not just a discrete fibration over 1), then a CCC-indexed family of categories would be a strict functor Cop→CatC^{\rm op} \to \mathrm{Cat}Cop→Cat, which is a sort of indexed category.
