Mike Shulman (Jun 01 2023 at 17:11):

In general the answer is yes, and one way to make it precise is with universal-algebraic tools such as "operads" and "Lawvere theories". For instance, the Lawvere theory of Boolean algebras is a category B\mathbf{B}B with finite products generated "freely" by one object XXX that has the structure of an internal Boolean algebra. Then the morphisms Xn→XX^n \to XXn→X in this category are precisely the "nnn-ary operations that can be defined in terms of the defining Boolean algebra operations". Moreover, freeness of B\mathbf{B}B means that to give an actual Boolean algebra is equivalent to giving a finite-product-preserving functor B→Set\mathbf{B} \to \rm SetB→Set, and similarly to give a Boolean algebra homomorphism is equivalent to giving a natural transformation between such functors. Naturality of such a transformation then tells you that any such homomorphism automatically commutes with any of the "definable operations". The case of categories is similar, except that you need something fancier instead of a finite-product category.

John Baez (Jun 02 2023 at 19:43):

That's because a model of a Lawvere theory TTT is a (nice) functor from that theory to Set, say F:T→SetF: T \to \mathsf{Set}F:T→Set, and a homomorphism between Lawvere theories is (any) natural transformation between such functors. As you know, a natural transformation between two functors out of TTT gives a commutative square for each morphism in TTT. These commutative squares say that homomorphisms between models of TTT commute with all the operations in TTT!

John Baez (Jun 02 2023 at 20:00):

The process by which these additional morphisms are formed in the Lawvere theory is not yet clear to me.

A Lawvere theory is a category with finite products, and this tells you how to get new morphisms from old: first by composing them (which you can do in any category), and then by a coupld other tricks, which are built into the definition of "finite products".

John Baez (Jun 02 2023 at 20:03):

For example in any category with finite products we have a "diagonal morphism" ΔX:X→X×X\Delta_X : X \to X \times XΔX​:X→X×X for any object XXX, so one operation we always get to use is something like

ΔX(x)=(x,x)\Delta_X(x) = (x,x)ΔX​(x)=(x,x)

This "duplicates a variable".

In the Lawvere theory for groups we also have an operation called "multiplication" m:X×X→Xm: X \times X \to Xm:X×X→X, which lets us multiply. For short we could write it like this:

x⋅y:=m(x,y) x \cdot y := m(x,y)x⋅y:=m(x,y)

Composing this with ΔX\Delta_XΔX​ we get a squaring operation m∘Δ:X→Xm \circ \Delta: X \to Xm∘Δ:X→X which does this:

m(Δ(x))=x⋅xm (\Delta(x)) = x \cdot xm(Δ(x))=x⋅x.

And so on. When you understand finite products, you know all the tricks here.

Yes, exactly! Here are three other things we can do in any category with finite products. Given a morphism f:X→Yf: X \to Yf:X→Y and a morphism f′:X′→Y′f' : X' \to Y'f′:X′→Y′ we can "set them side by side", forming the morphism

John Baez (Jun 02 2023 at 21:08):

Also, every category with finite products lets us "delete data", since there's a unique morphism from any XXX to the terminal object 111. This lets us take a morphism f:Xn→Xmf: X^n \to X^mf:Xn→Xm and "throw out" any one of the mmm outputs and get a morphism from XnX^nXn to Xm−1X^{m-1}Xm−1.

John Baez (Jun 02 2023 at 21:09):

Thirdly, there's a god-given isomorphism SX,Y:X×Y→Y×XS_{X,Y} : X \times Y \to Y \times XSX,Y​:X×Y→Y×X that lets us "switch things around".

John Baez (Jun 02 2023 at 21:11):

These three, together with composing morphisms and "duplicating data" using the diagonal are all the basic ways, to build new morphisms from old in a category with finite products.
