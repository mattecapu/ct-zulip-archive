Topic: smallest image subcategory

David Egolf (Jan 10 2022 at 00:59):

I have started reading "Generalized Congruences - Epimorphisms in Cat\mathsf{Cat}Cat" as part of an effort to understand questions related to those raised in in #learning: questions > equivalence relationships preserved by endofunctors.

The authors note that, given a functor F:A→BF: A \to BF:A→B, the image of FFF is not generally a category. However, they note it is possible to create a "smallest" subcategory of BBB containing the objects and morphisms of F(A)F(A)F(A).

The "smallest" of anything usually seems to be a limit or colimit of some kind. To get practice with this kind of thinking, I was trying to express "smallest subcategory of BBB containing the image F(A)F(A)F(A)" as a limit or colimit. However, it feels like I am running into a problem based on the fact that F(A)F(A)F(A) isn't necessarily a category. In particular, I'm not seeing how to diagrammatically describe a category CCC that contains the objects and morphisms of F(A)F(A)F(A).

Any hints are appreciated.

David Egolf (Jan 10 2022 at 02:28):

Intuitively, one reason this happens is because pairs of morphisms that weren't compatible to compose in AAA can become compatible to compose in BBB. By a pair of morphisms that are "compatible to compose", I mean two morphisms where the source of one is the target of the other. This can result in a situation where we can't compose some compatible morphisms in F(A)F(A)F(A). If this happens, then F(A)F(A)F(A) fails to be a category.

In more detail, say m1m_1m1​ and m2m_2m2​ are two non-compatible morphisms in AAA. Let F:A→BF: A \to BF:A→B act so that F(m1)F(m_1)F(m1​) and F(m2)F(m_2)F(m2​) become compatible to compose. For F(A)F(A)F(A) to be a category, we need to be able to compose these to form F(m1)∘F(m2)F(m_1) \circ F(m_2)F(m1​)∘F(m2​). If m1m_1m1​ and m2m_2m2​ were compatible in AAA, then we would have F(m1∘m2)=F(m1)∘F(m2)F(m_1 \circ m_2) = F(m_1) \circ F(m_2)F(m1​∘m2​)=F(m1​)∘F(m2​). That, is we would be able to form this composite in the image of FFF. However, since m1m_1m1​ and m2m_2m2​ aren't compatible, there is no guarantee we can form their composite in the image of FFF.

The authors of that paper have a nice figure illustrating this:
image of G is not a category

In the category on the right, the composite of the two morphisms in the middle is the morphism on the right. However, GGG sends hhh to a different morphism. G(f)G(f)G(f) and G(g)G(g)G(g) are compatible to compose, but are not actually possible to compose while staying within the image of GGG. So, the image of GGG is not a category here.

David Egolf (Jan 10 2022 at 02:31):

Incidentally, I suppose this particular problem only occurs when AAA has more than one object. So, unless there are other problems I'm not thinking of, we can expect the image of a functor from a category with one object to be a category. I suppose this is one way to see that the image of a group homomorphism has at least a chance to be a subgroup (should be closed under composition).

Hmmm, let's see.
We want any two morphisms in the image of F:A→BF: A \to BF:A→B to be composable into a morphism that is still in the image of FFF. This will let the image of FFF be a category.
So, for any composable morphisms F(u):S→TF(u): S \to TF(u):S→T and F(v):T→VF(v): T \to VF(v):T→V in the image of FFF (where u,vu,vu,v are morphisms in AAA) we need F(v)∘F(u)F(v) \circ F(u)F(v)∘F(u) to also be in the image of FFF. That is, we need F(v)∘F(u)=F(w)F(v) \circ F(u) = F(w)F(v)∘F(u)=F(w) for some morphism www in AAA. I think this is all we need.
