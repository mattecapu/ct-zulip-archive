Any species F:B→BF: B \to BF:B→B defines an endofunctor F^:Set→Set\hat{F}: \mathsf{Set} \to \mathsf{Set}F^:Set→Set by

F^(X)=∑n=0∞F(n)×XnSn \widehat{F}(X) = \displaystyle{ \sum_{n= 0}^\infty \frac{F(n) \times X^n}{S_n} } F(X)=n=0∑∞​Sn​F(n)×Xn​

where I'm identifying nnn with some nnn-element set, and the symmetric group SnS_nSn​ acts on both F(n)F(n)F(n) and XnX^nXn in the obvious ways. This formula should remind you strongly of a Taylor series, and functors of the form F^\widehat{F}F are called analytic functors.

Composition of species corresponds to composition of analytic functors:

(F⊙G)^≅F^∘G^ \widehat{(F \odot G)} \cong \widehat{F} \circ \widehat{G} (F⊙G)​≅F∘G

Very smart!

A monoid object in the category of species with its composition tensor product is exactly an operad. This is one of the slickest ways to define operads.

So, unpacking this a bit: an operad is a species with a multiplication m:F⊙F⇒Fm: F \odot F \Rightarrow Fm:F⊙F⇒F and also the unit i:I→Fi: I \to Fi:I→F where III is the unit object for the composition tensor product, and mmm and iii need to obey the associative law and left/right unit laws.

It's very good to take this definition and unpack it even further to get a longer and very explicit definition of 'operad'.

John Baez (Jul 25 2024 at 13:45):

So we can say:

1) A monoid object in the monoidal category of endofunctors on Set with its composition tensor product is a monad.

2) A monoid object in the monoidal category of species with its composition tensor product is an operad.

It's not a coincidence that they rhyme: Peter May did that on purpose.

Even better, using the formula I wrote down, any operad FFF gives a monad F^\widehat{F}F! But we only get certain specially nice monads this way.

Todd Trimble (Jul 25 2024 at 20:20):

for the equivalence between species and analytic functors, Joyal's paper is the standard one, but I found a very well-written article of Adamek quite enlightening

Warning! This is not an equivalence between the category of species and the category of analytic functors, which I think I've seen asserted incorrectly in the literature. However, Joyal did show that it is an equivalence between the groupoid of species and the groupoid of analytic functors.

For example, take the species XXX which is terminal at a set of cardinality 111 and initial for all other finite cardinalities, and take the species X⊗2/S2X^{\otimes 2}/S_2X⊗2/S2​ which is terminal at a set of cardinality 222 and initial for all other finite cardinalities. There is no species map X→X⊗2/S2X \to X^{\otimes 2}/S_2X→X⊗2/S2​. However, there is a natural transformation between their analytic functors, where the analytic functor on XXX is the identity functor on Set\mathsf{Set}Set and the analytic functor on X⊗2/S2X^{\otimes 2}/S_2X⊗2/S2​ takes a set SSS to its symmetric square [the quotient of S×SS \times SS×S that identifies (x,y)(x, y)(x,y) with (y,x)(y, x)(y,x)]. This natural transformation is induced by the diagonal map S→S×SS \to S \times SS→S×S.

For any monoid mmm in any monoidal category MMM, we can define an action of that monoid on an object x∈Mx \in Mx∈M to be a morphism a:m⊗x→xa : m \otimes x \to xa:m⊗x→x obeying the usual two laws of an action. I'm guessing you tried this and it didn't work.

But more generally, suppose XXX is any category on which monoidal category MMM acts - a so-called [[actegory]] of MMM. This means we have a functor A:M×X→XA : M \times X \to XA:M×X→X obeying the usual two laws of an action up to coherent isomorphism. Then we can define an action of a monoid m∈Mm \in Mm∈M on an object x∈Xx \in Xx∈X to be a morphism

A(m,x)→x A(m, x) \to x A(m,x)→x

obeying a version of the usual two laws. This is an example of the microcosm principle: just as a monoid likes to live in a monoidal category, an action of a monoid likes to live in an actegory!

The category of species is a monoidal category with its composition product, and an operad is a monoid in this monoidal category - you said that. But further, the category of sets is an actegory of this monoidal category! Thus, we can define an action of an operad on a set, and this is the usual notion of an 'algebra' of an operad!

The relation between operads and species is, in a sense, the same as the relation between categories and graphs.

Indeed, let  Pb=Pb →→ Setf \,{\mathbb P}{\rm b} = {\rm Pb\, ^\to _\to\, Set_f}\,Pb=Pb→→​Setf​ be the double category of pullback square in finite sets.
Then operads, in their unbiased form, can be defined as double categories  P=P →→ Setf \,{\mathbb P} = {\rm P\, ^\to _\to\, Set_f}\,P=P→→​Setf​ with a sum preserving discrete fibration p:P→Pbp:{\rm P\to Pb}p:P→Pb (making the obvious diagram commute, so that we have in fact a double functor  p:P→Pb \,p:{\mathbb P}\to{\mathbb P}{\rm b}\,p:P→Pb).
The idea is that the loose part of P{\mathbb P}P is the free prop on the operad; the discrete fibration condition corresponds to the classical axioms on the symmetries of arrows and their compatibility with composition; the sum preserving condition ensures that it is in fact a free prop on an operad.

Similarly, species can be defined as double graphs  P=P →→ Setf \,{\mathbb P} = {\rm P\, ^\to _\to\, Set_f}\,P=P→→​Setf​ with a sum preserving discrete fibration p:P→Pbp:{\rm P\to Pb}p:P→Pb.
Now, we may think of a loose arrow in P{\mathbb P}P as a mapping with a structure of that species on each fiber; the discrete fibration condition correspond to the transport of structures along bijections.

In this perspective, operads are then the monoids in the monoidal category of endospans in Cat{\rm Cat}Cat (double graphs) on Setf{\rm Set_f}Setf​ over  Pb\,{\mathbb P}{\rm b}Pb.
Explicitly, if P{\mathbb P}P and Q{\mathbb Q}Q are species, a loose arrow of P⊗Q{\mathbb P}\otimes{\mathbb Q}P⊗Q over a mapping f:I→Jf:I\to Jf:I→J is a factorization f=h∘gf = h\circ gf=h∘g, along with a loose P{\mathbb P}P-arrow over ggg and a loose Q{\mathbb Q}Q-arrow over hhh.

It is easy to recognize the usual composition product on species, when JJJ is terminal.

Here are, in this framework, some basic examples of operads/species with their algebras:

If  P=Pb\,{\mathbb P} = {\mathbb P}{\rm b}P=Pb, we clearly get the terminal species (one structure over each set) and the terminal operad. 
Its algebras are commutative monoids, its lax algebras are symmetric multicategories.

If  P⊆Pb\,{\mathbb P} \subseteq {\mathbb P}{\rm b}P⊆Pb is obtained by considering just those squares whose loose sides are bijections, we get the species characteristic of singletons and the (unary) operad with one object and one arrow (the identity). 
Its algebras are sets, its lax algebras are categories.

If  P\,{\mathbb P}P is obtained by Pb{\mathbb P}{\rm b}Pb by endowing each fiber of the loose arrows with a total oder (so that  P\,{\mathbb P}P is, by definition, the species of total orders), then it has naturally an operad structure (the so-called associative operad), since a total order of totally ordered sets has a natural total order.
Its algebras are monoids, its lax algebras are (plain) multicategories.

A less obvious operad  P\,{\mathbb P}P is obtained by endowing the loose arrows of  Pb\,{\mathbb P}{\rm b}Pb with spans over them.

That is, a loose arrow of  P\,{\mathbb P}P is a mapping f:I→Jf: I\to Jf:I→J along with a span (up to isomorphisms)
f’:K→If’:K\to If’:K→I and f’’:K→Jf’’:K\to Jf’’:K→J (with f∘f’=f’’f\circ f’ = f’’f∘f’=f’’).
As a species, structures over a set SSS are mapping T→ST\to ST→S (up to iso), that is isomorphism classes in Set/S{\rm Set}/SSet/S (or, equivalently, mappings to the set of natural numbers S→NS\to {\bf N}S→N ).
It has a natural operad structure with composition given by pullbacks. 

An algebra for this  P\,{\mathbb P}P is a commutative monoid along with an action of the monoid of natural numbers on it.

More interestingly,  P\,{\mathbb P}P is in fact a cartesian operad (which is one of the forms in which algebraic theories can be presented). Namely, it is the free cartesian operad on the terminal one; thus its cartesian algebras are again commutative monoids.
