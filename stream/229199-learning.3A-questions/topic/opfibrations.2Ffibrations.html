Jade Master (Oct 06 2020 at 18:54):

It might be illuminating to think about this composite in the case of functions. For a function f:X→Yf : X \to Yf:X→Y there is an inverse map

f−1:Y→Setf^{-1} : Y \to \mathsf{Set}f−1:Y→Set

It turns out that the grothendieck construction of f−1f^{-1}f−1 is the function fff. To be completely honest I usually think of the usual Grothendieck construction as a fancier version of this. In this context the composite is

f−1∘f:X→Setf^{-1} \circ f : X \to \mathsf{Set}f−1∘f:X→Set

If fff is injective, then this composite is the identity. In general it is multi-valued. You can use this intuition in full-version of the Grothendieck construction as well. F∘∫F F \circ \int FF∘∫F is like the multivalued result of a functor composed with its inverse.

John Baez (Oct 06 2020 at 18:58):

Everyone should think of the Grothendieck construction as a fancier version of what Jade just described.

Using f−1f^{-1}f−1 as a name for the map sending each point of YYY to its inverse image under fff may confuse some people here, so I hope they get the point: it's just the usual usage of f−1f^{-1}f−1 to mean "inverse image" (which confuses all beginners, since f−1f^{-1}f−1 has another meaning too, in which f−1f^{-1}f−1 exists iff fff is a bijection).

John Baez (Oct 06 2020 at 19:02):

Another nice way to think of the Grothendieck construction is that it's an analogue of this thing in topology:

If you have a fibration p:E→Bp: E \to Bp:E→B where the fiber is FFF, it has a classifying map

f:B→BG f: B \to B Gf:B→BG

where GGG is the group of homotopy autoequivalences of FFF, and BGBGBG is the classifying space of GGG. Conversely from the classifying map f:B→BGf: B \to BGf:B→BG we can reconstruct the fibration p:E→Bp: E \to Bp:E→B up to homotopy equivalence.

John Baez (Oct 06 2020 at 19:03):

So, we can ask Matteo's question in this context: what do you get if you compose fff and ppp? You get

f∘p:E→BG f \circ p: E \to BGf∘p:E→BG

John Baez (Oct 06 2020 at 19:04):

This is the classifying map for some fibration with EEE as its base space! Call this fibration

p~:E~→E \tilde{p}: \tilde{E} \to E p~​:E~→E

John Baez (Oct 06 2020 at 19:05):

The fiber of this fibration is still FFF (since we got it from a classifying map to BGB GBG).

John Baez (Oct 06 2020 at 19:06):

What is it? Well, there's only one reasonable choice: it's the pullback of the fibration

p:E→B p: E \to Bp:E→B

along itself!

John Baez (Oct 06 2020 at 19:07):

I'm too lazy to draw the pullback square. But this pullback will be a fibration over EEE with the same fiber as our original fibration, namely FFF.

John Baez (Oct 06 2020 at 19:08):

If you draw pictures of everything here - drawing topological spaces as blobs in the usual way, with points as dots - and also draw pictures of what Matteo was asking about - drawing categories as blobs in the usual way, with points as objects - you'll see they're the exact same pictures.

John Baez (Oct 06 2020 at 19:09):

So, we're "pulling back a fibration along itself" - either in the categorical sense or the topological sense.

John Baez (Oct 07 2020 at 05:00):

sarahzrf said:

like, i seem to recall Joe Moeller mentioning something way back about like, any functor between groupoids is equivalent to a fibration, or something like that—so the relevant fact about sets is not that they lack higher structure, but that they're groupoidal

Yes, I think I taught him this! Given any functor between groupoids p:E→Bp : E \to Bp:E→B, you can "puff up" EEE and get an equivalent groupoid E′E'E′ and a fibration p′:E′→Bp': E' \to Bp′:E′→B that's equivalent to ppp, in the sense that the triangle made from p:E→Bp : E \to Bp:E→B, p′:E′→Bp' : E' \to Bp′:E′→B and the equivalence commutes up to natural isomorphism.

John Baez (Oct 07 2020 at 05:02):

It's easy to cook up E′E'E′. An object of E′E'E′ is an object of e∈Ee \in Ee∈E together with an isomorphism f:p(e)→bf: p(e) \to bf:p(e)→b in BBB, where bbb is any object of BBB.

John Baez (Oct 07 2020 at 05:02):

And it's easy to cook up p′:E′→Bp': E' \to Bp′:E′→B: it sends f:p(e)→bf: p(e) \to bf:p(e)→b to bbb.

John Baez (Oct 07 2020 at 05:03):

The fibers of p′p'p′ are called the homotopy fibers of ppp.

John Baez (Oct 07 2020 at 05:03):

For example p′p'p′ is surjective on objects iff ppp is essentially surjective on objects.

John Baez (Oct 07 2020 at 06:36):

I guess in homotopy theory this trick is called taking the "homotopy cocylinder" of p:E→Bp: E \to Bp:E→B.
