Topic: Understanding Yoneda Lemma "naturalness in F"

Davi Sales Barreira (May 30 2022 at 16:59):

In the Yoneda Lemma, it's said that:

Given a functor F:Set→SetF:\mathbf{Set} \to \mathbf{Set}F:Set→Set, and a set SSS, there is an isomorphism

F(S)≅Nat(yS,F)F(S) \cong Nat(y^S, F)F(S)≅Nat(yS,F)

where NatNatNat is the set of natural transformations. Moreover, the equation above is natural in both SSS and FFF.

Now, the part I'm having trouble understanding is this final "natural in FFF". I mean, in the functor category [Set,Set][\mathbf{Set}, \mathbf{Set}][Set,Set], the natural transformations are morphisms, so what are the 'things' that work as "Natural Transformations" in this category?

Mike Shulman (May 30 2022 at 17:08):

It means that for any morphism F→GF\to GF→G, which is itself a natural transformation, an appropriate square commutes relating this isomorphism for FFF to this isomorphism for GGG along the maps F(S)→G(S)F(S) \to G(S)F(S)→G(S) and Nat(yS,F)→Nat(yS,G){\rm Nat}(y^S,F) \to {\rm Nat}(y^S,G)Nat(yS,F)→Nat(yS,G) induced by the natural transformation F→GF\to GF→G.
