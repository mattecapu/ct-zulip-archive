Jules Hedges (Jan 24 2022 at 13:09):

nLab says that a [[generalized element]] of an object is any morphism into that object. I feel like it's also reasonable to say that any covariant functor F:C→SetF : \mathcal C \to \mathbf{Set}F:C→Set is a "theory of generalised elements in C\mathcal CC", which generalises the usual definition further by allowing FFF to not be representable. The idea being that if you have x∈F(X)x \in F(X)x∈F(X) then you can apply any morphism to get F(f)(x)∈F(Y)F(f)(x) \in F(Y)F(f)(x)∈F(Y). I also instinctively feel that this is exactly the situation where the word "copresheaf" is appropriate. Is this a right perspective?

What I actually want to do is for monoidal C\mathcal CC to say that any lax monoidal functor F:C→SetF : \mathcal C \to \mathbf{Set}F:C→Set is a "theory of generalised states in C\mathcal CC". That is, states x∈F(X)x \in F(X)x∈F(X) and y∈F(Y)y \in F(Y)y∈F(Y) can be composed into a joint state x∇y∈F(X⊗Y)x \nabla y \in F (X \otimes Y)x∇y∈F(X⊗Y). This abstracts what seems to be the useful property of C(I,−)\mathcal C (I, -)C(I,−). Is this a perspective that already exists?

I've taken this seriously up to the point of using the string diagram language for ordinary states for these "generalised states". I didn't prove this is sound, although I strongly suspect it is

Martti Karvonen (Jan 24 2022 at 14:29):

A working hypothesis I have is that most/all resource theories arise by taking the category of elements of a lax monoidal functor. This is true for the resource theories constructed in that paper, and it's also true for resource theories of contextuality and of non-locality. As a result, I'm tempted to think of a lax monoidal functor into Set as something that gives for each object AAA the set of resouces of type AAA. At times the set of resources has more structure (it's convex, or has a metric on it etc) so instead of sets we land somewhere else, but that's the basic idea.

Jens Hemelaer (Jan 24 2022 at 18:26):

Jules Hedges said:

I'm vaguely optimistic that the view of not-necessarily-representable copresheaves as generalised points might appear already in Grothendieck

Points of the presheaf topos PSh(C)\mathbf{PSh}(\mathcal{C})PSh(C) are the same as flat functors C→Set\mathcal{C} \to \mathbf{Set}C→Set, and these often include non-representable ones.

In topos theory, arbitrary functors C→Set\mathcal{C} \to \mathbf{Set}C→Set agree with distributions on PSh(C)\mathbf{PSh}(\mathcal{C})PSh(C) (this idea was introduced by Lawvere). The points of the topos can then be identified with Dirac delta distributions, via the work of Bunge and Funk. So in this sense distributions are literally generalized points. Maybe the words "state" and "distribution" refer to the same kind of intuition.

If TTT is the 2-monad whose algebras are finite-limit categories, then a lax TTT-morphism is precisely a finite-limit-preserving functor (and is automatically a strong TTT-morphism). So there's a very precise analogy between finite-limit-preserving functors and lax monoidal functors.
