(I'm rotating it by 90∘90^\circ90∘ compared to his diagrams, so my horizontal morphisms are vertical lines.) There aren't any non-identity "vertical" morphisms in this first image, so it just looks like the diagrams for a monoid in a monoidal category.

image.png

So the blue areas are a set (the objects of C\mathscr{C}C) and the vertical black lines are a span from that set to itself, to be thought of as the morphisms of C\mathscr{C}C. The unit of the monoid picks out identity morphisms and the multiplication performs composition. I think of it as a map that takes the "formal composition" of two compatible morphisms, i.e. a pair (A→fB,B→gC)(A\xrightarrow{f}B, B\xrightarrow{g}C)(Af​B,Bg​C) and returns their composite A→f;gCA\xrightarrow{f;g}CAf;g​C.

Vertical morphisms (represented in these diagrams by horizontal lines) are functions between sets, and squares (which include the black dots above) are maps of spans that are compatible with them. So we can draw a functor F ⁣:D→CF\colon \mathscr{D}\to \mathscr{C}F:D→C like this:

image.png

It's a function (red line) from the objects of D\mathscr{D}D (green area) to the objects of C\mathscr{C}C (blue area) and also a map (white square) between the spans representing the morphisms of the two categories that preserves identities and composition.
