Topic: linearly distributive functors between *-autonomous cats

Matteo Capucci (he/him) (Oct 25 2025 at 08:32):

Does anyone know if the sub-2-category of symmetric [[linearly distributive categories]] spanned by [[star-autonomous]] categories is equivalent to that of symmetric *-autonomous categories with lax monoidal functors which also laxly preserve the duality, i.e. those equipped with a coherent map F(c∗)→F(c)∗F(c^*) \to F(c)^*F(c∗)→F(c)∗?

Max Demirdilek (Jan 25 2026 at 12:50):

@Matteo Capucci: Apologies for taking a while to share my promised comment :upside_down:

In Theorem 2.94 of my recent preprint, I show that the (2,1)(2,1)(2,1)-category of braided linearly distributive (LD) categories with negation, braided Frobenius linearly distributive (LD) functors, and morphisms between them is 222-equivalent to the (2,1)(2,1)(2,1)-category of braided Grothendieck-Verdier (GV) categories, braided Grothendieck-Verdier (GV) functors, and morphisms between them (apologies for the self-citation). Recall that (non-symmetric) star-autonomous categories are also known as GV-categories. 

This 222-equivalence restricts to the symmetric setting: the (2,1)(2,1)(2,1)-categories of symmetric LD-categories with negation and symmetric GV-categories are 222-equivalent. As expected, these are those full sub-(2,1)(2,1)(2,1)-categories given by braided LD-categories with negation (respectively, GV-categories) whose two braidings (respectively, single braiding) are symmetric.

Informally, a (braided) GV-functor between (braided) GV-categories is a (braided) lax monoidal functor equipped with a family of isomorphisms that witness the functor’s strong preservation of left and right duals. See Definition 2.39 for the precise formulation.  

The notion of functor suggested in your post appears to be more general. You ask for

lax monoidal functors which also laxly preserve the duality.

Any lax monoidal functor F ⁣:C→DF\colon \mathcal{C}\to \mathcal{D}F:C→D between GV-categories (C,K)(\mathcal{C},K)(C,K) and (D,k)(\mathcal{D},k)(D,k), together with a morphism F(K)→kF(K)\to kF(K)→k (what I call a form), induces morphisms F(X∗)→F(X)∗F(X^{\ast})\to F(X)^{\ast}F(X∗)→F(X)∗ and F(∗X)→∗F(X)F({^{\ast}X})\to {^{\ast}F(X)}F(∗X)→∗F(X) for all X∈CX\in \mathcal{C}X∈C (Definition 2.35). It seems that the resulting 222-category of (symmetric) GV-categories, (symmetric) lax monoidal functors equipped with a form, and morphisms between them (Definition 2.40) is 222-equivalent to the 222-category of (symmetric) LD-categories with negation, (symmetric) ⊗\otimes⊗-lax monoidal functors equipped with a form/counit morphism, and morphisms between them (again Definition 2.40). I haven’t checked the last claim carefully, though.

Max Demirdilek (Feb 02 2026 at 08:27):

That’s a fair question, and I understand why the naming can be irritating.

My choice of terminology is not intended to be disrespectful in any way. To my knowledge, the notion of a ribbon structure on a Grothendieck-Verdier category was introduced by Boyarchenko and Drinfeld. It has since played an important role in developments in representation theory and quantum topology. In that setting, the term ribbon Grothendieck-Verdier category (and similarly braided or pivotal) is widely used. Replacing it with "ribbon (non-symmetric) *-autonomous category" would make communication with that audience rather difficult.

I don’t read the terminology as a claim about priority or as an attempt to overwrite earlier work. My intention here is simply to follow the usage most familiar in the context in which I am working.
