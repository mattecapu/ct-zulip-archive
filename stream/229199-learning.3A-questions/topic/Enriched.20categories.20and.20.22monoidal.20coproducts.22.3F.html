Topic: Enriched categories and "monoidal coproducts"?

Paolo Perrone (Jun 21 2021 at 09:07):

In enriched category theory, composition of arrows is not always given by the cartesian product of the hom-objects, sometimes one enriches in a monoidal category instead.
A nice example is given by Lawvere metric spaces, where the enriching category is [0,∞][0,\infty][0,∞] ordered downwards, with the sum as the (monoidal) product.
An equivalent example, that can be obtained by applying the function e−xe^{-x}e−x to [0,∞][0,\infty][0,∞], is the unit interval [0,1][0,1][0,1], ordered upwards, with multiplication as operation. (This has been used, for example, in a recent preprint by Tai-Danae Bradley et al.)
In this last example, and in the preprint I referred to, the objects of [0,1][0,1][0,1] can be interpreted as probabilities, and the composition of arrows gives the product of probabilities, a bit like in a (discrete-state) Markov chain, which is very neat.

Paolo Perrone (Jun 21 2021 at 09:11):

Now consider a finite, weighted, directed graph as follows:

  • Vertices are seen as possible "states";
  • Weights are contained in [0,1][0,1][0,1], and for each vertex, the sum of the weights of its outcoming edges is 111;
  • So, in particular, the adjacency matrix is a stochastic matrix (and every stochastic matrix can be represented as such a graph).
    As above, interpret the weights as probabilties: an edge of weight 1/31/31/3 from xxx to yyy means a probability of 1/31/31/3 to "transition" from xxx to yyy, and so on.

Paolo Perrone (Jun 21 2021 at 09:17):

Suppose that we have two vertices xxx and yyy, which we view as "initial and final state", and that we want to see not quite what's the probability from xxx to yyy, but rather, what is the probability of getting from xxx to yyy in two steps.
For example, suppose there are two "intermediate" states aaa and bbb, and arrows as follows:

  • p(x→a)=1/2p(x\to a) = 1/2p(x→a)=1/2
  • p(x→b)=1/3p(x\to b) = 1/3p(x→b)=1/3
  • p(a→y)=1/2p(a\to y) = 1/2p(a→y)=1/2
  • p(b→y)=2/3p(b\to y) = 2/3p(b→y)=2/3
    (These are not all the arrows of our graph, but suppose that these are the only ones with arrows from xxx and to yyy.)
    We would like to say that the probability of going from xxx to yyy in two steps is

p(x→y)=p(x→a)⋅p(a→y)+p(x→b)⋅p(b→y)p(x\to y) = p(x\to a) \cdot p(a\to y) + p(x\to b) \cdot p(b\to y) p(x→y)=p(x→a)⋅p(a→y)+p(x→b)⋅p(b→y)

=1/2⋅1/2+1/3⋅2/3=1/4+1/2=3/4,= 1/2 \cdot 1/2 + 1/3 \cdot 2/3 = 1/4 + 1/2 = 3/4,=1/2⋅1/2+1/3⋅2/3=1/4+1/2=3/4,

since we can choose two possible routes: through aaa and through bbb.

Paolo Perrone (Jun 21 2021 at 09:22):

However: this is not how we usually work in enriched category theory! In particular, if we were to compose arrows in enriched category theory (for example, in the free enriched category theory generated by the graph, as given in Seven Sketches, section 2.5), we would have to use the maximum instead of the sum.
(The interpretation being, we only care about the "possibility" of going from xxx to yyy, or about the maximal probability.)
Categorically, one would have to take the colimit of the two numbers, and in a quantale, such as [0,1][0,1][0,1], the colimit commutes with the product, so that we can indeed do a form of matrix multiplication (see again Seven Sketches above, section 2.5.3).
