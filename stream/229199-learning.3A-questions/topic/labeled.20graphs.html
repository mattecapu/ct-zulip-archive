John Baez (Nov 09 2024 at 21:17):

Here's a question that came up in my research on polarities.

Given a set LLL, let's say an LLL-labeled graph is a graph

s,t:E→V s, t: E \to Vs,t:E→V

equipped a map sending edges to elements of LLL:

ℓ:E→L \ell: E \to Lℓ:E→L

We call ℓ(e)\ell(e)ℓ(e) the label of the edge eee.

There's an obvious category LGphL\mathsf{Gph}LGph of LLL-labeled graphs, where the morphisms are graph maps that preserve the labeling of edges. And there's a forgetful functor

U:LGph→Gph U: L\mathsf{Gph} \to \mathsf{Gph} U:LGph→Gph

that forgets the labels on edges.

Question. Is this is a fibration, an opfibration, or both?

John Baez (Nov 09 2024 at 21:57):

Here's why the forgetful functor from LLL-labeled graphs to graphs can't be an opfibration. I'll show some lift we'd need doesn't exist.

Let our label set LLL be {0,1}\{0,1\}{0,1}. Consider this LLL-labeled graph with 3 vertices and 2 edges:

a←0b→1c a \xleftarrow{0} b \xrightarrow{1} ca0​b1​c

Its underlying graph is

a←b→c a \leftarrow b \rightarrow ca←b→c

There's a unique map from this graph to the terminal graph, which has one vertex ∗\ast∗ and one edge. To lift this map to a map of labeled graphs, we'd need to choose a way to label that one edge. We can label that one edge with either 000 or 111, but either way there is no map from

a←0b→1c a \xleftarrow{0} b \xrightarrow{1} ca0​b1​c

to the resulting LLL-labeled graph, since 0≠10 \ne 10=1.
