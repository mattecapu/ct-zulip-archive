Ralph Sarkis (Jun 09 2025 at 13:39):

The notation A×BA \times BA×B only defines an object up to isomorphism, so saying that A×BA \times BA×B and B×AB \times AB×A are the same object is correct only because you consider them up to isomorphism. Their definition do not coincide exactly, although they are fully symmetric.

Consider the Cartesian product in Set\mathbf{Set}Set. If you give a specific construction for the product of AAA and BBB, say A×CartB={(a,b)∣a∈A,b∈B}A \times_{\text{Cart}} B = \{(a,b) \mid a \in A, b \in B\}A×Cart​B={(a,b)∣a∈A,b∈B}, then you clearly don't have that A×CartBA \times_{\text{Cart}} BA×Cart​B and B×CartAB \times_{\text{Cart}} AB×Cart​A are the same set (even though the definitions are fully symmetric). However, they both represent the categorical product A×BA\times BA×B, as you can show by coming up with the evident projections.

That will work when it works, but usually there is not just one isomorphism between A×BA \times BA×B and B×AB \times AB×A. For example if AAA and BBB are sets with 5 elements there are 15511210043330985984000000 isomorphisms between A×BA \times BA×B and B×AB \times AB×A.

So, a more common situation is that you pick an isomorphism between A×BA \times BA×B and B×AB \times AB×A. This then gives a way to replace any commutative diagram (or other expression) involving A×BA \times BA×B with one involving B×AB \times AB×A.

More generally: given two objects xxx and yyy in a category, if you choose an isomorphism f:xy∼f : x \stackrel{\sim} yf:xy∼​ you can replace xxx with yyy in anything you've been doing with xxx.

  1. X×YX \times YX×Y represents any object OOO that can be equipped with two morphisms, π1:O→X\pi_1:O \to Xπ1​:O→X and π2:O→Y\pi_2:O \to Yπ2​:O→Y, that satisfy the universal property.
