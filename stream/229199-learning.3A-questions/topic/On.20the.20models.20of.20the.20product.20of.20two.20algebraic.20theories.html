Arturo De Faveri (Oct 13 2024 at 15:20):

Yes, A×B \mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B}A×B is the product in the category of theories. A theory A\mathbf{A}A has a denumerable set of objects {1,x,x2,…}\{1, x, x^2, \ldots\}{1,x,x2,…} – which for brevity I will denote by {0,1,2,…}\{0, 1, 2, \ldots\}{0,1,2,…} since it's not at all important the 'name' of the generating object xxx – and some unspecified morphisms n→1n \to 1n→1 (of course there are the nnn projections but possibly more) which we may call the nnn-ary terms of the theory.

If B\mathbf{B}B is another theory then the morphisms n→1n \to 1n→1 in A×B\mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B}A×B are pairs of nnn-ary terms (s,t)(s,t)(s,t) of A\mathbf{A}A and B\mathbf{B}B respectively. The projection pA:A×B→Ap_\mathbf{A}: \mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{B} \to \mathbf{A}pA​:A×B→A acts on the objects stupidly (as every morphism of theories does, sending kkk to kkk for every kkk) and maps the morphism (s,t)(s,t)(s,t) to sss.

By the way, do not confuse the product with the "tensor product" of two theories. A model of the tensor A⊗B\mathbf{A} \otimes \mathbf{B}A⊗B is indeed a set in which somehow the A\mathbf{A}A-structure and the B\mathbf{B}B-structure coexist.

Chaitanya Leena Subramaniam (Oct 16 2024 at 01:39):

Arturo De Faveri said:

  1. do yo have any idea about the "bit of computation" that Lawvere had in mind?

I'm not sure what computation Lawvere had in mind, but here's one way of looking at it:

A single-sorted algebraic theory is given by an identity-on-objects and finite-product-preserving ("fpp") functor Finop→A\mathrm{Fin}^{op}\to AFinop→A, where Fin\mathrm{Fin}Fin is (a skeleton of) the category of finite sets. A morphism of single-sorted algebraic theories is a commutative triangle.
Then the product (in the category of single-sorted algebraic theories) of two single-sorted algebraic theories (Finop→A)(\mathrm{Fin}^{op}\to A)(Finop→A) and (Finop→B)(\mathrm{Fin}^{op}\to B)(Finop→B) is the algebraic theory Finop→A⊠B\mathrm{Fin}^{op}\to A\boxtimes BFinop→A⊠B obtained by the (identity-on-obects, fully faithful) factorisation of the fpp functor Finop→(A×B)\mathrm{Fin}^{op}\to (A\times B)Finop→(A×B) (where A×BA\times BA×B is the ordinary product of categories).

Since (as you noted) the left Kan extension of a fpp functor to Set\mathrm{Set}Set is also fpp, we get a coreflective subcategory
[A⊠B,Set]fpp↪[A×B,Set]fpp[A\boxtimes B,\mathrm{Set}]_{\mathrm{fpp}} \hookrightarrow [A\times B,\mathrm{Set}]_{\mathrm{fpp}}[A⊠B,Set]fpp​↪[A×B,Set]fpp​.

The unit of this adjunction is the natural isomorphism you're looking for :)

Remark: The 2-category of finite-product categories and fpp functors has finite biproducts (in a 2-categorical sense)—so we have a canonical equivalence
[A×B,Set]fpp≃[A,Set]fpp×[B,Set]fpp[A\times B,\mathrm{Set}]_{\mathrm{fpp}} \simeq [A,\mathrm{Set}]_{\mathrm{fpp}}\times[B,\mathrm{Set}]_{\mathrm{fpp}}[A×B,Set]fpp​≃[A,Set]fpp​×[B,Set]fpp​.

Side note regarding your calculation: Let MAM_AMA​ and MBM_BMB​ be the left Kan extensions of MMM along the projections A⊠B→AA\boxtimes B\to AA⊠B→A and A⊠B→BA\boxtimes B\to BA⊠B→B respectively, and let MA×BM_{A\times B}MA×B​ be the left Kan extension along the inclusion A⊠B↪A×BA\boxtimes B\hookrightarrow A\times BA⊠B↪A×B. Then we have MA(1)=MA×B(1,0)M_A(1) = M_{A\times B}(1,0)MA​(1)=MA×B​(1,0) and MB(1)=MA×B(0,1)M_B(1)=M_{A\times B}(0,1)MB​(1)=MA×B​(0,1).
Therefore MA(1)×MB(1)=MA×B(1,0)×MA×B(0,1)=MA×B(1,1)=M(1)M_A(1)\times M_B(1) = M_{A\times B}(1,0)\times M_{A\times B}(0,1)=M_{A\times B}(1,1) = M(1)MA​(1)×MB​(1)=MA×B​(1,0)×MA×B​(0,1)=MA×B​(1,1)=M(1).

By the way, for anyone seeking references for folklore facts, the case n=2n = 2n=2, using plain old bicategory theory, can be found as Theorem 5.2 here:

  • D. Schäppi, Ind-abelian categories and quasi-coherent sheaves. Math. Proc. Camb. Philos. Soc., 157(3):391–423, 2014. Available as arXiv:1211.3678.

He shows that given symmetric pseudomonoids M and M′ in a symmetric monoidal bicategory (C, ⊗, I), there is a natural way to make M ⊗ M′ into a symmetric pseudomonoid, which makes it into the bicategorical coproduct in the bicategory of symmetric pseudomonoids in (C, ⊗, I).

He doesn't just hand-wave the argument: he puts the proof in Appendix A, and he uses some string diagrams. But I'll admit I haven't carefully checked the proof because the result is so 'obvious'!
