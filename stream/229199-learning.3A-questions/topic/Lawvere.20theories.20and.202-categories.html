The terminal Lawvere theory has exactly one morphism f:m→n for each m,n. Since there is a unique morphism between each pair of objects, each object is in fact isomorphic. Thus, this theory is a terminal category. Algebras of the terminal Lawvere theory are terminal sets, singletons.

Mike Shulman (Oct 06 2023 at 20:21):

Observe that as a category, the terminal Lawvere theory is equivalent to the terminal category: any two objects are uniquely isomorphic. Similarly, the Lawvere theory for singleton-or-empty sets (i.e. [[subsingleton]] sets) is equivalent as a category to the arrow category (0→1)(0\to 1)(0→1): all the positive powers XnX^nXn are uniquely isomorphic, and there is a map Xn→X0=1X^n \to X^0 = 1Xn→X0=1 but not in the other direction.

Therefore:

  • The Lawvere theory of sets is equivalent to the opposite of the category of finite sets.
  • The Lawvere theory of subsingleton sets is equivalent to the opposite of the category of (finite) subsingleton sets.
  • The Lawvere theory of singleton sets is equivalent to the opposite of the category of (finite) singleton sets.

That looks like it should be the negative-thinking version of something that extends into higher categories too.

In general, for a ri(n)g RRR, the Lawvere theory Mat(R){\bf Mat}(R)Mat(R) is the theory for modules on RRR.
So, for instance, Mat(Z){\bf Mat}(Z)Mat(Z) is the theory for abelian groups.

It is interesting to confront the various kinds of theories that one can use in functorial semantics.
In particular (addressing the one sorted case) one can consider (among others)
Lawvere theories, props, operads (= one-object symmetric multicategories) or cartesian operads.
Cartesian operads and Lawvere theories are essentially equivalent (they capture the same categories of algebras).

Other pairs are related by adjunction. In particular, one has the free Prop O′O'O′ on an operad OOO, so that O′O'O′ and OOO have the same models in Set\bf SetSet.
For instance, if 111 is the terminal operad, 1′1'1′ is the Prop of finite sets and functions, so both have commutative monoids as models.
If DDD is the one-arrow operad, D′D'D′ is the Prop of finite sets and bijections, so both have sets as models.
Both these instance can be generalized to any monoid MMM in place of 111:
if M1M_1M1​ is the operad whose arrows are families of elements in the monoid MMM, then the free Prop M1′M_1'M1′​ is (the domain of ) the family fibration on the monoid. The models of M1M_1M1​ (and of M1′M_1'M1′​) are commutative monoids with an action of MMM on them;
on the other hand, if M2M_2M2​ is the operad with just unary arrows (the elements of MMM; here of course it is involved the adjunction between monoids and operads), then the arrows n→mn\to mn→m of free Prop M2′M_2'M2′​
are bijections n→mn\to mn→m with nnn elements of MMM. The models of M2M_2M2​ (and of M2′M_2'M2′​) are MMM-sets.
(Note that the prop O′O'O′ is the vertical part of the double category in my approach to operads).

One also has the free cartesian operad O′′O''O′′ on an operad OOO, so that O′′O''O′′ and OOO have the same models in Set\bf SetSet.
For instance, if we start from the operad M1M_1M1​ described above, M1′′M_1''M1′′​ has, as arrows, the finite families in the rig freely generated by the monoid MMM, namely the rig RMR_MRM​ of formal linear combinations of elements of MMM with coefficient in the natural numbers.
The fact that M1′′M_1''M1′′​ has again the same models as M1M_1M1​, is the fact modules LLL on RMR_MRM​ correspond to actions of MMM on the underlying monoid of LLL.
In particular, when M=1M = 1M=1, we get the cartesian operad for commutative monoids: its arrows are families of natural numbers
(which become matrixes of natural numbers in the corresponding Lawvere theory, where there is not just a single object, but also all its products).
