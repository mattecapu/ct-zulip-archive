Topic: Parameter Theorems

Ruby Khondaker (she/her) (Feb 04 2026 at 21:46):

After finally reading "Categories for the Working Mathematician", I realised that the 'parameter theorems' mentioned in the text fall out as a general consequence of [[representability determines functoriality]], whose statement I will reproduce here:

Suppose P:Cop×D→SetP : \mathcal{C}^\text{op} \times \mathcal{D} \to \mathbf{Set}P:Cop×D→Set is a profunctor that is representable in the Cop\mathcal{C}^\text{op}Cop argument - meaning, for each d∈Dd \in \mathcal{D}d∈D, the functor P(−,d) ⁣:Cop→SetP(-, d) \colon \mathcal{C}^\text{op} \to \mathbf{Set}P(−,d):Cop→Set is representable. Then, by choosing representations for each P(−,d)P(-, d)P(−,d), we obtain a functor F ⁣:D→CF \colon \mathcal{D} \to \mathcal{C}F:D→C with P(c,d)≅C(c,Fd)P(c, d) \cong \mathcal{C}(c, Fd)P(c,d)≅C(c,Fd).

I.e. a bifunctor which is "pointwise representable" in a particular argument determines a functor. Here I am using "representable in an argument" in the same sense as "linear in an argument" for multilinear maps - if all other arguments are fixed, the resulting functor is representable.

Let me illustrate the ways in which various "parameter theorems" fall out as consequences of this general one:

I've also found this notion of being "representable in an argument" helpful for understanding adjunctions and their variants:

  • An adjunction between two categories CCC and DDD consists of a bifunctor H ⁣:Cop×D→SetH \colon C^\text{op} \times D \to \mathbf{Set}H:Cop×D→Set that is representable in each argument, so that Hom(Fc,d)≅H(c,d)≅Hom(c,Gd)\text{Hom}(Fc, d) \cong H(c, d) \cong \text{Hom}(c, Gd)Hom(Fc,d)≅H(c,d)≅Hom(c,Gd).
  • A pair of "mutual right adjoints" is a bifunctor H ⁣:Cop×Dop→SetH \colon C^\text{op} \times D^\text{op} \to \mathbf{Set}H:Cop×Dop→Set that is representable in each argument, so that Hom(c,Fd)≅H(c,d)≅Hom(d,Gc)\text{Hom}(c, Fd) \cong H(c, d) \cong \text{Hom}(d, Gc)Hom(c,Fd)≅H(c,d)≅Hom(d,Gc). Similarly, a pair of "mutual left adjoints" is a bifunctor H ⁣:C×D→SetH \colon C \times D \to \mathbf{Set}H:C×D→Set that is representable in each argument.
  • A two-variable adjunction is a trifunctor H ⁣:Cop×Dop×E→SetH \colon C^\text{op} \times D^\text{op} \times E \to \mathbf{Set}H:Cop×Dop×E→Set that is representable in each argument, providing isomorphisms E(c⊗d,e)≅D(d,homl(c,e))≅C(c,homr(d,e))≅H(c,d,e)E(c \otimes d, e) \cong D(d, \text{hom}_l(c, e)) \cong C(c, \text{hom}_r(d, e)) \cong H(c, d, e)E(c⊗d,e)≅D(d,homl​(c,e))≅C(c,homr​(d,e))≅H(c,d,e).

In summary, I've found "multirepresentability" to be a helpful way to unify the various parameter theorems I've come across, as well as various notions of adjunctions. Is there a good reference that goes through this in more detail?

Nathanael Arkor (Feb 06 2026 at 11:01):

This is a good observation – unfortunately, I'm not aware of any expository reference for these ideas! There are several further related ideas that tie together very satisfactorily, but which I've never seen mentioned in the literature (in fact, I believe some of these connections have gone overlooked even by experts). The first observation is that the theorem you mention is a corollary of a more general result.

Theorem. Let P:∣B∣×A→SetP : |\mathcal B| \times \mathcal A \to \text{Set}P:∣B∣×A→Set and Q:Bop×∣A∣→SetQ : \mathcal B^{\text{op}} \times |\mathcal A| \to \text{Set}Q:Bop×∣A∣→Set be functors, equipped with a family of isomorphisms of sets P(b,a)≅Q(b,a)P(b, a) \cong Q(b, a)P(b,a)≅Q(b,a). Then PPP and QQQ are uniquely equipped with isomorphic functor structures for which P≅QP \cong QP≅Q is a natural isomorphism if and only if a single octagon condition holds.

I won't spell out the octagon condition for now, but I will explain how to derive it momentarily. This theorem says, in some sense, that "functoriality is for free". For instance, taking L:∣A∣→∣B∣L : |\mathcal A| \to |\mathcal B|L:∣A∣→∣B∣ and R:∣B∣→∣A∣R : |\mathcal B| \to |\mathcal A|R:∣B∣→∣A∣ to be functions and P:=A(1,R)P := \mathcal A(1, R)P:=A(1,R) and Q:=B(L,1)Q := \mathcal B(L, 1)Q:=B(L,1), this recovers Street's minimal presentation of an adjunction (as referenced on that nLab page). (In this case, the octagon condition reduces to a square.)

It may appear that the theorem above is very general, but it's a consequence of an elementary result: in fact, the very first proposition of Mac Lane's book!

Given functors F:∣X∣×Y→ZF : |\mathcal X| \times \mathcal Y \to \mathcal ZF:∣X∣×Y→Z and G:X×∣Y∣→ZG : \mathcal X \times |\mathcal Y| \to \mathcal ZG:X×∣Y∣→Z that agree on objects, they extend to a functor X×Y→Z\mathcal X \times \mathcal Y \to \mathcal ZX×Y→Z if and only if a single square condition holds.

How can we prove the theorem from this proposition? Well, the isomorphism P(b,a)≅Q(b,a)P(b, a) \cong Q(b, a)P(b,a)≅Q(b,a) equips PPP with the structure of a functor Bop×∣A∣→Set\mathcal B^{\text{op}} \times |\mathcal A| \to \text{Set}Bop×∣A∣→Set, and in this case two sides of the square expand into a composable triple (apply the isomorphism, apply functoriality of QQQ, apply the isomorphism), recovering the octagon condition.

So these parameter theorems that Mac Lane proves (some of which take a fair amount of manipulation to establish) are actually all consequences of the very first result he proves, but this is never (to my knowledge) observed.

Ruby Khondaker (she/her) (Feb 09 2026 at 21:59):

Incidentally, I believe I managed to find a way to organise the proof of "parametrised limit preservation":

  • The functors F(a,−) ⁣:B→CF(a, -) \colon B \to CF(a,−):B→C all preserving limits of JJJ is equivalent to the product functor B→Cob(A)B \to C^{\text{ob}(A) }B→Cob(A) preserving limits of JJJ, since limits in product categories are computed componentwise.
  • Then, the forgetful functor [A,C]→Cob(A)[A, C] \to C^{\text{ob}(A)}[A,C]→Cob(A) factorises this product functor, and reflects all limits.
  • Hence, by prop 3.5 on [[preserved limit]], the curried functor FT ⁣:B→[A,C]F^T \colon B \to [A, C]FT:B→[A,C] preserves limits of JJJ.

Nathanael Arkor said:

Given functors F:∣X∣×Y→ZF : |\mathcal X| \times \mathcal Y \to \mathcal ZF:∣X∣×Y→Z and G:X×∣Y∣→ZG : \mathcal X \times |\mathcal Y| \to \mathcal ZG:X×∣Y∣→Z that agree on objects, they extend to a functor X×Y→Z\mathcal X \times \mathcal Y \to \mathcal ZX×Y→Z if and only if a single square condition holds.

I'll point out that this is the essential lemma for proving that that currying of functors is an isomorphism. I.e., the data of these two functors F:∣X∣×Y→ZF : |\mathcal X| \times \mathcal Y \to \mathcal ZF:∣X∣×Y→Z and G:X×∣Y∣→ZG : \mathcal X \times |\mathcal Y| \to \mathcal ZG:X×∣Y∣→Z that agree on objects is exactly the same data as a functor H:X→UnNat(Y,Z)H : X \to \textrm{UnNat}(Y,Z)H:X→UnNat(Y,Z) where UnNat(Y,Z)\textrm{UnNat}(Y,Z)UnNat(Y,Z) is a category of functors and "unnatural" transformations, i.e., families of morphisms with no naturality square. Then the single square above is exactly saying that HHH lands in the subcategory of natural transformations.

image.png

The idea is that you can already determine the nerve of [C,D][C, D][C,D] purely by exploiting the universal property, and this in particular gets you the definitions of what natural transformations and their (vertical) composites should be.
