Todd Trimble (Dec 26 2021 at 20:24):

(Saw this while going through a huge pile of unread posts.)

In the case of Set\mathsf{Set}Set, taking BBB to have cardinality greater than 1, in every case the category of algebras is equivalent to Setop\mathsf{Set}^{op}Setop, i.e., [−,B]:Setop→Set[-, B]: \mathsf{Set}^{op} \to \mathsf{Set}[−,B]:Setop→Set is monadic.

It's already been pointed out that this has a left adjoint, namely [−,B]op:Set→Setop[-, B]^{op}: \mathsf{Set} \to \mathsf{Set}^{op}[−,B]op:Set→Setop. By the crude monadicity theorem, it suffices to check that [−,B][-, B][−,B] reflects isomorphisms and preserves reflexive coequalizers.

We know [−,2]:Setop→Set[-, 2]: \mathsf{Set}^{op} \to \mathsf{Set}[−,2]:Setop→Set preserves reflexive coequalizers -- that's a special case of how Paré proved his theorem that for a topos EEE, that [−,Ω]:Eop→E[-, \Omega]: E^{op} \to E[−,Ω]:Eop→E preserves reflexive coequalizers. Also, for any set PPP, we know that P×−:Set→SetP \times -: \mathsf{Set} \to \mathsf{Set}P×−:Set→Set preserves connected limits and in particular equalizers. Therefore, [−,2P]:Setop→Set[-, 2^P]: \mathsf{Set}^{op} \to \mathsf{Set}[−,2P]:Setop→Set, being isomorphic to a composite

Setop→(P×−)opSetop→[−,2]Set\mathsf{Set}^{op} \stackrel{(P \times -)^{op}}{\to} \mathsf{Set}^{op} \stackrel{[-, 2]}{\to} \mathsf{Set}Setop→(P×−)opSetop→[−,2]Set

of functors that preserve reflexive coequalizers, also preserves reflexive coequalizers. Then [−,B][-, B][−,B], being a retract of a functor [−,2B][-, 2^B][−,2B] that preserves reflexive coequalizers, must also preserve reflexive coequalizers.

Isomorphisms in Set\mathsf{Set}Set are epi-monos, so to see that [−,B][-, B][−,B] reflects isomorphisms, it suffices that it reflects epis and monos. For that, it suffices that [−,B][-, B][−,B] is faithful, or in other words that BBB is a cogenerator. But this in turn follows from the fact that 222 is a cogenerator and there is a monomorphism 2→B2 \to B2→B.

Todd Trimble (Dec 27 2021 at 11:29):

You're welcome! Of course, one would also like a more intuitive understanding as well. That shouldn't be too hard to cook up.

Seemingly related is the fact that [,V]:Vectop→Vect[, V]: \mathsf{Vect}^{op} \to \mathsf{Vect}[,V]:Vectop→Vect is also monadic for any non-zero vector space VVV. This fact came up around the time Tom Leinster was writing his series of articles about ultrafilters and measures. Again, what is crucial is the fact that such VVV is an injective cogenerator; see here.

What this also reminds me of is the fact that there are many Lawvere theories whose models are Boolean algebras. The usual Lawvere theory is opposite to the category of finitely generated free Boolean algebras, which by a baby version of Stone duality may be identified with the category of finite sets of cardinality 2n2^n2n. But now here comes a nice trick: if TTT is any category with finite products, and Tˉ\bar{T}Tˉ is its Cauchy completion, then Tˉ\bar{T}Tˉ also has finite products, and moreover the category of product-preserving functors T→SetT \to \mathsf{Set}T→Set is equivalent to the category of product-preserving functors Tˉ→Set\bar{T} \to \mathsf{Set}Tˉ→Set. For example, the Cauchy completion of finite sets of cardinality 2n2^n2n is the category Fin+\mathrm{Fin}_+Fin+​ of nonempty finite sets. But then, the same is true if we start instead with finite sets of cardinality of 3n3^n3n! It follows that if we take our Lawvere theory to be Fin3∙\mathrm{Fin}_{3^\bullet}Fin3∙​, then we get the same category of algebras, i.e., Boolean algebras!

That may seem a little weird. It shows that there are many "underlying set" functors U:Bool→SetU: \mathrm{Bool} \to \mathsf{Set}U:Bool→Set. But it makes a little more sense if you consider that the number of chains x≤yx \leq yx≤y in a Boolean algebra with 2n2^n2n elements is 3n3^n3n. Circling back around then to the top of this post, I propose that if we think of Setop\mathsf{Set}^{op}Setop as the category of complete atomic Boolean algebras, then the functor [−,3]:Setop→Set[-, 3]: \mathsf{Set}^{op} \to \mathsf{Set}[−,3]:Setop→Set sends a CABA = power set P(X)P(X)P(X) to the set of 2-fold chains a≤ba \leq ba≤b in P(X)P(X)P(X). (Well, that's one way of thinking about it; there are others.)
