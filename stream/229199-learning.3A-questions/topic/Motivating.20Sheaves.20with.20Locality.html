Chris Grossack (they/them) (Nov 04 2024 at 15:17):

I think you're right to observe that this works for presheaves, and thus that this isn't one of the problems that sheaf theory solves.

Indeed, in taking a section of a presheaf (over UUU, say) and passing to its germ at xxx, we've made the data more local. We took something defined on UUU and restricted it to get something defined only at a point xxx. In general, we can pass from "more global" to "more local" easily, by restriction (as we basically did here).

Sheaves let you solve the opposite problem. If you have local data in multiple places, when can you "glue" that local data together in order to get something globally defined. There's lots of examples, but here's one relating explicitly to stalks:

Theorem: If F\mathcal{F}F is a sheaf and φ\varphiφ is a geometric formula about F\mathcal{F}F (and its elements) then φ\varphiφ is true at the stalk xxx if and only if φ\varphiφ is true in some neighborhood of xxx!

So we see sheaf-ness lets geometric truth "spread out". By checking the property at a single point, we learn for free that the property holds in some open neighborhood! So we've moved from "more local" (truth at a point) to "more global" (truth in a neighborhood). This is great, because moving from local to global is generally quite hard!

Mike Shulman (Nov 04 2024 at 15:57):

Another manifestation of the same idea is that a sheaf is determined by its stalks in a certain sense, while a presheaf is not. Specifically, for a topological space BBB, the category of sheaves on BBB is equivalent to the full subcategory of Top/B\mathrm{Top}/BTop/B spanned by the local homeomorphisms, and in the local homeomorphism E→BE\to BE→B corresponding to a sheaf XXX, the fiber over b∈Bb\in Bb∈B is precisely the stalk at bbb. So if you remember all the stalks at all points, together with an induced topology on them, you can recover the original sheaf (as the sheaf of sections of this local homemorphism).

This is not true for an arbitrary presheaf. In fact, it is true for a presheaf precisely when that presheaf is a sheaf! See for instance this post by @Tom Leinster .

  • p:Λ(F)→Bp: \Lambda(F) \to Bp:Λ(F)→B is an etale space, i.e. a local homeomorphism.

  • Conversely, any bundle p:E→Bp: E \to Bp:E→B gives a presheaf on BBB called its presheaf of sections, Γ(E)\Gamma(E)Γ(E)... but this is actually a sheaf!

There is a straightforward definition of the stalks of a sheaf on an arbitrary site, i.e. an object of an arbitrary topos. But first you have to define what a "point" is of an arbitrary topos. The definition is that a "point" of a topos X\mathcal{X}X is a geometric morphism Set→X\mathrm{Set} \to \mathcal{X}Set→X. Since Set=Sh(∗)\mathrm{Set} = \mathrm{Sh}(*)Set=Sh(∗) is the topos of sheaves on a 1-point space, and the category of (sober) spaces embeds fully-faithfully in the category of topoi, this is a faithful generalization (up to isomorphism) of the notion of "point" of a topological space. Now the definition of a stalk is immediate: given a point x:Set→Xx:\mathrm{Set} \to \mathcal{X}x:Set→X, the stalk of F∈XF\in\mathcal{X}F∈X at xxx is x∗(F)x^*(F)x∗(F), where x∗:X→Setx^* : \mathcal{X} \to \mathrm{Set}x∗:X→Set is the inverse image part of the geometric morphism xxx.

However, you're also right that a sheaf on an arbitrary site/topos is not in general determined by its stalks, because a general topos may not have very many points. In fact, there are nontrivial toposes that have no points at all. So in the context of general toposes (and even general locales), the "local to global" nature of a sheaf is better understood as passing from infomation over smaller "open parts" to their "union", rather than from information over "points" to the set they constitute.
