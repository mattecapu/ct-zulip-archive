Ruby Khondaker (she/her) (Aug 02 2025 at 10:49):

The relevant adjunction here is Hom((Z,Z),(A,B))≅Hom(Z,A×B)\text{Hom}((Z, Z), (A, B)) \cong \text{Hom}(Z, A \times B)Hom((Z,Z),(A,B))≅Hom(Z,A×B)

I.e. you can “package” two arrows Z -> A, Z -> B into a single arrow Z -> A x B, reversibly!

So this ends up being a consequence of the yoneda lemma! The natural transformation Hom(−,A×B)→Hom(Δ(−),(A,B))\text{Hom}(-, A \times B) \to \text{Hom}(\Delta(-), (A, B))Hom(−,A×B)→Hom(Δ(−),(A,B)) is determined by where it sends the identity. So you look at Hom(A×B,A×B)→Hom((A×B,A×B),(A,B))\text{Hom}(A \times B, A \times B) \to \text{Hom}((A \times B, A \times B), (A, B))Hom(A×B,A×B)→Hom((A×B,A×B),(A,B)) - following the identity gives you the projections!

Then the "universal construction" follows from this natural transformation being an isomorphism, i.e. producing a bijection between Hom(Z,A×B)\text{Hom}(Z, A \times B)Hom(Z,A×B) and Hom((Z,Z),(A,B))\text{Hom}((Z, Z), (A, B))Hom((Z,Z),(A,B)). Specifically, the forward direction is post-composing with the projections πA,πB\pi_A, \pi_BπA​,πB​, and the backward direction is the "universal arrow making the diagram commute" part.

Ruby Khondaker (she/her) (Aug 03 2025 at 23:06):

It’s a little difficult for me to parse what you’re saying, but my gut instinct is that you’re overthinking this.

An element of Hom(Z,A×B)\text{Hom}(Z, A \times B)Hom(Z,A×B) is just a morphism h:Z→A×Bh : Z \to A \times Bh:Z→A×B. And an element of Hom((Z,Z),(A,B))\text{Hom}((Z, Z), (A, B))Hom((Z,Z),(A,B)) is just a pair of morphisms f:Z→A,g:Z→Bf : Z \to A, g : Z \to Bf:Z→A,g:Z→B.

Given h:Z→A×Bh : Z \to A \times Bh:Z→A×B, we get the pair (πA∘h,πB∘h)∈Hom((Z,Z),(A,B))(\pi_A \circ h, \pi_B \circ h) \in \text{Hom}((Z, Z), (A, B))(πA​∘h,πB​∘h)∈Hom((Z,Z),(A,B)). This is just postcomposition.

Moreover, the universal property tells us that given f:Z→A,g:Z→Bf : Z \to A, g : Z \to Bf:Z→A,g:Z→B, there is a unique h:Z→A×Bh : Z \to A \times Bh:Z→A×B such that f=πA∘h,g=πB∘hf = \pi_A \circ h, g = \pi_B \circ hf=πA​∘h,g=πB​∘h. This is precisely the statement that the above map is bijective, i.e. an isomorphism in Set!
