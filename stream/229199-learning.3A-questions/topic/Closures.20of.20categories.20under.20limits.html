Topic: Closures of categories under limits

John Onstead (Jul 01 2025 at 11:25):

Recently, I entered into a correspondence with a category theorist who has been giving me problems to work on to help improve my knowledge. Here's the most recent one I was given:
Problem: For a small category CCC, let Lim(C)\mathrm{Lim}(C)Lim(C) be the full subcategory of Psh(C)\mathrm{Psh}(C)Psh(C) on all small limits of representables- therefore, the limit closure of the representables. Show that Lim(C)\mathrm{Lim}(C)Lim(C) is not just complete (as it obviously is by definition) but also cocomplete.
He gave the following example: "Let CCC already have all limits, therefore Lim(C)≅C\mathrm{Lim}(C) \cong CLim(C)≅C. But any small complete category is a preorder, and any complete preorder is also cocomplete. Hence the theorem holds in this trivial case."

John Onstead (Jul 01 2025 at 11:26):

The proof seems quite straightforward. If a category is locally presentable, it's cocomplete, hence we can prove this by showing Lim(C)\mathrm{Lim}(C)Lim(C) is locally presentable. Using Theorem 2.48 of Adamek and Rosicky (The Reflection Theorem), any subcategory of a locally presentable category is itself locally presentable when closed under limits and kkk-filtered colimits for some kkk. Obviously, Psh(C)\mathrm{Psh}(C)Psh(C) is locally presentable, and Lim(C)\mathrm{Lim}(C)Lim(C) is by definition closed under limits, so already two conditions are met. We can then assume Vopenka's principle to make the final condition redundant and the proof is complete. In fact, the weak Vopenka's principle says that any limit-closed subcategory of a locally presentable category is reflective, so this tautologically proves the above result.

John Onstead (Jul 01 2025 at 11:27):

But my "tutor" wasn't satisfied and claimed that cocompleteness of Lim(C)\mathrm{Lim}(C)Lim(C) can be proven without Vopenka's principle. I was eventually able to switch tactics and apply SAFT to the inclusion of Lim(C)\mathrm{Lim}(C)Lim(C) into presheaves, and it seemed to satisfy him. But while this does prove cocompleteness by showing Lim(C)\mathrm{Lim}(C)Lim(C) is a reflective subcategory of presheaves, it doesn't get us to showing Lim(C)\mathrm{Lim}(C)Lim(C) is locally presentable, and I'm still curious about if that can be shown without Vopenka. So I wanted to know: is this true, and if so, how can this be shown? Thanks!

John Onstead (Jul 03 2025 at 11:46):

Here's a fun simple question. Both the Kaurobi completion and free coproduct completion have two definitions- the explicit one and the subcategory of presheaves one. In the explicit definition, it gives an actual way to construct the objects of these explicitly from objects in CCC- IE, the objects of the free coproduct completion are indexed sets of objects of CCC ("formal colimits" of objects of CCC), while those of the karoubi completion are objects of CCC equipped with idempotents. This explicit perspective allows for the easy proof of things that might be harder with just the "subcategory of presheaves" one.

Mike Shulman (Jul 03 2025 at 15:03):

John's Lim(C) is not the free completion, but the closure of the representables under limits in the ordinary Yoneda embedding. In particular, the functor C→Lim(C)C\to \mathrm{Lim}(C)C→Lim(C) preserves all existing limits in CCC. I don't know offhand a universal property of this.

John Onstead (Jul 03 2025 at 21:06):

Let's say we had some presheaf FFF that is a small limit of representables limiRi\mathrm{lim}_iR_ilimi​Ri​, and we apply F(coljXj)F(\mathrm{col}_jX_j)F(colj​Xj​) to some colimit in CCC. First, we compute limits pointwise in Set\mathrm{Set}Set, so we get limiRi(coljXj)\mathrm{lim}_iR_i(\mathrm{col}_jX_j)limi​Ri​(colj​Xj​). Representables send colimits to limits, so we get limilimjRi(Xj)\mathrm{lim}_i\mathrm{lim}_jR_i(X_j)limi​limj​Ri​(Xj​). Limits commute with limits, so we end up with limjlimiRi(Xj)\mathrm{lim}_j\mathrm{lim}_iR_i(X_j)limj​limi​Ri​(Xj​). But recalling the definition of FFF this is just limjF(Xj)\mathrm{lim}_jF(X_j)limj​F(Xj​), and therefore we find FFF sends colimits to limits! So that means not only does Lim(C)\mathrm{Lim}(C)Lim(C) embed into the category of limit preserving presheaves, but because the embedding of CCC into the latter preserves colimits and limits, so too does the embedding of CCC into Lim(C)\mathrm{Lim}(C)Lim(C)!

John Onstead (Jul 04 2025 at 00:26):

I did figure out another characterization of Lim(C)\mathrm{Lim}(C)Lim(C)- it's equivalent to the category whose objects are categories of cones in CCC. Given an index category JJJ, and a diagram F:J→CF: J \to CF:J→C, the category Cone(F)\mathrm{Cone}(F)Cone(F) is equivalent to the comma category (Δ/F)(\Delta / F)(Δ/F) in [J,C][J, C][J,C]. The category of all such cone categories and the fibered functors between them is equivalent to Lim(C)\mathrm{Lim}(C)Lim(C) via the Grothendieck construction (that sends a presheaf to the category of elements of that presheaf). This just follows from the universal property of limits that morphisms into them correspond to cones over their diagrams.
