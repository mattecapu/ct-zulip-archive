Topic: a ring as a category where the elements are objects

David Egolf (Aug 15 2023 at 16:17):

Working in a commutative ring RRR, we can introduce a relation as follows: for any two elements of the ring a,b∈Ra,b \in Ra,b∈R, we set a∼ba \sim ba∼b exactly when a=uba=uba=ub, where uuu is an element of the ring that has a multiplicative inverse (it is a "unit"). I was working an exercise where the aim was to show that ∼\sim∼ is an equivalence relationship.

When thinking about this, I found it interesting to introduce a category R′R'R′ where:

  • the objects are the elements of RRR
  • there is a morphism r:a→br: a \to b r:a→b exactly when ra=bra=bra=b
  • composition is by multiplication
  • the identity morphisms correspond to multiplication by 1

We have that a∼ba \sim ba∼b exactly when aaa and bbb are isomorphic in R′R'R′. I believe that isomorphism classes in any category correspond to an equivalence relationship, and so ∼\sim∼ is an equivalence relationship on RRR.

This seems like an interesting way to think about a ring. I was hoping that we could make R′R'R′ into a monoidal category, by letting ⊗\otimes⊗ be the +++ operation available in RRR. However, it seems like +:R′×R′→R′+:R' \times R' \to R'+:R′×R′→R′ doesn't define a functor. I then tried creating a new category R′′R''R′′ where we have a morphism r:a→br: a \to br:a→b exactly when r+a=br + a = br+a=b, in the hopes that I could make it into a monoidal category by letting ⊗\otimes⊗ be the multiplication operation ⋅\cdot⋅ from RRR. However, it seems like ⋅:R′′×R′′→R′′\cdot :R'' \times R'' \to R''⋅:R′′×R′′→R′′ isn't a functor.

Is there some way to define a category from a commutative ring RRR such that:

  • the objects of the category are the elements of the ring
  • both operations of the ring, +++ and ⋅\cdot⋅ are modelled in this category (e.g. as morphisms, or functors)
  • the way in which ⋅\cdot⋅ distributes over +++ (e.g. a⋅(b+c)=a⋅b+a⋅ca \cdot (b+c) = a\cdot b + a \cdot ca⋅(b+c)=a⋅b+a⋅c) is also modelled in this category?

Note that this is the slice category of the underlying monoid of the ring over its unique object
(or, equivalently, the category of elements of the functor represented by its unique object).

There is an interesting way to see a ring as a cartesian operad, rather than a monoidal category.

First, for a monoid MMM we can consider the operad (= one-object symmetric multicategory) M′M'M′
whose arrows are finite families of elements of MMM, with the obvious composition.
The algebras for this operad (the multifunctors M′→SetM' \to SetM′→Set, to the symmetric multicategory of sets and several variables functions)
are commutative monoids with an action of MMM on them.

Second, if RRR is a ring then the operad R′R'R′ on the underlying monoid has a cartesian structure.

Recall that SetSetSet (like any multicategory obtained from a cartesian monoidal category) has a cartesian structure
where contraction and weakening maps are given respectively by precomposing an arrow X×X→YX\times X \to YX×X→Y with the diagonal
and an arrow X→YX\to YX→Y with a projection.

In the case of the operad R′R'R′, contraction and weakening maps are instead given respectively by the sum of the family
and by inserting zero's in a family.
More than that, to give a rig structure on a monoid MMM amounts to giving a cartesian structure on the operad M′M'M′.
(Recall that rigs differ from rings in that the existence of negatives is not assumed).

Now, the algebras for this cartesian operad (the multifunctors R′→SetR' \to SetR′→Set preserving the cartesian structure)
amount to modules over RRR.

So as a functor C→SetC \to SetC→Set gives rise to a discrete fibration D→CD \to CD→C over CCC,
an algebra for an operad (or more generally for a multicategory) OOO, gives rise to a discrete fibration of multicategories D→OD \to OD→O.
In the case of (the algebra corresponding to) a module over RRR, the "multicategory of elements" DDD
has the elements of the module as objects and linear combinations as arrows:
if r1x1+⋯rnxn=y r_1 x_1 + \cdots r_n x_n = y r1​x1​+⋯rn​xn​=y we have an arrow x1,⋯ ,xn⊢r1,⋯ ,rny x_1, \cdots, x_n \vdash_{r_1,\cdots,r_n} yx1​,⋯,xn​⊢r1​,⋯,rn​​y in DDD.
The sequent style notation is motivated by the fact that DDD is itself cartesian so that the structural laws hold:
the contraction and weakening rules read
rx+sx=y  ⟹  (r+s)x=yr x + s x = y \implies (r+s)x = y rx+sx=y⟹(r+s)x=y and rx=z  ⟹  rx+0y=zr x = z \implies rx+0y = z rx=z⟹rx+0y=z.

In particular, if we consider RRR as a module over itself, we get a cartesian multicategory whose objects are the elements of RRR.
