David Egolf (Sep 24 2024 at 00:06):

John Baez said:

One can talk about presheaves on any category - do you know about that? A "site" is a category with enough structure on it that you can also talk about sheaves on it.

I see on the nLab that any functor F:Cop→SetF:C^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathsf{Set}F:Cop→Set is a presheaf on CCC. This generalizes the case we've been considering, where we have a presheaf F:O(X)op→SetF:\mathcal{O}(X)^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathsf{Set}F:O(X)op→Set. The open sets of a topological space form a poset, so the presheafs we've been thinking about so far have been defined on thin categories.

When CCC is not thin, I suppose that means that it doesn't necessarily convey the right idea to talk about "restricting" a presheaf element. That's because there can be multiple morphisms between objects now! In particular a "smaller" object can now be contained inside a "bigger" one in multiple different ways.

John Baez (Sep 24 2024 at 00:09):

Right. We can say "pulling back along a morphism" instead "restricting", but sometimes people even say "restricting along a morphism".

When you say "presheafs" you remind me of my friend Jim Dolan, who is so fond of forcing plurals to be regular that he even says "serieses".

Kevin Carlson (Sep 24 2024 at 00:09):

That's true, but it's often still useful to think about "restricting". For instance, a presheaf on the category V⇉EV\rightrightarrows EV⇉E is a (directed, multi)graph. "Restricting" along each of the two parallel arrows sends an edge to its source, respectively, its target, which does feel like restricting. Geometrically, you even have that the graph corresponding to VVV via the Yoneda embedding really embeds in the graph corresponding to EEE in two different ways, so at least up to Yoneda the "restrictions" are really restrictions. We can think quite geometrically of this situation, which is often the case. This story doesn't always work without stretching the concept of "restriction" to the breaking point, but it's pretty good at least for restricting along monomorphisms in the base category of our presheaf.

If I understand you correctly:

  • Let CCC be the category V⇉EV\rightrightarrows EV⇉E
  • Then a presheaf F:Cop→SetF:C^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathsf{Set}F:Cop→Set on CCC is a directed multigraph
  • We can note that C(−,V):Cop→SetC(-,V):C^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathsf{Set}C(−,V):Cop→Set and C(−,E):Cop→SetC(-,E):C^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathsf{Set}C(−,E):Cop→Set are also graphs
  • In particular, C(−,V)C(-,V)C(−,V) has a singleton vertex set and an empty edge set, while C(−,E)C(-,E)C(−,E) has two vertices and one edge, going from one vertex to another
  • So, geometrically C(−,V)C(-,V)C(−,V) is a "single vertex", which can be embedded in the "single arrow" graph described by C(−,E)C(-,E)C(−,E). There are two ways to do this.
  • Further, under the Yoneda embedding, s:V→Es:V \to Es:V→E and t:V→Et:V \to Et:V→E get mapped to natural transformations :C(−,V)→C(−,E):C(-,V) \to C(-,E):C(−,V)→C(−,E), and in fact describe these embeddings

David Egolf (Sep 24 2024 at 22:23):

Now, if we have a presheaf F:Cop→SetF:C^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathsf{Set}F:Cop→Set, we have two sets, F(V)F(V)F(V) and F(E)F(E)F(E). Given an edge, we can apply F(s):F(E)→F(V)F(s):F(E) \to F(V)F(s):F(E)→F(V) or F(t):F(E)→F(V)F(t):F(E) \to F(V)F(t):F(E)→F(V) to get its source and target vertices.

I guess the idea is to try and think about the source and target vertices of a directed edge as "smaller parts" of that directed edge. (Indeed, we just saw how to embed a single-vertex graph in a single-edge graph, in two ways).

David Egolf (Sep 24 2024 at 22:25):

From this angle, we might think of a restriction (in the context of a presheaf on a topological space) as taking data attached to a larger region and getting just some part of the data, now attached to a smaller region. So, we start with something bigger and "restrict" it to part of itself, to get something smaller.

David Egolf (Sep 24 2024 at 22:27):

I think that s,t:V→Es,t:V \to Es,t:V→E are both monomorphisms in CCC. Thinking of VVV as corresponding to a graph with a single vertex, and EEE as corresponding to a graph with a single directed edge (under the Yoneda embedding), these being monomorphisms makes intuitive sense!
