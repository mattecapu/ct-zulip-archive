The Fuglede-Putnam-Rosenblum theorem (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fuglede%27s_theorem) states that for any two normal elements xxx and yyy, and some zzz such that xz=zyxz=zyxz=zy, then also x∗z=zy∗x^*z = zy^*x∗z=zy∗.

The underlying reason why I'm asking btw is the following: for positive elements aaa and bbb in a C*-algebra, it is the case that aba=bab\sqrt{a}b\sqrt{a} = \sqrt{b}a\sqrt{b}a​ba​=b​ab​ if and only if ab=baab=baab=ba. The only way I've seen this proven is to use the Putnam theorem (the LHS aba=bab\sqrt{a}b\sqrt{a} = \sqrt{b}a\sqrt{b}a​ba​=b​ab​ states that ab\sqrt{a}\sqrt{b}a​b​ is normal).
When aaa and bbb are effects, the expression aba\sqrt{a}b\sqrt{a}a​ba​ denotes the sequential product of the effects that corresponds to first observing aaa and then bbb. This result then says "Observing aaa and then bbb is equal to observing bbb and then aaa if and only if they commute as operators". This is quite a neat result and it would be good if this could be proven in a more general categorical way

I'm again just gonna hijack this topic for a question that combines quantum mechanics with category theory.
The notion of `generalized Hilbert space' (introduced by Piron as far as I understand), seems like the natural generalisation for when you want to replace complex numbers by another division ring (see Propositional systems, Hilbert lattices and generalized Hilbert spaces for a definition), and seems like it should result in quite a a lot of pathological spaces.

John van de Wetering (Jul 14 2020 at 18:29):

A reason I care about this:
Call an orthomodular lattice an H-lattice when it is complete irreducible atomistic and satisfies the covering property.
Piron's representation theorem says that any H-lattice of rank at least 4 must be isomorphic to the set of closed subspaces of a generalized Hilbert space. So in particular, for infinite-rank H-lattices this gives you real/complex/quaternionic Hilbert space. This is a 'reconstruction theorem' for going from abstract lattice theory to quantum mechanics.

But here again we have this less nice condition of the space needing to be of rank at least 4. So perhaps if we consider a monoidal category of H-lattices the lower rank ones must also be projections of a generalized Hilbert space, and perhaps they must all be over the same division ring.

John van de Wetering (Jul 14 2020 at 18:30):

This would give quite a nice way to reconstruct quantum theory from lattice properties + tensor product

John Baez (Jul 14 2020 at 19:13):

I don't know a theorem like that, but I know some stuff. A "quaternionic Hilbert space" is often defined to be a left H\mathbb{H}H-module with some particular structure and properties (that I'm too lazy to list). Given this, there's not a nice way to tensor quaternionic Hilbert spaces and get another quaternionic Hilbert space, because the quaternions are not commutative.

You can functorially turn any left H\mathbb{H}H-module into a right H\mathbb{H}H-module, but not an H\mathbb{H}H-bimodule. So, if I have two left H\mathbb{H}H-modules AAA and BBB I can turn AAA into a right H\mathbb{H}H -module and form A⊗HBA \otimes_{\mathbb{H}} BA⊗H​B, but this is now just a real vector space.

Using this idea, I can tensor two quaternionic Hilbert spaces and get a real Hilbert space - not a quaternionic one!
