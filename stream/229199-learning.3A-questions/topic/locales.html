Spencer Breiner (Mar 23 2021 at 20:58):

Hi Vincent! There are two important things to remember here. First is that the direction of arrows on frames and spaces is reversed, second is that [1]={0,1} is the frame corresponding to a single point. That means that a frame morphism p:O(X)→[1]p:\mathcal{O}(X) \to [1]p:O(X)→[1] is the same as a point p∈Xp\in Xp∈X, in the ordinary sense. In particular p(U)=1p(U)=1p(U)=1 iff p∈Up\in Up∈U.

Correspondingly, given any frame FFF, we can build a topological space where the points are literally maps q:F→[1]q:F\to[1]q:F→[1]. The neighborhoods of the topology come from the elements of the frame: A∈F↔UA={q∣q(A)=1}A\in F \leftrightarrow U_A=\{q|q(A)=1\}A∈F↔UA​={q∣q(A)=1}.

Todd Trimble (Mar 23 2021 at 21:21):

Right: as in essentially all such dualities, the dualizing object, which here is the "topological frame" 2=[1]\mathbf{2} = [1]2=[1], is simultaneously a topological space (Sierpinski space) and a frame: in fact a frame internal to topological spaces. So when you hom a space XXX into it, the hom-set hom⁡Top(X,2)\hom_{Top}(X, \mathbf{2})homTop​(X,2) acquires a frame structure whose operations are defined pointwise. When you hom a frame AAA into it, the hom-set hom⁡Fram(A,2)\hom_{Fram}(A, \mathbf{2})homFram​(A,2) acquires a spatial structure (as a subspace of a power of Sierpinski space; this is equivalent to what Spencer was saying).

Likewise, the classical Stone duality between Boolean algebras and compact Hausdorff totally disconnected spaces is effected by homming into a topological Boolean algebra {0,1}\{0, 1\}{0,1} (internal to the category of compact Hausdorff spaces). I think that if you were to look inside Johnstone's Stone spaces, just about all the dualities arise according to this type of idiom.
