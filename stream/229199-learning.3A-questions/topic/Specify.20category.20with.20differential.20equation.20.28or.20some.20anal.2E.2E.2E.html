David Egolf (Apr 06 2025 at 20:54):

You could imagine a functor F:C→DF:C \to DF:C→D where we think of the objects of CCC as coming in different "kinds" depending on the isomorphism class of their image under FFF. We could then ask: which objects are of kind d∈Dd \in Dd∈D? (Which objects c∈Cc \in Cc∈C are such that F(c)≅dF(c) \cong dF(c)≅d?)

We could then take the full subcategory of CCC containing exactly the objects of kind ddd.

David Egolf (Apr 06 2025 at 20:55):

(So then the question "What are the objects of CCC of kind ddd?" would have answers that organize to form a category.)

John Baez (Apr 07 2025 at 00:09):

If you're interested in category theory and linear differential equations, you might (or on closer examination very possibly might not) want to learn about D-modules. The space of solutions of a system of linear partial differential equations defines a module of a certain noncommutative algebra helpfully called D. Unfortunately most of the work on this is done in the holomorphic context, which is great if you like complex analysis or algebraic geometry, but not relevant otherwise.

In this work, it's not as if a specific differential equation or a specific solution of a specific differential equation is a category. Rather, there's a 'category of D-modules', and some D-modules come from systems of linear differential equations in the manner I just sketched.

John Baez (Apr 09 2025 at 15:55):

Algebraic topology largely about discrete structures - groups like Z\mathbb{Z}Z or Z/n\mathbb{Z}/nZ/n that remain when you 'mod out' by all the squishiness. E.g. there's an interesting study of loops in topological spaces, but when we decree that two loops count as the same if there's a homotopy from one to the other, we're led to the concept of fundamental group.

They say a topologist can't tell the difference between a doughnut and a coffee cup. But an algebraic topologist can't tell the difference between either of those and a circle! And they call this thing BZB \mathbb{Z}BZ because it's the 'walking space with fundamental group Z\mathbb{Z}Z'.

John Baez (Apr 09 2025 at 16:01):

Chris Grossack (she/they) (Apr 10 2025 at 06:20):

Not exactly what you're looking for, but the [[Fukaya category]] of a symplectic manifold XXX is defined using extremely heavy duty analysis! The objects in this category are lagrangian submanifolds, and arrows between lagrangians are intersection points, which doesn't sound so bad. But composition in this category comes from understanding a moduli space of maps from a disk with 3 punctures into XXX satisfying a differential equation ("J-holomorphicity") and boundary conditions related to your lagrangians and intersection points.

Chris Grossack (she/they) (Apr 10 2025 at 06:30):

Oh, and I guess I should mention these fukaya categories are extremely interesting and well studied! They're central in the statement of homological mirror symmetry, which uses ideas from physics to make predictions about how algebraic geometry on one manifold should be related to analysis on another. This is made precise by letting "algebraic geometry" on one manifold mean "derived category of sheaves" and letting "analysis" on the mirror manifold mean "fukaya category".

There's a LOT to say here, but most of it is fairly technical. It's definitely worth looking into, though, if you want analytically defined categories!
