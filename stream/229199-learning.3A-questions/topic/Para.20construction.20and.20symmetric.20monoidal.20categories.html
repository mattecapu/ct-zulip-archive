Nathaniel Virgo (Nov 25 2023 at 08:15):

Suppose I have a monoidal category M\mathcal{M}M acting on a category C\mathcal{C}C. Then I can apply the Para construction, as described in Towards Foundations of Categorical Cybernetics. This gives me a bicategory Para(C)\mathbf{Para}(\mathcal{C})Para(C) where the objects are obejcts of C\mathcal{C}C, 1-cells are morphisms of C\mathcal{C}C parametrised by objects of M\mathcal{M}M, and 2-cells are re-parametrisations. (It can also be made into a double category.)

Now suppose that M\mathcal{M}M is a symmetric monoidal category. (But C\mathcal{C}C is still just a category.) It seems like the symmetry of M\mathcal{M}M ought to impose some extra structure on Para(C)\mathbf{Para}(\mathcal{C})Para(C), because a morphism parametrised by M⊗NM\otimes NM⊗N is canonically isomorphic to one parametrised by N⊗MN\otimes MN⊗M.

But if someone gives me a morphism in Para(C)\mathbf{Para}(\mathcal{C})Para(C) I don't seem to have any way to 'know' that it's parametrised by M⊗NM\otimes NM⊗N in order to apply the swap to it. So it's not obvious how to express this purely in terms of the bicategory Para(C)\mathbf{Para}(\mathcal{C})Para(C), but it seems like there should be a way. Does anyone know how to do it?

Philip Saville (Nov 25 2023 at 18:30):

I'm not sure how enlightening it is, but I can confirm that -- at least when you're looking at the Para construction of a symmetric monoidal category C\mathbb{C}C on itself -- the swap in C\mathbb{C}C does determine the swap of a symmetric monoidal bicategory structure on Para(C)\mathrm{Para}(\mathbb{C})Para(C). Every isomorphism in C\mathbb{C}C lifts to an equivalence in Para(C)\mathrm{Para} (\mathbb{C})Para(C) with parameter given by the unit, and every strong natural transformation lifts to a pseudonatural transformation. The braiding for the symmetric structure is then the lifting of the braiding through these two constructions -- Hugo Paquet and I sketched this here but ran out of time to write up the details in full. (The proof is not as difficult as it looks at first sight, because coherence of monoidal categories gives you the axioms for free.) I'm pretty sure all this falls out of Mike Shulman's double categorical framework too, though I never got round to checking for sure.

I am interested in knowing if there's a nicer / more universal way of saying parameters can be reordered, though, because for graded monads it would say something like "the effects can be combined in either order", which is neat.

This are indeed the problem and the solution. In particular David Jaz Myers came up with a clever construction which solves many problems of Para, including this one. So me and him are writing a paper right now.
One of the key innovations is studying Para in arbitrary 2-categories with strict 2-limits, such as MonCat\bf MonCatMonCat. Para instantiated in MonCat\bf MonCatMonCat yields a pseudocategory object (i.e. a [[double category]] in the modern, weak, sense) in MonCat\bf MonCatMonCat itself, hence a monoidal double category. From the actegories paper (which exists partly to answer the very question Nathaniel asked), we know the data of an actegory in MonCat\bf MonCatMonCat corresponds to a monoidal actegory, i.e. the compatible action of a braided monoidal category on a monoidal category (see Table 1, which Dylan posted above, from the paper). So in the end we get that para for a monoidal actegory yields a monoidal double category. Add symmetric to everything and you get a symmetric monoidal double category.

I believe so. Up to choosing the directions right, a lax actegory is a graded monad. Graded monads are classified by lax monoidal functors into [C,C][C, C][C,C]. And it seems to me that the interaction with the braiding doesn't actually change from the strong case (there are no 'lax braided functors'). So I feel confident to conjecture that the classification theorems extend to the lax case. Of course the devil is in the details...
