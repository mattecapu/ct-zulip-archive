Alex Kreitzberg (Jan 02 2026 at 01:47):

Before digging deeper, I'd like to test my understanding of the analogy.

A set XXX can be made into a vector space over R\mathbf{R}R by inheriting the vector space structure of R\mathbf{R}R via hom(X,R)\textrm{hom}(X, \mathbf{R})hom(X,R). So for example the set {x,y,z}\{x, y, z\}{x,y,z} gets sent to all linear combinations of the functions δx,δy,δz\delta_{x}, \delta_{y}, \delta_{z}δx​,δy​,δz​ where δ\deltaδ is the Kronecker delta. Functions between sets become linear maps between vector spaces via precomposition.

So by analogy,

A category C\mathcal{C}C can be made into its free cocompletion by inheriting the colimit structure of Set\textrm{Set}Set via hom(Cop,Set)\textrm{hom}(C^{\textrm{op}}, \textrm{Set})hom(Cop,Set). Explicitly, the objects of a given category C\mathcal{C}C are embedded into our presheaf category by sending them to their representable functors, the analogs of Kronecker deltas, whose colimits then, like a basis, express any presheaf by the co-yoneda lemma. Functors between categories then must lift to the relevant monoidal functors via precomposition.

In the case of finite sets often a bijection is made with the natural numbers {0,1,…,n−1}\{0, 1, \ldots, n - 1\}{0,1,…,n−1} which allows the deltas to be expressed as vectors [0,…,1,…,0]\left [0, \ldots, 1, \ldots, 0 \right ][0,…,1,…,0] with the position of the 111 indicating the element of our cardinal set.

A matrix viewed as a map X×Y→RX\times Y \rightarrow \mathbf{R}X×Y→R generalizes to profunctors Dop×C→Set\mathcal{D}^{op}\times\mathcal{C}\rightarrow \mathrm{Set}Dop×C→Set, which then have a notion of "matrix multiplication" given by a coend ∫c∈CF(x,c)⊗G(c,y)\int^{c \in \mathcal{C}} F(x, c) \otimes G(c, y)∫c∈CF(x,c)⊗G(c,y). Specializing GGG to a representable hom functor and FFF to a "one dimensional matrix", or just a presheaf, gives us an explicitly solvable expression by the ninja yoneda lemma, which Fosco shared above.

fosco (Jan 02 2026 at 10:35):

categorical probability people will show you that there is a certain spectrum of variation between probability monads:

  1. The usual monad of finite distributions sends a set X to the set of distributions DX={p:X→[0,1]∣∑xpx=1,p=0 a. e.}DX=\{p : X \to [0,1]\mid \sum_x px=1, p=0 \text{ a. e.}\}DX={p:X→[0,1]∣∑x​px=1,p=0 a. e.}
  2. The monad of subdistributions sends a set X to D≤X={p:X→[0,1]∣∑xpx≤1,p=0 a. e.}D_\le X=\{p : X \to [0,1]\mid \sum_x px\le 1, p=0 \text{ a. e.}\}D≤​X={p:X→[0,1]∣∑x​px≤1,p=0 a. e.}
  3. The monad of "unbounded distributions" sends X to D∞X={p:X→[0,∞)∣∑xpx<∞,p=0 a. e.}D_\infty X=\{p : X \to [0,\infty)\mid \sum_x px<\infty, p=0 \text{ a. e.}\}D∞​X={p:X→[0,∞)∣∑x​px<∞,p=0 a. e.} (I think I'm recalling it correctly, tell me if I'm wrong)

Clearly, these monads are related, and thus their algebras relate.

The third is the monad whose Eilenberg-Moore category is semivector spaces over the semiring of nonnegative, finite reals. And indeed, working with unbounded distribution feels not probability-ish, but instead linear algebra-ish.

Rémy Tuyéras (Jan 02 2026 at 12:07):

To me, linear algebra is a restriction of the logic you can study through (finite) sets and cartesian products.

Take a finite set X={x1,…,xn}X = \{x_1, \dots , x_n\}X={x1​,…,xn​}. You can write X=∑i=1nxiX = \sum_{i=1}^n x_iX=∑i=1n​xi​.

Now, take another finite set Y={y1,…,ym}=∑j=1myjY = \{y_1,\dots , y_m\} = \sum_{j=1}^m y_jY={y1​,…,ym​}=∑j=1m​yj​. Then the Cartesian product expands as:

X×Y=∑i,jxi⋅yjX \times Y = \sum_{i,j} x_i \cdot y_jX×Y=∑i,j​xi​⋅yj​

where xi⋅yj=(xi,yj)x_i \cdot y_j = (x_i,y_j)xi​⋅yj​=(xi​,yj​).

From this perspective, a matrix can be interpreted as anything that looks like a doubly-indexed (finite) set: M=(Xi,k)i,kM = (X_{i,k})_{i,k}M=(Xi,k​)i,k​.

Examples include (finite) categories: C=(C(x,y))x,y∈Obj(C)C = (C(x,y))_{x,y \in \mathsf{Obj}(C)}C=(C(x,y))x,y∈Obj(C)​, and also (finite) graphs. What categories add beyond graphs is composition: for categories you have a morphism

∘:C⋅C→C\circ: C \cdot C \to C∘:C⋅C→C

So, conversely, we could also say that linear algebra = a measured lens on graph theory in set.

Rémy Tuyéras (Jan 02 2026 at 12:39):

From this viewpoint, a functor

F:D(x,y)→C(F(x),F(y))F: D(x,y) \to C(F(x),F(y))F:D(x,y)→C(F(x),F(y))

can be seen as a non-monotone, non-injective extraction of a submatrix DDD from a matrix CCC. For intuition, this is the kind of situation I have in mind:

Screenshot 2026-01-02 at 7.15.27 AM.png

So when you form

F(x)×C(y,x),F(x) \times C(y, x),F(x)×C(y,x),

what you are really doing is pushing indices around: you take coefficients from CCC, but only along the index pattern specified by the submatrix extraction. Concretely, you can think of this as multiplying a matrix MMM by a vector JJJ of indices describing the extraction map, e.g.

(1,2,4,5,7,8,9,… ).(1,2,4,5,7,8,9, \dots).(1,2,4,5,7,8,9,…).

If you take MMM with coefficients in {0,1}\{0, 1\}{0,1} (say with rows summing to 1 or 0), then MJMJMJ acts like a "filter-and-shuffle" operation on JJJ. If you then apply max\mathsf{max}max, you get an equation of the form

max(MJ)=Jk\mathsf{max}(MJ) = J_{k}max(MJ)=Jk​

for some kkk.

Since max\mathsf{max}max can be seen as a colimit operation, this is reminiscent of the equation

∫XF(x)×C(y,x)=F(y).\int^X F(x) \times C(y, x) = F(y).∫XF(x)×C(y,x)=F(y).

So why restrict ourselves to matrices in {0,1}\{0, 1\}{0,1}, and can we do better? My instinct is that composition in categories, together with naturality (which is what gives Yoneda), is what forces this kind of restriction: naturality collapses the extra "free" arithmetic structure you would otherwise have over the integers.
