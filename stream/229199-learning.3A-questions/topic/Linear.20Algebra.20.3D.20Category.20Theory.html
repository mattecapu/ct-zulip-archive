To me, linear algebra is a restriction of the logic you can study through (finite) sets and cartesian products.

Take a finite set X={x1,…,xn}X = \{x_1, \dots , x_n\}X={x1​,…,xn​}. You can write X=∑i=1nxiX = \sum_{i=1}^n x_iX=∑i=1n​xi​.

Now, take another finite set Y={y1,…,ym}=∑j=1myjY = \{y_1,\dots , y_m\} = \sum_{j=1}^m y_jY={y1​,…,ym​}=∑j=1m​yj​. Then the Cartesian product expands as:

X×Y=∑i,jxi⋅yjX \times Y = \sum_{i,j} x_i \cdot y_jX×Y=∑i,j​xi​⋅yj​

where xi⋅yj=(xi,yj)x_i \cdot y_j = (x_i,y_j)xi​⋅yj​=(xi​,yj​).

From this perspective, a matrix can be interpreted as anything that looks like a doubly-indexed (finite) set: M=(Xi,k)i,kM = (X_{i,k})_{i,k}M=(Xi,k​)i,k​.

Examples include (finite) categories: C=(C(x,y))x,y∈Obj(C)C = (C(x,y))_{x,y \in \mathsf{Obj}(C)}C=(C(x,y))x,y∈Obj(C)​, and also (finite) graphs. What categories add beyond graphs is composition: for categories you have a morphism

∘:C⋅C→C\circ: C \cdot C \to C∘:C⋅C→C

So, conversely, we could also say that linear algebra = a measured lens on graph theory in set.

From this viewpoint, a functor

F:D(x,y)→C(F(x),F(y))F: D(x,y) \to C(F(x),F(y))F:D(x,y)→C(F(x),F(y))

can be seen as a non-monotone, non-injective extraction of a submatrix DDD from a matrix CCC. For intuition, this is the kind of situation I have in mind:

Screenshot 2026-01-02 at 7.15.27 AM.png

So when you form

F(x)×C(y,x),F(x) \times C(y, x),F(x)×C(y,x),

what you are really doing is pushing indices around: you take coefficients from CCC, but only along the index pattern specified by the submatrix extraction. Concretely, you can think of this as multiplying a matrix MMM by a vector JJJ of indices describing the extraction map, e.g.

(1,2,4,5,7,8,9,… ).(1,2,4,5,7,8,9, \dots).(1,2,4,5,7,8,9,…).

If you take MMM with coefficients in {0,1}\{0, 1\}{0,1} (say with rows summing to 1 or 0), then MJMJMJ acts like a "filter-and-shuffle" operation on JJJ. If you then apply max\mathsf{max}max, you get an equation of the form

max(MJ)=Jk\mathsf{max}(MJ) = J_{k}max(MJ)=Jk​

for some kkk.

Since max\mathsf{max}max can be seen as a colimit operation, this is reminiscent of the equation

∫XF(x)×C(y,x)=F(y).\int^X F(x) \times C(y, x) = F(y).∫XF(x)×C(y,x)=F(y).

So why restrict ourselves to matrices in {0,1}\{0, 1\}{0,1}, and can we do better? My instinct is that composition in categories, together with naturality (which is what gives Yoneda), is what forces this kind of restriction: naturality collapses the extra "free" arithmetic structure you would otherwise have over the integers.

Rémy Tuyéras (Jan 03 2026 at 14:00):

@John Baez said:

Again, I don't think this is a connection between linear algebra and category theory: I think it's a connection between linear algebra and Prof, the 2-category of profunctors.

Yes, I agree that profunctors have a role here, but I think we also need to put equal emphasis on how to interpret the coefficients.

More specifically, let CCC and DDD be small categories in Cat\mathbf{Cat}Cat. For every profunctor M:Cop×D→SetM : C^{\mathsf{op}} \times D \to \mathbf{Set}M:Cop×D→Set, I see two complementary sources of equations:

  • Structure coming from CCC and DDD should yield equations about formal rearrangement and shuffling of coefficients (what linear algebra would call index gymnastics).
  • Structure coming from Set\mathbf{Set}Set should yield equations about "measurements", in the sense of evaluation principles (for instance, Dirac-style sifting phenomena).

1) Sets

First, observe that Set\mathbf{Set}Set carries a weak-rig-like structure:

  • For any pair X,Y∈SetX, Y \in \mathbf{Set}X,Y∈Set, define X+Y:=X∪YX + Y := X \cup YX+Y:=X∪Y.

  • For any integer n≥0n \geq 0n≥0, choose a product operation ∏n\prod^n∏n and define, for any X1,…,Xn∈SetX_1, \dots, X_n \in \mathbf{Set}X1​,…,Xn​∈Set,
    X1⋅⋯⋅Xn:=∏i=1nXi.X_1 \cdot \dots \cdot X_n := \prod_{i=1}^n X_i.X1​⋅⋯⋅Xn​:=∏i=1n​Xi​.

  • Define 0:=∅0 := \emptyset0:=∅ and 1:={∅}1 := \{\emptyset\}1:={∅}.

Then all rig equations hold except that the product-related ones only hold up to canonical isomorphism (hence "weak"):

X⋅(Y⋅Z)≅(X⋅Y)⋅ZX \cdot (Y \cdot Z) \cong (X \cdot Y) \cdot ZX⋅(Y⋅Z)≅(X⋅Y)⋅Z

1⋅X≅X≅X⋅11 \cdot X \cong X \cong X \cdot 11⋅X≅X≅X⋅1

Let me denote this weak structure by SetRig\mathbf{SetRig}SetRig.

With this in mind, one can view a set-valued "matrix" as a functor M:Cop×D→SetRigM : C^{\mathsf{op}} \times D \to \mathbf{SetRig}M:Cop×D→SetRig.

2) Bounded coefficients

If we work directly in SetRig\mathbf{SetRig}SetRig, this is not a fair comparison with ordinary linear algebra, where one typically fixes a ring or field of coefficients for a given class of matrices.

So instead, fix a set SSS and consider the sub-collection of objects of SetRig\mathbf{SetRig}SetRig consisting of subsets of some power Sn=∏i=1nSS^{n} = \prod_{i=1}^n SSn=∏i=1n​S for some n≥0n \geq 0n≥0.

This yields an proper rig (not merely weak), because we can force products to land strictly in the expected power: if X⊆SpX \subseteq S^{p}X⊆Sp and Y⊆SqY \subseteq S^{q}Y⊆Sq, then we regard X⋅YX \cdot YX⋅Y as a subset of Sp+qS^{p+q}Sp+q via the canonical identification Sp×Sq=Sp+qS^{p} \times S^{q} = S^{p+q}Sp×Sq=Sp+q. With this convention we get strict equalities:

X⋅(Y⋅Z)=(X⋅Y)⋅ZX \cdot (Y \cdot Z) = (X \cdot Y) \cdot ZX⋅(Y⋅Z)=(X⋅Y)⋅Z

1⋅X=X=X⋅11 \cdot X = X = X \cdot 11⋅X=X=X⋅1

Let me denote this rig by Rig(S)\mathbf{Rig}(S)Rig(S).

3) Profunctors as matrices

For every subcategory C⊆Cat\mathbf{C} \subseteq \mathbf{Cat}C⊆Cat, define a C\mathbf{C}C-matrix in Rig(S)\mathbf{Rig}(S)Rig(S) to be:

  • a pair of objects C,D∈CC, D \in \mathbf{C}C,D∈C,
  • a functor M:Cop×D→SetM : C^{\mathsf{op}} \times D \to \mathbf{Set}M:Cop×D→Set with images in Rig(S)\mathbf{Rig}(S)Rig(S).

Examples:

  • If C\mathbf{C}C is the category whose objects are the finite ordinals [n]=1,…,n[n] = {1, \dots, n}[n]=1,…,n, then this recovers ordinary finite matrices with coefficients in Rig(S)\mathbf{Rig}(S)Rig(S).

  • If C\mathbf{C}C is the category of finite sets, this matches the setting of my previous post.

  • If C=Cat\mathbf{C} = \mathbf{Cat}C=Cat, then these are profunctors.

4) Null spaces for rigs

To connect Cat\mathbf{Cat}Cat-matrices with the usual arithmetic intuition from rings and fields, we would like rig-theoretic analogues of constructions such as kernels or null spaces.

In past work I developed a notion of null space in a rig setting (see Theorem 5.113) in the paper:

https://arxiv.org/pdf/1805.07004

Although the paper is motivated by an application to genetics, Section 5 is written in an application-agnostic way and is intended to stand on its own. The earlier sections mainly serve as motivation for why this rig-theoretic null space is a useful concept.

{b,c}+Z={a,b,c},\{b,c\} + Z = \{a,b,c\},{b,c}+Z={a,b,c},

This immediately implies that
Z={a}.Z = \{a\}.Z={a}.

Thus, even though there is no negation, we still obtain a mechanism reminiscent of Gaussian elimination. In Jacobs' paper, I believe this corresponds to working with the equaliser (μ,β)(\mu,\beta)(μ,β), where β\betaβ is the basis morphism

β:R→T(R).\beta : R \to T(R).β:R→T(R).

Concretely:

  • The equation
    β(a)+β(b)+β(Z)=β(a)+β(b)+β(c)\beta(a) + \beta(b) + \beta(Z) = \beta(a) + \beta(b) + \beta(c)β(a)+β(b)+β(Z)=β(a)+β(b)+β(c)
    implies that β(Z)\beta(Z)β(Z) can be expressed in terms of β(a),β(b),β(c)\beta(a), \beta(b), \beta(c)β(a),β(b),β(c).

  • The equation
    β(Z)+β(e)=β(e)+β(a)\beta(Z) + \beta(e) = \beta(e) + \beta(a)β(Z)+β(e)=β(e)+β(a)
    implies that β(Z)\beta(Z)β(Z) can be expressed in terms of β(e),β(a)\beta(e), \beta(a)β(e),β(a).

Together, these constraints are intended to characterize β(Z)\beta(Z)β(Z) in terms of the image of β\betaβ, rather than as an absolute element.

2) Matrix equations and the rig structure

If we now want to solve matrix equations of the form

MV=U,MV = U,MV=U,

the rig structure becomes necessary. The overall approach is similar, but the structure provides additional elements, extending the previous equations to string-like expressions.

For example:
{baa,cda}+Z⋅{a}={baa,cda,eaa},\{baa, cda\} + Z \cdot \{a\} = \{baa, cda, eaa\},{baa,cda}+Z⋅{a}={baa,cda,eaa},
{eaa}+Z⋅{a}={eaa,cda}.\{eaa\} + Z \cdot \{a\} = \{eaa, cda\}.{eaa}+Z⋅{a}={eaa,cda}.

In this case, the solution is
Z={ea,cd}.Z = \{ea, cd\}.Z={ea,cd}.

3) The non-free case

Finally, consider the non-free case. While linear algebra over the free-like field Q\mathbb{Q}Q is standard, we also work with linear algebra over structures such as R\mathbb{R}R or Q[i,2]\mathbb{Q}[i,\sqrt{2}]Q[i,2​]. This situation can be recovered within the rig framework by introducing additional relations (i.e. quotients) on the string-like elements, for example via letter shuffles:

{baa,cda}={b∣aa,c∣da}={bda,caa,baa,cda}.\{baa, cda\} = \{b|aa, c|da\} = \{bda, caa, baa, cda\}.{baa,cda}={b∣aa,c∣da}={bda,caa,baa,cda}.

In this setting, one must repeatedly refer back to the underlying free structure in order to interpret solutions.

Linear maps are represented by matrices through the Yoneda Lemma. For example, a ring RRR is represented by/expressed as an image of the Yoneda embedding Y(t,−):ModR→SetY(t,-): \mathsf{Mod}_R \to \mathbf{Set}Y(t,−):ModR​→Set for some ttt in the theory ModR\mathsf{Mod}_RModR​ for RRR-modules.

Now, the Yoneda lemma gives:
[ModR,Set](R,M)≅M[\mathsf{Mod}_R, \mathbf{Set}](R, M) \cong M[ModR​,Set](R,M)≅M

And then:
[ModR,Set](Rm,M)≅[ModR,Set](⊕i=1mR,M)≅Mm[\mathsf{Mod}_R, \mathbf{Set}](R^m, M) \cong [\mathsf{Mod}_R, \mathbf{Set}](\oplus^m_{i=1} R, M) \cong M^{m}[ModR​,Set](Rm,M)≅[ModR​,Set](⊕i=1m​R,M)≅Mm

So a linear map Rm→RnR^m \to R^nRm→Rn is an element of Rn×mR^{n \times m}Rn×m, i.e. a matrix of elements in RRR.
