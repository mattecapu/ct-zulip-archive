Ralph Sarkis (Oct 03 2022 at 02:47):

In short, the disjoint union of a family of sets is exactly the same thing as its coproducts (up to isomorphism) and the difference in notation is irrelevant (you might have also seen the notations A+BA+BA+B or ∑i∈IAi\sum_{i \in I} A_i∑i∈I​Ai​).

I am failing to see exactly what you are missing (if there is anything), so let me give some details and motivation.

The name 'disjoint union' probably comes from the fact when the AiA_iAi​'s are pairwise disjoint (Ai∩Aj=∅A_i \cap A_j = \emptysetAi​∩Aj​=∅ for all i≠ji\neq ji=j), the disjoint union and union of the family are isomorphic: ⊔i∈IAi≅∪i∈IAi\sqcup_{i \in I} A_i \cong \cup_{i \in I}A_i⊔i∈I​Ai​≅∪i∈I​Ai​. When the AiA_iAi​'s are not disjoint, the disjoint union is taking their union as if they were disjoint using the trick of identifying x∈Aix \in A_ix∈Ai​ with (x,i)(x,i)(x,i) (so that (x,i)≠(x,j)(x,i) \neq (x,j)(x,i)=(x,j) if i≠ji\neq ji=j). This is one of the possible tricks that allows this construction, but there are other ways to "force" the disjointness and then take the union.

In category theory, we prefer not to refer to elements of sets, and we love to extract the essence of a definition to get rid of all unnecessary information ∗\ast∗ . Thus, we came up ∗∗\ast\ast∗∗ with the definition of coproducts, which informally says that the coproduct of a family of sets {Ai}i∈I\{A_i\}_{i \in I}{Ai​}i∈I​ is a set that contains copies of each set (via the coprojections κi:Ai→⨿i∈IAi\kappa_i : A_i \to \amalg_{i \in I} A_iκi​:Ai​→⨿i∈I​Ai​) that are completely independent of each other (via the universal property: any way you want to map the AiA_iAi​'s into XXX, you can combine the functions to map the coproduct into XXX).

This is an appropriate definition because the disjoint union of sets (along with the coprojections Ai→⊔i∈IAi=x↦(x,i)A_i \to \sqcup_{i \in I}A_i = x \mapsto (x,i)Ai​→⊔i∈I​Ai​=x↦(x,i)) is the coproduct of the family {Ai}i∈I\{A_i\}_{i \in I}{Ai​}i∈I​ (up to isomorphism). However, it is possible that you have a different opinion about what the essence of disjoint unions is. Then, you might want to try formalizing another categorical definition that captures that essence and verify it coincides with disjoint union.

∗\ast∗ An example of unnecessary information in this case is that the elements of the disjoint union are pairs of elements of A=∪i∈IAiA = \cup_{i \in I}A_iA=∪i∈I​Ai​ and elements of III, that is, ⊔i∈IAi⊆A×I\sqcup_{i \in I} A_i \subseteq A \times I⊔i∈I​Ai​⊆A×I. This fact has nothing to do with our intuition of disjoint union (indeed, we usually don't bother to refer to elements of the disjoint union with the pairs except when doing very rigorous technical things), and the other "tricks" mentioned above might get rid of it, but inevitably bringing another piece of irrelevant information about the disjoint union.

John Baez (Oct 03 2022 at 08:28):

Jacob Zelko said:

  1. What is the difference between ⨆i∈IAi\bigsqcup_{i \in I} A_{i}⨆i∈I​Ai​ and ∐i∈IAi\coprod_{i \in I} A_{i}∐i∈I​Ai​ and X1∐X2X_{1} \coprod X_{2}X1​∐X2​ syntax notation (the latter notation concerns two specific sets)? I have seen all notations when referring to coproducts when viewed from both set theory and category theory perspectives. Is it a matter of aesthetic or is there a functional/semantic difference occurring?

There's no difference, people often use different notations for the same thing in math.

The notation ⨆\bigsqcup⨆ probably comes from people who write the disjoint union of two sets as X1⊔X2X_1 \sqcup X_2X1​⊔X2​ so that it looks similar to the union X1∪X2X_1 \cup X_2X1​∪X2​; these people tend to write ⋃i∈IXi\bigcup_{i \in I} X_{i}⋃i∈I​Xi​ for the union of a bunch of sets and ⨆i∈IXi\bigsqcup_{i \in I} X_{i}⨆i∈I​Xi​ for their disjoint union. The notation ∐\coprod∐ probably comes from people who noticed that the coproduct is dual to the product: they turned the product symbol ∏\prod∏ upside down.

  1. I have read that coproducts are also known as disjoint unions. I read about disjoint unions from a set theory perspective and also a category theory perspective but cannot really discern a functional (i.e. practical) difference from coproducts. Are coproducts and disjoint unions really effectively the same idea?

Probably the best thing to say is that coproducts in the category of sets, and various related categories like the category of topological spaces, are the same as disjoint unions.

Nobody I know calls coproducts 'disjoint unions' in an arbitrary category. For example coproducts in the category of vector spaces are called 'direct sums'. Everyone will understand you if you say 'direct sum of vector spaces', and category theorists will know this is their coproduct, but people would think you don't understand math if you say 'disjoint union of vector spaces'.
