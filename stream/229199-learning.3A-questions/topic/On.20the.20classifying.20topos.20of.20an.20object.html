Topic: On the classifying topos of an object

James Deikun (Apr 02 2025 at 15:42):

Let S\mathcal SS be an elementary topos with natural numbers object, S[X]\mathcal S[X]S[X] the classifying topos over S\mathcal SS of an object, and S[X∙]\mathcal S[X^\bullet]S[X∙] the classifying topos over S\mathcal SS of a pointed object. Are any of these true/well-known facts of topos theory?

  1. The morphism from S[X∙]\mathcal S[X^\bullet]S[X∙] to S[X]\mathcal S[X]S[X] picking out the object part of the generic pointed object is an [[etale geometric morphism]] which is equivalent to S[X]/X\mathcal S[X]/XS[X]/X where X is the generic object.
  2. Any [[etale geometric morphism]] E/E→E\mathcal E/E \to \mathcal EE/E→E over S\mathcal SS is a pullback of the above morphism along the geometric morphism classifying the object EEE.
  3. The global sections morphism of S[X]\mathcal S[X]S[X] represents a stack on S\mathcal SS whenever S\mathcal SS is such that it makes sense to ask if it represents a stack.
  4. Geometric morphisms that represent stacks are closed under precomposition by etale geometric morphisms, and whenever this makes sense, under precomposition by geometric morphisms that represent stacks.

Also are any of the equivalents in ∞\infty∞-topos theory known?

James Deikun (Apr 02 2025 at 19:18):

Evidence for the propositions above:

  1. A morphism into S[X∙]\mathcal S[X^\bullet]S[X∙] or S[X]/X\mathcal S[X]/XS[X]/X is an object equipped with a global section, so they classify the same theory.
  2. A morphism into E/E\mathcal E/EE/E is a whatever-E\mathcal EE-classifies with a constant of type EEE. This is equivalent to a pointed object and a whatever-E\mathcal EE-classifies with the pointed object happening to be the same object that represents the sort EEE.
  3. This one is just a feeling and I'm genuinely curious about it.
  4. Local homomorphisms of locales are closed under composition and geometric morphisms representing stacks are similar in nature.

James Deikun (Apr 03 2025 at 09:42):

Although it's not outright stated, I found enough proof in https://arxiv.org/abs/2107.04417 that if (3) is true then the represented stack is the canonical stack on S\mathcal SS.

James Deikun (Apr 04 2025 at 02:01):

In the paper I linked above they form a "fundamental adjunction" between stacks on a site (C,J)(\mathcal C,J)(C,J) and essential, locally connected geometric morphisms over the topos Sh(C,J)\mathbf{Sh}(\mathcal C,J)Sh(C,J), specifically given by continuous comorphisms over (C,J)(\mathcal C,J)(C,J). The left adjoint is given by Cartesian lifts in the fibration of the category of sites and comorphisms over the category of categories and functors. The right adjoint is given by homming the image of C\mathcal CC in EssTopos/Sh(C,J)\mathbf{EssTopos}/\mathbf{Sh}(\mathcal C,J)EssTopos/Sh(C,J) given by sheafifying the representables and slicing over them, the indexed version of what's called the "canonical stack", fiberwise into the argument, and taking the Grothendieck construction. I think when your site is the canonical topology on a topos the canonical stack is the self-indexing. This stack is not topos-valued; it's a different construction meant to represent all the small stacks (and it still gets some of the large ones like the canonical stack).

James Deikun (Apr 04 2025 at 10:30):

Okay, I think I figured it out. Set[X]\mathbf{Set}[X]Set[X] actually does represent the Set\mathbf{Set}Set-stack FinSetop\mathbf{FinSet}^\mathsf{op}FinSetop. The text had led me to think otherwise, but they really do mean the slice category EssToposco/S\mathbf{EssTopos}^\mathsf{co}/\mathcal SEssToposco/S, with the morphisms also being essential. You get away without this for sheaves because geometric morphisms are basically something similar to the Ind-completion of essential geometric morphisms (exactly the Ind-completion in the case of morphisms from stacks into Set[X]\mathbf{Set}[X]Set[X]) and Ind-completion acts trivially on discrete categories. This way everything makes sense, and the adjunction is idempotent, and the representing site for the canonical stack might be too big to present a topos but there are lots of smaller classifying objects for the small-enough sheaves. I think (3) is still true and the process to prove it is what you said.
