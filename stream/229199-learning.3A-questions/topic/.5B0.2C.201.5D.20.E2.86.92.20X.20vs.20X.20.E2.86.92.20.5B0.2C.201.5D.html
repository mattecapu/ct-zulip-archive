John Baez (Apr 19 2025 at 06:16):

In physics I like to think of a function R→X\mathbb{R} \rightarrow X R→X as a 'particle' and a function X→R X \rightarrow \mathbb{R}X→R as a 'field', where XXX is 'spacetime'. Particles and fields give dual ways of thinking about matter. I'd never noticed that particles are like drawings and fields are like paintings. Nice!

A space XXX with a function [0,1]→X[0,1] \to X[0,1]→X is called a space 'under' [0,1][0,1][0,1], and the category of all spaces under [0,1][0,1][0,1] is an example of an 'under category'.

A space XXX with a function X→[0,1] X \rightarrow [0,1]X→[0,1] is called a space 'over' [0,1][0,1][0,1], and the category of all spaces over [0,1][0,1][0,1] is an example of an 'over category'.

So one way to learn more is to learn about properties of [[over categories]] (also called 'slice categories') and [[under categories]] (also called 'coslice categories').

Lawvere wanted 'spaces', of a very general sort, to be objects of a topos. Then there's a nice theorem that the over category of any object in a topos is again a topos. This is often called the fundamental theorem of topos theory.

It doesn't work like this for under categories! The under category of an object in a topos is not usually a topos. So there's a built in asymmetry in the concept of topos (and other aspects of our concepts of space). If your category of spaces XXX is a topos, then the category of spaces over [0,1][0,1][0,1] (spaces XXX equipped with a map X→[0,1]X \to [0,1]X→[0,1]) is a topos, but the category of spaces under [0,1][0,1][0,1] (spaces XXX with a map [0,1]→X[0,1] \to X[0,1]→X) is usually not.

So, if you set things up nicely, you can get a topos of paintings, but maybe not a topos of drawings.
