John Baez (Sep 30 2023 at 21:16):

I don't know a reference, but I would try to prove like this. A smooth map between manifolds By definition f:X→Yf: X \to Yf:X→Y gives for each point x∈Xx \in Xx∈X a linear map, the differential df:TxX→TyYdf: T_x X \to T_y Ydf:Tx​X→Ty​Y. By definition f:X→Yf: X \to Yf:X→Y is a submersion iff for each x∈Xx \in Xx∈X the differential df:TxX→TyYdf: T_x X \to T_y Ydf:Tx​X→Ty​Y is an epimorphism.

A commutative square of smooth maps together with any point in the upper left corner (if you now what I mean) induces a commutative square of linear maps. I suspect that if this commutative square of smooth maps is a pullback square, it induces a pullback square of linear maps.

If the last statement is true we're done, because in the category of vector spaces and linear maps, epimorphisms are stable under pullback.

ʇɐ (Oct 01 2023 at 15:00):

I don’t know a reference off the top of my head either, but here’s a sketch proof: Since a submersion is transversal to any other map, the fiber product of it and any other map exists (see e.g. Lang’s Fundamentals of differential geometry (1999: pp. 31–32)). By the constant rank theorem, every submersion is a smooth fiber bundle (i.e. locally a product projection) (EDIT 2023-10-02: not (globally) a fiber bundle of course!), and product projections are stable under pullback, so pullbacks of submersions are locally product projections (and hence submersions).

David Michael Roberts (Oct 02 2023 at 08:11):

It really is a statement about charts on manifolds. A submersion p ⁣:X→Yp\colon X\to Yp:X→Y has the property that around every point x∈Xx\in Xx∈X, there is chart that looks like U×VU\times VU×V, where VVV is a chart in YYY around y=p(x)y=p(x)y=p(x) and UUU is a chart in p−1(y)p^{-1}(y)p−1(y) around xxx. Further, in this chart in XXX, the map ppp looks like the projection U×V→VU\times V\to VU×V→V (this is the underlying map of the linear projection E⊕F→FE\oplus F\to FE⊕F→F, where these are the vector spaces containing the open sets that are the charts. This means that on the topological space that is the pullback in Top\mathbf{Top}Top, one can build charts around each point.

So given f ⁣:M→Yf\colon M\to Yf:M→Y, and a point (m,x)∈M×YX(m,x)\in M\times_Y X(m,x)∈M×Y​X, the chart around it looks like U×WU\times WU×W, where UUU is the chart around xxx in the fibre from before, and WWW is a chart around m∈Mm \in Mm∈M small enough so that f∣Wf\big|_Wf​W​ lands inside VVV. The map down to MMM is, on this new chart, a projection (and in this way, you can show it is a submersion), and the projection map to XXX is given on the chart by the product idU×f∣W ⁣:U×W→U×V \mathrm{id}_U\times f\big|_W\colon U\times W \to U\times VidU​×f​W​:U×W→U×V. These two maps are smooth.

One has to check all the details here, of course, but this is how it goes.
