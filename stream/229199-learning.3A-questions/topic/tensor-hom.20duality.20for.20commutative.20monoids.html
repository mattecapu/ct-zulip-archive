It is well known [emphasis mine] that a tensor product A⊗BA \otimes BA⊗B can be defined in the category CMon\mathsf{CMon}CMon of commutative monoids so that, up to natural isomorphisms, ⊗\otimes⊗ is associative, commutative, and has as an identity. It is also well known that the monoid homomorphisms from A to B form a commutative monoid [A, B] and that there is a natural isomorphism

CMon(A⊗B,C)≅CMon(A,[B,C]) \mathsf{CMon}(A \otimes B, C) \cong \mathsf{CMon}(A, [B ,C]) CMon(A⊗B,C)≅CMon(A,[B,C])

in other words, the category of commutative monoids is a symmetric
monoidal closed category (MacLane, 1971). This is just like a Cartesian
closed category except that....

What I actually did was add the following to the paper @Adittya Chaudhuri and I are writing.

This [the theorem we're trying to prove] can be shown directly, but we sketch a proof using some facts, well known for abelian groups, which have easy analogues for commutative monoids [MeseguerMontanari1990] and even more general algebraic structures [Kock1971]. First, given A,B∈CommMonA, B \in \mathsf{CommMon}A,B∈CommMon, the set of homomorphisms A→BA \to BA→B can be made into a commutative monoid [A,B][A,B][A,B] using pointwise operations:

(f+g)(a)=f(a)+g(a),∀f,g∈[A,B],  a∈A. (f + g)(a) = f(a) + g(a) , \qquad \forall f,g \in [A,B], \; a \in A .(f+g)(a)=f(a)+g(a),∀f,g∈[A,B],a∈A.

Second, we can define a tensor product A⊗BA \otimes BA⊗B of commutative monoids such that homomorphisms A⊗B→CA \otimes B \to CA⊗B→C correspond naturally to maps A×B→CA \times B \to CA×B→C that are homomorphisms in each argument. This tensor product obeys hom-tensor adjointness: that is, there is a natural isomorphism

CommMon(A⊗B,C)≅CommMon(B,[A,C]). \mathsf{CommMon}(A \otimes B, C) \cong \mathsf{CommMon}(B, [A,C]) . CommMon(A⊗B,C)≅CommMon(B,[A,C]).
