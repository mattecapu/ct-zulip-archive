For a directed graph GGG we can form the "derived graph" or "line graph" G′G'G′ whose vertices are the edges of GGG and where there is an edge e→fe \to fe→f whenever the target of eee is the source of fff. If we represent GGG as a pair of (jointly injective) maps s,t:E→Vs, t : E \to Vs,t:E→V then E′=E×t,sEE' = E \times_{t, s} EE′=E×t,s​E and the pullback projections s′,t′:E′→Es', t' : E' \to Es′,t′:E′→E give the directed graph G′G'G′. ⋯⇉E′′⇉s′′t′′E′⇉s′t′E⇉stV.\dots \rightrightarrows E'' \underset{t''}{\overset{s''}{\rightrightarrows}} E' \underset{t'}{\overset{s'}{\rightrightarrows}} E \underset{t}{\overset{s}{\rightrightarrows}} V.⋯⇉E′′t′′⇉s′′​​E′t′⇉s′​​Et⇉s​​V.

In fact requiring injectivity is a little artifical, we could start with any endo-span s,t:Y→Xs, t : Y \to Xs,t:Y→X and get a diagram like the above. The maps here will satisfy the relation s′∘t=t′∘ss' \circ t = t' \circ ss′∘t=t′∘s, or more generally s(n+1)∘t(n)=t(n+1)∘s(n)s^{(n+1)} \circ t^{(n)} = t^{(n+1)} \circ s^{(n)}s(n+1)∘t(n)=t(n+1)∘s(n).
