Topic: ✔ when is restriction of scalars monadic?

John Baez (May 11 2023 at 02:22):

While discussing math with some people I thought of a question. Suppose you have a ring homomorphism f:A→Bf: A \to Bf:A→B. Restriction along fff gives a functor

f∗:Mod(B)→Mod(A) f^\ast: \mathsf{Mod}(B) \to \mathsf{Mod}(A)f∗:Mod(B)→Mod(A)

and this has a left adjoint, often called extension along fff:

f!:Mod(A)→Mod(B) f_!: \mathsf{Mod}(A) \to \mathsf{Mod}(B) f!​:Mod(A)→Mod(B)

Concretely we have f!(M)=B⊗AM f_!(M) = B \otimes_A M f!​(M)=B⊗A​M.

My question: when is the functor f∗:Mod(B)→Mod(A) f^\ast: \mathsf{Mod}(B) \to \mathsf{Mod}(A)f∗:Mod(B)→Mod(A) monadic?

Morgan Rogers (he/him) (May 11 2023 at 07:38):

It helps that restriction of scalars has an adjoint on both sides here - it makes checking the conditions in a monadicity theorem much easier. The answer is as Reid said: restriction of scalars is always monadic because it's always faithful (and hence conservative): restriction of scalars preserves the underlying functions so nothing gets identified in the process.

@Tobias Fritz regarding all morphisms of algebraic theories inducing monadic functors, it depends what morphisms of algebraic theories you allow. For single-sorted theories and morphisms which preserve the sort it is true, because a morphism is determined by its underlying function at the sort. However, if the sort of the theory is allowed to be sent to something more interesting (say we allow a functor between their Lawvere theories) we don't have that guarantee any more.

Simon Willerton (May 11 2023 at 08:48):

As I'm banging this particular drum at the moment, let me mention for those not familiar these ideas, that the extension of scalars can be viewed as left Kan extension.

We take Ab\mathsf{Ab}Ab to be the category of abelian groups, and take Aˉ\bar AAˉ to be the one-object Ab\mathsf{Ab}Ab-category with AAA as its hom-object. (On another day I might have notated that by BA\mathsf{B} ABA.). Then the category of left AAA-modules can be identified with a presheaf-like category of functors:

Mod(A)≅[Aˉ,Ab].\mathsf{Mod}(A) \cong [\bar A, \mathsf{Ab}].Mod(A)≅[Aˉ,Ab].

The ring homomorphsim f ⁣:A→Bf\colon A\to Bf:A→B corresponds to an Ab\mathsf{Ab}Ab-functor f ⁣:Aˉ→Bˉf\colon \bar A\to \bar Bf:Aˉ→Bˉ, and composition with fff gives the pullback functor

f∗ ⁣:[Bˉ,Ab]→[Aˉ,Ab].f^\ast\colon [\bar B, \mathsf{Ab}]\to [\bar A, \mathsf{Ab}].f∗:[Bˉ,Ab]→[Aˉ,Ab].

Left Kan extension along fff then gives the extension of scalars

f! ⁣:[Aˉ,Ab]→[Bˉ,Ab].f_! \colon [\bar A, \mathsf{Ab}]\to [\bar B, \mathsf{Ab}].f!​:[Aˉ,Ab]→[Bˉ,Ab].

Mike Shulman (May 11 2023 at 14:19):

I often find it easier to show a functor is monadic by directly comparing its domain to the category of algebras for the induced monad, rather than by mucking around with split or reflexive coequalizers. In this case an f∗f!f^*f_!f∗f!​-algebra is an AAA-module with a map B⊗AM→MB\otimes _A M \to MB⊗A​M→M satisfying some conditions, or equivalently an AAA-bilinear map (B,M)→M(B,M) \to M(B,M)→M satisfying some conditions. If you forget about AAA-bilinearity and just regard this map as Z\mathbb{Z}Z-bilinear, the conditions are precisely those that make it define a BBB-module, and then the extra AAA-bilinearity is equivalent to saying that the given AAA-module structure is the one induced from this BBB-module structure via fff.

John Baez (May 11 2023 at 20:31):

Morgan Rogers (he/him) said:

It helps that restriction of scalars has an adjoint on both sides here - it makes checking the conditions in a monadicity theorem much easier. The answer is as Reid said: restriction of scalars is always monadic because it's always faithful (and hence conservative)

It almost sounds like you're claiming faithful   ⟹  \implies⟹ conservative, so I just want to mention for beginners that this is not true. Let CCC be the walking arrow, a category with only one three morphisms, namely the nonidentity morphism f:x→yf: x \to yf:x→y and the identities on xxx and yyy. Let DDD be the walking isomorphism, a category with only four morphisms, namely three already mentioned and also an inverse f−1:y→xf^{-1} : y \to xf−1:y→x. There is a functor F:C→DF: C \to DF:C→D that includes CCC in DDD in the obvious way. This is faithful, but it's not conservative, since F(f)F(f)F(f) is an isomorphism while fff is not.
