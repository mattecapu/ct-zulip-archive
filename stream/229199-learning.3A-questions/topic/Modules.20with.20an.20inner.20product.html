John Baez (Jun 23 2025 at 20:38):

There's a lot of work on modules with bilinear forms. It's pretty easy to see that if R=C(X)R = C(X)R=C(X) is the ring of real continuous functions on a compact Hausdorff space XXX, a module MMM over RRR equipped with a nondegenerate bilinear form g:M⊗RM→Rg: M \otimes_R M \to Rg:M⊗R​M→R is the same as a real vector bundle over XXX equipped with an inner product. The only clever part is that nondegenerate here should mean that the map

♭:M→[M,R] \flat : M \to [M,R] ♭:M→[M,R]

that arises from currying ggg should be an isomorphism. Here the brackets mean the internal hom in the category of RRR-modules, and [M,R][M,R][M,R] is usually called the dual of MMM. There's a theorem that any module over any commutative ring that's isomorphic to its dual must be finitely generated and projective! Thus, we can apply Swan's theorem and conclude MMM is the module of sections of some vector bundle over XXX. The final step is to show that ggg comes from an inner product on MMM.

If this was too terse, the following equation may help:

♭(s)(t)=g(s⊗t) \flat (s) (t) = g(s \otimes t) ♭(s)(t)=g(s⊗t)

for any s,t∈Ms,t \in Ms,t∈M.

John Baez (Jun 23 2025 at 20:43):

I should just warn you that if we let RRR be the ring of complex continuous functions on XXX, the story changes, because an inner product on a complex vector bundle is not the same as a nondegenerate symmetric bilinear form on its module of sections: it corresponds to a nondegenerate sesquilinear form.

My question is, do people use this to do Riemannian geometry in an algebraic way?

John Baez (Jun 24 2025 at 08:18):

There's a kind of offshoot of Connes' approach to noncommutative geometry, which is some people (physicists mainly) using Swan's theorem to "algebraicize" differential geometry in the way we've been discussing. I can easily imagine them doing Riemannian geometry in the way I was just explaining. But I don't actually remember seeing this stuff. I worked on this back in the late 1980s but never published anything on it.

John Baez (Jun 24 2025 at 08:20):

Algebraic geometers are more interested in Kahler geometry than Riemannian geometry: that's where you have a complex variety, with its complex tangent bundle, and you put a nondegenerate sesquilinear form on that complex tangent bundle, which called a hermitian form. If it's very nice you call it a Kahler structure.
