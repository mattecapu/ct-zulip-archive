John Baez (May 29 2024 at 13:39):

For example we can look at a category internal to smooth manifolds. This will have a manifold of morphisms, say Mor\mathrm{Mor}Mor and we can do things like look at a smooth curve

γ:[0,∞)→Mor\gamma: [0,\infty) \to \mathrm{Mor}γ:[0,∞)→Mor

such that γ(0)\gamma(0)γ(0) is an identity morphism and the source of γ(t)\gamma(t)γ(t) is the same for all t∈[0,∞),t \in [0,\infty),t∈[0,∞), while the target is allowed to change. The tangent vector γ′(0)\gamma'(0)γ′(0) will be an 'infinitesimal morphism' pointing in the direction of how the morphism starts moving away from the identity at t=0t = 0t=0. γ′(t)\gamma'(t)γ′(t) at any time ttt will be the 'velocity' of the morphism at that time.

We can recover γ(t)\gamma(t)γ(t) from γ(0)\gamma(0)γ(0) and γ′(s)\gamma'(s)γ′(s) for 0≤s≤t 0 \le s \le t0≤s≤t by doing an integral. It sounds like this is what you wanted to do... or at least, one interpretation of what you wanted.

John Baez (May 29 2024 at 13:43):

All this stuff is most widely studied when we have a groupoid internal to smooth manifolds. The nicer ones of these are called [[Lie groupoids]]. People use these in differential geometry and highbrow mathematical physics.
