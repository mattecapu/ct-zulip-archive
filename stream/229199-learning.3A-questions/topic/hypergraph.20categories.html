Here's the basic idea of a hypergraph category.

When we have a wire we can "branch" it - attach it to two other wires, so that the current flowing in the original wire equals the sum of the currents flowing out the two wires going out. We can also have it "dead end" - just end it, so the current flowing down this wire is zero.

And these two operations, branching and dead-ending, obey a bunch of rules:

axioms for a special commutative frobenius monoid

And these rules are built into the definition of a hypergraph category!

Fabrizio Genovese (Nov 12 2020 at 22:43):

The very handwavy idea is that you can see polygraphs as an approach to give you free n-categories, with n going up to ∞\infty∞.

  • You can generate a set by just listing its elements. In this sense, sets coincide with their generators and are trivially free.
  • In "traditional" category theory, you can generate a free category by giving the objects and some generating morphisms. Then you add identities and you take all the possible compositions of all the generating morphisms, recursively. In practice, this amounts to specify all the possible paths on a given graph. The graph "generates the category" and if you want to be really precise you can say this neatly by saying that there is a monadic adjunction between the category of graphs and the category of categories.
  • In 2-category theory, you do the same but also specify some 2-morphisms generators. Now you see that you can't just "add all the possible compositions you can form" because 2-morphisms need to respect some (possibly up to isomorphism) equations, e.g. coherence conditions relating horizontal and vertical composition of 2-morphisms.
  • As you go up, these coherence conditions become heavier and heavier. It is impossible to explicitly list them very quickly. Already the full definition of a weak 3-category is a nightmare.
    Polygraphs are a nice tool to express, up to infinity-categories, how to form a free n-category over some generators (for 0-cells, 1-cells, all the way up to n-cells). So for instance they take automatically care of the fact that you cannot give an n-generator a→fba \xrightarrow{f} baf​b if you haven't already specified n-1 generators aaa and bbb, and so on

Directed graphs are used for studying representations of algebras, and then they are called quivers. For a quiver Γ\GammaΓ, the category of representations is the functor category Fun(Γ,Vect)\mathrm{Fun}(\Gamma,\mathbf{Vect})Fun(Γ,Vect) into the category of vector spaces, and this category is equivalent to the category of representations of the path algebra. What was the big idea mathematically behind the Grothendieck construction? Is it really a topological move? It must be an important concept to have received his name.

I've heard people say it's a surprisingly simple concept to have received his name. Of course it could be simple yet important.

I think he was considering examples very much like this: for each ring you have a category of modules, and for each homomorphism of rings you get a functor between these categories. You would like to "glue together" all these categories and get a single category where an object is a ring and a module of that ring. That's what the Grothendieck construction does.

But: in exactly the same way that Fong and Spivak related hypergraph categories in an elegant manner to the symmetric monoidal category FinCospan\mathsf{FinCospan}FinCospan of cospans between finite sets (with the "disjoint union" monoidal structure), we should be able to relate your proposed "subhypergraph" categories to the symmetric monoidal category 2Cob\mathsf{2Cob}2Cob of 2d cobordisms.
