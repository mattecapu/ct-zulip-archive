Mike Stay (Oct 01 2020 at 19:18):

Really? For example, let V = ℘(A*), the poset of sets of words in the alphabet A. This has a monoidal product • by taking all pairwise concatenations. Nathanael Arkor (Oct 01 2020 at 19:32):

The standard definition seems to require V to have finite products itself, so that a V-enriched product of XXX and YYY in C\mathbf CC is an object X×YX \times YX×Y such that there is a isomorphism C(A,X×Y)≅C(A,X)×C(A,Y)\mathbf C(A, X \times Y) \cong \mathbf C(A, X) \times \mathbf C(A, Y)C(A,X×Y)≅C(A,X)×C(A,Y) in V.

John Baez (Oct 01 2020 at 21:20):

If V is a monoidal category, there's a category VCat.
If V is braided monoidal, VCat gets to be monoidal.
If V is symmetric monoidal, VCat gets to be braided monoidal.
If V is symmetric monoidal, VCat gets to be symmetric monoidal.
If V is symmetric monoidal, VCat gets to be symmetric monoidal.

This looks a bit repetitive but the point is that we're going down one column of the periodic table; VCat is always "one up" from V in this column.

Is the notion of enriched Lawvere Theory expressible this way? The definition on the nLab mentions neither enriched products nor presheaves.

Nathanael Arkor (Oct 01 2020 at 21:47):

Enriched Lawvere theories are defined in terms of finite cotensor, rather than finite (enriched) products. John Power mentions why this is at the start of his paper Enriched Lawvere theories. Essentially, finite cotensor are the right generalisation to get a monad–theory correspondence, as in the unenriched setting.

Reid Barton (Oct 02 2020 at 01:26):

Normally something that's going to be called the product X×YX \times YX×Y should be equipped with maps (in the enriched case, maps in the "underlying category") X×Y→XX \times Y \to XX×Y→X and X×Y→YX \times Y \to YX×Y→Y, and then being the product is the statement that the particular map C(A,X×Y)→C(A,X)×C(A,Y)\mathbf{C}(A, X \times Y) \to \mathbf{C}(A, X) \times \mathbf{C}(A, Y)C(A,X×Y)→C(A,X)×C(A,Y) is an isomorphism for each AAA. In this setting, you don't need to worry about naturality in AAA because the map always exists and is natural in AAA. (Furthermore, you don't really need to assume that VVV has products, because you can rephrase the condition as "C(A,−)\mathbf{C}(A, -)C(A,−) takes the diagram to a product diagram in VVV for every AAA"--the representability form Dan mentioned. If VVV doesn't have products then it's just less likely that you will be able to find them in a VVV-enriched category.)

Reid Barton (Oct 02 2020 at 01:29):

On the other hand, you can also take "an isomorphism C(A,X×Y)→C(A,X)×C(A,Y)\mathbf{C}(A, X \times Y) \to \mathbf{C}(A, X) \times \mathbf{C}(A, Y)C(A,X×Y)→C(A,X)×C(A,Y)" as equipping X×YX \times YX×Y with the structure of the product of XXX and YYY, and reconstruct the maps X×Y→XX \times Y \to XX×Y→X and X×Y→YX \times Y \to YX×Y→Y by feeding in the identity of X×YX \times YX×Y to this isomorphism, but in that case I think the isomorphism had better be specifically VVV-natural in AAA.
