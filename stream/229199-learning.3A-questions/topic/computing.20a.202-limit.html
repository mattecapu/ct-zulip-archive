Reid Barton (Nov 05 2020 at 15:19):

The construction of the 2-limit of a pseudofunctor F : I -> Cat is pretty simple: an object consists of

  • for each object i, an object X(i) of the category F(i),
  • for each morphism of I, an isomorphism between whatever you can build out of X(i), X(j) and F,
  • for each composable pair of morphisms of I, an equation that relates the above isomorphisms.

Reid Barton (Nov 05 2020 at 15:21):

You can read this off from the definition of a limit by considering what it means to give a cone with vertex the "walking object" category ∗*∗.

John Baez (Nov 05 2020 at 19:00):

Some related simpler stuff:

Let BGBGBG be the category with one object ∗\ast∗ and the group GGG as endomorphisms.

A functor F:BG→SetF: BG \to \mathsf{Set}F:BG→Set is called a GGG-set since it's a set XXX with an action of GGG as permutations of this set.

The limit of FFF is the set of fixed points, {x∈X:gx=x}\{x \in X: gx = x\}{x∈X:gx=x}. A more fancy name for it is H0(G,X)H^0(G, X)H0(G,X), the zeroth cohomology of the group GGG with the GGG-set XXX as coefficients. In group cohomology we can define Hn(G,A)H^n(G,A)Hn(G,A) for any integer n≥0n \ge 0n≥0 and any abelian group AAA on which GGG acts, but for n=1n = 1n=1 we don't need AAA to be an abelian group and for n=0n = 0n=0 it can just be a set.

John Baez (Nov 05 2020 at 19:21):

Now let's consider a pseudofunctor F:BG→CatF: BG \to \mathsf{Cat}F:BG→Cat. This is a category XXX on which GGG has a 'weak' action: we don't demand F(g)F(h)=F(gh)F(g) F(h) = F(gh)F(g)F(h)=F(gh), all we need is natural isomorphisms ϕg,h:F(g)F(h)→F(gh)\phi_{g,h} : F(g) F(h) \to F(gh)ϕg,h​:F(g)F(h)→F(gh) obeying the obvious coherence law that makes the two isomorphisms from F(g)F(h)F(k)F(g) F(h) F(k)F(g)F(h)F(k) to F(ghk)F(ghk)F(ghk) equal.

Also, we don't demand F(1)=1XF(1) = 1_XF(1)=1X​; all we need is a natural isomorphism ϕ:1X→F(1)\phi : 1_X \to F(1)ϕ:1X​→F(1) obeying some obvious coherence law. If ϕ\phiϕ is the identity we say FFF is normalized, and I think it's pretty innocuous to impose this restriction.

Now we want to look at the pseudolimit or 'weak limit' of F:BG→CatF : BG \to \mathsf{Cat}F:BG→Cat. This could be called the category of weak fixed points of FFF, or homotopy fixed points.

Instead of objects x∈Xx \in Xx∈X such that F(g)xF(g) xF(g)x equals xxx, this category of weak fixed points will have, as objects, x∈Xx \in Xx∈X equipped with isomorphisms αg:F(g)x→x\alpha_g : F(g) x \to xαg​:F(g)x→x, obeying the obvious coherence law. This coherence law makes the two isomorphisms from F(g)F(h)xF(g) F(h) xF(g)F(h)x to xxx agree.

There will also be morphisms in the category of weak fixed points. One can again guess what these must be like, but the good news is that the pseudolimit of FFF automatically spits out the answer.

To make contact with group cohomology, we can suppose XXX is a one-object category. Since only isomorphisms matter in this game, there's no further loss of generality in assuming XXX is a one-object groupoid, say BAB ABA for some group AAA... not necessarily abelian: I'm just using the letter AAA since it's standard in group cohomology.

In this case F:BG→X=BAF: BG \to X = BAF:BG→X=BA will be just an action of GGG on the group AAA, as automorphisms.

Then we can look at the category of weak fixed points. The objects in here have a name: they are called 1-cocycles on GGG, valued in AAA. It's good to work out what they are explicitly. Two objects will be isomorphic if they are cohomologous: again one can work out what this means explicitly.

So, the set of isomorphism classes of weak fixed points, i.e. the decategorification of the pseudolimit of FFF, is called the set of cohomology classes of 1-cocycles on GGG, valued in AAA, or H1(G,A)H^1(G,A)H1(G,A) for short.

John Baez (Nov 05 2020 at 19:28):

It may seem sort of pathetic to limit our category XXX to being BAB ABA for some group AAA, but it's actually not, if all we care about is understanding the groupoid of weak fixed points of a pseudofunctor F:BG→CatF: BG \to \mathsf{Cat}F:BG→Cat. We can reduce the case of a general groupoid XXX to the case of (a bunch of) groupoids of the form BAB ABA. So, the traditional approach to this stuff, using group cohomology, is not as pathetic as it may seem... especially since people have gotten really good at actually computing things like H1(G,A)H^1(G,A)H1(G,A).

But, the more modern approach in terms of pseudolimits is more conceptual: it explains what you're 'really doing'.
