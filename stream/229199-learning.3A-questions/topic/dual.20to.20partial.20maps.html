Topic: dual to partial maps

Matteo Capucci (he/him) (Jul 05 2024 at 13:26):

A partial map X→YX \to YX→Y is a span X↩S→fYX \hookleftarrow S \xrightarrow{f} YX↩Sf​Y, where S↪XS \hookrightarrow XS↪X is its domain of definition and f:S→Yf:S \to Yf:S→Y the actual map.
One can dualize this definition: a 'copartial map' is a cospan of the form X→fQ↞YX \xrightarrow{f} Q \twoheadleftarrow YXf​Q↞Y. In Set\bf SetSet, if partial maps relax the totality condition of a function, copartial ones kinda relax single-valuedness by allowing fff to have many (non-repeating) values, squashed by the quotient Y↠QY \twoheadrightarrow QY↠Q. Of course this intuition is shaky outside of regular categories.

I wonder if anyone has seen something like this around?

Rémy Tuyéras (Jul 05 2024 at 13:51):

If you restrict your problem to Set\mathsf{Set}Set, then every surjection Q↞YQ \twoheadleftarrow YQ↞Y can be associated with its fiber map Q→P(Y)Q \to \mathcal{P}(Y)Q→P(Y) where P(Y)\mathcal{P}(Y)P(Y) is the power set on YYY. This means that your pair X→fQ↞YX \mathop{\rightarrow}\limits^{f} Q \twoheadleftarrow YX→fQ↞Y can be seen as a pair of composable functions:

X→fQ→P(Y)X \mathop{\rightarrow}\limits^{f} Q \to \mathcal{P}(Y)X→fQ→P(Y)

This potentially indicates that you might also want to look at these arrows in a Kleisli category, or in the category of idempotent commutative monoids.

Rémy Tuyéras (Jul 05 2024 at 17:22):

You have another example in Ring\mathbf{Ring}Ring with functions that try to solve the Chinese Remainder Theorem:

R→Z/pZ←Z/pqZR \to \mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z} \leftarrow \mathbb{Z}/pq\mathbb{Z}R→Z/pZ←Z/pqZ

The reason why these maps make sense is that the section Z/pZ→Z/pqZ\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Z}/pq\mathbb{Z} Z/pZ→Z/pqZ is not even a ring morphism. So the most sensical way to talk about potential solutions for the CRT in the category Ring\mathbf{Ring}Ring is by using the fibers of Z/pZ←Z/pqZ\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z} \leftarrow \mathbb{Z}/pq\mathbb{Z}Z/pZ←Z/pqZ

Rémy Tuyéras (Jul 05 2024 at 17:47):

The previous example consider maps Y→QY \to QY→Q where Y≅Q×FY \cong Q \times FY≅Q×F, so you could also look at fibrations. For example, when you have a sequence of fibrations:

⋯→E2→E1→E0→B\dots \to E_2 \to E_1 \to E_0 \to B⋯→E2​→E1​→E0​→B

then you can consider cospans of the form γ:[0,1]→B←E0\gamma:[0,1] \to B \leftarrow E_0γ:[0,1]→B←E0​ to talk about the possible liftings. Such liftings γ′:[0,1]→E0\gamma':[0,1] \to E_0γ′:[0,1]→E0​ can then be used to form another cospan

γ′:[0,1]→E0←E1\gamma':[0,1] \to E_0 \leftarrow E_1γ′:[0,1]→E0​←E1​

and so on...

Copartial maps relax the disjointness condition, but only insofar as two points in YYY are now allowed to have the same preimage - disjoint but overlapping preimages are still not allowed. This is also suggested by the approach I proposed above with the power set monad, where the only way to make the map ∩:P(Y)×P(Y)→P(Y)\cap:\mathcal{P}(Y) \times \mathcal{P}(Y) \to \mathcal{P}(Y)∩:P(Y)×P(Y)→P(Y) natural in the variable YYY would be to

  • either take the contravariant version of the power set monad P\mathcal{P}P (as opposed to what is usually done with the covariant version)
  • or consider inclusions with the covariant version of P\mathcal{P}P.

It's almost like the math would tell us to consider a setting where we look at copartial maps of the form X↪Q↞YX \hookrightarrow Q \twoheadleftarrow YX↪Q↞Y only.
