David Egolf (Feb 21 2024 at 19:13):

I tried to show that one can have a non-injective ring homomorphism between two integral domains with characteristic zero. Let i:Z→Z[x]i: \mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Z}[x]i:Z→Z[x] be the unique ring homomorphism, which sends a↦aa \mapsto aa↦a for all a∈Za \in \mathbb{Z}a∈Z. Note that Z[x]\mathbb{Z}[x]Z[x] is an integral domain with characteristic zero.

We are looking for a map π:Z[x]→Z[x]/I\pi: \mathbb{Z}[x] \to \mathbb{Z}[x]/Iπ:Z[x]→Z[x]/I for some ideal III, such that π∘i\pi \circ iπ∘i is still injective. If π∘i\pi \circ iπ∘i is injective, that means that Z[x]/I\mathbb{Z}[x]/IZ[x]/I has characteristic zero. We also want Z[x]/I\mathbb{Z}[x]/IZ[x]/I to be an integral domain. We can ensure the quotient is an integral domain by choosing III to be a prime ideal.

David Egolf (Feb 21 2024 at 19:14):

To meet these two conditions simultaneously, we pick a prime ideal of III that doesn't include any integer multiples of 1. We set I=⟨x+1⟩I= \langle x+1 \rangleI=⟨x+1⟩. This should be a prime ideal as x+1x+1x+1 is an irreducible polynomial.

We then consider Z→iZ[x]→πZ[x]/⟨x+1⟩\mathbb{Z} \to_i \mathbb{Z}[x] \to_\pi \mathbb{Z}[x]/\langle x+1 \rangleZ→i​Z[x]→π​Z[x]/⟨x+1⟩. Since the image of iii is disjoint from the kernel of π\piπ, the composite map is injective. The injectivity of π∘i\pi \circ iπ∘i implies that Z[x]/⟨x+1⟩\mathbb{Z}[x]/\langle x+1 \rangleZ[x]/⟨x+1⟩ has characteristic zero. And since ⟨x+1⟩\langle x+1 \rangle⟨x+1⟩ is a prime ideal, Z[x]/⟨x+1⟩\mathbb{Z}[x]/\langle x+1 \rangleZ[x]/⟨x+1⟩ is an integral domain.

Therefore, π:Z[x]→Z[x]/⟨x+1⟩\pi: \mathbb{Z}[x] \to \mathbb{Z}[x]/\langle x+1 \rangleπ:Z[x]→Z[x]/⟨x+1⟩ is a non-injective ring homomorphism between integral domains with characteristic zero.

(Hopefully I didn't make a mistake! I'm also not sure how this relates to the desired result for ordered integral domains).
