Testing and characteristic function

The usual way to characterise a subset V⊂YV \subset YV⊂Y is via a characteristic function χV:V→2\chi_V : V \rightarrow 2χV​:V→2. The inverse image is intensionally defined as:

f−1(V)={x∈X∣f(x)∈V} f^{-1}(V) = \{ x \in X | f(x) \in V \}f−1(V)={x∈X∣f(x)∈V}

χf−1(V)=χV∘f \chi_{f^{-1}(V)} = \chi_V \circ fχf−1(V)​=χV​∘f

So taking inverse images of subsets of YYY via fff is the same pre-composing characteristic functions with fff.

"Generating function"

Now, I wanted to look at these things from another point of view. I don't know exactly how to formalize this, but I would say that a subset UUU of XXX is "generatively defined or presented" when we have a "generating function" γU:I→U\gamma_U : I \rightarrow UγU​:I→U from some indexing set (e.g. natural numbers if UUU is countable).

I am not sure about the exact definition of a "generating function"; intuitively I would require it to be surjective at least, and I would allow it to be non-injective. All in all, I picture this function as a machine that produces elements of UUU in an exhaustive manner.

Matteo Capucci (he/him) (Apr 11 2024 at 07:22):

First, let me point out your usage of intensional and extensional is quite non-standard: both things you present are quite extensional in nature. At least as far as those words mean anything in this setting (I think of intensional as syntactic and extensional as semantical).
Anyway, I'd say what you describe is exactly the universal property of subobject classifiers: it relates 'generating functions' I→UI \to UI→U to characteristic functions U→ΩU \to \OmegaU→Ω. Two caveats. The 'generating function' has to be injective (mono), or: you must factor it through the inclusion of its image first. Second, the intuition of 'generating elements' is quite brittle in my opinion, since to claim γU\gamma_UγU​ is effectively enumerating elements of UUU requires to take seriously what 'effective' means, and a general set-theoretic function isn't effective at all. If you really want that you might want to work in the [[effective topos]].

Oscar Cunningham (Apr 14 2024 at 09:32):

Suppose a set AAA has nnn elements. Then AAA has 2n2^n2n subsets. There are also 2n2^n2n functions A→2A\to 2A→2, so it's no surprise that the subsets can be put in bijection with the characteristic functions.

But on the 'generating' side of things, the number of quotients of AAA would be the nthn\text{th}nth Bell number, which doesn't have a nice formula. So it's unlikely that there's a similar correspondence.

By the way, a related concept is exact sequences, which occur when a set can be described both by generating and by testing. The elements y of the form f(x) for some x are also the ones with g(y) = 0.

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Apr 14 2024 at 10:09):

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Apr 14 2024 at 16:15):

I will try to be more detailed than not, sorry if you already know some of these things.
The "prop of commutative [[Frobenius algebra]] s" is a monoidal category which shows up in a lot of contexts, and is a fundamental example of a “non-cartesian” algebraic theory.

Being a prop means, roughly, that its

  • objects are natural numbers,
  • the monoidal product on objects is addition

so you can take two morphisms f:n→mf: n \to mf:n→m and g:n′→m′g: n' \to m'g:n′→m′ and compose them horizontally to get a morphism f⊗g:n+n′→m+m′f \otimes g: n + n' \to m + m'f⊗g:n+n′→m+m′

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Apr 14 2024 at 16:21):

One cool fact about it is that it is equivalent to the monoidal category of 2-cobordisms: if you identify the object nnn with nnn copies of a circle, then morphisms n→mn \to mn→m are “ways of connecting nnn 'circular holes' to mmm 'circular holes' via a 2-dimensional surface”.
There is a nice book by Joachim Kock telling this story, it's called Frobenius Algebras and 2D Topological Quantum Field Theories

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Apr 14 2024 at 16:24):

But the nLab article also summarises this correspondence with pictures. This one I took from a post by John
commutative_frobenius_algebra.jpg

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Apr 14 2024 at 16:27):

The 4 cobordisms in the top row of the picture -- of type 0→10 \to 10→1, 2→12 \to 12→1, 1→01 \to 01→0, and 1→21 \to 21→2, respectively -- actually suffice to generate all the others, that is, you can assemble any cobordism (up to the appropriate notion of equality) by just assembling together copies of those 4.

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Apr 14 2024 at 16:28):

So you can ask the question: what sub-prop (sub-monoidal category) can I obtain by just using a subset of those 4?

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Apr 14 2024 at 16:30):

If you take only the first two, that is, 0→10 \to 10→1 and 2→12 \to 12→1, you get a sub-prop which is equivalent to the “prop of commutative monoids”.
Which is well-known-ish to be the equivalent to the monoidal category of finite sets and functions, with disjoint union as monoidal product.

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Apr 14 2024 at 16:30):

So: the morphisms n→mn \to mn→m that you can generate using just the 0→10 \to 10→1 and 2→12 \to 12→1 pieces correspond, bijectively, to functions from an nnn-element set to an mmm-element set.

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Apr 14 2024 at 16:31):

So you may as well call these morphisms “functions”.

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Apr 14 2024 at 16:33):

I do not know if there is a name for the morphisms generated using just the 2→12 \to 12→1 and 1→01 \to 01→0 pieces, but I was suggesting calling them “fusions” since they only allow you to “fuse” together some of the circular holes you started with into the same surface.

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Apr 14 2024 at 16:34):

Then what you have is (try it out): fusions n→0n \to 0n→0 correspond exactly to partitions of the set of nnn elements.

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Apr 14 2024 at 16:36):

My not-very-serious suggestion was:
Imagine that our familiarity with the exponential notation nmn^mnm comes from the fact that

  • we are used to thinking about functions,
  • nmn^mnm is the cardinality of the hom-set Hom(m,n)\mathrm{Hom}(m, n)Hom(m,n) in the category of finite sets and functions.

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Apr 14 2024 at 16:37):

Then in the parallel world where fusions are much better-known than functions, they defined nmn^mnm to be the cardinality of the hom-set Hom(m,n)\mathrm{Hom}(m, n)Hom(m,n) in the category of finite sets and fusions.

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Apr 14 2024 at 16:38):

And then 0n0^n0n is the nnn th Bell number.
