Ralph Sarkis (Jan 28 2021 at 22:02):

Because there is a single morphism 0→V0 \rightarrow V0→V for any vector space VVV (the trivial one), whatever you put in the corner with two well typed morphisms will make the square commute. In other words, this span does not put any restriction on the cospan (except for the endpoints) which means the colimit of this span is simply a universal cospan for AAA and BBB, i.e.: a coproduct (direct sum of vector spaces).

Conclusion : A⊗0B=A⊕BA\otimes_0B = A \oplus BA⊗0​B=A⊕B

John Baez (Jan 29 2021 at 02:50):

Eric Forgy said:

Both Set\mathsf{Set}Set and VectK\mathsf{Vect}_KVectK​ are bicomplete, but apparently the functor K[−]K[-]K[−] does not preserve limits (otherwise KKK would be terminal in VectK\mathbf{Vect}_KVectK​) :thinking:

The functor K[−]K[-]K[−] is a left adjoint: it's left adjoint to the forgetful functor VectK→Set\mathsf{Vect}_K \to \mathsf{Set}VectK​→Set.

Left adjoints preserve all colimits. So in particular K[−]K[-]K[−] preserves the initial object, and coproducts, and pushouts.

But it's very rare for them to preserve limits. And indeed K[−]K[-]K[−] does not preserves the terminal object, or products, or pullbacks.

Right adjoints, on the other hand, preserve limits. So the forgetful functor VectK→Set\mathsf{Vect}_K \to \mathsf{Set}VectK​→Set works the other way around: it preserves terminal objects, and products, and pullbacks, but not initial objects, or coproducts, or pushouts.

All this stuff should become second nature as you get used to category theory.

Okay, I'll say why. Your way to make C\mathbb{C}C into a bimodule is isomorphic to the usual where the left action is

z⊗m↦zmz \otimes m \mapsto zm z⊗m↦zm

and the right action is

m⊗z↦zmm \otimes z \mapsto z mm⊗z↦zm

The isomorphism is complex conjugation: m↦m∗m \mapsto m^\astm↦m∗ !

To see this, notice that if we apply your left action and then the isomorphism:

z⊗m↦z∗m↦(z∗m)∗=zm∗ z \otimes m \mapsto z^\ast m \mapsto (z^\ast m)^\ast = z m^\astz⊗m↦z∗m↦(z∗m)∗=zm∗

it's the same as applying the isomorphism to mmm and then applying the usual left action:

z⊗m↦z⊗m∗↦zm∗ z \otimes m \mapsto z \otimes m^\ast \mapsto z m^\ast z⊗m↦z⊗m∗↦zm∗

The same is true for the right action.
