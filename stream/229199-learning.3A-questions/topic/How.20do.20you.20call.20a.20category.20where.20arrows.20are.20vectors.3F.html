Topic: How do you call a category where arrows are vectors?

Ignat Insarov (May 14 2025 at 11:46):

Take a directed graph with edges EEE and a function h:E→Mh: E → Mh:E→M, where MMM is a vector space, or a semi-module, or a commutative monoid with 000 and +++. Now, take the reflexive and transitive closure as usual to get a free category, but also extend hhh like so:

  • send every reflexive arrow id⁡X\operatorname {id}_XidX​ to the identity element 0∈M0 ∈ M0∈M
  • send every transitive arrow g∘fg ∘ fg∘f to h(g)+h(f)h (g) + h (f)h(g)+h(f)

Now, to avoid possible confusion, consider any two arrows with the same source and target that hhh sends to the same vector to be equal. So, essentially arrows are vectors, though technically equipped with source and target assignment (just as functions are in categories of sets and functions).

Now, as seems obvious:

  • define identity arrows to be those arrows with equal source and target that hhh sends to 000
  • define composition of arrows by addition of the underlying vectors, so that h(g∘f)≔h(g)+h(f)h (g ∘ f) ≔ h (g) + h (f)h(g∘f):=h(g)+h(f)

The requirements of category are fulfilled at once, as 000 is the identity of the associative +++ in any vector, semi-module or commutative monoid.

How do I call categories so constructed? Is there any literature that describes them?

Ignat Insarov (May 14 2025 at 11:49):

David Spivak and Brendan Fong describe something similar in chapter 2 of the book Seven Sketches in Compositionality, but as far as I recall they only consider the case of a single number where I want to have a set of vectors, and the whole machinery of enrichment they spin up is not needed in my construction.

John Baez (May 14 2025 at 12:12):

This construction is in the paper @Adittya Chaudhuri and I are writing! It's probably not new. If I understand what you're talking about, it works for any monoid MMM: I don't see commutativity being used.

We can describe it like this. Any monoid MMM gives a category called its [[delooping]] BMBMBM. This has one object, one morphism for each m∈Mm \in Mm∈M, and composition is the monoid operation.

There's a forgetful functor from categories to graphs

U:Cat→Gph U : \mathsf{Cat} \to \mathsf{Gph} U:Cat→Gph

So, we also get a graph UBMU BMUBM with one vertex and one edge for each element of MMM.

Now the fun starts: a graph GGG with edges labeled by elements of MMM is the same as a map of graphs

h:G→UBM h: G \to U BMh:G→UBM

The forgetful functor UUU has a left adjoint

F:Gph→Cat F : \mathsf{Gph} \to \mathsf{Cat}F:Gph→Cat

By the way adjoints work, the map of graphs

h:G→UBM h: G \to U BMh:G→UBM

gives a functor between categories

h~:FG→BM \tilde{h} : F G \to BMh~:FG→BM

And I believe this is what you want. h~\tilde{h}h~ maps each morphism in the free category on GGG to an element of your monoid MMM, and since it's a functor it obeys

h~(g∘f)=h~(g)h~(f) \tilde{h} (g \circ f) = \tilde{h}(g) \tilde{h}(f) h~(g∘f)=h~(g)h~(f)

Matteo Capucci (he/him) (May 14 2025 at 14:23):

another perspective on this is that you're defining an enrichment in MMM; indeed such enrichments correspond to lax functors from a category (such as FGFGFG) to BMBMBM (aka [[polyad]]). since MMM is a mere monoid your lax functor is in fact strict.
