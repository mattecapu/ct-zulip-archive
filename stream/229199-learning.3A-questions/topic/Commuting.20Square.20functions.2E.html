Keith Elliott Peterson (Nov 07 2024 at 21:55):

I tried to find the expontential object for the topos Set→Set^{\rightarrow}Set→.

The solution I found was,

fg::∑h∈cod(f)cod(g)∏i∈cod(g)f−1(i)g−1(I)→cod(f)cod(g).f^g:: \sum_{h\in{\text{cod}(f)}^{\text{cod}(g)}} \prod_{i\in\text{cod}(g)} f^{-1}(i)^{g^{-1}(I)} \rightarrow {\text{cod}(f)}^{\text{cod}(g)}.fg::h∈cod(f)cod(g)∑​i∈cod(g)∏​f−1(i)g−1(I)→cod(f)cod(g).

It certainly looks the part.

I suppose this means that just as there are function sets (in SetSetSet) there is also a commuting square sets (in SetSetSet). In fact, commuting n-hypercube sets as well. Neat, but nothing surprising.

My question is, how does this extend to exponential objects in categories of presheaves for other ordinals? Specifically, SetωSet^{\omega}Setω and SetωopSet^{\omega^{op}}Setωop (and I guess of other limit ordinals as well).

There's a general formula for exponentials in presheaf categories: XY(c)=SetC(c^,XY)=SetC(Y×c^,X)X^Y(c)=\mathrm{Set}^C(\hat c,X^Y)=\mathrm{Set}^C(Y\times \hat c,X)XY(c)=SetC(c^,XY)=SetC(Y×c^,X), where X,Y:C→Set,cX,Y:C\to\mathrm{Set}, cX,Y:C→Set,c is an object of C,C,C, and c^\hat cc^ is the representable functor. For ordinals, the representables are either downward- or upward-closed chains and so Y×c^Y\times \hat cY×c^ just erases one "end" or the other of YYY; maybe you'd like to see whether you can find a conclusion to the computation that satisfies your understanding.

I agree that the general formula of exponentials in presheaf topos is practical to some extent to this particular example. Here, I would like to mention something a little more non-general phenomenon here.

Observation: An object of the topos Set→\mathrm{Set}^{\to}Set→ can be regarded as an indexed family (= formal coproduct) of sets {Xi}i∈I\{X_i\}_{i\in I}{Xi​}i∈I​. From this point of view, we have

{Xi}i∈I{Yj}j∈J=∏j∈J{XiYj}i∈I\{X_i\}_{i\in I}^{\{Y_j\}_{j\in J}} = \prod_{j\in J} \{X_i^{Y_j}\}_{i\in I}{Xi​}i∈I{Yj​}j∈J​​=∏j∈J​{XiYj​​}i∈I​.

We can generalize this observation. For a small category CCC, we can consider a new category C◃C^\triangleleftC◃ equipped with a new formal initial object. Finite ordinals are recursively obtained by n+1=n◃n+1 = n^{\triangleleft}n+1=n◃.
My claim is: PSh(C◃)\mathrm{PSh}(C^\triangleleft)PSh(C◃) is the category of families of objects of PSh(C)\mathrm{PSh}(C)PSh(C), and the exponentials in PSh(C◃)\mathrm{PSh}(C^\triangleleft)PSh(C◃) is calculated by the same formula above with the exponentials in PSh(C)\mathrm{PSh}(C)PSh(C).

(Here, we use not only the "exponential laws", but also the fact that the topos PSh(C◃)\mathrm{PSh}(C^{\triangleleft})PSh(C◃) is stably locally connected. This ensures that the exponential functor −X ⁣:E→E{-}^X\colon \mathcal{E}\to \mathcal{E}−X:E→E for a connected object XXX preserves coproducts.)

This allows us to calculate exponentials in PSh(n)\mathrm{PSh}(n)PSh(n) inductively.
Even for ω\omegaω, we can calculate exponentials with this formula from lower index n∈ωn\in \omegan∈ω. Exponential at index n∈ωn\in \omegan∈ω does not depend on data of bigger indices m>nm>nm>n since the forgetful functor PSh(ω)→PSh(n)\mathrm{PSh}(\omega) \to \mathrm{PSh}(n)PSh(ω)→PSh(n) preserves exponentials (since this is a sheafification functor for an open subtopos).

This method get stuck for ω\omegaω since ω◃=ω\omega^{\triangleleft}= \omegaω◃=ω.
