Mike Shulman (May 16 2023 at 01:21):

As you may know, doing this for abelian groups is tricky, because if an Ab-enriched category has finite products (equivalently, finite coproducts), then its Ab-enrichment is uniquely determined. So any such example must lack finite products and coproducts. Which would be the case for your suggestion of using the one-object case. That doesn't really help me come up with an example, though.

Mike Shulman (May 16 2023 at 01:22):

Another way of stating the one-object version is: find two rings whose multiplicative monoids are isomorphic, but for which the isomorphism doesn't preserve addition. Somehow that version sounds more approachable to me...

JS PL (he/him) (May 16 2023 at 01:23):

Also, no matter the enrichment, the zero maps will always be the same. So if you're looking for a monoid that has more than one addition which makes it into a ring, the zero element will be the same for both addition operations.

Reid Barton (May 16 2023 at 06:31):

Take a subfield RRR of R\mathbb{R}R which contains all nnn-th roots of all of its positive elements (e.g., RRR = the real algebraic numbers). Its positive elements R>0R_{> 0}R>0​, as a multiplicative group, form a Q\mathbb{Q}Q-vector space (because each element is uniquely divisible). We get the whole multiplicative monoid of RRR by taking R×=R>0×{±1}R^{\times} = R_{> 0} \times \{\pm 1\}R×=R>0​×{±1} (as groups) and then adjoining an absorbing zero element.

The dimension of R>0R_{> 0}R>0​ and hence the isomorphism type of the resulting monoid RRR can only depend on the cardinality of R>0R_{> 0}R>0​. So for example any two such countable fields will have isomorphic multiplicative monoids, but we can make them nonisomorphic as fields. For example R1R_1R1​ = the real algebraic numbers, R2R_2R2​ = the real numbers algebraic over Q(π)\mathbb{Q}(\pi)Q(π). (In R2R_2R2​, not every element is algebraic over the prime field.)

This feels overkill; I feel there should be a simpler example out there...

Tobias Fritz (May 16 2023 at 07:42):

For potentially simpler examples, consider a unique factorization domain RRR: disregarding zero, the multiplicative monoid is isomorphic to R××NIR^\times \times \mathbb{N}^IR××NI, where R×R^\timesR× is the group of units and III is the set of prime elements modulo units. So two UFDs have isomorphic multiplicative monoids as soon as they have isomorphic groups of units and the same number of primes modulo units.

A simple example could be Z\mathbb{Z}Z and (Z/3)[X](\mathbb{Z}/3)[X](Z/3)[X], both of which have the two-element group as the group of units and countably many classes of primes. So they have isomorphic multiplicative monoids, but they're clearly not isomorphic as rings. for example because Z\mathbb{Z}Z is initiial in the category of rings while (Z/3)[X](\mathbb{Z}/3)[X](Z/3)[X] is not.

Tobias Fritz (May 16 2023 at 07:54):

MathOverflow even has Non isomorphic finite rings with isomorphic additive and multiplicative structure. The question notes that polynomial rings F[X]F[X]F[X] and F[X,Y]F[X,Y]F[X,Y] over the same field FFF have isomorphic additive groups and isomorphic multiplicative monoids, but are not isomorphic as rings!
