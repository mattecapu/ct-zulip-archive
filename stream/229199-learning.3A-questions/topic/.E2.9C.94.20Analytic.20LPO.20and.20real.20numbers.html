Topic: ✔ Analytic LPO and real numbers

Madeleine Birchfield (Jan 18 2024 at 04:19):

In constructive mathematics, does the analytic LPO, which states that the Dedekind real numbers have decidable equality, imply that the Dedekind real numbers are equivalent to the Cauchy real numbers, which are equivalence classes of Cauchy sequences of rationals?

Madeleine Birchfield (Jan 18 2024 at 04:51):

The answer to this question is yes. Suppose that the Dedekind real numbers have decidable equality. This implies that the negation of equality x≠yx \neq yx=y is a tight apartness relation, which by definition of ordered field implies that

x≠y  ⟺  (x<y)∨(y<x)x \neq y \iff (x \lt y) \vee (y \lt x)x=y⟺(x<y)∨(y<x)

Thus, we have for all x,y∈Rx, y \in \mathbb{R}x,y∈R that

(x<y)∨(x=y)∨(x>y)(x \lt y) \vee (x = y) \vee (x \gt y)(x<y)∨(x=y)∨(x>y)

and that (x<y)∨¬(x<y)(x \lt y) \vee \neg (x \lt y)(x<y)∨¬(x<y).

Auke Booij proved in theorem 6.10.3 of his thesis https://etheses.bham.ac.uk/id/eprint/10411/7/Booij2020PhD.pdf about real analysis that for every Cauchy real number there exists a locator. In dependent type theory, a locator for a real number xx x is an element of the dependent product

∏y:R∏z:R(y<z)→(y<x)+(x<z)\prod_{y:\mathbb{R}} \prod_{z:\mathbb{R}} (y \lt z) \to (y \lt x) + (x \lt z)y:R∏​z:R∏​(y<z)→(y<x)+(x<z)

Then, since (x<y)∨¬(x<y)(x \lt y) \vee \neg (x \lt y)(x<y)∨¬(x<y) for all real numbers xxx and yyy we could just adapt the proof of Lemma 6.1.3 in his thesis to construct a locator for every Dedekind real number, which by theorem 6.10.3 then implies that every Dedekind real number is a Cauchy real number. Since the Cauchy real numbers embed into the Dedekind real numbers, this implies that the Dedekind and Cauchy real numbers are the same.

Madeleine Birchfield (Jan 18 2024 at 05:28):

Also, I guess I just assumed that if the negation ¬P\neg P¬P of a proposition PPP is decidable, then the proposition itself is decidable. But that isn't true; the double negation ¬¬P\neg \neg P¬¬P of any proposition PPP is always decidable, but ¬P\neg P¬P is in general not provable to be decidable.

And that's why there are different versions of the analytic LPO for apartness and for equality.
