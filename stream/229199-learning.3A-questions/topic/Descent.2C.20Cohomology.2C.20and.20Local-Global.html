John Baez (Jul 31 2024 at 09:58):

For what it's worth, this concept is a generalization of [[localization]] in ring theory, where we take a ring and get a new ring by decreeing that a certain set of elements are invertible - i.e., by throwing in formal inverses of these elements. In algebraic geometry this is a way of chopping out part of an affine scheme and thus working "locally" in the rest of this affine scheme - that's the origin of the term "local". But can do localization not just for rings, for any monoid. And then you can do it for it categories. And eventually it becomes what you're talking about.

Todd Trimble (Aug 24 2024 at 11:57):

Along lines similar to what John suggests, one source of examples is sheaves as local objects in a category of presheaves. This is relatively easy to explain in the classical case of (set-valued) sheaves over a topological space, but it readily generalizes to the fancier case of sheaves with respect to a Grothendieck topology.

Recall that a presheaf on a topological space XXX is a functor O(X)op→Set\mathcal{O}(X)^{op} \to \mathsf{Set}O(X)op→Set where O(X)\mathcal{O}(X)O(X) is the category of open subsets and inclusions between them. A morphism between presheaves is a natural transformation between such functors. For the class SSS of presheaf morphisms you want to localize with respect to, take inclusions of the form

C↪hom⁡(−,U)C \hookrightarrow \hom(-, U)C↪hom(−,U)

where CCC is the presheaf obtained as a coequalizer attached to a covering {Ui}\{U_i\}{Ui​} of a given open set UUU:

∑i,jhom⁡(−,Ui∩Uj)⇉∑ihom⁡(−,Ui)→C.\sum_{i, j} \hom(-, U_i \cap U_j) \rightrightarrows \sum_i \hom(-, U_i) \to C.∑i,j​hom(−,Ui​∩Uj​)⇉∑i​hom(−,Ui​)→C.

In case it's unclear what those two parallel arrows are, they are defined so that their restrictions to a typical summand hom⁡(−,Ui∩Uj)\hom(-, U_i \cap U_j)hom(−,Ui​∩Uj​) are induced by the inclusions Ui∩Uj↪UiU_i \cap U_j \hookrightarrow U_iUi​∩Uj​↪Ui​, Ui∩Uj↪UjU_i \cap U_j \hookrightarrow U_jUi​∩Uj​↪Uj​:

hom⁡(−,Ui∩Uj)↪hom⁡(−,Ui)↪∑ihom⁡(−,Ui),\hom(-, U_i \cap U_j) \hookrightarrow \hom(-, U_i) \hookrightarrow \sum_i \hom(-, U_i),hom(−,Ui​∩Uj​)↪hom(−,Ui​)↪∑i​hom(−,Ui​),

hom⁡(−,Ui∩Uj)↪hom⁡(−,Uj)↪∑ihom⁡(−,Ui).\hom(-, U_i \cap U_j) \hookrightarrow \hom(-, U_j) \hookrightarrow \sum_i \hom(-, U_i).hom(−,Ui​∩Uj​)↪hom(−,Uj​)↪∑i​hom(−,Ui​).

What's going on here is that given a presheaf FFF, a natural transformation σ:C→F\sigma: C \to Fσ:C→F is precisely tantamount to a compatible family of sections si∈F(Ui)s_i \in F(U_i)si​∈F(Ui​). The sis_isi​ arise from the composition

∑ihom⁡(−,Ui)→C→σF\sum_i \hom(-, U_i) \to C \overset{\sigma}{\to} F∑i​hom(−,Ui​)→C→σF

whose restriction to a typical summand hom⁡(−,Ui)\hom(-, U_i)hom(−,Ui​) is a transformation hom⁡(−,Ui)→F\hom(-, U_i) \to Fhom(−,Ui​)→F; by the Yoneda lemma, this corresponds to an element si∈F(Ui)s_i \in F(U_i)si​∈F(Ui​). The fact that the map ∑ihom⁡(−,Ui)→C→σF\sum_i \hom(-, U_i) \to C \overset{\sigma}{\to} F∑i​hom(−,Ui​)→C→σF must coequalize the two parallel arrows gives you precisely the compatibility condition.

Thus, the sheaf condition -- for all coverings {Ui}\{U_i\}{Ui​} of open sets UUU, the set F(U)F(U)F(U) is the equalizer

F(U)→∏iF(Ui)⇉∏i,jF(Ui∩Uj)F(U) \to \prod_i F(U_i) \rightrightarrows \prod_{i, j} F(U_i \cap U_j)F(U)→∏i​F(Ui​)⇉∏i,j​F(Ui​∩Uj​)

-- is neatly encapsulated by realizing that a priori, this equalizer is really the set of transformations [O(X)op,Set](C,F)[\mathcal{O}(X)^{op}, \mathsf{Set}](C, F)[O(X)op,Set](C,F), and F(U)≅[O(X)op,Set](hom⁡(−,U),F)F(U) \cong [\mathcal{O}(X)^{op}, \mathsf{Set}](\hom(-, U), F)F(U)≅[O(X)op,Set](hom(−,U),F) (again by Yoneda), so the sheaf condition is saying that homming any SSS-morphism C→hom⁡(−,U)C \to \hom(-, U)C→hom(−,U) into FFF produces an isomorphism

[O(X)op,Set](hom⁡(−,U),F)→[O(X)op,Set](C,F)[\mathcal{O}(X)^{op}, \mathsf{Set}](\hom(-, U), F) \to [\mathcal{O}(X)^{op}, \mathsf{Set}](C, F)[O(X)op,Set](hom(−,U),F)→[O(X)op,Set](C,F).

(In the more general case of a Grothendieck topology j:Ω→Ωj: \Omega \to \Omegaj:Ω→Ω, the sheaf condition similarly says that sheaves are local objects with respect to "jjj-dense inclusions". It takes a bit of mental energy to work through this.)

What a coincidence, I've just watched this lecture (Laurent Lafforgue, on topos theory).

He does spend some time discussing inclusion of topos. It is defined as a geometric morphism (f∗,f∗):EU→EX(f^*, f_*) : \mathcal{E}_U \to \mathcal{E}_X(f∗,f∗​):EU​→EX​ such that the direct image functor f∗f_*f∗​ is fully faithful.

He justifies this definition by mentioning the case of sheaf toposes of topological spaces: an inclusion of topological space X⊆YX \subseteq YX⊆Y yields a geometric morphism Sh(X)→Sh(Y)\text{Sh}(X) \to \text{Sh}(Y)Sh(X)→Sh(Y) whose direct image functor is fully faithful.
