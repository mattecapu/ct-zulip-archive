Topic: How do I call this equivalence of arrows?

Ignat Insarov (Apr 16 2025 at 19:31):

Consider the equation g=f∘ag = f ∘ ag=f∘a. In some sense it tells us that ggg is longer than fff, or at least as long as fff. This can be formalized like so:

  1. Take the category of arrows and commutative squares over an ambient category C\mathcal CC.
  2. Given two arrows ggg and fff in C\mathcal CC, squish all commutative squares of form g∘id⁡=b∘fg ∘ \operatorname {id} = b ∘ fg∘id=b∘f and of form id⁡∘g=f∘a\operatorname {id} ∘ g = f ∘ aid∘g=f∘a into the symbol g≤fg ≤ fg≤f.
  3. Take the transitive closure of the relation defined by ≤≤≤, for good measure. It is automatically reflexive because f∘id⁡=id⁡∘ff ∘ \operatorname {id} = \operatorname {id} ∘ ff∘id=id∘f.

Equivalently, g≤f≔∃ab.g=b∘f∘ag ≤ f ≔ ∃ab. g = b ∘ f ∘ ag≤f:=∃ab.g=b∘f∘a.

Thus we attain a preorder on arrows of C\mathcal CC. This preorder has the equivalence fragment ≡≡≡ where g≡f≔g≤f∧f≤gg ≡ f ≔ g ≤ f ∧ f ≤ gg≡f:=g≤f∧f≤g. This is indeed an equivalence. It is symmetric by symmetry of ∧∧∧, reflexive because ≤≤≤ is, and it is transitive like so:

h≡g∧g≡fh≤g∧g≤h∧g≤f∧f≤gh≤f∧f≤hh≡fh ≡ g ∧ g ≡ f \\ h ≤ g ∧ g ≤ h ∧ g ≤ f ∧ f ≤ g \\ h ≤ f ∧ f ≤ h \\ h ≡ fh≡g∧g≡fh≤g∧g≤h∧g≤f∧f≤gh≤f∧f≤hh≡f

For a concrete category, this notion is weaker than set theoretic pointwise equality of functions, and yet it captures what we care about. Does this equivalence have a name? And is the relation ≤≤≤ good for anything?

What is a graph of a profunctor?

Matteo Capucci (he/him) (Apr 17 2025 at 14:38):

every profunctor P:A→BP:\cal A \to BP:A→B corresponds to a [[two-sided fibration]] A←∫P→B{\cal A} \leftarrow \int P \to \cal BA←∫P→B where ∫P\int P∫P has objects triples (a,b,p∈P(a,b))(a,b,p \in P(a,b))(a,b,p∈P(a,b)) and as maps (a,b,p)→(a′,b′,p′)(a,b,p) \to (a',b',p')(a,b,p)→(a′,b′,p′) pairs (f:a→a′,g:b′→b)(f:a \to a',g:b' \to b)(f:a→a′,g:b′→b) such that P(f,g)(p′)=pP(f,g)(p') = pP(f,g)(p′)=p.
