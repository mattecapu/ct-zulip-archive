Madeleine Birchfield (Dec 07 2024 at 18:27):

One example, in an (∞,1)(\infty, 1)(∞,1)-topos, the unique morphism from the coproduct 1∐11 \coprod 11∐1 to the terminal object 111 is an effective epimorphism but not an epimorphism. The former fact follows from the fact that the both 1∐11 \coprod 11∐1 and 111 is 0-truncated and the unique map is an effective epimorphism in the 1-topos of 0-truncated objects, and Buchholtz et al showed the latter fact using the internal language of the (∞,1)(\infty, 1)(∞,1)-topos in the introduction of this paper.

I don't agree with this statement, but it's a matter of terminology.

It seems uncontroversial that the monos in an ∞\infty∞-category are the maps f:A→Bf:A\to Bf:A→B for which the map hom⁡(X,A)→hom⁡(X,B)\hom(X,A)\to\hom(X,B)hom(X,A)→hom(X,B) between homotopy types "000-truncated", ie an "embedding".

For me the most reasonable definition of epi in an ∞\infty∞-category is obtained by dualizing that: fff is an epi iff hom⁡(f,X)\hom(f,X)hom(f,X) is an embedding for all XXX.

It is true that this conflicts with Lurie's notion of effective epi in ∞\infty∞-toposes : these are the maps that are the quotients of their Cech nerves, equivalently those that are right orthogonal to embeddings.

But it is not clear whether this notion is meaningful in arbitrary ∞\infty∞-categories, and in my opinion the idea that epis are dual to monos is so fundamental that it should be carried over to ∞\infty∞-categories, and Lurie's "effective epis" should be called "covers" , "surjections" (between h-types)), or "quotient maps" instead.

So in my opinion, 2→12\to 12→1 is not an epi in eg in the ∞\infty∞-category S\mathcal{S}S of homotopy types, because δA:A→A2\delta_A:A\to A^2δA​:A→A2 is not a mono in general, contrary to Set\mathsf{Set}Set.
