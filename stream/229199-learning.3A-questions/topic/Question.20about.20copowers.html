Topic: Question about copowers

Bruno Gavranović (Nov 26 2023 at 13:56):

Let V\mathcal{V}V be a monoidal closed category. Let C\mathcal{C}C be a V\mathcal{V}V-category.

Then, a copower of of C\mathcal{C}C over V\mathcal{V}V is defined to be a functor

∙:V×C→C\bullet : \mathcal{V} \times \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{C}∙:V×C→C

such that V‾(A,C(B,C))≅C(A∙B,C)\underline{\mathcal{V}}(A, \mathcal{C}(B, C)) \cong \mathcal{C}(A \bullet B, C)V​(A,C(B,C))≅C(A∙B,C) holds naturally in all variables, where V‾\underline{\mathcal{V}}V​ represents the internal hom of V\mathcal{V}V. This is according to the nLab page on copowers and Coend calculus book(Def. 2.2.3).
But isn't this wrong?

C\mathcal{C}C isn't a (Set\mathbf{Set}Set-)category, so we can't have a functor ∙\bullet∙ - only a V\mathcal{V}V-functor.

This seems like an innocuous mistake, so I set out to look online for a reference which actually states that ∙\bullet∙ is a V\mathcal{V}V-functor explicitly. To my big suprise, I couldn't.

I'm pretty sure that it has to be, because otherwise the definition doesn't make sense, but out of an abundance of caution I want to ask about it here. I'd be surprised either way - either I got something very wrong or there's no easily found reference which states that ∙\bullet∙ is a V\mathcal{V}V-functor :)

Reid Barton (Nov 26 2023 at 14:37):

If C\mathcal{C}C is a V\mathcal{V}V-category then it is an ordinary category, via Hom(X,Y)=V(1,C(X,Y))\mathrm{Hom}(X,Y) = \mathcal{V}(1, \mathcal{C}(X, Y))Hom(X,Y)=V(1,C(X,Y)).

Reid Barton (Nov 26 2023 at 14:46):

This notion of tensored category comes up in model category theory and in that setting it's common to regard an "enriched model category" as an ordinary category together with extra enrichment/tensored/cotensored structure, each of which determines the other two. So in that case, the tensor is viewed as an ordinary (bi)functor between ordinary categories, and in fact, it can be taken as the primary structure, with the enrichment derived from it.

Reid Barton (Nov 26 2023 at 14:56):

If you're really not assuming that V\mathcal{V}V is symmetric monoidal, then what I wrote above still works, but (as far as I know) the tensor product can't be a V\mathcal{V}V-functor, because there is no tensor product of V\mathcal{V}V-categories to serve as its domain.

Reid Barton (Nov 26 2023 at 15:02):

Assuming you want V\mathcal{V}V to be symmetric monoidal, then it's also possible to stay entirely within the world of V\mathcal{V}V categories and to define the tensor A⊗BA \otimes BA⊗B (A∈VA \in \mathcal{V}A∈V, B∈CB \in \mathcal{C}B∈C) by its universal property, as a weighted colimit. I assume it is also automatically V\mathcal{V}V-functorial in AAA and BBB.

Reid Barton (Nov 26 2023 at 15:18):

So in summary, I would say that this notion of "tensoring over a category" mostly comes up when we indeed regard C\mathcal{C}C primarily as an ordinary category, rather than as a V\mathcal{V}V-category.

Todd Trimble (Nov 27 2023 at 13:06):

To resolve this question, what you need to do is keep in mind what Kelly says earlier in the book. This is a special case of indexed or weighted limits. Earlier, where he discusses generalities of indexed limits/colimits, he says explicitly that taking indexed limits/colimits is VVV-functorial: see the top of page 39.

Also read carefully the paragraph at the top of page 11 on terminological and notational conventions. If Kelly had meant "ordinary functor", he either would have said so or indicated so through his choice of notation.

Mike Shulman (Nov 28 2023 at 15:38):

You don't need a braiding either, not just to define copowers (although making them VVV-functorial is a different question). Copowers in a non-symmetric monoidal category are sketched in section 6 of my paper Contravariance through enrichment; I don't know offhand of another reference specifically about monoidal categories, but Street's paper "Absolute colimits in enriched categories" does the even more general case of enriching over a bicategory.
