Topic: Domains of naturality

Tobias Fritz (Jul 14 2025 at 23:56):

I'm interested in understanding in what ways a non-natural transformation between functors can fail to be natural. Or put differently, what are the possible domains on which naturality can hold?

Tobias Fritz (Jul 14 2025 at 23:56):

To formulate the question precisely, let C\mathsf{C}C be any category. Then for any other category D\mathsf{D}D and functors F,G:C→DF, G : \mathsf{C} \to \mathsf{D}F,G:C→D, let's say that the domain of naturality of a not necessarily natural transformation t:F⇒Gt : F \Rightarrow Gt:F⇒G is the class of morphisms in C\mathsf{C}C with respect to which ttt is natural. Obviously this class is a subcategory, and it is wide (contains all identity morphisms). Now my question is, which wide subcategories can arise as domains of naturality?

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Jul 15 2025 at 05:24):

Sorry if this is a too trivial observation, but domains of naturality in your sense should correspond exactly to functors that arise as pullbacks in Cat\mathrm{Cat}Cat of the evident inclusions

Arr(C)↪Arrnc(C)\mathrm{Arr}(\mathsf{C}) \hookrightarrow \mathrm{Arr}_{nc}(\mathsf{C})Arr(C)↪Arrnc​(C)

of the category of arrows and commutative squares into the category of arrows and not necessarily commutative squares in C\mathsf{C}C. So it seems to me like a good strategy would be to focus on these as “universal” domains of naturality, and then properties of arbitrary domains of naturality would be exactly those that are pullback-stable.

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Jul 15 2025 at 05:30):

(In particular this implies that the pasting law for pullbacks applies to domains of naturality, i.e. the pullback of a domain of naturality along an arbitrary functor is a domain of naturality, and if you have a domain of naturality such that the pullback square exhibiting it factors through another pullback square of categories, then the functor “in the middle” of the factorisation is also a domain of naturality.)

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Jul 15 2025 at 07:05):

Another semi-trivial observation would be that, given a wide subcategory W\mathsf{W}W of C\mathsf{C}C, one can concoct a "minimal" transformation whose domain of naturality contains it.

We let I□WCI \square_\mathsf{W} \mathsf{C}I□W​C be

  • the "funny tensor product" of the arrow with C\mathsf{C}C, which is generated by two copies of C\mathsf{C}C labelled 0 and 1, together with morphisms ta:(a,0)→(a,1)t_a: (a, 0) \to (a, 1)ta​:(a,0)→(a,1) for each object aaa in C\mathsf{C}C,
  • quotiented by the relation tb∘(f,0)=(f,1)∘tat_b \circ (f, 0) = (f, 1) \circ t_atb​∘(f,0)=(f,1)∘ta​ for all morphisms f:a→bf: a \to bf:a→b in W\mathsf{W}W.

Then there is an evident transformation from the inclusion (−,0)(-, 0)(−,0) to the inclusion (−,1)(-, 1)(−,1), such that its domain of naturality contains W\mathsf{W}W, and this must be the smallest such domain of naturality.

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Jul 15 2025 at 07:09):

Then the following are equivalent:

  • the smallest naturality domain containing W\mathsf{W}W is the whole category,
  • the canonical functor I□WC→I×CI \square_\mathsf{W} \mathsf{C} \to I \times \mathsf{C}I□W​C→I×C is an isomorphism

Morgan Rogers (he/him) (Jul 17 2025 at 06:30):

To expand on Amar's pullback law condition:
Suppose for f∈Cf\in Cf∈C you have a jointly epic collection of morphisms in NNN each of which stays in NNN when postcomposed with fff; then fff will be in NNN too.
This does nothing for your example, though: if a map is discontinuous at some point then any jointly epimorphic family will have discontinuous preimages of the point.
