John Baez (Jun 04 2020 at 16:49):

"I was in the cafeteria and some guy, fooling around, throws a plate in the air. As the plate went up in the air I saw it wobble, and I noticed the red medallion of Cornell on the plate going around. It was pretty obvious to me that the medallion went around faster than the wobbling. I had nothing to do, so I start figuring out the motion of the rotating plate. I discovered that when the angle is very slight, the medallion rotates twice as fast as the wobble rate—two to one. It came out of a complicated equation! I went on to work out equations for wobbles. Then I thought about how the electron orbits start to move in relativity. Then there's the Dirac equation in electrodynamics. And then quantum electrodynamics. And before I knew it… the whole business that I got the Nobel prize for came from that piddling around with the wobbling plate."

The significance of teaching was brought into figural salience for all to see by Professor F. William Lawvere. In this context, it may be relevant to consider Grassmann’s theory of pedagogy, along with the role of foundational concepts and the not quite a happy history of MathEd.

I think of teaching as learning-with-students. After every lecture / course, I ask: how did my understanding of the subject change as a result of teaching the same subject yet again; did I learn anything more? I write term papers along with the students; they evaluate mine as I evaluate (rewrite) theirs.
(my 5mBTC ;)

P.S. I thought of adding something closer to our home turf: a number, say 1969, can be thought of as a function with places and values as domain and codomain sets, respectively. Functions, in turn, can be thought of as a subcategory of graphs (target = source). With numbers as loops, we have an interesting situation from the Lawvere & Schanuel Objective Number Theory with analogs in graphs, all of which reminded me of Professor Charles Wells: The way you can word a definition affects the properties of the thing defined.
