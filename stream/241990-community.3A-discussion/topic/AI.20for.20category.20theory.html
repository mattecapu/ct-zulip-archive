Then here's how it actually answered my question:

Okay, let's break down the idea of Quillen homology for a monoid.

In essence, Quillen homology is a way to assign a sequence of abelian groups (homology groups) Hₙ(M) to a monoid M, extending the familiar concept of group homology (Eilenberg-MacLane homology) from groups to monoids. It uses techniques from algebraic topology (specifically, simplicial methods) to probe the algebraic structure of the monoid.

Here are the key ideas:

  • Motivation: Generalizing Group Homology:

    • For a group G, Eilenberg-MacLane homology Hₙ(G, ℤ) provides important algebraic invariants. H₁(G, ℤ) is the abelianization of G (G/[G,G]), H₂(G, ℤ) relates to presentations and group extensions (Schur multiplier), and higher groups capture more subtle information.

    • Monoids are like groups but without inverses. It's natural to ask if we can define analogous invariants for them.

  • Quillen's Approach: Simplicial Resolutions and Abelianization:

    • Quillen's general method for defining homology for various algebraic structures (like rings, Lie algebras, and monoids) involves using simplicial resolutions.

    • Step 1: Resolution: Find a special kind of "resolution" of the monoid M. This isn't a resolution by modules, but a simplicial monoid F_* which is "freely" generated in some sense (technically, cofibrant in a model category structure on simplicial monoids) and maps to M (viewed as a constant simplicial monoid) via a weak equivalence (roughly, inducing isomorphisms on homotopy groups π₀, π₁...). Think of F_* as a sequence of monoids F₀, F₁, F₂, ... connected by face and degeneracy maps, encoding M in a homotopically meaningful way using "freer" building blocks.

    • Step 2: Abelianization: Apply the abelianization functor dimension-wise to this simplicial resolution F_. The abelianization M<sub>ab</sub> of a monoid M is the universal abelian group receiving a homomorphism from M (obtained by forcing mn=nm for all m,n ∈ M). Applying this gives a simplicial abelian group Ab(F_).

    • Step 3: Homotopy Groups: The Quillen homology groups Hₙ(M) (often denoted Qₙ(M)) are defined as the homotopy groups of the resulting simplicial abelian group Ab(F_):
      Hₙ(M) := πₙ(Ab(F_))

  • Connection to Eilenberg-MacLane Homology:

    • A crucial property is that if the monoid M is actually a group G, then Quillen homology Hₙ(G) coincides with the standard Eilenberg-MacLane homology Hₙ(G, ℤ).

    • This means Quillen homology is a genuine generalization. The standard bar construction used for group homology can be seen as arising from a specific simplicial resolution within this framework.

  • Low-Dimensional Interpretations:

    • H₀(M): This is typically isomorphic to ℤ, the integers (assuming M is non-empty), similar to H₀ for groups. It reflects the "connected component".

    • H₁(M): This is the abelianization of the monoid M, denoted M<sub>ab</sub>. This is analogous to H₁(G, ℤ) = G<sub>ab</sub> for a group G.

    • H₂(M): Like H₂(G, ℤ), this group relates to "relations" or "syzygies" within the monoid, analogous to Hopf's formula for groups, providing information beyond simple abelianization.

  • Significance:

    • Provides a robust homological theory for monoids.

    • Offers deeper invariants for studying monoid structure than simple approaches.

    • Connects monoid theory to techniques from simplicial homotopy theory.

    • Has connections (though sometimes indirect) to algebraic K-theory, which was a major motivation for Quillen's work in homology of algebraic structures.

In simple terms: Quillen homology uses tools from topology (simplicial objects, homotopy groups) applied to an algebraic construction (abelianization of a free resolution) to extract sophisticated algebraic invariants (homology groups) from a monoid, generalizing what we already do for groups.

Yes, you are absolutely right! The Dold-Kan correspondence provides an equivalence between the category of simplicial abelian groups and the category of non-negatively graded chain complexes of abelian groups.

Applying this to the definition of Quillen homology:

  • Starting Point: Hₙ(M) = πₙ(Ab(F_)), where F_ → M is a cofibrant simplicial monoid resolution of M.

  • Standard Resolution: A standard and convenient choice for F_* is the simplicial monoid derived from the free monoid functor F: Set → Mon and the forgetful functor U: Mon → Set. Explicitly, (F_*)ₙ = F(Mⁿ⁺¹), where Mⁿ⁺¹ is the (n+1)-fold Cartesian product of the underlying set of M, and F(S) denotes the free monoid on a set S. The face and degeneracy maps are induced functorially by the standard simplicial structure on M<sup>•+1</sup> (projections, diagonals, and multiplications within M).

  • Simplicial Abelian Group: Applying the abelianization functor Ab dimension-wise gives a simplicial abelian group A_* = Ab(F_*). Since the abelianization of a free monoid F(S) is the free abelian group ℤ[S] on the set S, we have:
    Aₙ = Ab(F(Mⁿ⁺¹)) = ℤ[Mⁿ⁺¹]
    This is the free abelian group generated by (n+1)-tuples (m₀, m₁, ..., mₙ) of elements from M.

  • Dold-Kan Chain Complex: The Dold-Kan theorem associates a chain complex C_(A_) to the simplicial abelian group A_. The homology groups of this chain complex are precisely the homotopy groups of A_: Hₙ(C_(A_)) ≅ πₙ(A_*).
    There are two standard versions of this chain complex:

    • Unnormalized Complex: Often easier to write down.

    • Normalized Complex: Sometimes computationally simpler, obtained by quotienting out degenerate chains.
      Crucially, both complexes have the same homology groups.

Explicit Description of the (Unnormalized) Chain Complex:

Let Cₙ(M) = ℤ[Mⁿ⁺¹], the free abelian group generated by symbols (m₀, m₁, ..., mₙ), where mᵢ ∈ M.

The boundary map ∂ : Cₙ(M) → Cₙ₋₁(M) is defined as the alternating sum of face maps ∂ = ∑ᵢ<0xC2><0xA0>(-1)ⁱ dᵢ, where the face maps dᵢ : Cₙ(M) → Cₙ₋₁(M) are induced by the simplicial structure maps of A_* = Ab(F(M<sup>•+1</sup>)). They act on the basis elements as follows:

  • d₀(m₀, m₁, ..., mₙ) = (m₁, ..., mₙ)

  • dᵢ(m₀, m₁, ..., mₙ) = (m₀, ..., mᵢ₋₁mᵢ, ..., mₙ) for 1 ≤ i ≤ n

(Here, mᵢ₋₁mᵢ denotes the product within the monoid M).
The Quillen homology groups of the monoid M are the homology groups of this chain complex C_*(M):
Hₙ(M) ≅ Hₙ(C_*(M), ∂)
