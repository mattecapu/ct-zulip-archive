♀ A feminist perspective ♀
(Women, both cis and trans, please correct me if I’m wrong. I’m describing this situation in good faith, but I know I don’t have the lived experience. I’m sharing what some women have told me and the concerns they’ve raised, and I want to do so without mansplaining)

I completely understand your (I am talking to the whole male community) hesitation about implementing an AI exam, as the administrative burden and the potential for new, automated biases are very real concerns. It makes sense to be cautious about replacing human judgment with an algorithm.

However, I'd love to offer a slightly different perspective on the current endorsement system, particularly the idea that "false negatives" are rare. That assumption overlooks the severe structural barriers inherent in network-based gatekeeping.

The current system relies heavily on a small pool of established gatekeepers for career advancement and endorsements. As physicist Angela Collier details in her discussion on sexual harassment in astronomy and physics (minute 13 of the video), this power structure can easily trap early-career researchers. When marginalized scholars -particularly women- face harassment, reporting or fleeing an abusive mentor often means permanently losing access to the exact professional network required to get an arXiv endorsement. These scholars aren't falling through the cracks because they lack "Ramanujan"-level genius; they are frequently pushed out by unsafe power dynamics, making these "false negatives" invisible to the established network rather than rare.

This is precisely where an AI exam could serve as a powerful tool for equity. While relying on an algorithm might feel cold or impersonal, it effectively bypasses the subjective "old boys' network." An AI evaluator does not care who your advisor is, whether you attended the right after-hours conference socials, or if you had to leave a prestigious lab to escape a hostile environment. It evaluates the work on its own merits.

For many women and marginalized researchers, having an objective, automated threshold to clear -even an imperfect one- is actually far less "dehumanizing" than being forced to beg for subjective validation from a social network that historically excludes them. While your practical critiques of the AI exam are completely valid, it might be worth considering it not as a burden on the establishment, but as a much-needed safety valve for competent researchers who are simply locked out of the room.

Angela Collier, Sexual harassment and assault in Astronomy and Physics

Furthermore, female students in this position often face stereotype threat or imposter syndrome, which can severely complicate their academic progression and their ability to self-advocate in a heavily skewed power structure.

​Female Professor / Male Student
In this cross-cutting dynamic, the professor's institutional authority conflicts directly with traditional societal gender hierarchies, where the male student holds baseline systemic privilege. Additionally, female professors navigating this dynamic often face implicit expectations to perform uncompensated emotional labor or adopt a "maternal" mentoring style, rather than being evaluated and respected purely for their academic rigor.

​Male Professor / Male Student
This relationship is characterized by a traditional institutional hierarchy that is heavily smoothed by shared male socialization, effectively neutralizing gender as a point of friction. Because authority figures naturally tend to gravitate toward subordinates who reflect their own identities, this dynamic frequently benefits from "homosocial reproduction" or the "old boys' club" effect. Consequently, male students in this dyad are statistically more likely to receive active career sponsorship, informal networking opportunities, and relaxed professional boundaries that can rapidly and disproportionately accelerate their academic trajectories.

​Female Professor / Female Student
This dyad is defined by hierarchical solidarity, where the professor's institutional power is often buffered by the shared lived experience of navigating heavily patriarchal academic systems. While this dynamic frequently yields strong, empathetic, and highly supportive mentorship, it carries its own unique set of risks, most notably the blurring of professional boundaries due to assumed in-group solidarity. Conversely, it can sometimes trigger the "Queen Bee" syndrome, where a female professor enforces excessively harsh standards on her female students under the misguided belief that she must "toughen them up" to survive in a male-dominated field.
