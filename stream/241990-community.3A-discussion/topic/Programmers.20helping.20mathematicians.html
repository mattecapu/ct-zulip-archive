Emilio Minichiello (Dec 16 2024 at 22:11):

I think there is something really interesting here. I don't know if I would call this mathematical etiquette, but I've had many attempted mathematical conversations with people who were not trained as PhD mathematicians that left me annoyed or frustrated. Now I've certainly done the same to others countless times, but upon reflection, I think there are a couple of really key points that can make mathematical conversations go better. Here are some of my thoughts, I'm curious what other people think.

Tips for talking with a mathematician:

  1. Be as rigorous as possible. Know what mathematical objects you are talking about. Give them letters. My advisor used to drill this into me as a student, we used to go for walks and I would have to explain to him what I had learned just using my voice. This was an incredibly helpful practice for me. I would actually practice saying out loud "Let M be a finite dimensional smooth manifold such that ..." In other words, know how to make a mathematical statement.
    Example: "I learned that if you've got a space, then at every point there's an R^n attached to it" versus "I learned that if M is an n-dimensional smooth manifold, then at every point x in M you can define a vector space T_x, called the tangent space at x, defined as ..."

  2. Don't bullshit. If you don't know something, or don't understand it well enough to talk about at a level of rigor, just say that and figure out what common ground of understanding you can find.

  3. Keep it short and focused. In my experience, mathematicians sometimes tend to be rather terse, especially when talking about mathematics. If a rigorous discussion is flooded with extra irrelevant remarks or the topic keeps switching, then it may be hard to follow.
  4. Hedge, but don't unnecessarily hedge. Instead of saying "it may possibly be the fact, even though I'm really not sure, and you probably have a much better understanding than me but..." just say "i'm not sure if this is true, but ..." This helps out a lot with point 3.
  5. If you make a mathematical claim, be prepared to sketch a proof of it, or provide a reference. It can be annoying for somebody to make a bunch of claims but not remember where they had heard it from or have any idea why its true.
  6. Don't interrupt if the other person is speaking. Even if you are excited, let them finish their thought before interjecting.
  7. Make sure you are all on the same page. Take breaks to ask if the other person understands what you are saying or if they have any questions. If you get lost at some point, say so.

David Egolf (Dec 17 2024 at 01:49):

I'll list below some things I've found helpful in communicating with mathematicians in text format, coming from an engineering background. I've formatted the list as if I was giving advice to my younger self:

  • Try to make it easy for mathematicians to engage with what you say! If you want to talk about a possible application to engineering things, resist the temptation to describe your application in technical detail. At least, don't do this right away!
  • Again, make it easy for mathematicians to engage with what you say. One way to do this is to ask a broad/intuitive/conceptual question, ideally paired with a short example that illustrates a possible specific context for discussion. Providing an example you understand will make it more likely that you will understand the resulting discussion.
  • Don't mix vague intuition and precise examples in a confusing way. Make it clear that you understand (or don't understand!) a mathematical argument, while keeping this separate from concerns or questions you have at a more vague conceptual level.
  • People - mathematicians included - like to explain things they understand and think are interesting and helpful. So if a mathematician takes your topic of interest on what looks like a tangent, try to view this as an opportunity to learn and not an inconvenient distraction. The "tangent" may have more relevance than you suspect! But be willing to re-centre a discussion, too.
  • Try to copy standard notation and conventions. Continually consider if there are any small ways in which you can communicate more clearly.
  • If you get lost when trying to communicate to a mathematician, don't despair. Instead try to explain to yourself exactly how you are lost, ideally with an example. Often this will help you get un-lost. And if you are still lost, you will be in a better place to ask the mathematician clarifying questions.
  • You want to make it interesting and rewarding for mathematicians to engage with what you say to them. Thank them for their time and effort, even if you can't provide a response that engages more directly with the technical aspects of what they just said. Try to show mathematicians who talk to you that you spend time and energy thinking about what they said. This can double as a great way to further an interesting conversation.
  • Do not overstate how much you understand something. Don't do this when talking to others, and don't do it when talking to yourself. Recognize that learning math is a long road, and that often you won't be as far along as you could wish you were.
  • Recognize that you will make mistakes, and make it clear that you are grateful to have them pointed out. That being said, try to make clear and correct comments. Proofread what you say.
  • Recognize that you will not only make minor mistakes: you will make major conceptual mistakes. These will likely call into question your entire approach to some application or area of interest. Don't panic or despair when this happens, and instead be happy that you've made such a big step forward in your understanding.
  • Try to create conversational settings (either through your own initiative, or through your cooperation with the initiative of someone else) where there is a specific goal. This helps mathematicians have a clearer idea of what to say to you, which makes it more likely they will engage with what you are saying. Solving exercises or "puzzles" can be an excellent way to do this. Sometimes a mathematician will state a precise puzzle on their own, but don't feel afraid to state a precise puzzle yourself and ask for help with it. Recognize that your efforts to create conversational will not always succeed, and that is okay.
  • Recognize that a mathematical proof in practice is just something that convinces mathematicians. Try to not worry about making things "rigorous" unless a mathematician tells you that you need to worry about this more than you are. At the same time, try to ensure that any proof you write is clear and compelling to you.
  • Overall, try to view conversation with mathematicians as a wonderful and exciting thing! Approach conversations with enthusiasm, and be willing to put a lot of work in - it's worth the effort.

David Egolf (Dec 17 2024 at 02:04):

Oh - one last thing comes to mind:

  • Recognize that examples and concepts are often more "primitive" than definitions or theorems. They are what lead to definitions/theorems, and what illustrate definitions/theorems. So it's often a good idea to move a discussion to a specific example of interest.
