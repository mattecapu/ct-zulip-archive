John Baez (Nov 24 2024 at 00:33):

Here's a very nice classification problem in category theory: classify all Lawvere theories for which all finitely generated models are free. There turn out to be 6 kinds. Instead of describing the Lawvere theories syntactically, I'll just say what their models are:

  • sets
  • pointed sets
  • sets where all elements are equal
  • pointed sets where all elements are equal
  • affine spaces over a chosen division ring
  • pointed affine spaces over a chosen division ring

The last one is usually described as "modules over a chosen division ring", and if the division ring is commutative they're usually called "vector spaces over a chosen field". But I wanted to emphasize the parallels.

This classification theorem is apparently not at all trivial.

By the way, I forgot that even within classical mathematics there's a very nice theory of which classifications can be 'Borel reduced' to which other classifications. This allows us to make precise the idea that some classifications are harder than others:

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Dec 16 2024 at 21:22):

Sorry to change the topic, but there is a conjecture that I have been throwing at people for a while and I think would also make a nice thesis project, but I don't think I will ever want to tackle myself.
I will give it in the case of symmetric monoidal categories because it involves more familiar structures, but there are also similar versions for bicategories and (planar) monoidal categories.

For the purposes of this conjecture, let Prop\mathsf{Prop}Prop be the category whose

  • objects are colored [[prop]] s,
  • morphisms f:T→Sf: T \to Sf:T→S are "monoidal functors" that send colors of TTT to colors of SSS
    (it may be that one needs a slightly different notion of morphism, in the case of bicategories that I have thought about the most I recall that to be the case)

Write an object of a colored prop as a list (a1,…,an)(a_1, \ldots, a_n)(a1​,…,an​) of colors.
A representable prop is a colored prop where

  • every binary object (a,b)(a, b)(a,b) is isomorphic to a unary object (a⊗b)(a \otimes b)(a⊗b),
  • the unit i.e. the zero-ary object ()()() is isomorphic to a unary object (i)(i)(i).
    It is a folklore result stated e.g. by Shulman that representable props and their morphisms are equivalent in an appropriate sense to symmetric monoidal categories and strong symmetric monoidal functors.

There is also an adjunction between Prop\mathsf{Prop}Prop and the category of symmetric strict monoidal categories (i.e. "permutative categories") and their strict symmetric monoidal functors, where the left adjoint is the "free ssmc on a prop", and the right adjoint takes a ssmc to a prop whose colors are all the objects of the ssmc. Let S\mathrm{S}S be the monad on Prop\mathsf{Prop}Prop induced by this adjunction.

The conjecture is the following:

There exists a model structure on Prop\mathsf{Prop}Prop such that

  • all objects are cofibrant,
  • the fibrant objects are the representable props, so in particular the full subcategory on the fibrant objects is equivalent to the category of symmetric monoidal categories and strong symmetric monoidal functors,
  • the weak equivalences between fibrant objects are exactly the symmetric monoidal equivalences between the corresponding symmetric monoidal categories,
  • the unit of S\mathrm{S}S is a fibrant replacement; in particular, when applied to an object which is already fibrant, we recover that the inclusion of a smc into its strictification is a symmetric monoidal equivalence.

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Dec 16 2024 at 21:26):

In addition to giving a kind of "homotopical" footing to strictification of monoidal categories, as I mentioned in another thread a while ago, I think this kind of result would be useful in giving some systematic tools for transporting constructions which are "easy on prop-like objects" (strict, with objects freely generated...) to "weak" structures; for example, pass from the tensor product of props to the tensor product of symmetric monoidal categories.
