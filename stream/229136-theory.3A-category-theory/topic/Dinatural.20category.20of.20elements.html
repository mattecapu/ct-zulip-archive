Topic: Dinatural category of elements

Patrick Nicodemus (Mar 26 2025 at 14:28):

A colleague of mine (who I'm not sure is on this server, so I'm asking on their behalf) came up with/discovered an interesting construction.

Let F:C×Cop→SetsF : \mathcal{C}\times\mathcal{C}^{\rm op}\to\mathbf{Sets}F:C×Cop→Sets be a functor. The "dinatural category of elements" of FFF is the category whose objects are pairs (c,x)(c, x)(c,x) with x∈F(c,c)x\in F(c,c)x∈F(c,c). A morphism (c,x)→(d,y)(c,x)\to (d,y)(c,x)→(d,y) is f:c→df : c\to df:c→d such that F(f,1)(x)=F(1,f)(y)F(f,1)(x) = F(1,f)(y)F(f,1)(x)=F(1,f)(y). Composition and identities are clear. This is equipped with a projection functor π:el(F)→C\pi : \mathrm{el}(F)\to Cπ:el(F)→C, but it is apparently not a discrete fibration like the usual category of elements. The connected components of el(F)\mathrm{el}(F)el(F) correspond to elements of the coend of FFF, similarly to how the colimit of a presheaf is the set of connected components of its category of elements. The set of sections of π\piπ in Cat\mathbf{Cat}Cat correpond to elements of the end of FFF, similar to how sections of the discrete fibration of the category of elements of a presheaf correspond to elements of the limit of FFF.

Can others elaborate on this? Does it have a name?

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Mar 26 2025 at 15:11):

Just another way you can see this category.
In general, given a functor π:C→D\pi: \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{D}π:C→D, one can consider a category of sections of π\piπ, whose

  • objects are sections of π\piπ, that is, functors s:D→Cs: \mathcal{D} \to \mathcal{C}s:D→C such that π∘s=idD\pi \circ s = \mathrm{id}_\mathcal{D}π∘s=idD​,
  • morphisms from sss to ttt are natural transformations α:s⇒t\alpha: s \Rightarrow tα:s⇒t such that, for all objects ddd of D\mathcal{D}D, π(αd)=idd\pi(\alpha_d) = \mathrm{id}_dπ(αd​)=idd​.

Let N\mathbb{N}N denote the monoid of natural numbers with addition, seen as a category with a single object.
An endo-profunctor FFF on C\mathcal{C}C determines and can be reconstructed from a functor πF:CF→N\pi_F: \mathcal{C}_F \to \mathbb{N}πF​:CF​→N, where

  • CF\mathcal{C}_FCF​ is obtained by adjoining a morphism x:c→dx: c \to dx:c→d to C\mathcal{C}C for every element x∈F(c,d)x \in F(c, d)x∈F(c,d), and quotienting by the equations gxf=ygxf = ygxf=y whenever F(g,f)(x)=yF(g, f)(x) = yF(g,f)(x)=y,
  • πF\pi_FπF​ is defined by πF(f)=0\pi_F(f) = 0πF​(f)=0 if fff is a morphism in C\mathcal{C}C and πF(x)=1\pi_F(x) = 1πF​(x)=1 if xxx is an element of the profunctor.

Then an equivalent description of your "dinatural category of elements" is the category of sections of πF\pi_FπF​.

Mike Shulman (Mar 26 2025 at 16:16):

I think the "standard" construction that this is most closely related to is the "two-sided category of elements", which associates to any profunctor C↛DC\nrightarrow DC↛D a [[two-sided discrete fibration]] C←E→DC \leftarrow E \to DC←E→D, which is a fibration over DDD and an opfibration over CCC, compatibly, and discrete over C×DC\times DC×D (but not over CCC or DDD individually). Then if it should happen that C=DC=DC=D, you can pull back along the diagonal C→C×CC\to C\times CC→C×C and I think you get your construction.

anuyts (Oct 13 2025 at 15:23):

Patrick Nicodemus said:

The connected components of el(F)\mathrm{el}(F)el(F) correspond to elements of the coend of FFF, similarly to how the colimit of a presheaf is the set of connected components of its category of elements.

I think this is not true. A morphism f:c→df : c \to df:c→d in C\mathcal CC is sent by the endoprofunctor FFF to a commuting square in Set. The coend will only identify elements of F(c,c)F(c, c)F(c,c) and F(d,d)F(d, d)F(d,d) if they come from the same element of F(c,d)F(c, d)F(c,d), which is a stronger condition than asking that they map to the same element in F(d,c)F(d, c)F(d,c), which is the condition to be in the same connected component of this dinatural category of elements.
