Topic: representability of multirepresentable functors

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Apr 24 2021 at 13:00):

(This is more of a question-out-of-curiosity, not something I need for research)

One way of saying that a category has (III-indexed) products is to say that the functor ∏i∈IHom(−,Xi)\prod_{i\in I} \mathrm{Hom}(-,X_i)∏i∈I​Hom(−,Xi​) is representable for all III-indexed families of objects {Xi}i∈I\{X_i\}_{i \in I}{Xi​}i∈I​. So this kind of representability is fairly common.

What happens if we ask, instead, that ∑i∈IHom(−,Xi)\sum_{i\in I} \mathrm{Hom}(-,X_i)∑i∈I​Hom(−,Xi​) be representable?
I don't think I've really met this “in the wild”. Are there “reasonable” examples of categories where this holds?

These functors are called “multirepresentable” so the question can be rephrased as “what are examples of categories where multirepresentability implies representability?”.

Amar Hadzihasanovic (Apr 24 2021 at 18:39):

Oh, that's right, thanks Mike!

There is something that still puzzles me, as I found the existence of non-trivial examples at least intuitively plausible. A logical interpretation of such a representing object would be a proposition SSS whose each proof is a proof of exactly one of the XiX_iXi​, which sounds like a propositions-as-types interpretation of a generalised exclusive or. This feels like a somewhat “classical” connective so I was expecting it to have quite degenerate categorical semantics... but at least some semantics.
The issue seems to be that having a natural isomorphism forces a “uniform” choice of which one of the XiX_iXi​ is proven, which in turn would force all the other XjX_jXj​ to “have no proofs in any context whatsoever”, which is impossible because every proposition can be proven from itself.

So I'd like to amend the question and ask if there are (weaker) ways to give consistent categorical semantics to the intuition above?

That's not what it sounds like to me. For an exclusive or I would expect a proof to be something like a proof of one XiX_iXi​ together with a refutation of all the others. (And that's exactly what it is, in a Chu construction.)

To me, "a proposition SSS whose each proof is a proof of exactly one of the XiX_iXi​" sounds like a description of a disjoint coproduct. This doesn't quite match your proposed universal property, but I would say the fault lies with the latter: it fails take into account that the hypotheses of such a proof might also involve disjunctions, so that a proof might involve case splits that choose a different one of the XiX_iXi​'s to prove in each branch.

But now I think there may be examples that form semicategories, i.e. non-unital categories. One such toy example would have sets as objects, and functions whose image is a single element as morphisms. This is not a subcategory of Set\mathbf{Set}Set because the only identity it can contain is the one on a single-element set, but it is closed under composition, so it is a sub-semicategory.
