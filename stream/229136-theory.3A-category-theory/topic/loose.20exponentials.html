Topic: loose exponentials

Matteo Capucci (he/him) (Aug 17 2023 at 21:53):

I have a monoidal double category D\mathbf DD whose loose arrows 'form' a closed bicategory. That is, loose arrows A→B⊗CA \to B \otimes CA→B⊗C are in bijection with loose arrows A→[B,C]A \to [B, C]A→[B,C] for a certain object [B,C][B, C][B,C] which I call the 'loose exponential'. Crucially, this is not a closed structure for the monoidal category of tight arrows.

The chief examples are Rel and Prof, where one actually has a coclosed structure too. Notice [B,C]=B×C[B, C] = B \times C[B,C]=B×C in Rel, so you can see this is quite different from CBC^BCB, the 'tight exponential'.

I'm not completely sure how to express this structure, in particular what is exactly the universal property (I said above there is a bijection, but I suspect one could improve this to take into account tight morphisms as well). Before spending time fiddling with it I figured someone here might know something. Does anybody know if 'loose exponentials' have been studied before?

Mike Shulman (Aug 18 2023 at 16:10):

I don't know if there is a more double-categorical version of loose monoidal closedness. But there is a more double-categorical version of loose compact closedness! Unfortunately I don't think it has been written down in the literature anywhere, but @Christian Williams and I had some ideas about a way to formulate it, at least when the double category is fibrant and hence equivalent to a proarrow equipment. In the compact closed case, instead of a two-variable internal-hom functor you have a one-variable "dual" functor Dlop→D\mathbb{D}^{lop} \to \mathbb{D}Dlop→D which should be vertically covariant and strong monoidal, and then there should be bijections of squales like the one you drew but with Bo⊗CB^o \otimes CBo⊗C on the right. But it's not entirely clear what coherence axioms should be assumed of such bijections.

Mike Shulman (Aug 18 2023 at 16:13):

However, if you change from a fibrant double category to a proarrow equipment (−)∙:K→Map(M)(-)_\bullet : \mathcal{K} \to\mathrm{Map}(\mathcal{M})(−)∙​:K→Map(M) (where Map denotes the sub-bicategory of left adjoints), then you can say that the structure of (−)o(-)^o(−)o should be a [[duality involution]] on K\mathcal{K}K, a compact-closed structure on M\mathcal{M}M which therefore induces a duality involution on Map(M)\mathrm{Map}(\mathcal{M})Map(M), and that (−)∙(-)_\bullet(−)∙​ is a morphism of duality involutions.
