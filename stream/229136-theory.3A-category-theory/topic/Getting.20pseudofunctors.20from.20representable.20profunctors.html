Anyway, yes, there is something to figure out how to state and prove rigorously here, and it's not as obvious as it might appear. For instance, if a given 1-cell fff in a bicategory is invertible, then the choice of an inverse ggg and isomorphisms fg≅1f g \cong 1fg≅1 and gf≅1g f \cong 1gf≅1 is not unique. I am wondering if this pattern works in general. If we replace bicategories with weak nnn categories, in a setting where we have a notion of Grothendieck construction / tabulation, given a lift of the 0-cells of CCC to ∫F\int F∫F, and given fully coherent weak n−1n-1n−1 equivalences for the local functors πC\pi_CπC​, I think it's plausible that these local n−1n-1n−1-equivalences (which are all chosen independently with no relationships between them) glue together into a global weak nnn-functor in a unique way (i.e., all remaining coherence isomorphisms are uniquely determined by the structure of the local equivalences)

If we sum this up as "representing n-functors for n-profunctors are determined by choosing independently a family of 0-cells with n-universal properties and then (n-1) local adjoint equivalences", then the k vs n-k idea I mentioned earlier then follows by induction on this principle, in a sense. That is, to construct an (n-1)-dimensional adjoint equivalence, it suffices to choose where to send the 0-cells, and then construct an independent family of (n-2)-dimensional local adjoint equivalences, and so on. As long as you keep choosing the lifts for cells at stage kkk to have the desired n−kn-kn−k universal property, you can keep going, and your remaining choices form an (n−k)(n-k)(n−k)-dimensional contractible groupoid.
