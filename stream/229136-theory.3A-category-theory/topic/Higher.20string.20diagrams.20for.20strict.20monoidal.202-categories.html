John Baez (Mar 12 2026 at 15:48):

Stabilization has essentially nothing to do with isomorphisms becoming equations. It's more related to isomorphisms obeying more equations. For example, when we stabilize and go from braided to symmetric monoidal categories, the braiding isomorphism does not become an equation; instead, it obeys a new equation.

John Baez (Mar 12 2026 at 15:54):

But when I say "equation" here, that's because in a category, it's not too strict to say 1-morphisms are isomorphisms, and obey equations. If we were in a higher category we'd have to adjust what I said. For example, when we stabilize once and go from braided to sylleptic monoidal bicategories, the braiding equivalence gets equipped with a new iso-2-morphism, the "syllepsis". Then when we stabilize again and go from sylleptic to symmetric monoidal bicategories, the syllepsis obeys a new equation. But nothing is ever strict, i.e. evil.

John Baez (Mar 12 2026 at 15:56):

What's true is that in the strict world stabilization happens in just two steps! You get strict n-categories, strict monoidal n-categories, and then strict commutative monoidal n-categories. That's it.

Simon Burton (Mar 12 2026 at 18:02):

Arrow diagrams (globular diagrams) have the objects as zero dimensional, 1-morphisms are 1d and 2-morphisms are 2-d, etc., and string diagrams dualize this: objects are now 2d, 1-morphisms are 1d and 2-morphisms are 0d.

Simon Burton (Mar 12 2026 at 18:03):

So a category here is 2d, a functor is a 1d line sepearating regions, and a natural transform is a 0d point separating two lines.
