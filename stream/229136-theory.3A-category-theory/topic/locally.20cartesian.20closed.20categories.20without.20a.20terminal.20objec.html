Emily Riehl (Jun 21 2026 at 22:55):

I'm in the process of revising my category theory book. You can see the current text of the second edition on my website. (At some point later I'd repost a PDF of the first edition.)

While I'm trying not to add too many new pages, I have added one new section on locally cartesian closed categories. Readers are very welcome, especially if they can give me comments before August.

In writing a motivating example, I realized that the category of sets and monomorphisms is locally cartesian closed... except that it doesn't have a terminal object (or products). It seems like most people use the term "locally cartesian closed" for the case with a terminal object and I like following the dominant convention. But is there a non-horrible name that I can use to refer to the case where I'm just asking for pullbacks and their adjoints? Some of the results in this section apply more generally and it would be cleaner to state them at that level of generality. But I'm also trying not to be too technical, so perhaps I should just give this up...

Nathanael Arkor (Jun 22 2026 at 06:42):

I'd like to suggest that including a terminal object in the definition of "locally cartesian closed" is not the dominant usage. From what I can tell, the two conventions are roughly equal. I spent ten minutes skimming references on Google Scholar, and found plenty of recent references (here, "later" = "since Awodey's textbook") that take a locally cartesian category to be one without a terminal object. Here is a sample of papers using the convention without a terminal object (in no particular order, just the one Google Scholar gave me for searching for "locally cartesian closed category"):

  • Convenient Categories of Smooth Spaces (2008)
  • The Interpretation of Intuitionistic Type Theory in Locally Cartesian Closed Categories an Intuitionistic Perspective
  • Local fibred right adjoints are polynomial (2010)
  • Data types with symmetries and polynomial functors over groupoids (2012)
  • Algebraic Exponentiation in General Categorie (2011)
  • Locally cartesian closed categories, coalgebras, and containers (2013)
  • Formalizing Category Theory in Agda (2021)
  • Locally Cartesian Closed Categories (2022)
  • Terms and derivatives of polynomial functors via negation (2022)
  • Homotopical models of UF and their relationship to infty-topoi
  • Completeness and Cocompleteness Transfer for Internal Group Objects with Geometric Obstructions (2025)

(Furthermore, I found several papers with definitions of the form "a category with finite limits is locally cartesian closed", leaving open the possibility also of defining local cartesian closure differently for arbitrary categories.)

Nathanael Arkor (Jun 22 2026 at 06:45):

I therefore think it would not be going against the dominant usage not to assume "locally cartesian closed" includes a terminal object. I think it is more useful not to include a terminal object in the definition, both because the terminology is more consistent this way ("locally" properly being used only to refer to the behaviour of slice categories, not global structure), and because there are plenty of examples of LCCCs without terminal objects.

If a locally cartesian closed category admits a terminal object, then it is itself (globally) cartesian closed: taking 'locally cartesian closed' to mean that every slice is cartesian closed, with C≅C/1C \cong C / 1C≅C/1 you immediately see this.

I don't have time to find more examples right now, although Peter Johnstone's most recent email on the categories mailing list gives evidence that the convention exists. To quote him:

When I wrote the first two parts of the Elephant, I thought quite a lot about terminological questions like this. For "locally cartesian closed", I decided to break the usual rule that "locally P" means "all slices have property P" but not that the category itself has P. The reason was that there is, as far as I know, only one not-wholly-artificial example of a category whose slices are all cartesian closed but which is not itself cc (namely the category of all topological spaces and local homeomorphisms); and it didn't seem worth burdening oneself with more complicated terminology for the sake of this one counterexample.

According to Johnstone's own reasoning, now that we have a number of "un-artifical" examples, it would be better to follow "the usual rule" and not impose the existence of the terminal object.

Morgan Rogers (he/him) (Jun 25 2026 at 15:12):

Just to weigh in on another instance of "locally" (and at risk of muddling the water), the standard (site-theoretic rather than syntactic) version of Deligne's theorem proves that the topos of sheaves on a locally coherent site, meaning a a site whose slices are coherent categories and whose covers are (generated by) finite families, has enough points. This is quite nice from the point of view that disjoint unions of coherent categories are locally coherent in this sense, and makes apparent that the presence or absence of the terminal object in the site is irrelevant to the proof.

Unfortunately, there is a naming convention in topos theory which attaches the same adjective to the topos as the descriptor of the site it is built from. Thus a regular topos is one equivalent to (regular) sheaves on a regular category, a coherent topos is equivalent to (coherent) sheaves on a coherent category etc. If this convention were to be followed, that would mean that Deligne's theorem describes "locally coherent toposes", which are not slice-wise coherent but rather have "enough" slices which are coherent toposes (namely, the slices over representable sheaves). In this vein, I have seen toposes described as "locally P" if they are covered by slices having property P. This clashes with other naming conventions frequently. Most notably "locally compact" has a topological meaning not at all captured by a slice-wise definition. Hence the chosen title for my paper that Nathanael referenced above...

All of that said, I think as a property of categories in full generality, reserving "locally" for "slice-wise" is a sensible choice that can be adequately defended from encounters with topological notions of locality.
