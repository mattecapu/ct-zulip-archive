Topic: strict localizations

Jonas Frey (Dec 13 2022 at 23:19):

Is there anything written about "strict" localizations, ie the universal solution to mapping a set of arrows in a category to identities?

Specifically I'm looking for references for the following two statements.

  1. 222-categorical universal property: given a small category C\mathbb CC and a set III of arrows in C\mathbb CC, there exists a functor E:C→L(C,I)E:\mathbb C\to L(\mathbb C, I)E:C→L(C,I) which which sends III-maps to identities, and such that for every category X\mathbb XX, precomposition with EEE induces an isomorphism of categories between [L(C,I),X][L(\mathbb C, I),\mathbb X][L(C,I),X] and the full subcategory of [C,X][\mathbb C,\mathbb X][C,X] on functors sending III-maps to identities.
  2. Given a diagram D:J→CatD:\mathbb J\to\mathbf{Cat}D:J→Cat of small categories, colim(D)\mathsf{colim}(D)colim(D) can be computed as strict localization of ∫D\int D∫D (the covariant Grothendieck construction) at the cocartesian arrows.

Thanks!

Jonas Frey (Dec 14 2022 at 01:31):

I'm trying to come up with a slick argument that for D:J→CatD:\mathbb J\to \mathbf{Cat}D:J→Cat, the canonical arrow E:∫D→colim(D)E : \int D\to\mathsf{colim} (D)E:∫D→colim(D) is final. One can show that

  • a functor FFF is final whenever it is "co-fully faithful", ie precomposition by FFF is fully faithful, and
  • a strict localization functor is co-fully faithful whenever it satisfies the 2-dimensional universal property.

It remains to show that the localization by cocartesian arrows is actually the colimit, but I think that's straightforward by writing out the definitions.

From this, one gets a nice proof of the colimit decomposition formula [1], which says that for C:J→CatC:J\to \mathsf{Cat}C:J→Cat and D:colim(C)→XD:\mathsf{colim}(C)\to\mathbb XD:colim(C)→X with X\mathbb XX cocomplete, one has

colim(D)=colimj∈Jcolimc∈CjDσj(c)\mathsf{colim}(D) = \mathsf{colim}_{j\in J}\mathsf{colim}_{c\in C_j} D_{\sigma_j(c)}colim(D)=colimj∈J​colimc∈Cj​​Dσj​(c)​,

where σj:Cj→colim(C)\sigma_j:C_j\to \mathsf{colim}(C)σj​:Cj​→colim(C) is the colimit injection.

The proof is as follows: We have colim(D)=colim(D∘E)\mathsf{colim}(D) = \mathsf{colim}(D\circ E)colim(D)=colim(D∘E) since EEE is final, and this colimit can be computed by left-Kan-extending along ∫C→J→1\int C\to J\to 1∫C→J→1. By this , the first Kan-extension is computed by taking colimits over fibers, which recovers exactly the stated formula.

[1] Peschke, George, and Walter Tholen. "Diagrams, fibrations, and the decomposition of colimits." arXiv preprint arXiv:2006.10890 (2020).

In case anybody is reading this, one correction: colim(D)\mathsf{colim}(D)colim(D) is not the "strict localization" (better: coidentifier) of ∫D\int D∫D at all cocartesian arrows, but at the cocartesian arrows of a split cleavage of the split opfibration ∫D→J\int D\to \mathbb J∫D→J. Contracting all cocartesian arrows would kill all the automorphisms, which is obviously wrong.
