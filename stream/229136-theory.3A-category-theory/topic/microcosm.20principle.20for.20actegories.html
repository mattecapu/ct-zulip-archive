Topic: microcosm principle for actegories

Bruno Gavranović (Apr 02 2021 at 18:30):

Hi, @Jules Hedges here writing from Bruno's laptop trying to survive on a Croatian keyboard. Let MMM be a monoidal category. There's a 2-category M−ActM\mathrm{-Act}M−Act whose morphisms are MMM-actegories and morphisms are functors that preserve the action. Is there a natural way to view M−ActM\mathrm{-Act}M−Act itself as a (large) MMM-actegory?

The first thought is that for an MMM-actegory C\mathcal CC and an object m:Mm : Mm:M, define the MMM-actegory m⋅Cm \cdot \mathcal Cm⋅C to have the same underlying category C\mathcal CC, and the action given by m′⋅x=m′⋅(m⋅x)=(m′⊗m)⋅xm' \cdot x = m' \cdot (m \cdot x) = (m' \otimes m) \cdot xm′⋅x=m′⋅(m⋅x)=(m′⊗m)⋅x. Unfortunately this appears to not satisfy the condition to define an actegory. Does anyone know anything about this?

Mike Shulman (Apr 02 2021 at 19:00):

Not exactly an answer to your question, but I would say the application of the "microcosm principle" to actegories is that if CCC is a category with an MMM-action, for MMM a monoidal category, then one can define internally the notion of an object x∈Cx\in Cx∈C with an mmm-action for a monoid object m∈Mm\in Mm∈M. One step up, the notion of a category CCC with an action by a monoidal category MMM would be defined internally to the "2-actegory" of Cat\rm CatCat with an action by the monoidal category Cat\rm CatCat.
