Mike Shulman (Jan 16 2023 at 19:43):

The first observation is that x⇒y≤j(x)⇒j(y)x\Rightarrow y \le j(x) \Rightarrow j(y)x⇒y≤j(x)⇒j(y). This is because x⇒yx\Rightarrow yx⇒y is the same as the internal statement x≤yx\le yx≤y (because Ω\OmegaΩ is the object of internal truth values), so this is just the fact that jjj is internally monotonic. This is what is meant by being V-enriched. By adjunction, it tells us that (x⇒y)∧j(x)≤j(y)(x\Rightarrow y) \wedge j(x) \le j(y)(x⇒y)∧j(x)≤j(y).

Now we use the fact at [[strong monad]] stated as the converse in the section "Left-strong monads are enriched monads": we have x≤y⇒(x∧y)x \le y \Rightarrow (x\wedge y)x≤y⇒(x∧y), hence x∧j(y)≤(y⇒(x∧y))∧j(y)≤j(x∧y)x\wedge j(y) \le (y \Rightarrow (x\wedge y)) \wedge j(y) \le j(x\wedge y)x∧j(y)≤(y⇒(x∧y))∧j(y)≤j(x∧y), using the observation above.

Now we just apply this twice: j(x)∧j(y)≤j(j(x)∧y)≤j(j(x∧y))=j(x∧y)j(x)\wedge j(y) \le j(j(x)\wedge y) \le j(j(x\wedge y)) = j(x\wedge y)j(x)∧j(y)≤j(j(x)∧y)≤j(j(x∧y))=j(x∧y).
