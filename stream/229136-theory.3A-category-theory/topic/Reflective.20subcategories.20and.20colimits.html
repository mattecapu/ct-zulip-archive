X→Q(X)→Q(Q(X))→⋯→Qn(X)→… X \to Q(X) \to Q(Q(X)) \to \dots \to Q^n(X) \to \dots X→Q(X)→Q(Q(X))→⋯→Qn(X)→…

If you can show that X→Q(X)X \to Q(X)X→Q(X) is an isomorphism, then the sequence above becomes a transfinite colimit of isomorphisms, implying that L(X)≅XL(X) \cong XL(X)≅X.

Where is the challenge? It lies in how you construct η:1⇒Q\eta:1 \Rightarrow Qη:1⇒Q, as there are many ways to define such a η\etaη. The variations between these pointed endofunctors come from how the data is quotiented.

For example, some versions of η:1⇒Q\eta:1 \Rightarrow Qη:1⇒Q may not quotient all the data necessary for ηX\eta_XηX​ to be an isomorphism, even if L(X)≅XL(X) \cong XL(X)≅X. This means that you need to find a suitable η:1⇒Q\eta:1 \Rightarrow Qη:1⇒Q that quotients all essential data to make the argument above work.

The reason I am asking about the lifting properties and descent conditions (which, after some reformulation, seem to apply to your case study) is that these elements could help you build the appropriate η:1⇒Q\eta:1 \Rightarrow Qη:1⇒Q.

Rémy Tuyéras (Oct 10 2024 at 21:46):

Below, my goal is to develop a formal method/algorithm to detect objects for which the construction η:1⇒Q\eta:1 \Rightarrow Qη:1⇒Q either adds new elements or does not. If it does not, the object is a model for the theory. You could potentially apply this detection criterion to your colimits.

Descent condition

My first step will be to translate your setting into a descent-condition-like framework. For this, I will assume that your horn inclusions are defined by arrows of the form

Λ→Δ\Lambda \to \DeltaΛ→Δ

where Δ\DeltaΔ is a colimit Λ∪Δ′\Lambda \cup \Delta'Λ∪Δ′. More formally, you likely have a cospan of the form:

Λ→Δ←Δ′\Lambda \rightarrow \Delta \leftarrow \Delta'Λ→Δ←Δ′

Now, for an object XXX to be a model for your theory, you likely require any arrow Λ→X\Lambda \to XΛ→X to factor through your horn inclusion. We can reformulate this using spans of homsets:

C(Λ,X)←C(Δ,X)→C(Δ′,X)\mathcal{C}(\Lambda,X) \leftarrow \mathcal{C}(\Delta,X) \rightarrow \mathcal{C}(\Delta',X)C(Λ,X)←C(Δ,X)→C(Δ′,X)

With a slight reformulation of your theory, you can probably express this span as:

limdX(d)←X(d2)→X(d1)\mathsf{lim}_d X(d) \leftarrow X(d_2) \rightarrow X(d_1)limd​X(d)←X(d2​)→X(d1​)

Lifting properties

In this context, your lifting property would be reinterpreted as stating that X(d2)→limdX(d)X(d_2) \to \mathsf{lim}_d X(d)X(d2​)→limd​X(d) is a bijection (or a surjection), while the arrow X(d2)→X(d1)X(d_2) \rightarrow X(d_1)X(d2​)→X(d1​) forces the addition of elements such that the object XXX becomes a model.

By forcing the arrow X(d2)→limdX(d)X(d_2) \to \mathsf{lim}_d X(d)X(d2​)→limd​X(d) to be a surjection, you are requesting that arrow to satisfy a right lifting property with respect to the arrow 0⇒1\mathbf{0} \Rightarrow \mathbf{1}0⇒1 in Set2\mathbf{Set}^2Set2. Specifically, this arrow is represented by the commutative square going from 0=(∅→{∗})\mathbf{0} = (\emptyset \to \{\ast\})0=(∅→{∗}) to 1=({∗}→{∗})\mathbf{1} = (\{\ast\} \to \{\ast\})1=({∗}→{∗}).

To enforce this lifting property in a small-object-argument-like fashion, you would form the pushouts of the resulting spans {∗}←∅→X(d2)\{\ast\} \leftarrow \emptyset \rightarrow X(d_2){∗}←∅→X(d2​). This process would functorially grow your object XXX by adding elements, thereby creating a new object X1X_1X1​.

If the arrow X(d2)→limdX(d)X(d_2) \to \mathsf{lim}_d X(d)X(d2​)→limd​X(d) also needs to be injective (ensuring uniqueness of the lifting), you would apply a similar construction to the commutative square δ⇒1\delta \Rightarrow \mathbf{1}δ⇒1, where δ=({∗,∗}→{∗})\delta = (\{\ast,\ast\} \to \{\ast\})δ=({∗,∗}→{∗}).

These pushouts would yield your object X1=Q(X)X_1 = Q(X)X1​=Q(X), and repeating these pushouts would give you the transfinite sequence I mentioned earlier.

Better-behaved quotients

This is only one way to create QQQ, and the construction described above is not entirely effective at forming all the necessary quotients. Indeed, the pushout with respect to the commutative square involving 0⇒1\mathbf{0} \Rightarrow \mathbf{1}0⇒1 introduces too much freedom. This excess freedom would likely be contracted in the second step, X2X_2X2​, when applying the pushout with respect to δ⇒1\delta \Rightarrow \mathbf{1}δ⇒1, but it could be addressed earlier.

To see this, note that you can form a pushout in the commutative square 0⇒1\mathbf{0} \Rightarrow \mathbf{1}0⇒1. This pushout will give you (by universality) spans of the form:

{∗}←{∗,∗}→limdX(d)\{\ast\} \leftarrow \{\ast,\ast\} \to \mathsf{lim}_d X(d){∗}←{∗,∗}→limd​X(d)

These spans are designed to correct for the free elements. By incorporating these spans into the pushout construction of X1X_1X1​, the resulting object X1=Q′(X)X_1 = Q'(X)X1​=Q′(X) will have more quotients.

Conclusion

The construction above provides a formal framework for reasoning about quotients in colimits. I am not sure exactly how this might apply to your context, but you could potentially reverse-engineer the construction back to your original setting and determine how to detect colimits XXX that do not add new elements when completed.
