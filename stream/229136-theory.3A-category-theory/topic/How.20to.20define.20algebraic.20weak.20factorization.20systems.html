Topic: How to define algebraic weak factorization systems

James Deikun (Jun 06 2026 at 04:51):

The normal definition of algebraic weak factorization systems is complex and full of redundant data. It seems like it would be simpler to define an AWFS as a functorial factorization (section of composition) that is skew-coassociative -- that is:

  • each (rooted) binary tree induces a higher-arity factorization operation
  • binary trees are partially ordered by reachability via left rotations
  • the function from binary trees to higher-arity factorization operations extends to a functor.

This seems to lead to the same definition but i'm having a little trouble proving it because of the complexity of the original definition -- making sure I've verified all the laws and there are no extras. Maybe there is a more conceptual approach via the "Beck theorem"?

James Deikun (Jun 09 2026 at 09:50):

@Tom Hirschowitz, let's call [n][n][n] the category that's just an nnn-arrow path. Then C[1]\mathcal C^{[1]}C[1] is the arrow category of C\mathcal CC, and the inclusion of [1][1][1] in [n][n][n] as the longest arrow induces by restriction a standard composition functor ∘n:C[n]→C[1]\circ_n : \mathcal{C}^{[n]} \to \mathcal{C}^{[1]}∘n​:C[n]→C[1]. Call a section of ∘n\circ_n∘n​ an nnn-ary functorial factorization. The full subcategory of Cat(C[1],C[n])\mathbf{Cat}(\mathcal{C}^{[1]}, \mathcal{C}^{[n]})Cat(C[1],C[n]) on functorial factorizations will be known as Ffacn(C)\mathbf{Ffac}_n(\mathcal C)Ffacn​(C).

James Deikun (Jun 09 2026 at 09:58):

Now, the identity functor is the unique unary functorial factorization, and because of associativity of composition in categories, and the fact that [n][n][n] is an nnn-ary pushout of copies of [1][1][1], there's an operation where you can "plug" nnn functorial factorizations FiF_iFi​ into an nnn-ary functorial factorization GGG to get a functorial factorization G(F1,...,Fn)G(F_1,...,F_n)G(F1​,...,Fn​). This has the effect of factoring an arrow with GGG and then factoring arrow number iii in the path further with FiF_iFi​, then stringing all the resulting paths together. This operation is functorial in GGG and all the FiF_iFi​.

James Deikun (Jun 09 2026 at 10:13):

So we start with a generating binary factorization FFF and then we use this operation and the identity along with FFF to generate all the functorial factorizations we can. Then assume we happen to have a morphism (natural transformation) α:F(Id,F)→F(F,Id)\alpha : F(\mathrm{Id},F) \to F(F,\mathrm{Id})α:F(Id,F)→F(F,Id). If FfFfFf as a diagram in C\mathcal CC looks like X→LfEf→RfYX \xrightarrow{Lf} Ef \xrightarrow{Rf} YXLf​EfRf​Y, then αf\alpha_fαf​ looks like:

X→LfEf→LRfERF→RRfY∥Δf↓↓μf∥X→LLfELf→RLfEf→RfY\begin{CD} X @>Lf>> Ef @>LRf>> ERF @>RRf>> Y \\ @| @V\Delta_fVV @VV\mu_fV @| \\ X @>>LLf> ELf @>>RLf> Ef @>>Rf> Y \\ \end{CD}X​X​Lf​LLf​​EfΔf​↓⏐​ELf​LRf​RLf​​ERF↓⏐​μf​Ef​RRf​Rf​​Y​Y​

where the left square will become, in the conventional definition of AWFS, the comultiplication of the comonad LLL and the right square will become the multiplication of the monad RRR, and the middle square the distributive law between them.

James Deikun (Jun 09 2026 at 11:10):

The next step is to generate all the natural transformations we can by plugging α\alphaα in various places using the functorial action of −(...){-}(...)−(...), and then we want any two parallel arrows we generate freely this way to be equal. It turns out due to various previously-known facts about coherences that this happens exactly when the "pentagon law" holds: α(F,Id,Id)∘α(Id,Id,F)=F(Id,α)∘α∘F(α,Id)\alpha(F,\mathrm{Id},\mathrm{Id}) \circ \alpha(\mathrm{Id},\mathrm{Id},F) = F(\mathrm{Id},\alpha) \circ \alpha \circ F(\alpha, \mathrm{Id})α(F,Id,Id)∘α(Id,Id,F)=F(Id,α)∘α∘F(α,Id). This can be reexpressed pointwise as this hard-to-read diagram in C\mathcal CC.

James Deikun (Jun 09 2026 at 11:16):

The pentagon law is equivalent to three laws, if you can manage to follow the diagram: the comonad comultiplication law for LLL, the monad multiplication law for RRR, and the "only nontrivial law for the distributive law". I think the comonad counit laws for LLL and the monad unit laws for RRR are true basically by definition. Is that all the actual laws for an AWFS? It's hard to tell, for me.
