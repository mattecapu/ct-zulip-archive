Topic: Isomorphism in algebraic presentation of monads

Aloïs Rosset (Oct 18 2021 at 13:57):

Hello! I'm reading an article ( https://arxiv.org/pdf/1808.00923.pdf ) by Bonchi, Sokolova and Vignudelli, and there is a paragraph on page 11 that puzzles me. It talks about monads and algebraic presentation of Set-monads:

Since (Σ,E)(Σ, E)(Σ,E)-algebras define the monad TΣ,ET_{Σ,E}TΣ,E​, it follows from [Mac71, Theorem VI.8.1] that (Σ,E)(Σ, E)(Σ,E) is a presentation of the monad TΣ,ET_{Σ,E}TΣ,E​, i.e., (Σ,E)(Σ, E)(Σ,E)-algebras are (isomorphic to) the Eilenberg-Moore algebras of TΣ,ET_{Σ,E}TΣ,E​. Furthermore, if (Σ,E)(Σ, E)(Σ,E) is a presentation of a monad MMM, then MMM is isomorphic to the monad TΣ,ET_{Σ,E}TΣ,E​, i.e., there is an isomorphism monad map between them. This follows from the fact that every monad is defined by its Eilenberg-Moore algebras, see e.g. [Mac71, Theorem VI.2.1]. In particular, recall that the free MMM-algebra generated by XXX is the algebra (MX,µ)(MX, µ)(MX,µ). Hence, if (Σ,E)(Σ, E)(Σ,E) is a presentation of MMM then MXMXMX is isomorphic to the set of ΣΣΣ-terms with variables in XXX modulo EEE-equations.

The situation is as follows:

image.png

  • The monad TΣ,ET_{\Sigma,E}TΣ,E​ is defined as UΣFΣU^\Sigma F^\SigmaUΣFΣ.
  • The isomorphism HHH comes from Beck's monadicity theorem, so it is the comparison functor and thus it commutes with the free and forgetful functors FΣ,F,UΣF^\Sigma, F, U^\SigmaFΣ,F,UΣ, and UUU, i.e., the triangle on the right commutes.
  • The isomorphism GGG is there to signify that (Σ,E)(\Sigma,E)(Σ,E) presents MMM, but it no way does that imply that the triangle on the left commutes.

Hence my question: how can we conclude that MMM and TΣ,ET_{\Sigma,E}TΣ,E​ are ismorphic monads, or that the free objects, once considered in Set\mathsf{Set}Set, are in bijections? To have the isomorphism of monads, we would need a monad map M=>TΣ,EM => T_{\Sigma,E}M=>TΣ,E​, but I see no way of defining such a natural transformation. The article invokes the theorem that says that a monad is equal to the composition of both adjoint functor to it's EM category. But since the triangle on the left does not commute, to me it looks like the reasoning quickly comes to an end.

Could it be possible that in Set\mathsf{Set}Set, an isomorphism such as GGG can always be chosen so that it is a concrete isomorphism?

Thank you in advance for any help!

Nathanael Arkor (Oct 18 2021 at 15:32):

If you are happy that the category of algebras for T(Σ,E)T_{(\Sigma, E)}T(Σ,E)​ is (concretely) equivalent to the category of algebras for MMM (by definition of "being a presentation"), then it follows that the two monads are isomorphic. You can prove this directly, or deduce it abstractly.

Nathanael Arkor (Oct 18 2021 at 15:38):

@Dylan McDermott just pointed out to me that Definition 3.5 of that paper seems wrong. A presentation should require that the two categories of algebras are concretely isomorphic, i.e. isomorphic over Set\mathrm{Set}Set. I'm not convinced that with their definition the result holds.

Mike Shulman (Oct 18 2021 at 17:43):

It's certainly not true for monads on an arbitrary category that an isomorphism between categories of algebras implies an isomorphism of monads. For instance, the lattice of subsets of {0,1}\{0,1\}{0,1} has two different reflective sublattices that are abstractly isomorphic. I suppose it's possible that something sneaky happens in the special case of monads on Set\rm SetSet, but even if so, I think it would be bad form to rely on that.
