Many of you who had to tolerate my stubborness lately, know that I've been interested in "differential 2-rigs" https://arxiv.org/abs/2103.00938; now, mostly thanks to the fact that @Todd Trimble joined @Fabrizio Genovese and me, the work has almost doubled in length, and many other interesting ideas emerged.

As you can see, the paper ends with an account of "future prospects"; one the ideas is to study differential equations in a 2-rig. Let me sketch the main lines of the discussion.

Given a 2-rig C\mathcal CC with a derivation, it is a natural question to investigate the fixed points of a derivation ∂\partial∂ on C\mathcal{C}C, when ∂\partial∂ is regarded as an endofunctor, if by 'fixed point' we mean an initial algebra or a terminal coalgebra of ∂\partial∂.

One could legitimately call an X∈CX\in \mathcal{C}X∈C such that ∂X≅X\partial X \cong X∂X≅X a 'Napier object', (for the obvious reason that 'exponential object' already has a different meaning).

Now, given that ∂\partial∂ preserves coproducts, initial ∂\partial∂-algebras are seldom an interesting object; the initial chain

∅→∂∅→∂∂∅→∂∂∂∅→… \varnothing \to \partial \varnothing \to \partial\partial \varnothing \to \partial\partial\partial \varnothing \to \dots∅→∂∅→∂∂∅→∂∂∂∅→…

needed to build the initial ∂\partial∂-algebra stops after a single step.

On the other hand, terminal colgebras turn out to be linked to the behaviour of the object ∂1\partial 1∂1: the terminal cochain is

1←∂1←∂∂1←∂∂∂1←… 1\leftarrow \partial 1 \leftarrow \partial\partial 1 \leftarrow \partial\partial\partial 1 \leftarrow \dots1←∂1←∂∂1←∂∂∂1←…

thus the first ordinal λ\lambdaλ for which the transition morphism v:∂λ1←∂λ+11v : \partial^\lambda 1 \leftarrow \partial^{\lambda + 1} 1v:∂λ1←∂λ+11 is invertible realises the terminal coalgebra. This is related to an old question of mine, where I was asking what's up with ∂1\partial 1∂1, and whether I can expect it to be zero just as a consequence of the axioms.

Now, it takes just a little bit of imagination to zoom-out from this particular instance of a fixed point to speak of the following in a 2-rig with duals: if we interpret the word 'solution' to mean 'terminal coalgebra', then we can define also

  1. hyperbolic sines, i.e. 'solutions' to 'equations' of the form ∂∂X≅X\partial\partial X \cong X∂∂X≅X; and in a category with duals XL,XRX^L, X^RXL,XR for every object,
  2. left (resp., right) logarithms, i.e. 'solutions' to 'equations' of the form ∂X≅XL\partial X \cong X^L∂X≅XL (resp., ∂X≅XR\partial X\cong X^R∂X≅XR).
  3. left (resp., right) sine objects in a differential 2-rig, i.e. 'solutions' to 'equations' of the form ∂∂X≅XL\partial\partial X \cong X^L∂∂X≅XL (resp., ∂∂X≅XR\partial\partial X \cong X^R∂∂X≅XR);

This paves the way to the following

Definition (Differential-polynomial equation). A (ordinary) differential-polynomial equation (DPE for short) is (slightly informally, the paper gives a better definition) a functor that can be obtained from iterate composition of 'polynomial operations' (sum and product) and derivation.

An example of a DPEs 'with constant coefficients' is

X↦A⊗X(2)∪B⊗X(1)∪C; X\mapsto A\otimes X^{(2)} \cup B\otimes X^{(1)} \cup C;X↦A⊗X(2)∪B⊗X(1)∪C;

a solution for a given DPE F:C→CF : \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{C}F:C→C consists of a terminal coalgebra for FFF, regarded as an endofunctor of C\mathcal{C}C. Clearly, a Napier object is a solution for the DPE FX=X(1)FX = X^{(1)}FX=X(1), whereas a hyperbolic sine object is a solution for GX=X(2)GX=X^{(2)}GX=X(2).

Now, let's switch to the theory of differential equations in the category of species; it has a long history; first introduced in the initial works by Joyal, it was mostly developed by Leroux and Viennot, Labelle, Rajan...

My claim now is that the general theory of combinatorial differential equations studied in these papers might be framed into a more general theory of DPEs and their solutions. As far as I understand, no one attempted to develop this general theory of DPEs in a differential 2-rig. And determining the 'solutions' of a DPE in species has always been a matter of hard group theory (see e.g. the work of Rajan on the solutions of ∂kY=Xn\partial^k Y = X^n∂kY=Xn), hard combinatorics (see various papers by Labelle) or clever manipulations only valid in special cases.

In fact, a remarkable theorem of Labelle says that a given ODE in species can have nnn distinct solutions for every 1<n≤2ℵ01 < n \le 2^{\aleph_0}1<n≤2ℵ0​, except ℵ0\aleph_0ℵ0​. Now, among these solutions, by definition, there must be the terminal coalgebra for the endofunctor that defines the ODE, and my claim is that that universal object deserves the name of "the solution" of the ODE.

A remarkable example of this multiplicity of solutions lives in the category of virtual species (more or less, formal differences of species, like one would do in a cancellative monoid to obtain its Grothendieck group); there, we can solve all combinatorial differential equations ∂X=G\partial X = G∂X=G where GGG is a given species: its general solution is of the form A+∫GA + \int GA+∫G where AAA is a solution of ∂A=∅\partial A =\varnothing∂A=∅, and

∫G:=∑k=1∞(−1)k+1Ek∂k−1G {\textstyle \int G} := \sum_{k=1}^\infty (-1)^{k+1} E_k \partial^{k-1} G∫G:=∑k=1∞​(−1)k+1Ek​∂k−1G

where EkE_kEk​ is the species of kkk-element sets, and ∂Ek≅Ek−1\partial E_k \cong E_{k-1}∂Ek​≅Ek−1​; it just takes the Leibniz rule to prove that in fact ∂(A+∫G)≅G\partial(A+\int G) \cong G∂(A+∫G)≅G.

An enticing piece of the theory of combinatorial species that might turn out to be useful in order to attack the study of DPEs relies on the results outlined by Rajan in "The adjoints to the derivative functor on species".

The upshot of the paper is that the good behaviour of the derivative functor on species can be at least partly motivated with the fact that ∂\partial∂ sits in a triple of adjoints

L⊣∂⊣R.L\dashv \partial \dashv R.L⊣∂⊣R.

This formalism is rather powerful: for example, it can prove by completely formal means the isomorphism ℘≅t∗t\wp\cong \mathbf{t} \ast \mathbf{t}℘≅t∗t between the species of powersets and the two-fold convolution of the terminal species.

Another remarkable result on the structure of the category of species was due to Yeh (afaicu, a student of Labelle) whose doctoral thesis "On the Combinatorial Species of Joyal" proved the following: let's call a species a molecule if it is indecomposable with respect to coproduct, and an atom if it is indecomposable with respect to product.

It's easy to see that a molecule is concentrated at a single cardinality nM=∣A∣n_M=|A|nM​=∣A∣ of those AAA's for which MAMAMA is nonempty, i.e. MB≅∅MB\cong\varnothingMB≅∅ for every other BBB whose cardinality is not nMn_MnM​; the number so determined is the degree of the molecule.

Every species can be uniquely written as a sum of molecules (in fact, as a sum of its molecular sub-species), and every molecule can be uniquely written as a product of atoms.

Then, we have the following

Theorem (Yeh decomposition). The rig of isomorphism classes of species is isomorphic to the rig of polynomials with natural coefficients, on a countably infinite set of indeterminates that can be taken to form an ordered enumeration of its atoms:

Spc≅N⟦A1⊔A2⊔A3⊔…⟧\mathsf{Spc} \cong \mathbb N\llbracket A_1 \sqcup A_2 \sqcup A_3 \sqcup \dots\rrbracketSpc≅N[[A1​⊔A2​⊔A3​⊔…]]

Evidently, the definition of molecule and atom transports in a straightforward way to the case of general 2-rigs. This leads to the following definition.

Definition (Scopic 2-rig). A 2-rig C\mathcal{C}C is called scopic if it has a countable set of atoms A\mathcal{A}A, and

C≅Fin⟦ζ1,ζ2,…⟧\mathcal{C} \cong \mathsf{Fin}\llbracket \zeta_1,\zeta_2,\dots\rrbracketC≅Fin[[ζ1​,ζ2​,…]]

where ζ‾={ζ1,ζ2,… }\underline{\zeta} = \{\zeta_1,\zeta_2,\dots\}ζ​={ζ1​,ζ2​,…} is an enumeration of A\mathcal{A}A, and Fin⟦ζ‾⟧\mathsf{Fin}\llbracket\underline{\zeta}\rrbracketFin[[ζ​]] is a suitably define 'free 2-rig' with coefficients in Fin\mathsf{Fin}Fin.

In addition, in Spc\mathsf{Spc}Spc there is just a finite set of molecules of a given degree; in fact, Yeh proves the following: did_idi​ is equal to the number of conjugacy classes of subgroups of the symmetric group on iii elements, and there is an explicit procedure to build all the did_idi​ molecules of a given degree. As a side remark, this suggests that there exists a very tight connection between the theory of differential equations over Spc\mathsf{Spc}Spc and the representation theory of symmetric groups; this tight connection reflects on the theory of differential equations, and a remarkable example of this connection is the main result in Rajan.

In a general 2-rig the notion of degree does not seem to make sense.

Groupoid-valued species (also called stuff types) are the same thing as finitary polynomial functors over groupoids, meaning functors represented by a map of groupoids E -> B with finite discrete fibres. The equivalence goes like this: there is a classifier for finite sets, given by BB' -> BB where BB is the groupoid of finite sets and bijections, and BB' is the groupoid of finite pointed sets and basepoint preserving bijections. Since it is a classifier, any map E -> B induces a map B -> BB. That's essentially a species: you can extract a species by taking F: BB -> Grpd to be the functor sending n to the homotopy fibre over n. Conversely, given a species F: BB -> Grpd, take the Grothendieck construction to get something over BB, say B -> BB and then pullback the universal family to get something over B.

John Baez (May 06 2021 at 17:05):

The classic example to keep in mind is when XXX is the groupoid of finite sets and bijections, made monoidal using disjoint union. By the way, disjoint union is not coproduct in XXX, but it is the coproduct in a larger category X′X'X′ containing XXX, namely the category of finite sets and functions. We can take the coproduct monoidal structure on X′X'X′ and restrict it to XXX to get a monoidal structure on XXX.

John Baez (May 06 2021 at 17:08):

All my other claimed examples of scopic 2-rig will have the same general flavor; in particular the groupoid XXX will always be the core of some category X′X'X′ with finite coproducts, and XXX will get its monoidal structure that way.

John Baez (May 06 2021 at 17:13):

Here are some examples of X′X'X′:

  • the category of finite simple graphs
  • the category of finite directed graphs
  • the category of finite directed multigraphs (or "quivers" - the graphs that category theorists like)
  • the category of finite-dimensional vector spaces over some finite field (there's one finite field for each prime power)
  • the category of finite posets

What do these have in common, that makes X^\hat{X}X^ with Day convolution be scopic?

John Baez (May 06 2021 at 17:14):

First of all, every object of X′X'X′ has a finite group of automorphisms.

John Baez (May 06 2021 at 17:16):

This guarantees that XXX is equivalent to a coproduct of finite groups - or, more pedantically, the coproduct of 1-object groupoids that are deloopings of finite groups.
