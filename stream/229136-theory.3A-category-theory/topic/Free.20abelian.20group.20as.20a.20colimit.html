This is what I found. This is all related to the topic of this thread in that the proof of the following lemma proceeds by constructing the free commutative monoid as a filtered colimit, more precisely as the copruduct over the "symmetric powers" of an object:

Lemma [Lemma 4.4.5 in Brandenburg, p. 67]. Let CCC be a countably cocomplete symmetric monoidal category category. Then CMon(C)→C\mathrm{CMon}(C) \to CCMon(C)→C has a left adjoint S:C→CMon(C)S: C \to \mathrm{CMon}(C)S:C→CMon(C).

Remark. The cited work calls symmetric monoidal categories "tensor categories" and assumes cococompleteness of C, but only countable cocompleteness is used.

Lemma (no reference found). Let CCC be a countably cocomplete symmetric monoidal category category. Then CMon(C)→C\mathrm{CMon}(C) \to CCMon(C)→C is monadic.
Proof sketch. Use the previous lemma and write down an explicit (quasi-)inverse to the comparison functor.

Corollary. Let CCC be a countably cocomplete cartesian closed category. Then SSS, viewed as an endofunctor on CCC, acquires the structure of a symmetric monoidal monad (a.k.a. commutative monad).
Very-sketchy attempt at a proof sketch. SSS should canonically be a CCC-enriched monad (whatever that is), and according to the lab page of "enriched monad", we get the structure of a strong monad, which we can check to be commutative, and which is then "the same" as a symmetric monoidal monad.

Corollary. Let CCC be a finitely complete, countably cocomplete cartesian closed category. Then CMon(C)\mathrm{CMon}(C)CMon(C) is a closed symmetric monoidal category.
Proof sketch. This follows from [Brandenburg, Corollary 6.5.4.], applied to the symmetric monoidal monad $S$ and using the previous lemma (i.e. that Alg(S)≅CMon(C)Alg(S)\cong CMon(C)Alg(S)≅CMon(C)).

Remark. [Brandenburg, Corollary 6.5.4.] assumes cocompleteness of CCC (instead of just countable cocompleteness), but I don't think this is used in the proof.
