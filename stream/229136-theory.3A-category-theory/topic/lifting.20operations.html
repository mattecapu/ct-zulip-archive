We can lift this operation to subsets, opˉ:P(A)×P(B)→P(C)\bar{op}:P(A)\times P(B)\to P(C)opˉ​:P(A)×P(B)→P(C) by defining opˉ(U,V)={op(a,b)  ∣  a∈U∧b∈V}\bar{op}(U,V) = \{op(a,b)\; | \; a\in U\land b\in V\}opˉ​(U,V)={op(a,b)∣a∈U∧b∈V}. Christian Williams (May 06 2020 at 01:28):

In Jacobs' Categorical Logic and Type Theory, they discuss "fiberwise structure" -- an indexed category F:C→CatF:C\to \mathrm{Cat}F:C→Cat has "fibered --'s" if every F(c)F(c)F(c) has "--" and reindexing functors F(f):F(c)→F(d)F(f):F(c)\to F(d)F(f):F(c)→F(d) preserve those "--'s".

Mike Shulman (May 06 2020 at 19:41):

I said this to you privately, but I'll add it to the discussion here too in case anyone else wants to add to it. The objects of your ∫P\int P∫P are monomorphisms in Set; if you remove this restriction and look at just the arrow category of Set, and regard it as the Artin gluing of the identity functor Set→Set\mathrm{Set} \to \mathrm{Set}Set→Set, i.e. the comma category (IdSet↓IdSet)(\mathrm{Id}_{\mathrm{Set}} \downarrow \mathrm{Id}_{\mathrm{Set}} )(IdSet​↓IdSet​), then this is the usual construction of cartesian-closed structure on a gluing category, which happens to preserve monos.
