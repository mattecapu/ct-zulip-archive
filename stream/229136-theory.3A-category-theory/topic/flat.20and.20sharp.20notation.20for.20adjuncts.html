Topic: flat and sharp notation for adjuncts

Tim Hosgood (Jan 13 2023 at 14:19):

Sometimes people like to use ♭\flat♭ and ♯\sharp♯ for adjuncts, but there seem to be two opposite conventions:

  • given f ⁣:Lx→yf\colon Lx\to yf:Lx→y and g ⁣:x→Ryg\colon x\to Ryg:x→Ry, the nLab ([[adjunct]]) writes g=f♯g=f^\sharpg=f♯ and f=g♭f=g^\flatf=g♭ (so "flats on the left, sharps on the right")
  • Category Theory in Context (p. 116) writes f♯ ⁣:Lx→yf^\sharp\colon Lx\to yf♯:Lx→y and f♭ ⁣:x→Ryf^\flat\colon x\to Ryf♭:x→Ry (so "sharps on the left, flats on the right")

I know that convention is convention, but is there a reason to prefer one over the other? I guess in cohesion, the flat modality is left adjoint to the sharp modality, so maybe this is why the nLab shows this preference.

Cole Comfort (Jan 13 2023 at 14:36):

In music theory flat means "lower in pitch" and sharp means "higher in pitch". Take the inclusion ι:N→R\iota: \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{R} ι:N→R, then ⌈−⌉⊣i⊣⌊−⌋ \lceil - \rceil \dashv i \dashv \lfloor - \rfloor ⌈−⌉⊣i⊣⌊−⌋. So in this example it seems like it would be more appropriate to call the left adjoint to ι \iota ι sharp and the right adjoint flat because the floor is lower and the ceiling is higher.

The reason for the choice of ♭\flat♭ and ♯\sharp♯ in cohesion, by the way, is that then ♭BG\flat BG♭BG is the classifier for GGG-bundles with a flat connection, in the geometric sense of "no curvature".
