Topic: “Natural cotransformations”

Emily (May 02 2024 at 02:22):

The set of natural transformations between two functors F,G ⁣:C→DF,G\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{D}F,G:C→D is given by

Nat(F,G)=def∫A∈CHomD(F(A),G(A)).\mathrm{Nat}(F,G)\mathbin{\overset{\mathrm{def}}{=}}\int_{A\in\mathcal{C}}\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{D}}(F(A),G(A)).Nat(F,G)=def∫A∈C​HomD​(F(A),G(A)).

Define the set of “natural cotransformations” from FFF to GGG by

CoNat(F,G)=def∫A∈CHomD(F(A),G(A)).\mathrm{CoNat}(F,G)\mathbin{\overset{\mathrm{def}}{=}}\int^{A\in\mathcal{C}}\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{D}}(F(A),G(A)).CoNat(F,G)=def∫A∈CHomD​(F(A),G(A)).

Explicitly, CoNat(F,G)\mathrm{CoNat}(F,G)CoNat(F,G) is the quotient of ∐A∈CHomD(F(A),G(A))\coprod_{A\in\mathcal{C}}\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{D}}(F(A),G(A))∐A∈C​HomD​(F(A),G(A)) by the equivalence relation ∼\mathord{\sim}∼ generated by declaring two elements (A,αA ⁣:F(A)→G(A))(A,\alpha_{A}\colon F(A)\to G(A))(A,αA​:F(A)→G(A)) and (B,βB ⁣:F(B)→G(B))(B,\beta_{B}\colon F(B)\to G(B))(B,βB​:F(B)→G(B)) of this set equivalent if there exist:

  1. A morphism f ⁣:A→Bf\colon A\to Bf:A→B of C\mathcal{C}C;
  2. A morphism ϕ ⁣:F(B)→G(A)\phi\colon F(B)\to G(A)ϕ:F(B)→G(A) of D\mathcal{D}D;

making the diagrams

image.png

commute. A “natural cotransformation” from FFF to GGG is then an equivalence class [(A,αA)][(A,\alpha_{A})][(A,αA​)] under this relation.

Two important examples of this are the following:

  1. When FFF and GGG are both given by the identity functor idC\mathrm{id}_{\mathcal{C}}idC​ of a category C\mathcal{C}C, we have

CoNat(idC,idC)≅Tr(C),\mathrm{CoNat}(\mathrm{id}_{\mathcal{C}},\mathrm{id}_{\mathcal{C}})\cong\mathrm{Tr}(\mathcal{C}),CoNat(idC​,idC​)≅Tr(C),

where Tr(C)\mathrm{Tr}(\mathcal{C})Tr(C) is the trace of C\mathcal{C}C. For instance, when C=BG\mathcal{C}=\mathbf{B}GC=BG is the one-object category associated to a group GGG, the trace Tr(BG)\mathrm{Tr}(\mathbf{B}G)Tr(BG) is precisely the set of conjugacy classes of GGG.

  1. Given morphisms of monoids f,g ⁣:BA→BBf,g\colon\mathbf{B}A\to\mathbf{B}Bf,g:BA→BB, the set of natural cotransformations from Bf\mathbf{B}fBf to Bg\mathbf{B}gBg is given by the quotient of BBB by the equivalence relation generated by declaring b∼b′b\sim b'b∼b′ if there exist a∈Aa\in Aa∈A and k∈Bk\in Bk∈B such that we have

b=kf(a),b′=g(a)k.\begin{align*} b &= kf(a),\\ b' &= g(a)k. \end{align*}bb′​=kf(a),=g(a)k.​

From how natural the definition is and the importance of the example CoNat(idC,idC)≅Tr(C)\mathrm{CoNat}(\mathrm{id}_{\mathcal{C}},\mathrm{id}_{\mathcal{C}})\cong\mathrm{Tr}(\mathcal{C})CoNat(idC​,idC​)≅Tr(C), I can't help but feel like this might be a kind of fundamental notion in category theory. However, I haven't really ever found anything written about them.

Has anyone here ever wondered about these "natural cotransformations" before, want to talk about them, or try to discuss/cook up some examples/results together in this chat?

@Peva Blanchard I've thought about these questions, and it seems there are lots of interesting things going on. Here are some of the things I found:

On Composition I.
There doesn't seem to be a vertical composition map of the form

CoNat(G,H)×CoNat(F,G)→CoNat(F,H)\mathrm{CoNat}(G,H)\times\mathrm{CoNat}(F,G)\to\mathrm{CoNat}(F,H)CoNat(G,H)×CoNat(F,G)→CoNat(F,H)

since:

  • An element of CoNat(F,G)\mathrm{CoNat}(F,G)CoNat(F,G) will be an equivalence class [(A,αA)][(A,\alpha_{A})][(A,αA​)] with αA ⁣:F(A)→G(A)\alpha_{A}\colon F(A)\to G(A)αA​:F(A)→G(A);
  • An element of CoNat(G,H)\mathrm{CoNat}(G,H)CoNat(G,H) will be an equivalence class [(B,βB)][(B,\beta_{B})][(B,βB​)] with βB ⁣:G(B)→H(B)\beta_{B}\colon G(B)\to H(B)βB​:G(B)→H(B);

and in general there isn't a way to compose αA ⁣:F(A)→G(A)\alpha_{A}\colon F(A)\to G(A)αA​:F(A)→G(A) with βB ⁣:G(B)→H(B)\beta_{B}\colon G(B)\to H(B)βB​:G(B)→H(B).

That said, it seems that when both equivalence classes admit a different representative with the same object, e.g. we have [(A,αA)]=[(K,αK′)][(A,\alpha_{A})]=[(K,\alpha'_{K})][(A,αA​)]=[(K,αK′​)] and [(B,βB)]=[(K,βK′)][(B,\beta_{B})]=[(K,\beta'_K)][(B,βB​)]=[(K,βK′​)], we can define their composition as [(K,βK′∘αK′)][(K,\beta'_{K}\circ\alpha'_{K})][(K,βK′​∘αK′​)], and this seems to be well-defined.

In particular, for one-object categories we do have such a composition.

On Composition II.
In a different direction, there seem to be composition maps of the form

Nat(G,H)×CoNat(F,G)→CoNat(F,H),CoNat(G,H)×Nat(F,G)→CoNat(F,H).\begin{align*} \mathrm{Nat}(G,H)\times\mathrm{CoNat}(F,G) \to \mathrm{CoNat}(F,H),\\ \mathrm{CoNat}(G,H)\times\mathrm{Nat}(F,G) \to \mathrm{CoNat}(F,H). \end{align*}Nat(G,H)×CoNat(F,G)→CoNat(F,H),CoNat(G,H)×Nat(F,G)→CoNat(F,H).​

(There's a kind of analogy between co/ends and Hochschild co/homology, where the co/products and co/equalisers formula for computing co/ends look like a Hochschild co/simplicial set, and under this analogy these composition maps mixing natural transformations with "natural cotransformations" would correspond to a cap product on degree (0,0)(0,0)(0,0).)

On Composition III.
When C\mathcal{C}C is the one-object groupoid associated to a group GGG, these recover the action of the centre of GGG on the set of conjugacy classes of GGG:

Z(G)×Cl(G)→Cl(G),Cl(G)×Z(G)→Cl(G).\begin{align*} \mathrm{Z}(G)\times\mathrm{Cl}(G) \to \mathrm{Cl}(G),\\ \mathrm{Cl}(G)\times\mathrm{Z}(G) \to \mathrm{Cl}(G). \end{align*}Z(G)×Cl(G)→Cl(G),Cl(G)×Z(G)→Cl(G).​

On Composition IV: We Do Get a Profunctor :tada: .
In addition, a consequence of the existence of these maps (which I haven't checked yet, but plan to do in a bit) would be that the assignment (F,G)↦CoNat(F,G)(F,G)\mapsto\mathrm{CoNat}(F,G)(F,G)↦CoNat(F,G) would define a functor

CoNat(−1,−2) ⁣:Fun(C,D)op×Fun(C,D)→Sets,\mathrm{CoNat}(-_{1},-_{2})\colon\mathrm{Fun}(\mathcal{C},\mathcal{D})^{\mathsf{op}}\times\mathrm{Fun}(\mathcal{C},\mathcal{D})\to\mathsf{Sets},CoNat(−1​,−2​):Fun(C,D)op×Fun(C,D)→Sets,

When FFF or GGG Are Representable.

I think there's a lot of things to explore here, but I've found a nice example that seems interesting:

  • Take C=BAop\mathcal{C}=\mathbf{B}A^{\mathsf{op}}C=BAop to be the opposite of the one-object category on a monoid AAA;
  • Take D=Sets\mathcal{D}=\mathsf{Sets}D=Sets;
  • A functor F ⁣:BAopF\colon\mathbf{B}A^{\mathsf{op}}F:BAop then corresponds to a left AAA-set XXX, where we write (a,x)↦a◃Xx(a,x)\mapsto a\mathbin{\triangleleft_{X}}x(a,x)↦a◃X​x for the left AAA-action A×X→XA\times X\to XA×X→X of AAA on XXX;

Then:

  1. The set Nat(h∙,X)\mathrm{Nat}(h_{\bullet},X)Nat(h∙​,X) is the set of morphisms of left AAA-sets from AAA to XXX, corresponding to maps of sets f ⁣:A→Xf\colon A\to Xf:A→X satisfying f(ax)=a◃Xf(x)f(ax)=a\mathbin{\triangleleft_{X}}f(x)f(ax)=a◃X​f(x).
  2. The set CoNat(h∙,X)\mathrm{CoNat}(h_{\bullet},X)CoNat(h∙​,X) is the quotient of Sets(A,X)\mathsf{Sets}(A,X)Sets(A,X) by the equivalence relation generated by f(ax)∼a◃Xf(x)f(ax)\sim a\mathbin{\triangleleft_{X}}f(x)f(ax)∼a◃X​f(x).

A Dual Notion of Morphisms of Left AAA-Sets.

More generally, taking FFF and GGG to be functors BAop→Sets\mathbf{B}A^\mathsf{op}\to\mathsf{Sets}BAop→Sets corresponding to left AAA-sets XXX and YYY, we have another nice example illustrating natural transformations vs. natural "cotransformations":

  • The set Nat(F,G)\mathrm{Nat}(F,G)Nat(F,G) is the equaliser of Sets(X,Y)\mathsf{Sets}(X,Y)Sets(X,Y) by the maps f↦[x↦f(a◃Xx)]f\mapsto[x\mapsto f(a\mathbin{\triangleleft_{X}}x)]f↦[x↦f(a◃X​x)] and f↦[x↦a◃Yf(x)]f\mapsto[x\mapsto a\mathbin{\triangleleft_{Y}}f(x)]f↦[x↦a◃Y​f(x)], i.e., we have

  • The set CoNat(F,G)\mathrm{CoNat}(F,G)CoNat(F,G) is the coequaliser of Sets(X,Y)\mathsf{Sets}(X,Y)Sets(X,Y) by these maps, quotienting Sets(X,Y)\mathsf{Sets}(X,Y)Sets(X,Y) by the relation generated by f(a◃Xx)∼a◃Yf(x)f(a\mathbin{\triangleleft_{X}}x)\sim a\mathbin{\triangleleft_{Y}}f(x)f(a◃X​x)∼a◃Y​f(x).

Rémy Tuyéras (May 16 2024 at 11:41):

When I see the definition of natural cotransformations, I almost want to look at them as spaces that would be classified by natural transformations. More specifically, I feel like they could be studied with respect to a set of coefficients to bring them back to structures that we know better.

To expand on this, I think we would need to compare it to when the theory of categories is redefined relative to a given set, say VVV (maybe this would make more sense in enriched categories, but I will stick to sets for simplicity).

Part 1

Specifically, for any graph GGG, we can define the graph GVG_VGV​ as follows:

Obj(GV)=Obj(G)Obj(G_V) = Obj(G)Obj(GV​)=Obj(G)

GV(a,b)=Hom(V,G(a,b))G_V(a,b) = Hom(V,G(a,b))GV​(a,b)=Hom(V,G(a,b))

Now, let us call a category DDD that is of the form GVG_VGV​ a VVV-pointed category. If GGG happens to be a category that induces the category structure of GVG_VGV​, then we can say that DDD is VVV-represented by GGG.

A functor F:C→GVF:C \to G_VF:C→GV​ would then be said to be represented by a functor F′:C→GF':C\to GF′:C→G if we have natural isomorphisms in aaa:

GV(F(a),−)≅Hom(V,G(F′(a),−))G_V(F(a),-) \cong Hom(V,G(F'(a), -))GV​(F(a),−)≅Hom(V,G(F′(a),−))

and

GV(−,F(a))≅Hom(V,G(−,F′(a)))G_V(-,F(a)) \cong Hom(V,G(-,F'(a)))GV​(−,F(a))≅Hom(V,G(−,F′(a)))

(hopefully I am not missing conditions here)

Now, for two functors F,G:C→DVF,G:C \to D_VF,G:C→DV​ landing in a VVV-pointed category, we have

Nat(F,G)=∫aDV(F(a),G(a))Nat(F,G)=\int_a D_V(F(a), G(a))Nat(F,G)=∫a​DV​(F(a),G(a))

If DDD is a category, and FFF and GGG are represented by functors F′,G′:C→DF',G':C\to DF′,G′:C→D, then we have:

Nat(F,G)=∫aHom(V,D(F′(a),G′(a)))Nat(F,G) =\int_a Hom(V,D(F'(a),G'(a)))Nat(F,G)=∫a​Hom(V,D(F′(a),G′(a)))

≅Hom(V,∫aD(F′(a),G′(a))) \cong Hom(V,\int_a D(F'(a),G'(a)))≅Hom(V,∫a​D(F′(a),G′(a)))

=Hom(V,Nat(F′,G′))= Hom(V,Nat(F',G'))=Hom(V,Nat(F′,G′))

For example, we have the fact that 111-pointed categories correspond to categories.

Part 2

Similarly, for any graph GGG, we can define the graph GVG^VGV as follows:

Obj(GV)=Obj(G)Obj(G^V) = Obj(G)Obj(GV)=Obj(G)

GV(a,b)=Hom(G(a,b),V)G^V(a,b) = Hom(G(a,b),V)GV(a,b)=Hom(G(a,b),V)

Now, let us call a category DDD that is of the form GVG^VGV a VVV-valued category.

In contrast to part 1, it is not obvious how to formalize the relation being represented by. Still, it seems that this is where the concept of natural cotransformation belongs.

Specifically, for two functors F,G:C→DVF,G:C \to D^VF,G:C→DV landing in a VVV-valued category, we have

Nat(F,G)=∫aDV(F(a),G(a))Nat(F,G)=\int_a D^V(F(a), G(a))Nat(F,G)=∫a​DV(F(a),G(a))

Ideally, there would be a concept of representation that would allow us to say that:

Nat(F,G)≅Hom(CoNat(F′,G′),V)Nat(F,G) \cong Hom(CoNat(F',G'),V)Nat(F,G)≅Hom(CoNat(F′,G′),V)

Somehow, I feel like the concept of natural cotransformation belongs to a general framework copying Part 1. This framework would specifically be built on a definition that characterizes the structures DDD to make DVD^VDV a category

This structure should probably be categorized relative to the set VVV (as with cohomologies and their coefficients) because it is intuitively easier to use functions landing in VVV with a category structure on them. Studying colimit-based structures might be too "confusing" as these look less like operations-based structures.

Upon reading "the set of natural transformations", one immediate question, which may be tangential to all that followed, is: with natural transformations as maps between functors, wouldn't the totality of maps between two functors be a map object of the functor category? The set of natural transformations between functors can be extracted as the set of points of the map object. Of course, if the category of functors is not cartesian closed, then one have to be content with a set of natural transformations (Lawvere & Schanuel, Conceptual Mathematics, p. 322).

Once I noticed that it is not about natural transformations but about natural cotransformations, I thought of moving on, but here I'm with my 5mBTC ;)

Natural cotransformation is to natural transformation what equivalence relation is to function (Arbib & Manes, Arrows, Structures, and Functors, pp. 16 - 23). A function and its corresponding [opposite] equivalence relation (speaking of which: is equivalence relation ever referred to as cofunction?, which in turn reminds me of co-element; Lawvere & Rosebrugh, Sets for Mathematics, pp. 127 - 128) structure both domain and codomain sets, and based on which corresponding categories can be constructed (Lawvere, Perugia Notes, pp. 9 - 11).

The above parallels raise a question: is there more, conceptually speaking, to:

natural transformation vs. natural cotransformation

than there is in the relatively simplistic dual:

function vs. equivalence relation.

In any case, if I may, I'd like to share a few things that I find fascinating about natural transformations.

(i) Maps of any category, beginning with functions between sets, can be represented as natural transformations (Lawvere & Schanuel, Conceptual Mathematics, p. 378).

(ii) "Naturality of the transformations between spaces and the transformations within thought" (Lawvere, Interview, p. 6), which can be approached via a relatively well-defined naturality relating geometry and algebra (Lawvere, Birkhoff's Theorem, p. 2).

(iii) Naturality figures prominently in the most precise scientific model of concept formation (Lawvere, Fifty Years of Functorial Semantics, p. 7).

(iv) The adequacy of one-dimensional figures needs to be explained because it occurs in many different examples.
Many kinds of cohesion (algebraic geometry, smooth geometry, continuous geometry) are well-expressed as a subtopos of the classifying topos of a finitary single-sorted algebraic theory. But often that algebraic theory is determined by its monoid M of unary operations via naturality only: for example, the binary operations, instead of being independently specified, are just the maps of right M-sets from M^2 to M. If a common explanation can be found for this adequacy of one-dimensional considerations in the determination of n-dimensional and infinite-dimensional functionals, in so many disparate cases, then it would further establish that the Eilenberg-Mac Lane notion of naturality is far more powerful than the mere tautology it is sometimes considered to be (Lawvere, Cartesian Closed Categories, p. 2).
