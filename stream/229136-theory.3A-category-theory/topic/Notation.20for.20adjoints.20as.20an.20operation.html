Topic: Notation for adjoints as an operation

Mike Shulman (May 04 2023 at 18:15):

Suppose fff is a morphism in a 2-category (or just a functor, or just an object of a non-symmetric monoidal category). What is a good pair of dual-looking notations for the left and right adjoints (duals) of fff, if they exist?

I've recently been writing f∗f^*f∗ for the left adjoint and f∗f_*f∗​ for the right adjoint, so that f∗⊣f⊣f∗f^* \dashv f \dashv f_*f∗⊣f⊣f∗​, but this could be confusing since often X∗X^*X∗ just means "the" dual in a symmetric monoidal category, and in many contexts (e.g. geometric morphisms) we have f∗⊣f∗f^* \dashv f_*f∗⊣f∗​ without an fff in the middle.

David Egolf (May 04 2023 at 18:56):

I don't know if you want a pre-existing notation, or if you're considering inventing a new one. So, I don't know what already exists - but how about f⊢⊣f⊣f⊣f^\vdash \dashv f \dashv f ^\dashvf⊢⊣f⊣f⊣? The left adjoint has the vertical bar on the left, and the right adjoint has the vertical bar on the right.

Mike Shulman (May 04 2023 at 19:02):

I don't think there's an existing "standard" notation, or at least if there is it's not standard enough for me to have heard about it. But I'd be interested both in notations that have been used already and proposals for new ones.

I can see f⊣f^{\dashv}f⊣ for the right adjoint, but I don't really like f⊢f^{\vdash}f⊢ for the left adjoint, because ⊢\vdash⊢ is hardly ever used to denote adjunctions, and has other meanings such as a logical turnstile. Would ⊣f{}^{\dashv}f⊣f be too ugly?

Mike Shulman (May 04 2023 at 21:51):

In the notation of Fausk-Hu-May Isomorphisms between left and right adjoints, we have f∗⊣f∗f^* \dashv f_*f∗⊣f∗​ and f!⊣f∗f_! \dashv f^*f!​⊣f∗, while sometimes f∗=f!f_* = f_!f∗​=f!​ and other times f∗=f!f^* = f^!f∗=f!. So if we identified fff itself with f∗f_*f∗​, we could have f∗⊣f⊣f!f^* \dashv f \dashv f^!f∗⊣f⊣f!.

On the other hand, Chaitanya pointed out to me that upper and lower stars and shrieks are all usually used for an induced action on presheaves/modules/etc., so it would be confusing to also use them for maps of domains. For instance, if f:A→Bf:A\to Bf:A→B is a functor in Cat\rm CatCat, then f∗f^*f∗ often denotes the induced precomposition functor PB→PAPB \to PAPB→PA on presheaves, so we wouldn't want to also use it for a left adjoint functor B→AB\to AB→A.
