Brendan Murphy (Apr 22 2024 at 06:04):

In Joyal's notes on quasicategories he discusses a factorization system on small 1-categories (Cat is considered as a 1-category here) with left class "iterated strict localizations" and right class conservative functors. Here a strict localization is a functor isomorphic to something of the form C→C[W−1]\mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{C}[W^{-1}]C→C[W−1] for a small category C\mathcal{C}C and set of maps WWW in C\mathcal{C}C and an iterated strict localization is a transfinite composite of these. He says we need to talk about iterated localizations because if we have a functor F:C→DF : \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{D}F:C→D and set W={f∣F(f) is an isomorphism}W = \{ f \mid F(f) \text{ is an isomorphism} \}W={f∣F(f) is an isomorphism}, giving a factorization of FFF into a strict localization P:C→C[W−1]P : \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{C}[W^{-1}]P:C→C[W−1] followed by a functor G:C[W−1]→DG : \mathcal{C}[W^{-1}] \to \mathcal{D}G:C[W−1]→D, the resulting GGG might not be conservative. So even after inverting everything inverted by FFF we may still need to invert yet more maps V⊆C[W−1]\mathcal{V} \subseteq \mathcal{C}[W^{-1}]V⊆C[W−1], those inverted by GGG. And P−1(V)=WP^{-1}(\mathcal{V}) = \mathcal{W}P−1(V)=W, so these are genuinely new things created in the localization. What's an example of a functor FFF where this occurs? An example where we need to iterate transfinitely many times would be appreciated

Let C=(w→fx←gy→hz)C = (w \xrightarrow{f} x \xleftarrow{g} y \xrightarrow{h} z)C=(wf​xg​yh​z), let DDD be the walking section-retraction pair with s:a→bs:a\to bs:a→b and r:b→ar:b\to ar:b→a and rs=1ar s = 1_ars=1a​, and let F:C→DF:C\to DF:C→D be defined by F(f)=sF(f) = sF(f)=s, F(g)=1bF(g) = 1_bF(g)=1b​, and F(h)=rF(h) = rF(h)=r. Mike Shulman (Apr 22 2024 at 18:29):

Interesting question! Here's an attempt at a slick proof using factorization systems. As you say, suppose F:J→CF:J\to CF:J→C has a limit, call it ℓ\ellℓ, and that F=GPF = GPF=GP with PPP left orthogonal to all conservative functors. (In fact I don't think we need that GGG is conservative.) Then we know that the map Cone(x,F)→C(x,ℓ)\mathrm{Cone}(x,F) \to C(x,\ell)Cone(x,F)→C(x,ℓ) is an isomorphism, and we want to show that the map Cone(x,G)→C(x,ℓ)\mathrm{Cone}(x,G) \to C(x,\ell)Cone(x,G)→C(x,ℓ) is an isomorphism. By 3-for-2, it suffices to show that the map Cone(x,G)→Cone(x,F)\mathrm{Cone}(x,G) \to \mathrm{Cone}(x,F)Cone(x,G)→Cone(x,F) is an isomorphism, and now we don't need any more to mention the fact that FFF has a limit. So we can try to prove that for all functors F:J→CF:J\to CF:J→C with a factorization F=GPF = GPF=GP where PPP is left orthogonal to conservative functors, we have that the map Cone(x,G)→Cone(x,F)\mathrm{Cone}(x,G) \to \mathrm{Cone}(x,F)Cone(x,G)→Cone(x,F) is an isomorphism.

Next observe that to give a functor F:J→CF:J\to CF:J→C and a cone over it with vertex xxx is equivalent to giving a functor F′:J→x/CF' : J \to x/CF′:J→x/C to the co-slice under xxx. And therefore, given F:J→CF:J\to CF:J→C, to give a cone over it with vertex xxx is to give a lifting of FFF along the projection Ux:x/C→CU_x : x/C \to CUx​:x/C→C. But UxU_xUx​ is conservative! Thus, given FFF and a cone over it, we have a commutative square G∘P=Ux∘F′G \circ P = U_x \circ F'G∘P=Ux​∘F′, where UxU_xUx​ is conservative and PPP is left orthogonal to conservative functors. Therefore, there is a unique lifting, mapping the codomain of PPP to x/Cx/Cx/C, i.e. a unique extension of our given cone over FFF to a cone over GGG with vertex xxx. This is what we wanted.
