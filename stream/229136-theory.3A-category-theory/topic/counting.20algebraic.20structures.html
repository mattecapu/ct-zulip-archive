Topic: counting algebraic structures

John Baez (Sep 09 2023 at 08:54):

The number of semigroups with nnn elements goes like this:

1, 1, 5, 24, 188, 1915, 28634, 1627672, 3684030417, 105978177936292, ...

1, 1, 2, 1, 1, 1, 1, 1, ...

John Baez (Sep 09 2023 at 08:55):

No! Not if by "algebraic gadget" we mean something described by a bunch of operations obeying equational laws --- that is, by a Lawvere theory.

This follows from a result of László Lovász in 1967:

  • László Lovász, Operations with structures, Acta Mathematica Academiae Scientiarum Hungarica 18, (1967) 321--328.

On Mastodon, Omar Antolín sketched a proof that greases the wheels with more category theory. It relies on a rather shocking lemma:

Super-Yoneda Lemma. Let C\mathsf{C}C be the category of algebras of some Lawvere theory, and let A,B∈CA, B \in \mathsf{C}A,B∈C be two algebras whose underlying sets are finite. If the functors hom(−,A)\mathrm{hom}(-,A)hom(−,A) and hom(−,A)\mathrm{hom}(-,A)hom(−,A) are unnaturally isomorphic, then A≅BA \cong BA≅B.

John Baez (Sep 09 2023 at 08:55):

Here we say the functors hom(−,A)\mathrm{hom}(-,A)hom(−,A) and hom(−,B)\mathrm{hom}(-,B)hom(−,B) are unnaturally isomorphic if

hom(X,A)≅hom(X,B) \mathrm{hom}(X,A) \cong \mathrm{hom}(X,B) hom(X,A)≅hom(X,B)

for all X∈CX \in \mathsf{C}X∈C. We're not imposing the usual commuting naturality square --- indeed we can't, since we're not even giving any specific choice of isomorphism!

If hom(−,A)\mathrm{hom}(-,A)hom(−,A) and hom(−,B)\mathrm{hom}(-,B)hom(−,B) are naturally isomorphic, we can show A≅BA \cong BA≅B using the Yoneda Lemma. But when they're unnaturally isomorphic, we have to break the glass and pull out the Super-Yoneda Lemma.

John Baez (Sep 09 2023 at 08:56):

Given this shocking lemma, it's easy to show this:

Theorem. Let A,BA, BA,B be two algebras of a Lawvere theory whose underlying sets are finite. If Ak≅BkA^k \cong B^kAk≅Bk for some natural number kkk then A≅BA \cong BA≅B.

Here's how. Since Ak≅BkA^k \cong B^kAk≅Bk, we have natural isomorphisms

hom(−,A)k≅hom(−,Ak)≅hom(−,Bk)≅hom(−,B)k \mathrm{hom}(-,A)^k \cong \mathrm{hom}(-, A^k) \cong \mathrm{hom}(-, B^k) \cong \mathrm{hom}(-,B)^k hom(−,A)k≅hom(−,Ak)≅hom(−,Bk)≅hom(−,B)k

so for any X∈CX \in \mathsf{C}X∈C the sets hom(X,A)k\mathrm{hom}(X,A)^khom(X,A)k and hom(X,B)k\mathrm{hom}(X,B)^khom(X,B)k have the same cardinality. This means we have an unnatural isomorphism

hom(−,A)≅hom(−,B) \mathrm{hom}(-,A) \cong \mathrm{hom}(-,B) hom(−,A)≅hom(−,B)

The lemma magically lets us conclude that

A≅B A \cong B A≅B

John Baez (Sep 09 2023 at 08:57):

Now, how do we use this to solve our puzzle? Let a(n)a(n)a(n) be the number of isomorphism classes of algebras whose underlying set has nnn elements. We must have

a(nk)≥a(n) a(n^k) \ge a(n) a(nk)≥a(n)

since we've just seen that nonisomorphic algebras with nnn elements give nonisomorphic algebras with nkn^knk elements. So, for example, we can never have a(4)<a(2)a(4) \lt a(2)a(4)<a(2), since 4=224 = 2^24=22. Thus, the sequence can't look like the one I showed you, with

a(0)=1,  a(1)=1,  a(2)=2,  a(3)=1,  a(4)=1,... a(0) = 1, \; a(1) = 1, \; a(2) = 2, \; a(3) = 1,\; a(4) = 1, ... a(0)=1,a(1)=1,a(2)=2,a(3)=1,a(4)=1,...

Nice! So let's turn to the lemma, which is the really interesting part.

John Baez (Sep 09 2023 at 08:58):

I'll just quote Omar Antolín's proof, since I can't improve on it. Remember, AAA and BBB are algebras of some Lawvere theory whose underlying sets are finite:

Let mon(X,A)\mathrm{mon}(X, A)mon(X,A) be the set of monomorphisms, which here are just homomorphisms that are injective functions. I claim you can compute the cardinality of mon(X,A)\mathrm{mon}(X, A)mon(X,A) using the inclusion-exclusion principle in terms of the cardinalities of hom(Q,A)\mathrm{hom}(Q, A)hom(Q,A) for various quotients of XXX.

Indeed, for any pair of elements x,y∈Xx, y \in Xx,y∈X, let S(x,y)S(x, y)S(x,y) be the set for homomorphisms f ⁣:X→Af \colon X \to Af:X→A such that f(x)=f(y)f(x) = f(y)f(x)=f(y). The monomorphisms are just the homomorphisms that belong to none of the sets S(x,y)S(x, y)S(x,y), so you can compute how many there are via the inclusion-exclusion formula: you'll just need the cardinality of intersections of several S(xi,yi)S(x_i, y_i)S(xi​,yi​).

Now, the intersection of some S(xi,yi)S(x_i, y_i)S(xi​,yi​) is the set of homorphisms fff such that for all iii, f(xi)=f(yi)f(x_i) = f(y_i)f(xi​)=f(yi​). Those are in bijection with the homorphisms Q→AQ \to AQ→A where QQQ is the quotient of XXX obtained by adding the relations xi=yix_i=y_ixi​=yi​ for each iii.

So far I hope I've convinced you that if hom(−,A)\mathrm{hom}(-, A)hom(−,A) and hom(−,B)\mathrm{hom}(-, B)hom(−,B) are unnaturally isomorphic, so are mon(−,A)\mathrm{mon}(-, A)mon(−,A) and mon(−,B)\mathrm{mon}(-, B)mon(−,B). Now it's easy to finish: since mon(A,A)\mathrm{mon}(A, A)mon(A,A) is non-empty, so is mon(A,B)\mathrm{mon}(A, B)mon(A,B), so AAA is isomorphic to a subobject of BBB. Similarly BBB is isomorphic to a subobject of AAA, and since they are finite, they must be isomorphic.

Beautiful!

John Baez (Sep 09 2023 at 08:59):

But if you look at this argument you'll see we didn't use the full force of the assumptions. We didn't need AAA and BBB to be algebras of a Lawvere theory! They could have been topological spaces, or posets, or various other things. All we really need is a category C\mathsf{C}C of gadgets with a forgetful functor

U ⁣:C→FinSet U \colon \mathsf{C} \to \mathsf{FinSet} U:C→FinSet

that is faithful and has some extra property... roughly, that we can take an object in C\mathsf{C}C and take a quotient of it where we impose a bunch of extra relations xi=yix_i = y_ixi​=yi​, and maps out of this quotient will behave as you'd expect. More precisely, I think the extra property is this:

Given any X∈CX \in \mathsf{C}X∈C and any surjection p ⁣:U(X)→Sp \colon U(X) \to Sp:U(X)→S, there is a morphism j ⁣:X→Qj \colon X \to Qj:X→Q such that the morphisms f ⁣:X→Yf \colon X \to Yf:X→Y that factor through jjj are precisely those for which U(f)U(f)U(f) factors through ppp.

By the way: if TTT is any Lawvere theory, its fine spectrum is the sequence whose $$n$$th term is the number of isomorphism classes of TTT-algebras with nnn elements. The idea was introduced here:

  • Walter Taylor, The fine spectrum of a variety, Algebra Universalis 5 (1975), 263--303.

though Taylor used not Lawvere theories but an equivalent framework: 'varieties' in the sense of universal algebra. For a bit more on this, go here.

I'm thinking Omar's proof will go through in the following context:

  • A category C\mathbf CC, concrete over finite sets, such that
  • There's a proper factorization system (E,M)(E,M)(E,M), i.e. the EEE-maps are epimorphisms, and the underlying function of an EEE-map is surjective, while
  • The EEE-quotient object lattice of an object X∈CX\in \mathbf CX∈C has joins and is mapped injectively by the forgetful functor into the quotient set lattice of its underlying set (So a bijection under XXX between underlying sets of two quotient objects lifts to an isomorphism, though the underlying functor is perhaps not actually conservative or injective on isomorphism classes.
