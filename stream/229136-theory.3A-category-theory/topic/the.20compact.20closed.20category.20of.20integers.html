While we’re here, something related: Abramsky has a paper (2005) where he observes that the free symmetric monoidal category FSM(C)\mathsf{FSM}(\mathbb{C})FSM(C) on some C\mathbb{C}C is always traced. If we start with the terminal category then FSM(C)\mathsf{FSM}(\mathbb{C})FSM(C) is a good N\mathbb{N}N in the above sense, and the Int construction gives Z\mathbb{Z}Z. So we have Z\mathbb{Z}Z as Int(FSM(1))\mathsf{Int}(\mathsf{FSM}(\mathbb{1}))Int(FSM(1)).

Jules Hedges (Sep 30 2020 at 13:19):

Chad Nester said:

Abramsky has a paper (2005) where he observes that the free symmetric monoidal category FSM(C)\mathsf{FSM}(\mathbb{C})FSM(C) on some C\mathbb{C}C is always traced

I was thinking about this and I think I see morally why it's true. The morphisms of the free monoidal category on a category C\mathbb CC should be string diagrams whose wires are labelled by objects of C\mathbb CC and boxes are labelled by morphisms of C\mathbb CC, modulo planar isotopy + the equations of C\mathbb CC. In particular, every box has exactly one input and one output wire. That means that every use of a trace is 1 of 2 cases: either it does not create a loop and so the string can be pulled straight, or it does create a loop but in a disconnected part of the diagram

Jules Hedges (Sep 30 2020 at 13:21):

So you can define a trace by pulling the string straight in case 1, and contracting/ignoring the loop in case 2

John Baez (Sep 30 2020 at 21:31):
