Patrick Nicodemus (Aug 21 2023 at 02:43):

Some of your comments may be distractors as they are just stating things that hold in general.
Every monoidal category V\mathcal{V}V has a unit object 111. The Hom functor Hom(1,−)Hom(1,-)Hom(1,−) is a lax monoidal functor to Sets. In this sense there is always a forgetful functor from V-Cat to Cat. It may lose a lot of information as in the case where we want to view metric spaces as categories enriched over R^{+}\cup {\infty}, or very little.
It may not always be faithful; It is faithful precisely when 1 is a generator. it need not always have a left adjoint. If it does, it will necessarily send any set X to the coproduct of |X| many copies of the unit object.
If 1 is a generator of V then there are good theorems in general about gluing together objects in V out of copies of 1.

https://ncatlab.org/nlab/show/commutative+algebraic+theory

Perhaps section 3 of this page is relevant to you. It says that if T is a monad on a monoidal category (such as Sets with Cartesian product) and it is a commutative monad, then the category of algebras for that monad inherit a monoidal structure (such as the tensor product or the smash product of pointed sets).

So, if your monoidal category V is monadic over Sets, and this monoidal structure on V is the one inherited from the monoidal structure, then lifting C to a V-cat is the same as constructing T-algebra structures on each Hom set compatible with the monoidal product of T-algebras.

Patrick Nicodemus (Aug 21 2023 at 03:19):

Well, I personally have my own theoretical explanation for the Koszul convention.

I don't wish to appear as though I have a one track mind, but hear me out.
First of all, when you say "you have it backwards, the symmetry isomorphism determines the choice of differential on the tensor product" I prefer to disagree. The tensor product of chain complexes is the one that makes Dold-Kan strong monoidal, because it is inherited across that equivalence from the Day convolution of simplicial Abelian groups.
The augmented simplex category is a monoidal category, but it is not a symmetric monoidal category.
However, surprisingly enough, the free Ab-enriched category Z[Δ]\mathbb{Z}[\Delta]Z[Δ] - which is also a monoidal category - can be equipped with a symmetry isomorphism. It is easy to see that this isomorphism is uniquely determined by the isomorphism (1 + 1) \cong (1+1).
By Yoneda, this passes to a symmetry on the Day convolution monoidal structure of presheaves, which is carried across the Dold-Kan correspondence, and that is how we get the symmetry convention on the tensor product.

Patrick Nicodemus (Aug 21 2023 at 03:30):

I can give you a simple intuitive answer as to where the alternating signs come from "categorically".
The tensor product of chain complexes is cocontinuous in both arguments. If you take some simple chain complexes like S^n concentrated in a single degree, it's not obvious how to extrapolate the sign convention, but if you care about cocontinuity then T(X)\otimes T(Y) should be a colimit of copies of T(S^n) and T(S^m). And colimits involve gluing, and gluing in Abelian categories involves taking kernels, and that involves identifying f with negative g.
That's where the signs come from in my perspective, the gluing/quotienting involved in forming the tensor product.

Steve Huntsman (Aug 21 2023 at 12:23):

Brendan Murphy said:

Is there some general result about when we can endow an ordinary category with an enrichment by "reflecting" back along a functor like this?

This does not seem to answer your question as intended, but it does speak to the title of "easily checking that a category is enriched." Suppose someone hands you a finite digraph with data on the arcs and asks you to determine if it yields an enriched category. Pick a spanning polytree and pick any arc not in the polytree. Check that composition holds for that arc and the adjoining arcs on the polytree. Until/unless composition fails, repeat. This gives an algorithm linear in the number of arcs/putative morphisms. This can be slightly nontrivial because morphisms need not be invertible. Explicit examples along these lines are in https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.08334
