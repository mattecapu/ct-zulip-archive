My go-to definition of “cartesian monoidal category” is a monoidal category (C,⊗,I)(C, \otimes, I)(C,⊗,I) such that

  • the monoidal unit is a terminal object, so for each object AAA there is a unique morphism !A:A→I!_A: A \to I!A​:A→I,
  • for each pair of objects A,BA, BA,B, the “projections” ρA∘(A ⊗ !B):A⊗B→A\rho_A \circ (A \,\otimes\, !_B): A\otimes B \to AρA​∘(A⊗!B​):A⊗B→A and λB∘(!A⊗B):A⊗B→B\lambda_B \circ (!_A \otimes B): A \otimes B \to BλB​∘(!A​⊗B):A⊗B→B are product spans.

I know for sure that this definition is one that makes “Fox's theorem” work, that is, a symmetric monoidal category is cartesian if and only if it has a (necessarily unique) supply of natural comonoids.
Of course there may be other more succinct equivalent definitions.
