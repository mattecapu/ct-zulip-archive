John Baez (Mar 13 2024 at 20:36):

If Ab\mathsf{Ab}Ab is the category of abelian groups, I tend to think of an Ab\mathsf{Ab}Ab-enriched category as a 'ringoid', since a one-object one is just a ring. I define a module of an Ab\mathsf{Ab}Ab-enriched category AAA as an Ab\mathsf{Ab}Ab-enriched functor F:A→AbF: A \to \mathsf{Ab}F:A→Ab.

I define the category AModA \mathsf{Mod}AMod to be the category of modules and natural transformations between them. In the case where AAA is a ring, this is the usual category of modules of that ring.

Since AModA \mathsf{Mod}AMod is is naturally Ab\mathsf{Ab}Ab-enriched, we can then go on and define AModModA \mathsf{Mod} \mathsf{Mod}AModMod... at least if we're not worrying about size issues, or we choose a way to deal with them.

Kevin Carlson (aka Arlin) (Mar 13 2024 at 20:37):

Yeah, and this is finitely presentable modules so there's no size issues. It just seems kind of crazy to see a ModMod having a nice universal property.

John Baez (Mar 13 2024 at 20:43):

If we let fgProj(A)\mathsf{fgProj}(\mathsf{A})fgProj(A) be the category of finitely generated presentable modules of a ring or ringoid A\mathsf{A}A, I believe fgProj(A)\mathsf{fgProj}(\mathsf{A})fgProj(A) is the [[Cauchy completion]] of that ringoid in the 2-category of Ab\mathsf{Ab}Ab-enriched categories. So my guess is that fgProj\mathsf{fgProj}fgProj has fairly nice properties when you apply it twice - like a [[lax idempotent 2-monad]].
