Tobias Fritz (Jul 07 2020 at 14:04):

Hi all. Perhaps I'm misunderstanding the question, but how about considering combs with a "hole" of type I→II \to II→I, i.e. the case B=C=IB = C = IB=C=I in Mario's notation? Then plugging in morphisms into the hole doesn't really do anything, and the question amounts to asking merely whether morphisms A→DA \to DA→D are in bijection with composable pairs of morphisms A→W→DA \to W \to DA→W→D modulo the equivalence (f,gh)∼(hf,g)(f,gh) \sim (hf,g)(f,gh)∼(hf,g). Right?

Assuming that this is correct, then this happens if and only if every commutative square, with AAA top left and DDD bottom right, has a diagonal filler (bottom left to top right). The monoidal structure now doesn't even play a role anymore. And of course, in most categories it's easy to find particular squares which have no such lift, for example in Set or Rel.

This phenomenon feels vaguely related to Hochschild homology, and perhaps there is a precise relation to the cohomology of the nerve of the category under consideration, but I'm not sure.

Guillaume Boisseau (Jul 07 2020 at 14:08):

It actually is the case that C(x,z)C(x, z)C(x,z) is in bijection with ∫yC(x,y)×C(y,z)\int^y C(x, y) \times C(y, z)∫yC(x,y)×C(y,z) in any category. That's an instance of coend calculus/Yoneda. What you're missing is that W is not fixed here, and that makes the bijection go through. So when the hole has type I→II \rightarrow II→I the property always holds.

Aleks Kissinger (Jul 07 2020 at 14:57):

I think it will be true any time a category has a faithful monoidal embedding into a compact closed category. so, a possible place to look for counter-examples are categories with no such faithful embedding. i only know artificial examples of this. e.g. categories you build from generators and relations where adding caps and cups enable more "non-convex" ways to match rules than you had before

Aleks Kissinger (Jul 07 2020 at 14:59):

e.g. a PROP where you have X : 2 -> 0, Y : 0 -> 2, and a rule whose LHS and (Y+id);(id+SWAP);(X+id), where '+' is monoidal product here

Aleks Kissinger (Jul 07 2020 at 15:01):

then, suppose there is also a Z : 1 -> 1. Then Y;(1+Z);X may or may not contain a redex for the rule, depending on whether caps/cups are allowed

Guillaume Boisseau (Jul 07 2020 at 15:14):

Aleks Kissinger said:

I think it will be true any time a category has a faithful monoidal embedding into a compact closed category

I don't think that's true. If we monoidally embed, we get two combs that coincide only on the image of the embedding. And this image does not contain the cups and caps we need to deduce that the original optics are equal.

Tobias Fritz (Jul 07 2020 at 18:25):

So here's a (rather degenerate) way of constructing different optics of the same type which are contextually equivalent in @Aleks Kissinger sense, meaning that they behave the same with respect to plugging in any morphism (with external wires) into their "hole". Hopefully it's correct now.

Let M\mathcal{M}M be any commutative monoid, written additively, and consider the symmetric monoidal category with M\mathcal{M}M as its monoid of objects and only identity morphisms. Then the set of optics

∫WHom(A,W⊗B)×Hom(W⊗C,D)\int^W \mathrm{Hom}(A,W \otimes B) \times \mathrm{Hom}(W \otimes C, D)∫WHom(A,W⊗B)×Hom(W⊗C,D)

degenerates to

{W∈M∣A=W+B ∧ D=W+C}.\{W \in \mathcal{M} \mid A = W + B \: \land \: D = W + C \}.{W∈M∣A=W+B∧D=W+C}.

Since any two parallel morphisms are equal, the contextual equivalence with respect to plugging in morphisms with external wires into the holes is trivial: any two optics of the same type are contextually equivalent. However, they nevertheless need not be equal: it's easy to find M\mathcal{M}M for which the above equations for WWW can have multiple solutions. For example, take M\mathcal{M}M to be a monoid of subsets of a fixed ground set with respect to union as addition, and take A=B=C=DA = B = C = DA=B=C=D.

If this construction looks too degenerate to be interesting, then try to consider the skeleton of your favourite SMC, and there's a good chance that you'll find instances where the equations A≅W⊗BA \cong W \otimes BA≅W⊗B and D≅W⊗CD \cong W \otimes CD≅W⊗C will have multiple non-isomorphic solutions for WWW. This happens for example in the category of abelian groups, where Z/nZ⊗Z/mZ≅0\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z} \otimes \mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z} \cong 0Z/nZ⊗Z/mZ≅0 as soon as mmm and nnn are coprime.
