Logical independence versus Probabilistic independence

Recently, I found a formula that clarifies the link between stochastic and logical independence, but I'm still struggling to escape the measure theory aspect. Ideally, my goal would be to abstract both concepts of independence in a single setting.

For anyone reading this: I am potentially looking for insights or references that could help clarify the content below in terms of category theory. Any direction or references would be welcome.

Logical independence

For a given set of axioms BBB (say written in a logic L\mathcal{L}L which uses propositional logic as a basis), define ¬B:={¬φ ∣ φ∈B}\neg B:= \{\neg \varphi~|~\varphi \in B\}¬B:={¬φ ∣ φ∈B}.

One says that a set BBB of axioms in L\mathcal{L}L is independent from another set AAA of axioms in L\mathcal{L}L if we can find two models M\mathcal{M}M and M′\mathcal{M}'M′ such that:

M⊨A∪¬B\mathcal{M} \vDash A \cup \neg BM⊨A∪¬B

M′⊨A∪B\mathcal{M}' \vDash A \cup BM′⊨A∪B

Stochastic independence

Let (Ω,E,P)(\Omega,\mathcal{E},P)(Ω,E,P) be a measure space for a probability measure P:E→[0,1]P:\mathcal{E} \to [0,1]P:E→[0,1].

For every pair (A,B)(A,B)(A,B) of events A∈EA \in \mathcal{E}A∈E and B∈EB \in \mathcal{E}B∈E, we define δ(A,B)=P(A∩B)−P(A)P(B)\delta(A,B) = P(A\cap B)-P(A)P(B)δ(A,B)=P(A∩B)−P(A)P(B). We say that the events AAA and BBB are independent if the formula δ(A,B)=0\delta(A,B) = 0δ(A,B)=0 holds.

Proposition. The formula δ(A,B)=P(A∩B)P(Ac∩Bc)−P(A∩Bc)P(Ac∩B)\delta(A,B) = P(A \cap B)P(A^c \cap B^c) - P(A \cap B^c)P(A^c \cap B)δ(A,B)=P(A∩B)P(Ac∩Bc)−P(A∩Bc)P(Ac∩B) holds.
proof. Write P(A)=P(A∩B)+P(A∩Bc)P(A) = P(A \cap B) + P(A \cap B^c)P(A)=P(A∩B)+P(A∩Bc) and P(B)=P(B∩A)+P(B∩Ac)P(B) = P(B \cap A) + P(B \cap A^c)P(B)=P(B∩A)+P(B∩Ac) and insert these expressions in the expression for δ(A,B)\delta(A,B)δ(A,B). Expand, then factor out P(B∩A)P(B \cap A) P(B∩A) and finally observe that
P(Ac∩Bc)=1−P(B∩A)−P(B∩Ac)−P(A∩Bc)P(A^c \cap B^c) = 1- P(B \cap A) -P(B \cap A^c)-P(A \cap B^c)P(Ac∩Bc)=1−P(B∩A)−P(B∩Ac)−P(A∩Bc)
□\square□

Now, our goal is to reformulate the expression above. For this, we can use the product probability measure P2:E⊗E→[0,1]P_2:\mathcal{E}\otimes \mathcal{E}\to [0,1]P2​:E⊗E→[0,1], which is generated by the images P2(X×Y)=P(X)P(Y)P_2(X \times Y) = P(X)P(Y)P2​(X×Y)=P(X)P(Y) for every (X,Y)∈E×E(X,Y) \in \mathcal{E} \times \mathcal{E}(X,Y)∈E×E. Also, for every pair (X,Y)(X,Y)(X,Y) of events in E×E\mathcal{E}\times\mathcal{E}E×E, let us denote X⊕Y=(X∩Yc)×(Xc∩Y)X \oplus Y = (X \cap Y^c) \times (X^c \cap Y)X⊕Y=(X∩Yc)×(Xc∩Y).

Our new notations reduce the expression of δ(A,B)\delta(A,B)δ(A,B) to this:

δ(A,B)=P2(A⊕Bc)−P2(A⊕B)\delta(A,B) = P_2\big(A \oplus B^c\big) - P_2\big(A \oplus B\big)δ(A,B)=P2​(A⊕Bc)−P2​(A⊕B)

In other words, two events AAA and BBB are independent if the probability of finding a configuration in Ω⊗Ω\Omega \otimes \OmegaΩ⊗Ω that validates the event A⊕BcA \oplus B^cA⊕Bc is equal to the probability of finding a configuration in Ω⊗Ω\Omega \otimes \OmegaΩ⊗Ω that validates the event A⊕BA \oplus BA⊕B.

Conclusion and question

There seems to be an analogy between stochastic and logical independence here. I tried to come up with an algebraic definition of stochastic independence by requiring a bijection A⊕Bc≅A⊕BA \oplus B^c \cong A \oplus BA⊕Bc≅A⊕B, but this hasn't helped me escape the probability measure context.

Does anyone have intuitions on this or know some references that have explored this?

Sam Staton (Jul 08 2024 at 07:59):

I might be misunderstanding this discussion, but if not, one nice angle is effect algebras. These sort-of generalize Boolean algebras by thinking in terms of partial operations.

On a finite set nnn, a probability measure is the same thing as an effect algebra homomorphism P(n)→[0,1]P(n)\to [0,1]P(n)→[0,1]. Here P(n)P(n)P(n) is regarded with the partial monoid structure given by disjoint union, and [0,1][0,1][0,1] is regarded with the partial monoid structure given by addition where it is possible.

It's not as ad hoc as it might sound because these things fully embed in presheaves on finite Boolean algebras.

For the infinitary case, there are σ\sigmaσ-effect algebras which have the analogous correspondence, since every σ\sigmaσ-algebra is also a σ\sigmaσ-effect algebra.

This Saturday, I will be flying to Tokyo to give a talk at the FHE & MPC Tokyo Residency, organized by the Ethereum community. Ethereum is also hosting the EDCON 2024 in Tokyo during that time.

At the residency, I will present my paper on Constructing a Fully Homomorphic Encryption Scheme with the Yoneda Lemma. The audience will have diverse math backgrounds, though most will not be familiar with category theory.

In a few lines, asymmetric cryptography has been relying on the commutativity of the Yoneda Lemma to produce cryptosystems with sound correctness. For example:

  1. In ElGamal, you take a (commutative) group GGG, choose a secret key x∈Zx \in \mathbb{Z}x∈Z and publish the power gxg^xgx for some publicly known g∈Gg \in Gg∈G. To encrypt a message m∈Gm \in Gm∈G, choose a homomorphism of the form h:f↦fyh:f \mapsto f^yh:f↦fy and send the ciphertext (h(g),m⋅h(gx))(h(g),m\cdot h(g^x))(h(g),m⋅h(gx)).

    How do you decrypt this ciphertext? If you have the secret key xxx, you can compute m=m⋅h(gx)⋅(h(g)x)−1m = m\cdot h(g^x) \cdot (h(g)^{x})^{-1}m=m⋅h(gx)⋅(h(g)x)−1.

  2. With lattic-based frameworks, you take an RRR-module MMM, choose a secret key x∈Mnx \in M^nx∈Mn and publish a vector of the form Ax+eAx+eAx+e for some publicly known (n,N)(n,N)(n,N)-matrix AAA and (forgettable) small random vector e∈MNe \in M^Ne∈MN. To encrypt a message m∈Mm \in Mm∈M, choose a homomorphism of the form h:f↦yTfh:f \mapsto y^Tfh:f↦yTf and send the ciphertext (h(A),m+h(Ax+e))(h(A),m + h(Ax+e))(h(A),m+h(Ax+e)).

    How do you decrypt this ciphertext? If you have the secret key xxx, you can compute m≃m+h(Ax+e)−h(A)xm \simeq m+h(Ax+e)-h(A)xm≃m+h(Ax+e)−h(A)x.

Conclusion: the main ingredients to the decryption algorithms are the commutativity equations:

  • h(gx)=h(g)xh(g^x) = h(g)^xh(gx)=h(g)x and
  • h(Ax)=h(A)xh(Ax) = h(A)xh(Ax)=h(A)x.

Now where is Yoneda? Well, for a given limit sketch TTT, the Yoneda Lemma gives us an isomorphism

Hom(T(d,−),X)≅X(d)\mathsf{Hom}(T(d,-),X) \cong X(d)Hom(T(d,−),X)≅X(d)

representing any morphism g:T(d,−)→Xg:T(d,-) \to Xg:T(d,−)→X as some map "x↦⟨g,x⟩x \mapsto \langle g, x\ranglex↦⟨g,x⟩" for some representative g∈X(d)g \in X(d)g∈X(d). And for every morphism h:X→Yh:X \to Yh:X→Y, it also gives us the naturality property

h(⟨g,x⟩)=⟨h(g),x⟩,h(\langle g, x\rangle) = \langle h(g), x\rangle,h(⟨g,x⟩)=⟨h(g),x⟩,

which is basically recovering the commutative ingredient needed to decrypt in ElGamal and lattice-based systems. After noticing that, you can write down what you need to recover most FHE cryptosystems using the Yoneda Lemma. As a direct application, you can choose arbitrary theories TTT and see what kind of cryptosystem you get.

Rémy Tuyéras (Sep 18 2025 at 11:07):

Hello all, it has been a while.

I wanted to share an update that connects directly to higher category theory and agent systems.

TL;DR. I have been building Summoner, a Python SDK with a Rust server that orchestrates multi-agent systems using the language of category theory and higher structures (2-categories, weak higher categories). The client–server layer can be modeled as a localization of models

[T,Set]→Mod(T)↪[T,Set][T,\mathbf{Set}] \to \mathbf{Mod}(T) \hookrightarrow [T,\mathbf{Set}][T,Set]→Mod(T)↪[T,Set]

for a Lawvere theory TTT.

Context

If you have explored multi-agent frameworks such as LangChain, CrewAI, LangGraph, MCP, or Google's A2A:

  • LangChain / CrewAI: application frameworks where you compose behaviors as a graph.
  • LangGraph: a graph engine/DSL for stateful agent workflows on top of LangChain.
  • Google A2A: a client-server protocol that exchanges JSON-encoded actions (JSON-RPC by default).
  • MCP: a one-client/many-server protocol for model-to-tool requests and data connections.

Summoner aims to unify these views in a single language grounded in categories and higher structure, while keeping the ergonomics of decorator-based Python.

From decorators to arrows

In MCP-style stacks you often register a tool by decorating a function:

@mcp.tool("signal_X")
def process(the_input):
    # ...
    return my_output

Semantically, when the server supplies an input tagged signal_X, the system applies process:

(the_input,signal_X)→  process  my_output.(\texttt{the\_input}, \texttt{signal\_X}) \xrightarrow{\;\texttt{process}\;} \texttt{my\_output}.(the_input,signal_X)process​my_output.

This is simple and local; many users start here.

What graph-based frameworks look like in practice

Graph frameworks then ask you to wire nodes and edges explicitly. For readers who have not used them, the code often looks like:

workflow = SomeFramework()

def agent_behavior(...):
    # define some agent behavior
    ...

def another_agent_behavior(...):
    ...

# associate objects with functions
workflow.add_node("object_A", agent_behavior)
workflow.add_node("object_B", another_agent_behavior)

# draw the edge
workflow.add_edge("object_A", "object_B")

This is workable for small demos. At scale it becomes heavy on bookkeeping: keeping names synchronized with functions, reviewing edge lists for correctness, and debugging topologies instead of focusing on agent behavior.

Summoner: routes as arrows, flows as automata

Summoner combines decorator ergonomics with categorical structure.

You program routes that you read as labeled arrows and higher cells. Objects are routable names, and arrows are routable labels between them. You write handlers with decorators, and the runtime treats incoming messages as transitions in a small automaton generated by those routes.

Objects A,BA, BA,B:

# Object-activated behaviors
@agent.receive(route="object_A")
def on_A(input_A):
    ...

@agent.receive(route="object_B")
def on_B(input_B):
    ...

Arrows f,g:A→Bf, g: A \to Bf,g:A→B as labeled routes (read A⇒fBA \xRightarrow{f} BAf​B):

# A ==[ arr_f ]==> B
@agent.receive(route="object_A --[ arr_f ]--> object_B")
def on_f(payload):
    ...

# A ==[ arr_g ]==> B
@agent.receive(route="object_A --[ arr_g ]--> object_B")
def on_g(payload):
    ...

A 2-cell η:f⇒g\eta: f \Rightarrow gη:f⇒g as a route between arrows:

# f =={ eta }==> g
@agent.receive(route="arr_f =={ eta }==> arr_g")
def on_eta(event):
    ...

Custom arrow notation

You can customize the surface grammar of arrows to match the structure you want to emphasize, including double-category-style notations:

flow = agent.flow().activate()
flow.add_arrow_style(stem="-", brackets=("[", "]"), separator=",", tip=">")
flow.add_arrow_style(stem="=", brackets=("{", "}"), separator=",", tip=")")

# Use your preferred notations in routes:
@agent.receive(route="object_A --[ arr_f ]--> object_B")
def on_f(...): ...

@agent.receive(route="arr_f =={ eta }==) arr_g")
def on_eta(...): ...

Semantics sketch

Operationally, routes generate the path category of the underlying graph, and message handling interprets this free category into an effects category (for example, the Kleisli category of an async monad). Equivalently, one can view this using the underlying localization of a (higher) Lawvere theory, where:

  • a message mmm is an element m∈F(t)m \in F(t)m∈F(t);
  • the route = t label is a type in the theory;
  • computing the underlying model of F:T→SetF: T \to \mathbf{Set}F:T→Set amounts to building transfinite closures over pairs (m,t)(m,t)(m,t), yielding F′(t)F'(t)F′(t) for a localized model F′:T→SetF': T \to \mathbf{Set}F′:T→Set.

I would welcome feedback on a functorial account, naturality properties, or an open-systems perspective using cospans and pushouts for wiring. A merge operator already exists to compute colimits of the route graph.

What this enables in practice. Using this route language, we have implemented end-to-end agent workflows: protocol handshakes (challenge–response with nonces and replay protection), capability and version negotiation, and multi-round coordination (bids/asks, quorum/commit).

Happy to discuss, and to align the notation with whatever categorical lens the community prefers.

Hello CT community! I have some news on my project Summoner, which I think could interest category theorists that are currently exploring applications to the industry.

1) AI-first businesses are increasingly exploring graph-like structures...

...and category theory could be next!

Understanding the impact of decisions in a business process is high-stakes. Since LLM-based agents do not reliably anticipate the operational impact of their choices, many AI-first companies are turning toward graph-structured representations to make decision traces, dependencies, and alternatives explicit.

The main trigger that helped push this framing is the "context graphs" article from Foundation Capital: AI’s trillion-dollar opportunity: Context graphs

After that, a large number of articles flooded the AI business space, for example:

  • https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/how-do-you-build-context-graph-jaya-gupta-xicwe/
  • https://subramanya.ai/2026/01/14/context-graphs-are-a-trillion-dollar-opportunity-but-who-captures-it/
  • and many more

At Summoner, we see a natural next step after graphs: compositional and/or higher structures. Once you start taking graphs seriously, you quickly run into questions about how you represent not only decisions, but also revisions, exceptions, and amendments, and how these interact with the rest of the process. This is where the internal category-theoretic language becomes useful.

This is also the direction we have been taking on the business side: leveraging the internal category-theoretic language of Summoner to provide analytics on decisions and their dependencies, including counterfactual comparisons of "what happened" versus "what could have happened".

Concretely, we have recently demonstrated how businesses can leverage two-level category-like structures to analyze the impact of AI agents’ decisions on business operations: mini demo here

gif

These structures can be compared to 2-categories, if you infer a whiskering-style logic for composing decisions and amendments between decisions. In that framing, you can reason about "arrows between arrows", and track how amendments propagate through an execution trace.
