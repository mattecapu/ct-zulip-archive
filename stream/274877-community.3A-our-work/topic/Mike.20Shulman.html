Fawzi Hreiki (Jan 28 2021 at 16:07):

Well I guess the way I understand it, there are two notions or layers of model theory in categorical logic.

There’s the question of presenting certain kinds of categories syntactically (via generators and relations) and reasoning internal to them (using deduction rules).
So, like how we can present and reason in CCCs using lambda calculus.

There’s also the question of studying what the corresponding categories of models look like (by homming into sets or some other ‘big’ category).

A lot has been done on the model theory of ‘lower strength’ logics (e.g. propositional, algebraic, predicate) but I haven’t really seen much on the model theory of higher or dependent types.

Jacques Carette (Sep 08 2021 at 16:06):

There are indeed many different 'tactic' styles. The styles that hide a lot of computation in an unobservable medium annoy me - it's all black magic that does not provide much explanation. Proofs should have explanatory power (they do in normal mathematics). Proofs whose contents is merely "it's true, really, I've checked, you don't need to worry about it" feel rather unsatisfying. PVS's grind is perhaps the worst offender!

Even though I have published a lot of papers on meta-programming, somehow the MTAC language as a means to give instructions on how to build my proofs doesn't really resonate with me. I agree 100% that Agda hides that part, because that is mostly done in an invisible way in the IDE (the equivalent of 'intro' and 'intros' and 'case split' and ... all exist in Agda too, but they are IDE "gestures" rather that residuated in the proof).

As it turns out, for the kinds of results that I'm interested in, the explicit proof terms are very important to me. So I like seeing them, Agda/Idris style. Whether it's PL meta-theory or category theory in a constructive setting, I like to be forced to do explicit equational proofs. One huge advantage: when doing n-category theory, one can immediately see what will be hard work in (n+1)-category theory. This is also a huge disadvantage if you have no intention of going there! It's a similar issue with strict/weak, for that matter.

Note that, for the exact example of associativity, that can be done in Agda, in multiple ways.

Personally, I'm very happy for the proliferation of systems. The design space is huge, and it should be well explored. And I don't think there is a global optimum, just local ones, that cater to different styles.

Title: Lifting Grothendieck universes to Grothendieck toposes

Abstract: A Grothendieck topos is a category that behaves much like the category of sets, and in particular interprets constructive type theory. Now that the semester is over, here's a report on how the class went. I went the route of "this is the abstract definition of a category. Mike Shulman (Dec 18 2024 at 07:49):

It was interesting to see which concepts can be simplified in the concrete case and which can't. I think the concrete case was helpful in introducing products, because I could first define "concrete products" to be literally the cartesian product set with some object-structure and such that pairings and projections preserve morphism-ness, and then generalize from there to the abstract notion. And although the familiar concrete categories have concrete products, it's not too hard to give examples of concrete categories with non-concrete products (e.g. Set×Set\rm Set\times SetSet×Set, with concretization functor the disjoint union -- I described this as the category of "two-colored sets"), to make the point that the abstract notion is useful to have.

And after that, we then dualized the abstract notion of product to give the abstract notion of coproduct, which has no useful "concrete" version since the forgetful functors to Set very rarely preserve coproducts (for the sort of algebraic categories we were studying), further justifying the abstract version.

Mike Shulman (Dec 18 2024 at 07:52):

Mike Shulman (Dec 18 2024 at 07:57):

I was a bit surprised to find that concrete categories did not help in talking about subobjects. I expected it to be easy to just say "a subobject of XXX is a sub set of XXX that is an object and such that the inclusion function is a morphism", and not have to bring in abstract notions like monomorphism. But it turns out that in order to prove basically anything useful about "subobjects" in a concrete category, you have to assume a stronger property that if f:A→Xf:A\to Xf:A→X is any morphism that factors through the subobject as a function, the factorization is also a morphism. This is complicated enough that I didn't want to get into it; one might argue it's even more complicated than the notion of monomorphism. So in the end, I didn't talk about subobjects abstractly at all. I did talk about kernels abstractly, with the appropriate universal property in any category with zero morphisms, because I then wanted to dualize it in order to introduce cokernels and quotient objects. But I just remarked that in all the categories we're interested in, a kernel can be constructed as the subset of elements that map to zero, without trying to be more general.

Mike Shulman (Aug 12 2025 at 16:46):

Today I discovered (the hard way) that there's more of a difference between monads and applicative functors (as usually defined in OCaml) than I thought.

Mike Shulman (Aug 12 2025 at 16:46):

Before explaining it, let me review applicative functors briefly, since they're a bit less well-known than monads. To a category theorist, an applicative functor is just a lax monoidal (strong) functor. This is the same as a functor of multicategories, which means (in functional programming language) you have a map from any multi-argument function type such as a -> b -> c -> d to F a -> F b -> F c -> F d. If the basic syntax of a monad's "bind" operation M a -> (a -> M b) -> M b is written (as in usually is in OCaml) with a let-binding:

let* x = mx in
my

where mx : M a and fun x -> my : a -> M b, then the basic operation of an applicative functor can be written (and usually is in OCaml) as

let+ x = mx
and+ y = my
and+ z = mz in
w

where x : F a, y : F b, z : F c, and fun x y z -> w : a -> b -> c -> d. This looks similar to the iterated monadic syntax

let* x = mx in
let* y = my in
let* z = mz in
return w

with the differences being that (1) in the monadic syntax, each monadic value such asmy can depend on the previously bound variables such as x, whereas in the applicative case none of the applicative values in a let+/and+ can depend on other variables bound in it, and (2) in the monadic case the body (such as return w) is also monadic, whereas in the applicative case it is not.

And indeed, one can prove mathematically that any strong monad is a lax monoidal functor.

Mike Shulman (Aug 12 2025 at 16:49):

I thought that this meant that if I had a function defined for any monad (which in OCaml means it must be defined in a parametrized module, i.e. what ML programmers call a "functor"), and my definition actually worked for any applicative functor (that is, all of its nested let* sequences could be rewritten as let+/and+ sequences), then I could just as well define it in that generality. Then in order to use it with monads as I was before, I could just make all my monads into applicative functors and use the applicative version.

But it turns out this is wrong, at least in a language like OCaml that also has non-monadic (and non-applicative) effects.

Mike Shulman (Aug 12 2025 at 16:54):

A nested let* operation like

let* x = mx in
let* y = my in
return z

is desugared to

bind mx (fun x -> bind my (fun y -> return z))

In particular, this means that my is passed to bind inside a closure, and therefore is not evaluated unless bind chooses to call that closure. Which it usually will, but it might not, e.g. for an error monad if the first argument of bind already produces an error it will not even call the closure (and indeed, in the monadic case it couldn't, because it won't have a value to pass it).

By contrast, the corresponding let+/and+ sequence

let+ x = mx
and+ y = my in
z

is desugared to

apply (zip mx my) (fun (x,y) -> z)

where apply and zip are the primitives of an applicative functor. Note that here my is not inside a closure, and therefore (since OCaml is a strict language) it will always be evaluated, regardless of the result of evaluating mx. If my causes external effects, such as altering mutable variables or throwing exceptions or performing algebraic effects, this difference can matter (as I discovered).

Mike Shulman (Aug 12 2025 at 17:00):

Currently I have worked around this by redefining my "applicative functors" to thunk the arguments of zip, so that instead of the above let+/and+ syntax (which doesn't support the thunked version) I have to write by hand

apply (zip (fun () -> mx) (fun () -> my)) (fun (x,y) -> z)

This is a little more annoying, but it allows me to make a monad into an "applicative functor" in a more careful way, where the second thunk is only forced inside the closure argument of bind. That way when the error monad is made into an "applicative functor", again the second value won't be evaluated at all if the first one errors, which is what I wanted.

I put "applicative functor" in quotes because it's quite possible the original semantics is what everyone else intends by that phrase, so maybe this version should be called something else. I think the most obvious way to distinguish them would be to call the original version "strict", in the functional-programming sense of a "strict function" being one that always evaluates all its arguments, but maybe I could instead call this one "non-strict".
