Well, in the spirit of sharing WIP, I am finalising a short note that puts strong constraints on the classification of finite-dimensional bundle gerbes on manifolds. If you are stack-minded, this is saying something about how one can give finite-dimensional Lie groupoid presentations of differentiable stacks over a manifold, of a certain sort.

A nice corollary is that no smooth 2-group extension of a compact simple connected Lie group (not necessarily simply-connected) can be topologically nontrivial and given by a finite-dimensional crossed module of Lie groups at the same time.

It also implies that on a manifold MMM with finite π1\pi_1π1​, every classical twist of K-theory of MMM arising from a principal GGG-bundle and a central extension of GGG by U(1)U(1)U(1) is torsion, where GGG is any finite-dimensional Lie group (this is well-known to be true for G=PU(n)G=PU(n)G=PU(n)).

More geometry/topology, but there some category theory hiding in there.

Regarding 2., it turned out very nice: given a Milnor regular infinite dimensional Lie group (i.e. almost any nice one you can think of) GGG with Lie algebra g\mathfrak{g}g, define the abelian Lie algebra a=g/[g,g]‾\mathfrak{a} = \mathfrak{g}/\overline{[\mathfrak{g},\mathfrak{g}]}a=g/[g,g]​. This has as underlying additive group a locally convex tvs (and is a Milnor regular Lie group, too). Then the space of bi-invariant 2-forms on GGG is isomorphic to the space of continuous alternating bilinear forms on a\mathfrak{a}a. One might call these "presymplectic" forms on a\mathfrak{a}a, where the "pre-" means you don't ask for non-degeneracy, and so don't have to worry about what is going on in the more subtle tvs setting with that.

More specifically, given a Milnor regular Lie group GGG, the universal cover G~\widetilde{G}G is also one, and it admits a smooth homomorphism to the additive group a\mathfrak{a}a by various nasty technical theorems. The article does the finite-dimensional case first, where the technicalities are just part of the toolkit of people working in finite-dimensional Lie theory. Classifying bi-invariant 2-forms on infinite-dimensional Lie groups

A bi-invariant differential 2-form on a Lie group G is a highly constrained object, being determined by purely linear data: an Ad-invariant alternating bilinear form on the Lie algebra of G. On a compact connected Lie group these have an known classification, in terms of de Rham cohomology, which is here generalised to arbitrary finite-dimensional Lie groups, at the cost of losing the connection to cohomology. This expanded classification extends further to all Milnor regular infinite-dimensional Lie groups. I give some examples of (structured) diffeomorphism groups to which the result on bi-invariant forms applies. For symplectomorphism and volume-preserving diffeomorphism groups the spaces of bi-invariant 2-forms are finite-dimensional, and related to the de Rham cohomology of the original compact manifold. In the particular case of the infinite-dimensional projective unitary group PU(H) the classification invalidates an assumption made by Mathai and the author about a certain 2-form on this Banach Lie group.

Another funny thing is that Cantor gives his notion of what we would call order isomorphism between well-ordered sets, calling them 'ähnlich', whereas for plain sets he called them 'äquivalent' if they are in bijection.

Dedekind has a precisely-dated edit to his June-July 1887 draft of Was sind und was sollen die Zahlen, namely where he adopts the term 'ähnlich' to describe a function that is a bijection of 'systems' (i.e. sets)—10th July 1887—dropping the earlier term 'deutlich' (perhaps familiar from 'eineindeutig', one-to-one, or 'eindeutig', single-valued).

Cantor had told Dedekind in 1882 he didn't know how to prove CSB in general (he'd proved it for countable sets and those of cardinality ω1\omega_1ω1​, and recall for Cantor everything is well-orderable). So five years later that he published it without proof was maybe a little bit annoying for the very methodical and precise Dedekind.

If it's a coincidence that Cantor's paper with the CSB theorem was published sans proof, and Dedekind proved CSB, and also adopted a term that appeared in Cantor's paper, independently within a week, that's a mighty coincidence.
