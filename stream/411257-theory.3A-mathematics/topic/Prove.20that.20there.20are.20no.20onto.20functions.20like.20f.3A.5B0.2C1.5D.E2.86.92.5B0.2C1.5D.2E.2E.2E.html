It seems like we can simplify the idea of a function. “Left-total” could be called “completeness” (in the sense that the set XXX from X×YX \times YX×Y must be fully “present” or “represented” in (the first position of each ordered pair in) RRR. “Right-unique” roughly means “non-duplication” (of elements in the first position of each ordered pair in RRR).

So we could say that a function is a subset of a set SSS that is “complete” and “non-duplicating” (I think). Categorical products are a way of expressing “combinations of things”; maybe we can envision that if you take an nnn-ary product of sets (like X×Y×Z×…X \times Y \times Z \times …X×Y×Z×…), and then you take a subset of that set, whichever of the sets is represented “completely and without duplication” (which could be multiple) are the “domain sets” of the function. Todd Trimble (Jun 15 2024 at 16:24):

How much have you played with this? There's a slogan that a function (a total well-defined relation) is precisely a relation with a right adjoint. That is to say, a relation RRR from XXX to YYY is well-defined if for the opposite relation Rop:Y→XR^{op}: Y \to XRop:Y→X the composition R∘RopR \circ R^{op}R∘Rop is contained in the identity relation on YYY (the diagonal subset in Y×YY \times YY×Y). And that RRR is total if the identity relation on XXX is contained in Rop∘RR^{op} \circ RRop∘R. And if S:Y→XS: Y \to XS:Y→X satisfies both R∘S⊆1YR \circ S \subseteq 1_YR∘S⊆1Y​ and 1X⊆S∘R1_X \subseteq S \circ R1X​⊆S∘R, then S=RopS = R^{op}S=Rop.

All this has been very heavily investigated under the banner of "cartesian bicategory". The concepts of injectivity, surjectivity, etc. can also be treated in these terms.

Todd Trimble (Jun 21 2024 at 15:50):

So in a 2-category, 2-cells are functors.

No, and I'd better say some more about this.

The 2-category that most everyone first learns about when studying category theory is Cat\mathsf{Cat}Cat, where the 0-cells are categories C\mathbf{C}C, where the 1-cells F:C→DF: \mathbf{C} \to \mathbf{D}F:C→D are functors, and where the 2-cells θ:F→G\theta: F \to Gθ:F→G are natural transformations between functors. This is parallel to how practically the first category people learn about is the category of sets, where the objects (or 0-cells) are sets and the morphisms (or 1-cells) are functions.

Todd Trimble (Jun 21 2024 at 15:50):

But people who take categories seriously learn pretty early on that while, yes, many large categories consist of structured sets (groups, rings, topological spaces, what have you) as 0-cells, and structure-preserving functions as 1-cells, not all categories are of that type. For example, any group can be considered as a category with one object, whose elements give you the morphisms. Another example is a partially ordered set, where the objects are elements of that set, and the morphisms x→yx \to yx→y are instances x≤yx \leq yx≤y of the order relation. For either of these examples, there is no presupposition that the morphisms have to be "structure-preserving functions". There is much more flexibility as to what categories can be, a point that Bourbaki (Weil in particular) didn't fully appreciate when there was early debate over what role categories might play in their grand opus.

Likewise, when it comes to 2-categories, while many large 2-categories consist of structured categories and suitable structure-preserving functors between them (and further natural transformations between those, that are suitably compatible with this structure), not all 2-categories have to be considered that way. One should think of the axioms for 2-categories as permitting a lot of flexibility in what the 0-cells, 1-cells, and 2-cells could be. One example is Rel\mathsf{Rel}Rel, as described above. There is not an automatic sense that the 1-cells (you said 2-cells, but the same point applies) should be considered functors of some type.

Two functors are adjoint not if their composition (in both directions) equals an identity functor, but more “loosely”: if there is a certain “correspondence”, not between fg and gf, but even “looser” (I think) - a correspondence between a map from the identity functor 1X​ to gf, and a map from fg to 1Y​. This “correspondence” is given by “triangular equations”.

The background story takes a while to set out. You may be most familiar with adjoint functors, where you have two functors F:C→DF: \mathbf{C} \to \mathbf{D}F:C→D and G:D→CG: \mathbf{D} \to \mathbf{C}G:D→C and a natural bijection of the form

Returning now to adjoint functors: the familiar way of defining them in terms of isomorphisms of hom-sets says that there is an isomorphism of two Set\mathsf{Set}Set-valued functors:

hom⁡D(F−,−)≅hom⁡C(−,G−)\hom_\mathbf{D}(F-, -) \cong \hom_\mathbf{C}(-, G-)homD​(F−,−)≅homC​(−,G−)

This is not wholly within the language of category theory, since it brings in an "extraneous" category of sets which is not itself definable in the language of category theory. Fortunately, by a clever application of the Yoneda lemma, there's a way to circumvent this.

Todd Trimble (Jun 21 2024 at 21:00):

The other triangular equation (for forth and back) can be left as an exercise to see whether you've been following all this, but the answer in the back of the book is that the composite

Gd⟶ηGdGFGd⟶GεdGdGd \overset{\eta_{Gd}}{\longrightarrow} GFGd \overset{G\varepsilon_d}{\longrightarrow} GdGd⟶ηGd​​GFGd⟶Gεd​​Gd

The slight miracle is that this apparatus -- functors FFF and GGG, natural transformations η:1C→GF\eta: 1_\mathbf{C} \to GFη:1C​→GF and ε:FG→1D\varepsilon: FG \to 1_\mathbf{D}ε:FG→1D​, satisfying the two triangular equations -- is equivalent to having an adjunction F⊣GF \dashv GF⊣G, but look Ma: no category of sets! For example, the categories didn't even need to be locally small (i.e., have homs valued in some prior category of sets) -- we can still make sense in this way of an adjoint pair of functors. Lawvere would say that this apparatus is an elementary definition of adjunction. It is certainly given wholly in the language of categories, functors, and natural transformations.
