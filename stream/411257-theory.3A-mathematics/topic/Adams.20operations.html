The free λ\lambdaλ-ring on one generator, Λ\LambdaΛ, is also known as the ring of symmetric functions. I will explain its real significance in a minute. It has various important elements:

  • the exterior power elements λi\lambda^iλi
  • the symmetric power elements σi\sigma^iσi
  • the Adams operations ψi\psi^iψi

The first two sequences of elements can be nicely understood using 2-rigs. Given any object rrr in any 2-rig R\mathsf{R}R we can define its ith exterior power Λi(r)\Lambda^i(r)Λi(r) and ith symmetric power Si(r)S^i(r)Si(r), using the same constructions that work in the familiar case R=Vect\mathsf{R} = \mathsf{Vect}R=Vect. In particular we can do this in the free 2-rig on one generator xxx, which happens to be called kS‾\overline{k\mathsf{S}}kS for reasons I could explain. So, we get important objects

Λi(x),Si(x)∈kS‾ \Lambda^i(x), S^i(x) \in \overline{k\mathsf{S}}Λi(x),Si(x)∈kS

You can think of these as the 'walking' or 'generic' ith exterior power and ith symmetric power, respectively.

Now, the really important thing about Λ\LambdaΛ is that it's the [[Grothendieck group]] of the free 2-rig on one generator. Thus, we get elements

λi=[Λi(x)] \lambda^i = [\Lambda^i(x)] λi=[Λi(x)]
σi=[Si(x)] \sigma^i = [S^i(x)] σi=[Si(x)]

in Λ\LambdaΛ.

But the [[Adams operations]] ψi\psi^iψi cannot be defined as the equivalence classes of certain objects in kS‾\overline{k \mathsf{S}}kS. And that makes them more tantalizing.

Why do we care about Adams operations? There are many reasons, but one is this.

As you probably know, you can apply a polynomial in Z[x]\mathbb{Z}[x]Z[x] to any element of any ring. Why is this? Ultimately it's because Z[x]\mathbb{Z}[x]Z[x] is the free ring on one generator, namely xxx. So, if we have any element rrr of any ring RRR, there's a unique ring homomorphism from Z[x]\mathbb{Z}[x]Z[x] to RRR sending xxx to rrr. And then this homomorphism sends any polynomial PPP to some element of RRR, which we call P(r)P(r)P(r). And this is how we apply a polynomial to an element of a ring!

So, the free ring on one generator acts on any ring. (Not as ring homomorphisms, mind you! I'm just saying any polynomial P∈Z[x]P \in \mathbb{Z}[x]P∈Z[x] acts to send elements r∈Rr \in Rr∈R to new elements P(r)∈RP(r) \in RP(r)∈R.)

This is a completely general trick. So, the free 2-rig on one generator acts on any 2-rig. Taking Grothendieck groups, we get a baby version of this fact: the free lambda-ring on one generator, Λ\LambdaΛ, acts on any lambda-ring.

Being the free lambda-ring on one generator, Λ\LambdaΛ acts on any lambda-ring.
