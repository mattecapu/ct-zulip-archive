Second - sorry, I'm just plowing ahead here before I forget - there's the question of what's the free group on a heap. There's a forgetful functor

U:Grp→HeapU : \mathsf{Grp} \to \mathsf{Heap} U:Grp→Heap

sending any group GGG to the underlying set of GGG made into a heap with operation t(a,b,c)=ab−1ct(a,b,c) = a b^{-1} ct(a,b,c)=ab−1c. This must have a left adjoint (by general nonsense about Lawvere theories), so:

And Oscar Cunningham proposed an answer. First, recall that there's a functor

Str:Heap→Grp \mathrm{Str}: \mathsf{Heap} \to \mathsf{Grp} Str:Heap→Grp

sending any heap HHH to the group of all maps h↦t(a,b,h)h \mapsto t(a,b,h)h↦t(a,b,h) from HHH to itself, where a,ba,ba,b are arbitrary elements of HHH. (If we think of a heap as a group-torsor pair, this functor picks out the group in that pair.)

I foolishly ventured that the left adjoint of U:Grp→HeapU : \mathsf{Grp} \to \mathsf{Heap}U:Grp→Heap was this functor Str\mathsf{Str}Str, but Oscar improved that guess:

Conjecture. The left adjoint of U:Grp→HeapU : \mathsf{Grp} \to \mathsf{Heap}U:Grp→Heap sends any heap HHH to the group Z∗Str(H)\mathbb{Z} \ast \mathsf{Str}(H)Z∗Str(H), where ∗\ast∗ is the "free product", or coproduct, of groups.

@John Baez This is an abuse of language on my part and my fault for trying to go too fast. I usually see a limit sketch as a pair (C,L)(C, L)(C,L) where CCC is a small category and LLL is a set of cones in CCC. Here, I define a cone as a natural transformation of the form ΔI(X)⇒F\Delta_I(X) \Rightarrow FΔI​(X)⇒F where:

  • III is a small category,
  • XXX is an object in CCC,
  • Δ:C→[I,C]\Delta: C \to [I, C]Δ:C→[I,C] is the obvious constant functor,
  • F:I→CF: I \to CF:I→C is a functor.

If all the small categories III associated with the cones in LLL are discrete categories, then I would tend to call (C,L)(C, L)(C,L) a product sketch.

I define a model for a limit sketch as a functor M:C→SetM: C \to \mathbf{Set}M:C→Set sending every cone a:ΔI(X)⇒Fa:\Delta_I(X) \Rightarrow Fa:ΔI​(X)⇒F in LLL to a limit cone M(a):ΔI(M(X))⇒M∘FM(a):\Delta_I(M(X)) \Rightarrow M \circ FM(a):ΔI​(M(X))⇒M∘F in Set\mathbf{Set}Set.

While this is one definition, there are other common definitions for limit sketches. For instance, one might assume that the cones in LLL must be limit cones in CCC. In that case, the models send the limit cones in LLL to limit cones in Set\mathsf{Set}Set.

Based on my experience and interactions, I usually prefer to use the term limit-preserving to evoke the idea that these functors send the "equipped (synthetic) limits" to limits in Set\mathbf{Set}Set, avoiding potential confusion with the various meanings of "model". Todd Trimble (Jun 29 2024 at 18:26):

Question. Concretely as possible, what is the left adjoint of U:Grp→Heap?

And Oscar Cunningham proposed an answer.

The answer Oscar gave is the answer I would have given (without trying too hard to be concrete). In general, if you have monads G,HG, HG,H on a category C\mathbf{C}C with reflexive coequalizers, with a monad morphism ϕ:H→G\phi: H \to Gϕ:H→G, and CG→CH\mathbf{C}^G \to \mathbf{C}^HCG→CH denotes the forgetful functor between the categories of algebras that is induced by the monad morphism, then the left adjoint to this forgetful functor takes an HHH-algebra XXX to the coequalizer of a pair of arrows

GHX⇉GλρXGX.GHX \overset{\rho X}{\underset{G\lambda}{\rightrightarrows}} GX.GHXGλ⇉​ρX​GX.

This is just like the situation for writing down the tensor product of a right RRR-module YYY with a left RRR-module XXX, as a coequalizer:

Y⊗R⊗X⇉Y⊗X→Y⊗RXY \otimes R \otimes X \rightrightarrows Y \otimes X \to Y \otimes_R XY⊗R⊗X⇉Y⊗X→Y⊗R​X.

Only this time, HHH acts on GGG on the right by a composite GH→GϕGG→μGGH \overset{G\phi}{\to} GG \overset{\mu}{\to} GGH→GϕGG→μG, and on the left on an HHH-algebra XXX by the algebra structure λ:HX→X\lambda: HX \to Xλ:HX→X.

Think for example of the way that the universal enveloping algebra of a Lie algebra works: by taking a certain quotient of the tensor algebra (which plays the role of GXGXGX). Oscar's proposal is working exactly the same way.
