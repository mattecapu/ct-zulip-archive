Just to reply to John, and not adding particularly to the current discussion: in bicategories like Rel\mathsf{Rel}Rel or Span\mathsf{Span}Span, those relations or spans X↛YX \nrightarrow YX↛Y that come from functions X→YX \to YX→Y have right adjoints, and so diagonal maps Δ:X→X×X\Delta: X \to X \times XΔ:X→X×X (which I will rewrite as Δ:X→X⊗X\Delta: X \to X \otimes XΔ:X→X⊗X since the setwise product ×\times× is not a cartesian product in those bicategories), which are the comultiplications for cocommutative comonoid structures on such XXX, will have right adjoints μ:X⊗X→X\mu: X \otimes X \to Xμ:X⊗X→X. Those right adjoints are multiplications for commutative monoid structures on XXX, mated to the cocommutative comonoid structures we started with.

By playing with adjoints like Δ⊣μ\Delta \dashv \muΔ⊣μ, coassociativity isomorphisms like

(1⊗Δ)∘Δ→∼(Δ⊗1)Δ(1 \otimes \Delta) \circ \Delta \overset{\sim}{\to} (\Delta \otimes 1)\Delta(1⊗Δ)∘Δ→∼(Δ⊗1)Δ

induce morphisms

Δμ→(1⊗μ)(Δ⊗1)\Delta \mu \to (1 \otimes \mu)(\Delta \otimes 1)Δμ→(1⊗μ)(Δ⊗1)

which looks like "one half" of Frobenius reciprocity. You get the other half if these morphisms are invertible. That turns out to be the case in Rel\mathsf{Rel}Rel and Span\mathsf{Span}Span, but not generally for other cases of cartesian bicategories, like the bicategory consisting of categories and profunctors.

Part of the groupoidification story is that these merely "semi-Frobenius" morphisms, which are always present in cartesian bicategories, turn out to be invertible if you work not with categories and profunctors, but instead with groupoids and profunctors between them.

  • Construct a quantum channel Φ:B(H)→B(H)\Phi : \mathcal{B}(\mathcal{H}) \to \mathcal{B}(\mathcal{H})Φ:B(H)→B(H) by mapping a density matrix ρ\rhoρ to the partial trace of ΔρΔ∗\Delta \rho \Delta^*ΔρΔ∗. The idea is that Φ\PhiΦ first uses Δ\DeltaΔ to "copy" the state and then forgets one of the copies.
  • Using associativity and isometricity of Δ\DeltaΔ, show that Φ\PhiΦ is idempotent. (I think I can see how to do this but would need to check the details.)

In the case Δ(ei)=ei⊗ei\Delta(e_i) = e_i \otimes e_iΔ(ei​)=ei​⊗ei​, the channel Φ\PhiΦ is precisely the "dephasing" channel which erases all off-diagonal elements from a density matrix. In other words, it conducts a measurement in the given basis and forgets the result. So the fixed points of Φ\PhiΦ are precisely those density matrices that are diagonal in the given basis. Hence for general Δ\DeltaΔ, it's natural to look at the fixed points of Φ\PhiΦ. The pure states in this set of fixed points form the basis that we're looking for.

  • Based on the idempotency, Φ\PhiΦ should be a conditional expectation onto a sub-C*-algebra of B(H)\mathcal{B}(\mathcal{H})B(H). Use the commutativity of Δ\DeltaΔ to show that this subalgebra is commutative. Define the basis {ei}\{e_i\}{ei​} now as the basis with respect to which this algebra is precisely the diagonal matrices.
  • Then Δ(ei)=ei⊗ei\Delta(e_i) = e_i \otimes e_iΔ(ei​)=ei​⊗ei​ will be clear.

I guess the main difficulty lies in the penultimate step, where one needs to show that Φ\PhiΦ has sufficiently many fixed points in order to show that the dimension of the algebra coincides with dim(H)\mathrm{dim}(\mathcal{H})dim(H), and I don't yet know how to go about this. But overall, the appeal of this approach is that it would give a relatively concrete way to get at the eie_iei​.

The basic idea is to observe that every pair (e,x)(e, x)(e,x) in H\mathcal{H}H with

Whoops! It looks like I had forgotten to write up the final part of the proof. I've now added it as an extra paragraph above.

The high-level story boils down to showing that in the non-unital case, a Δ\DeltaΔ with the desired properties cannot exist. However, the overall proof is an induction argument which proves the statement "Every Δ\DeltaΔ with the required properties is a copy map, regardless of unitality" by induction on the dimension. The induction step treats the unital and non-unital cases separately, where the induction assumption is used only in the non-unital case, and in this case one arrives at a contradiction.
