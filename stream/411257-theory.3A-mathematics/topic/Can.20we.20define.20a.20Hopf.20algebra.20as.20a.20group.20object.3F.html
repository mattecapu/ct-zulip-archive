Topic: Can we define a Hopf algebra as a group object?

Jack Jia (May 01 2025 at 17:45):

The antipode of a Hopf algebra is almost the same as the inverse of a group object.

I am wondering if a group object in Algkop \mathsf{Alg}_{\mathbb{k}}^{\text{op}}Algkop​ is precisely a Hopf algebra over k\mathbb{k}k, where the comultiplication, counit, and antipode are given by the (opposites of) group multiplication, group unit, and the inverse, respectively.

[For clarity, I am defining the category Algk\mathsf{Alg}_{\mathbb{k}}Algk​ to be the category of (unital) rings sliced under k\mathbb{k}k, I am taking for granted that the 2 definitions of k\mathbb{k}k-algebra (one as a monoid in k\mathbb{k}k-vector spaces, another as a ring that k\mathbb{k}k maps into) coincide.]

I think all the diagrams that are supposed to commute translates between these, but the types of arrows don't quite check out. For example, the (opposite of) the inverse map is a map of k\mathbb{k}k-algebras, but the antipode of a Hopf algebra is only required to be k\mathbb{k}k-linear.

Mike Shulman (May 01 2025 at 18:43):

I'm not sure about the antipode, but a different point is that you do need to be talking about commutative rings in order that the tensor product in Algk\mathsf{Alg}_{\Bbbk}Algk​ coincides with the cartesian product in Algkop\mathsf{Alg}_{\Bbbk}^{\mathrm{op}}Algkop​. But maybe you're one of the people for whom all rings are commutative? (-:

Mike Shulman (May 01 2025 at 18:44):

That restriction does also mean that you only end up with commutative Hopf algebras this way. (I guess the non-commutative ones are "quantum groups".)

won't "commutative Hopf algebra" and "affine group scheme" both be on the "affine scheme" side?

Mike Shulman (May 01 2025 at 19:49):

A homomorphism of commutative Hopf algebras is in particular a homomorphism of underlying commutative rings, which goes in the opposite direction from the map of affine (group) schemes.
