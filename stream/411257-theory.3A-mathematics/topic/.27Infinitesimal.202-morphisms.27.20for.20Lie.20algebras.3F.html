Topic: 'Infinitesimal 2-morphisms' for Lie algebras?

Tobias Fritz (Aug 27 2025 at 07:08):

With Grp denoting the category of groups, it's a standard fact (often given as an exercise) that Grp really is a 2-category, where a 2-morphism between group homomorphisms ϕ,ψ:G→H\phi, \psi : G \to Hϕ,ψ:G→H is an element h∈Hh \in Hh∈H with ϕ(g)=hψ(g)h−1\phi(g) = h \psi(g) h^{-1}ϕ(g)=hψ(g)h−1 for all g∈Gg \in Gg∈G. Upon considering the groups as categories and the homomorphisms as functors, these 2-morphisms are precisely the natural transformations.

Tobias Fritz (Aug 27 2025 at 07:09):

My question now is: do we get a similar structure for Lie algebras, but with 'infinitesimal 2-morphisms' instead, and what exactly does this mean? In more detail, consider the naturality equation ϕ(g)=hψ(g)h−1\phi(g) = h \psi(g) h^{-1}ϕ(g)=hψ(g)h−1 for Lie groups, specialized to elements of the form g=exg = e^xg=ex and h=eyh = e^yh=ey where xxx and yyy live in the Lie algebra. Then abusing notation a bit by using the same notation for the associated Lie algebra homomorphisms as well, this means ϕ(x)=ψ(x)+[y,ψ(x)]+O(y2)\phi(x) = \psi(x) + [y, \psi(x)] + O(y^2)ϕ(x)=ψ(x)+[y,ψ(x)]+O(y2).

Paolo Perrone (Aug 27 2025 at 07:37):

In Lie algebra cohomology, similarly to what happens for groups, the first cohomology group is the space of derivations modulo inner derivations, where inner derivations are exactly the Lie brackets that you are considering.
That seems to go somewhat in the direction of your question, but of course, this leads to further questions:

  • Does this homology theory actually comes from a higher category?
  • Is there a model structure anywhere?
  • Is this an instance of some sort of Dold-Kan correspondence?
  • Is there a Lie algebra equivalent of the Eilenberg-MacLane space of a group?

(All the questions above are however way out of my expertise, I wouldn't even know how to state them properly.)

In a nutshell, inner automorphisms (which can be understood categorically as elements of the isotropy group) endow any category whatsoever with 2-cells, and more generally, so does any crossed C\mathbf{C}C-module (a (co)presheaf GGG of groups with homomorphisms G(A)→Aut(A,A)G(A)\to Aut(A,A)G(A)→Aut(A,A) for all objects satisfying some conditions). Graham Manuell (Aug 27 2025 at 09:18):

Groups and Lie-algebras are both action-representable semiabelian categories. In such categories there is a universal split extension with a given kernel. In the case of groups, split extensions with kernel KKK and cokernel HHH correspond to homorphisms from HHH to Aut(K)\mathrm{Aut}(K)Aut(K) and the universal split extension is K→Hol(K)→Aut(K)K \to \mathrm{Hol}(K) \to \mathrm{Aut}(K)K→Hol(K)→Aut(K) where Hol(K)\mathrm{Hol}(K)Hol(K) is the holomorph of KKK. This split exact sequence gives Aut(K)\mathrm{Aut}(K)Aut(K) the structure of an internal category / a crossed module. The 2-cells are precisely the 2-morphisms from the 2-category of groups. If instead of considering homomorphisms from HHH to Aut(K)\mathrm{Aut}(K)Aut(K), you equip HHH with the structure of a discrete internal category and consider internal anafunctors from it this internal category, you classify all extensions instead of split extensions. A similar thing works with Lie algebras where Aut(K)\mathrm{Aut}(K)Aut(K) is replaced with the Lie algebra of derivations Der(K)\mathrm{Der}(K)Der(K). I think this is also how to make the link with Lie algebra cohomology that @Paolo Perrone mentioned.

Tobias Fritz (Aug 27 2025 at 09:30):

Martti Karvonen said:

For this to work, one doesn't need an actual automorphism, as long as there is a way to extract an honest one in a nice way. That said, I guess that fails here too, so that an infinitesimal automorphism does not induce an actual automorphism in any reasonable way?

That's right. A map of the form [y,−][y,-][y,−] on a Lie algebra is not an inner automorphism, but rather an inner derivation; the Jacobi identity is precisely the Leibniz rule! Over R\mathbb{R}R or C\mathbb{C}C, one can exponentiate such a map to a 1-parameter family of actual automorphisms. But I think that a good structural account of what's going on should work over any ground field. Hence we can't expect to have genuine inner automorphisms.
