Kuratowski's closure definition and Pervin's single-equation expression is very cool (in fact Pervin's equation is satisfied also for modular lattices, not just topologies!) but there are good reasons that others like Moore used different axioms.

If you want to think about topology using algebra, Moore closure is what I would focus more on (so, split out axiom K4 into K4' and K4''). The reason is very practical: lattices of submonoids, sub-algebras, etc. organized by generators do not satisfy X∪Y‾=X‾∪Y‾\overline{X\cup Y}=\overline{X}\cup\overline{Y}X∪Y=X∪Y, but they do satisfy K1-K3 and monotonicity.

Lets say a set of geneators JGJ_GJG​ generates a group GGG usually written G=⟨JG⟩G=\langle J_G\rangleG=⟨JG​⟩; you can think of the generators as bijective functions, and generation as composing them with eachother ad infinitum. (And everything here is a subgroup of some symmetric group if any readers are wondering where the generating relations went) ⟨..⟩\langle ..\rangle⟨..⟩ acts as a closure operator by K1-K3+K4' and yet for H,K≤GH,K\leq GH,K≤G Ithat is H,KH,KH,K are subgroups of GGG):

$$\langle J_H;\rho_G\rangle\cup\langle J_K;\rho_G\rangle \neq \langle J_H\cup J_K;\rho_G\rangle.

In fact, the LHS isn't even a group unless JH⊆JKJ_H\subseteq J_KJH​⊆JK​ or JK⊆JHJ_K\subseteq J_HJK​⊆JH​!

Which is very useful, because it allows us to think of a new join operator on the sublattice of ⟨..⟩\langle..\rangle⟨..⟩ fixed points. ⟨JH⟩∨⟨JK⟩:=⟨JH∪JK⟩\langle J_H\rangle\vee\langle J_K\rangle:=\langle J_H\cup J_K\rangle⟨JH​⟩∨⟨JK​⟩:=⟨JH​∪JK​⟩. We can do this because of a theorem of Knaster and Tarski that the fixed points of monotonic functions on posets form a lattice. And so we can think of non-associative algebraic structures as the lattice points that are not fixed by the closure under composition.

In contrast, is every case of closure operators in algebra (at least of which I am aware)

c(a∧b)=c(a)∧c(b)c(a\land b)= c(a)\land c(b)c(a∧b)=c(a)∧c(b)

And this survives even up into the mountain-tops of abstraction in the [[Lawvere-Tierney topology]].

So if you like algebra I would recommend developing intuition for Moore closures rather than Kuratowski's, and accept that at some point, you may need to discard extensivity. That should require the least re-learning, I think?

Another takeaway is that the shortest most "elegant" description accessible to beginners at one level of abstraction (such as Pervin's single equation) is rarely the most durable/general at higher levels. The exception to this rule are things written by Lawvere. :)

Well, yes and no. Yes: for any algebraic theory (which we here in category land often like to think of in terms of Lawvere theories, or alternatively in terms of monads, or even something else altogether), if you have a model VVV of the theory, then you can define a closure operator c:P(V)→P(V)c: P(V) \to P(V)c:P(V)→P(V) that takes any subset SSS of VVV to the intersection of all substructures or submodels of VVV that contain SSS. This intersection c(S)c(S)c(S) is again a submodel, and the operation ccc thus defined is a Moore closure operation, i.e., gives a monad on the poset P(V)P(V)P(V). It's a nice exercise to prove this (and it's not very difficult).

Todd Trimble (May 26 2024 at 18:12):

But also no, in the sense that you don't have to start with a monad on Set\mathsf{Set}Set; the construction works much more broadly. For example, if VVV is a field, then you could define c:P(V)→P(V)c: P(V) \to P(V)c:P(V)→P(V) so that c(S)c(S)c(S) is the smallest subfield that contains SSS, i.e., the intersection of all subfields that contain SSS. But fields are not monadic over sets!

Basically, any time you have a collection of subsets of a set VVV that is closed under arbitrary intersections of subcollections, then you can likewise define a Moore closure operator in the manner described above. In fact, this thread illustrates that: if VVV is a set equipped with a topology, and you take the collection of subsets of VVV that are closed relative to the topology, then that collection is closed (different meaning of "closed"!) under arbitrary intersections of subcollections.

These are very pleasant and easy things to prove.

Todd Trimble (May 26 2024 at 18:12):

As a pure aside: you may have heard of the Kuratowski 14 problem. Given a topological space VVV, and starting with a subset SSS, how many different sets can you generate from SSS by applying the closure operator and complementation operator? Well, it depends on which VVV and which SSS, but 14 is the upper limit. This is a famous and classical exercise. What seems less well known is that actually, the same is true for any Moore closure operator whatsoever; topological closure is a bit beside the point. (I don't say you can always hit the upper bound 14, but 14 is an upper bound regardless.) For example, if you start with the theory of monoids, and take for example the free monoid on two letters, and take the Moore closure associated with the collection of submonoids, then for a suitable S∈P(2∗)S \in P(\mathbf{2}^\ast)S∈P(2∗) you can get 14; this is a nontrivial "exercise" in the theory of formal languages.
