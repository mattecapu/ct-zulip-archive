John Baez (Apr 14 2024 at 09:01):

Your approach is too abstract for my feeble brain. Luckily, I realized that my cute little "circle versus hyperbola" example can be modified to give a bunch of nonisomorphic affine schemes over Z\mathbb{Z}Z that become isomorphic over Q\mathbb{Q}Q. Namely, ellipses!

For example, the circle

⟨u,v∣u2+v2=1⟩ \langle u,v \vert u^2 + v^2 = 1 \rangle ⟨u,v∣u2+v2=1⟩

is isomorphic to the ellipse

⟨x,y∣x2+4y2=1⟩ \langle x, y \vert x^2 + 4 y^2 = 1 \rangle ⟨x,y∣x2+4y2=1⟩

as schemes over Q\mathbb{Q}Q, using the coordinate change

u↦x,v↦2y u \mapsto x, v \mapsto 2 y u↦x,v↦2y

This map is invertible as a map of schemes over Q\mathbb{Q}Q because we can define the inverse

x↦u,y↦v/2 x \mapsto u, y \mapsto v/2 x↦u,y↦v/2

But as a map of schemes over Z\mathbb{Z}Z it's not invertible because we can't divide by 2!

Indeed, there are infinitely many nonisomorphic schemes over Z\mathbb{Z}Z - different shapes of ellipses - that become isomorphic over Q\mathbb{Q}Q.

This was kind of my point earlier! Here's a proof theoretic angle, which might convince you, though I'm still not sure how to make it totally precise:

If XXX has two presentations over Q\mathbb{Q}Q, then there should be a proof of this fact (by which I mean something like a sequence of tietze transformations but for ring presentations). But any such proof is finitely long, so one only has to divide finitely many times. But then the same proof will work over any Fp\mathbb{F}_pFp​ with ppp large enough for all those divisions to be allowed!
