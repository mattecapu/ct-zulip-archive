  • Be more expository.

Btw, the paper I started to work on was less ambitious, but I think could still be interesting. While writing my research statement, I did a quick catch up on some of the cool things that happened while I was away and I am still blown away by:

  • Quantum Reality: Space, Time, and Entanglement (link points to the part where the mind blowing point starts )

I don't know if there is any meaningful relation to my work, but something there really resonated. Entanglement on a boundary QFT gives rise to geometry in the bulk space. Removing entanglement leads to splitting up of the bulk geometry until, in the extreme, geometry disapears when entanglement is removed completely.

Something maybe related happens in discrete differential geometry. A fundamental "discrete 1-form" we denote GGG and call the "graph operator" gives both the differential structure as well as the geometry (at least on "diamond graphs", i.e. cubic graphs with time flowing along the major diagonal). Discrete 0-forms f∈Ω0f\in\Omega^0f∈Ω0 are basically elements of the free vector space on the set of nodes of a directed graph written as

f=∑i∈G0f(i)eif = \sum_{i\in\mathcal{G}^0} f(i) e^if=∑i∈G0​f(i)ei

with unit element 1=∑i∈G0ei1 = \sum_{i\in\mathcal{G}^0} e^i1=∑i∈G0​ei and discrete 1-forms α∈Ω1⊂Ω0⊗Ω0\alpha\in\Omega^1\subset\Omega^0\otimes\Omega^0α∈Ω1⊂Ω0⊗Ω0 are basically elements of the free vector space on the set of directed edges written as

α=∑(i,j)∈G1α(i,j)ei⊗ej\alpha = \sum_{(i,j)\in\mathcal{G}^1} \alpha(i,j) e^i\otimes e^jα=∑(i,j)∈G1​α(i,j)ei⊗ej

with the graph operator

G=∑(i,j)∈G1ei⊗ejG = \sum_{(i,j)\in\mathcal{G}^1} e^i\otimes e^jG=∑(i,j)∈G1​ei⊗ej

which plays a role similar to an adjacency matrix.

When the directed graph is complete, i.e. there is an edge i→ji\to ji→j for every pair of nodes iii and jjj, the graph operator can be written as

G=1⊗1.G = 1\otimes 1.G=1⊗1.

The discrete differential geometry resulting from this graph operator is the "universal differential envelope" (see e.g. this). This DDG is not very interesting (similar to how bulk geometry is not very interesting when you remove all entanglement).

Now, if you consider Ω0\Omega^0Ω0 to be a Hilbert space (because it is), then Ω1\Omega^1Ω1 is a subset of the product Hilbert space Ω0⊗Ω0\Omega^0\otimes\Omega^0Ω0⊗Ω0 (Note: When G=1⊗1G=1\otimes 1G=1⊗1 we have Ω1=Ω0⊗Ω0\Omega^1=\Omega^0\otimes\Omega^0Ω1=Ω0⊗Ω0) and you can interpret G=1⊗1G = 1\otimes 1G=1⊗1 as having no "entanglement" (because it isn't entangled). You introduce entanglement by removing directed edges from GGG. In doing so, you start introducing meaningful geometry to the resulting DDG.

I don't know if this DDG version of Entanglement↔Geometry\mathsf{Entanglement}\leftrightarrow\mathsf{Geometry}Entanglement↔Geometry has anything to do with the holographic version of Entanglement↔Geometry\mathsf{Entanglement}\leftrightarrow\mathsf{Geometry}Entanglement↔Geometry, but I think it would still make a nice short article looking at "graph entanglement".
