Kris Brown (Apr 30 2025 at 01:43):

The core problem is that we are interested in computing the elements of a hom-set, say Hom(X,G){\rm Hom}(X,G)Hom(X,G), in some (adhesive) category C\mathsf{C}C. We could call XXX our query and the hom set the answer to our query. What's special about this problem is that we imagine GGG is making little changes very frequently: we want an algorithm which updates our hom-set with respect to a small change rather than having to recompute the whole thing from scratch after each little change.

Kris Brown (Apr 30 2025 at 01:45):

Our formal model of a change is the application of a rewrite rule f:L↣Rf: L \rightarrowtail Rf:L↣R (our rules right now will be purely additive via pushout, rather than the usual pushout-complement-followed-by-pushout kind of rewrite rule). Here's an example in the category of graphs:

Screenshot 2025-04-29 at 6.31.31 PM.png

In that example, GGG got 'updated' to HHH via applying f:L↣Rf: L\rightarrowtail Rf:L↣R via a match morphism m:L↣Gm: L \rightarrowtail Gm:L↣G. Our incremental search problem is: suppose we already knew Hom(X,G){\rm Hom}(X,G)Hom(X,G) for some XXX: how would we figure out what Hom(X,H){\rm Hom}(X,H)Hom(X,H) is? We clearly have a subset of it, Hom(X,G)⋅Δ{\rm Hom}(X,G)\cdot \DeltaHom(X,G)⋅Δ, but there are a bunch of new morphisms which sprang into existence due to the stuff that got added from the rewrite rule.

Kris Brown (Apr 30 2025 at 01:46):

In order to explain this, I'll need to make some computational assumptions about C\mathsf{C}C to make explicit what it means to "find" morphisms. The following assumptions are motivated by the category of graphs: objects can be small or large, for practical purposes. The objects which appear in rules or queries are small, whereas the objects like GGG and HHH could be large. The computational complexity of enumerating Hom(A,B){\rm Hom}(A,B)Hom(A,B) is assumed to be intractable for arbitrary AAA and BBB (see: the subgraph isomorphism problem). However, we will assume it is tractable when performed between small objects. Furthermore, we assume we can efficiently enumerate some subsets of Hom(A,B){\rm Hom}(A,B)Hom(A,B) at runtime, in particular, whenever we have a partial map A↢O→BA \leftarrowtail O \rightarrow BA↢O→B where OOO is nonempty and AAA is small and connected. Extending such a partial map to the subset of Hom(A,B){\rm Hom}(A,B)Hom(A,B) which agrees on the overlap corresponds to a rooted subgraph isomorphism problem (which is tractable). We can think of the overlap as initializing certain assignments of parts of AAA to parts of BBB in a search procedure, with the remaining unassigned values determined purely by the connectivity of AAA.

Screenshot 2025-04-29 at 6.41.14 PM.png

Kris Brown (Apr 30 2025 at 01:47):

So our strategy will be to systematically transform something that seems like a subgraph isomorphism problem into something that looks like the rooted subgraph isomorphism problem, and because of this we'll be much more efficient. And with that I hope I've communicated what the problem is that I'm trying to solve! I should probably stop there before charging ahead to a solution!

Kris Brown (May 02 2025 at 07:45):

The key fact about adhesive categories that I rely on is that any map into the apex of a pushout (e.g. a map h:X→Hh: X \rightarrow Hh:X→H into the result of the rewrite of Fig 1 above) induces what I'll call an "adhesive cube", where the side faces are all determined by taking pullbacks with hhh. Because C{\mathsf C}C is adhesive, the top face of this cube is itself a pushout: X≅XG+XLXRX\cong X_G +_{X_L} X_RX≅XG​+XL​​XR​. That is, something which maps into G+LRG +_L RG+L​R is itself made up of the part that mapped into GGG (i.e. XGX_GXG​) and the part of XXX that mapped into RRR (i.e. XRX_RXR​), glued together by the part which mapped into LLL (i.e. XLX_LXL​).

Screenshot 2025-05-02 at 12.31.18 AM.png

Kris Brown (May 02 2025 at 07:45):

So whenever we apply a rewrite rule, we have the bottom face of an adhesive cube. Any match from our pattern XXX into the result of the rule application, h:X→Hh: X\rightarrow Hh:X→H, has an associated adhesive cube. The top face of the cube is a pushout of subobjects of XXX. One thing we can do is consider all the ways we can express XXX as a pushout of two of its subobjects. Maybe this takes some time, but we only need to do it once and store the result, plus we can do it in advance of any of the rewrite rule applications. So let us have all possible top faces of an adhesive cube at our disposal (we can call these decompositions of our pattern XXX).

Kris Brown (May 02 2025 at 07:47):

One of the side faces of the adhesive cube lives above the rewrite rule (let's call it the rule face of the cube). We assume we are in a setting where we know all of the rewrite rules that might be used, and we know all possible top edges of a rule face (they are subobject morphisms, XL↣XRX_L\rightarrowtail X_RXL​↣XR​). So we can actually compute all possible rule faces for a given subobject morphism + rule (say, f:L↣Rf: L\rightarrowtail Rf:L↣R) by looking for pairs of maps hL:XL→Lh_L: X_L\rightarrow LhL​:XL​→L and hR:XR→Rh_R: X_R\rightarrow RhR​:XR​→R such that they form a pullback square. I'll call this a set of interactions between a subobject morphism XL↣XRX_L\rightarrowtail X_RXL​↣XR​ and a rewrite rule fff.

Kris Brown (May 02 2025 at 07:52):

When it comes time to actually find maps h:X→Hh: X\rightarrow Hh:X→H, given that a rewrite just occurred, we can iterate over all decompositions and interactions between the XL↣XRX_L\rightarrowtail X_RXL​↣XR​ and the rewrite rule that was used:

Screenshot 2025-05-02 at 12.49.47 AM.png

In this figure, things in black are "free" (we computed and stored them a long time ago), things in red are what are provided to us (the result of the rewrite), and the remainder in green is what we'll compute: we want to find all of the ways to complete this partial adhesive cube into a full one. In doing so, we'll find all of the matches Hom(X,H){\rm Hom}(X,H)Hom(X,H).

Kris Brown (May 02 2025 at 08:05):

It looks like we need to find two morphisms, but we really just need to find all hG:XG→Gh_G: X_G\rightarrow GhG​:XG​→G such that the face that lives over mmm is a pullback square. Then we get h:X→Hh: X\rightarrow Hh:X→H for free via copairing [hG⋅Δ,hR⋅r][h_G\cdot \Delta, h_R\cdot r][hG​⋅Δ,hR​⋅r]. Luckily, we can efficiently find all such possible hGh_GhG​ because we have the partial map XG↢XL→hL⋅mGX_G \leftarrowtail X_L \xrightarrow{h_L\cdot m} GXG​↢XL​hL​⋅m​G. If we filter these results to just those which form a pullback square with mmm, then we will have successfully found all matches because we exhaustively checked all partial adhesive cubes.

Kris Brown (May 02 2025 at 08:06):

A couple of details: there is a particular trivial decomposition where XG=XX_G=XXG​=X and XL=XR=0X_L=X_R=0XL​=XR​=0 which we ignore: we do this because we are solving the incremental search problem, and that decomposition will lead to all of (and only) the 'old' matches which we were trying to avoid, i.e. elements of Hom(X,G)⋅Δ{\rm Hom}(X,G)\cdot \DeltaHom(X,G)⋅Δ.

There's a little argument I'll need to give tomorrow to convince you that, other than for the trivial decomposition, that the partial map we use to enumerate hGh_GhG​ candidates will always meet the criteria for corresponding to a 'rooted subgraph isomorphism' problem.

Kris Brown (May 05 2025 at 23:28):

Let me check if my "optimized algorithm" makes sense: when we are fortunate enough that C\mathsf{C}C has complements (terminology I get from Evan and David Jaz's blog post, whereas nlab calls them supplements) we can do something even faster. It's faster in two ways: 1.) you might be worried there are a lot of ways to express XXX as a pushout XG+XLXRX_G +_{X_L} X_RXG​+XL​​XR​ that we have to iterate over, and 2.) it might seem wasteful that, when we were trying to turn our partial adhesive cubes into complete ones via morphisms we found by extending a partial map from XGX_GXG​ to GGG, we had to filter all the possible extensions by ones which formed a pullback for the side face of the adhesive cube. This optimized algorithm says we'll also solve the problem if we do the same process but 1.) only consider decompositions where XR=∼XGX_R = {\sim}X_GXR​=∼XG​ 2.) use all of the the extensions of the partial map.

Kris Brown (May 05 2025 at 23:44):

So the algorithm is structurally the same: each hhh has a unique cube associated with it, so loop over all partial cubes and try to complete them. But it's nicer to loop over partial minimal adhesive cubes because the possible top faces are generally are a small subset of all possible decompositions of XXX. Also, the way to complete these cubes is simply looking for maps hGh_GhG​ which extend the partial map XG↢∂XG→h∂⋅mGX_G \leftarrowtail \partial X_G \xrightarrow{h_\partial \cdot m} GXG​↢∂XG​h∂​⋅m​G (no filter step necessary). By the same argument as above, this partial map induces a rooted search problem because XXX is connected and we are excluding the trivial decomposition.
