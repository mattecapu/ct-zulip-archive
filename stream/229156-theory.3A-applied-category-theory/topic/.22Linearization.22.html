Topic: "Linearization"

John Baez (May 28 2020 at 05:34):

Matrices, Markov kernels and profunctors are all examples of "linearization". We have some sort of rig or 2-rig like real numbers, nonnegative real numbers or sets, and we define a morphism from XXX to YYY to be a map from X×YX \times YX×Y, or Xop×YX^{\mathrm{op}} \times YXop×Y in the categorified case, to this rig or 2-rig.

John Baez (May 28 2020 at 05:35):

We can do most of the same tricks in each case.

John Baez (May 28 2020 at 05:36):

Relations are another example since a relation from a set XXX to a set YYY the same as a function from X×YX \times YX×Y to the boolean rig, {0,1}\{0,1\}{0,1}.

John Baez (May 28 2020 at 05:37):

Spans are another example since a span from a set XXX to a set YYY is the same as function from X×YX \times YX×Y to Set\mathsf{Set}Set.

John Baez (May 28 2020 at 18:42):

James Fairbanks said:

Can I ask, why is this called linearization? I don't see a connection to total orders or linear maps.

It sounds like you got it by now, but:

In the cases I'm talking about, we have a rig or 2-rig VVV, and for any set or category XXX we can form the free VVV-module on XXX by taking VXopV^{X^{\mathrm{op}}}VXop. There's always a Yoneda-like embedding

X↦Xop X \mapsto {X^{\mathrm{op}}}X↦Xop

We can then describe any map of free VVV-modules

F:VXop→VYopF: V^{X^{\mathrm{op}}} \to V^{Y^{\mathrm{op}}}F:VXop→VYop

using a "matrix"

T:Y×Xop→VT: Y \times X^{\mathrm{op}} \to VT:Y×Xop→V

Furthermore we can compose these maps by matrix multiplication.

A popular example is X=mX = mX=m, Y=nY = nY=n, V=RV = \mathbb{R}V=R. Then

VXop=RmV^{X^{\mathrm{op}}} = \mathbb{R}^mVXop=Rm

VXop=RnV^{X^{\mathrm{op}}} = \mathbb{R}^nVXop=Rn

and I'm saying we can describe a linear map from Rm\mathbb{R}^mRm to Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn using an n×mn \times mn×m matrix of real numbers,

T:n×m→RT: n \times m \to \mathbb{R}T:n×m→R

In this situation the Yoneda-like embedding

n→Rnn \to \mathbb{R}^nn→Rn

is just the inclusion of the n-element set into the vector space Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn as the standard basis!

The same story works for Markov kernels, relations, spans of sets, and profunctors. In every case are taking the category of sets (or 2-category of categories) and embedding it in a "linear" category (or 2-category) - that is, one where we can add maps, and we can compose maps by matrix multiplication. This lets us apply the ideas of linear algebra.

John Baez (May 28 2020 at 22:51):

You're right, Jade: the free VVV-module on a set XXX is only VXV^XVX when XXX is finite.

Category theory works a bit better: for example the free cocompletion of a category XXX is SetXop\mathsf{Set}^{X^{\mathrm{op}}}SetXop whenever XXX is small.

When a set XXX is not finite, to get the free VVV-module on it we need to take a subset of VXV^XVX consisting of finite linear combinations of elements of XXX.

When a category XXX is not small, to get its free cocompletion we need to take a subcategory of SetXop\mathsf{Set}^{X^{\mathrm{op}}}SetXop consisting of small colimits of representables.

So "small" is the big boys' version of "finite".

John Baez (May 28 2020 at 22:52):

By the way what I just said about SetXop\mathsf{Set}^{X^{\mathrm{op}}}SetXop should work for VXopV^{X^{\mathrm{op}}}VXop more generally, but I'm too lazy to remember/write down the fine print. Steve Lack has a paper about cocompletions of large enriched categories.

John Baez (May 28 2020 at 22:53):

Gershom (May 29 2020 at 07:04):

Here is a dumb "pattern-matching" inspired question. There is a slogan that "homology is the linearization of homotopy". is there any way at all that fits with this picture, or are the two just different uses of the term "linearization"?

John Baez (May 29 2020 at 19:42):

It's pretty darn similar. When we take homotopy theory and "linearize" it to get homology theory, what we're doing is taking an infinity-groupoid and turning it into a strict stable infinity-groupoid, which is the same as chain complex of abelian groups.

Another way to think of the exact same thing that we're taking a simplicial set and freely turning it into a simplicial abelian group. (Simplicial sets are a model for infinity-groupoids, and the Dold-Kan theorem says simplicial abelian groups are the same as chain complexes of abelian groups.)

The second perspective is probably more helpful now. Taking a simplicial set and freely turning it into a simplical abelian group has, as a special case, forming the free abelian group on a set SSS. This is just ZS\mathbb{Z}^SZS when SSS is finite. And this process S↦ZSS \mapsto \mathbb{Z}^SS↦ZS is precisely an example of the "linearization" idea I was just talking about!
