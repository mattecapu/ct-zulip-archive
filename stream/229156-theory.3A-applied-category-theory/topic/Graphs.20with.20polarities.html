John Baez (Apr 02 2025 at 16:34):

So, we want a formalism where the absence of an edge is on the same footing as a labeled edge. And I realized such a formalism already exists! If VVV is a monoidal category people talk about categories enriched in VVV, or VVV-categories for short, which have a set of objects and for each pair of objects x,yx,yx,y an object hom(x,y)∈V\text{hom}(x,y) \in Vhom(x,y)∈V, and composition and identity-assigning maps obeying the usual properties.

But some people (who? I've seen it somewhere!) also define VVV-graphs, which are like VVV-categories without the composition and identity-assigning maps. A VVV-graph is just a set of vertices and for each pair of vertices x,yx,yx,y an object hom(x,y)∈V\text{hom}(x,y) \in Vhom(x,y)∈V. That's all.

People do this because there's something like a monad on the category of VVV-graphs whose algebras are VVV-categories: the 'free enriched category on an enriched graph' monad.

But if we take VVV to be a mere monoid, thought of as a monoidal category with only identity morphisms, then a VVV-graph is like a graph with polarities!

If we take VVV to be the boolean monoid $$\{T,F}$$ then a VVV-graph is just a graph, where TTT means the presence of an edge and FFF means the absence of an edge.

But if we take VVV to be the multiplicative monoid of Z/3\mathbb{Z}/3Z/3, then a VVV-graph is the same as a LLL-labeled graph where L={+,−}L = \{+,-\}L={+,−} - just as you said.
