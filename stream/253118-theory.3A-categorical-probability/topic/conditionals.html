Tobias Fritz (Aug 19 2021 at 07:28):

Nice idea! I need to think about it a little more and need to go soon, but for now let me just comment that your terminology and notation is a bit non-standard, which may impede communication with other category theorists. Specifically:

  • There is no such thing as a contravariant cone. Isn't your P∗\mathbf{P}^*P∗ just a cone in the usual sense? (It's clearly not a cocone!)
  • You first say that Θ\ThetaΘ is a category, but then you use notation implying that Θ\ThetaΘ is a functor, since it's the codomain of a natural transformation.
  • Your δ\deltaδ does can't actually be a natural transformation, since it seems to go from a contravariant functor to a covariant one. For a natural transformation, the domain and codomain must be functors of the same type.
  • Most importantly, you give an equational condition for a bunch of morphisms and call it a "universal property". This is not what we mean by a universal property. A universal property is what you have when you characterize an object by saying how to map into it [or out of it] from any other object. For example, "Giving a map into a product A×BA \times BA×B amounts to giving a map into AAA and a map into BBB".

This is not to criticize the idea, which I think goes in the right direction. (More on that later.) It's normal that your parlance will be a bit non-standard at the beginning, especially if you haven't talked to category theorists much before. I'm trying to point it out early so that it won't perpetuate.

Tobias Fritz (Aug 19 2021 at 13:54):

Now how about that definition of conditionals itself? Let me first try to formulate it in my own words, and then see whether this is indeed what you have in mind.

Let Meas\mathsf{Meas}Meas be the category of measurable spaces and measurable maps, and let Stoch\mathsf{Stoch}Stoch be the Kleisli category of the Giry monad on Meas\mathsf{Meas}Meas, so that its morphisms are Markov kernels between measurable spaces. Then we have a canonical functor Meas→Stoch\mathsf{Meas} \to \mathsf{Stoch}Meas→Stoch. It turns every measurable map into the corresponding (deterministic) Markov kernel. It's worth noting that this functor is not faithful.

Suppose that we have f:Ω1→Ω0f : \Omega_1 \to \Omega_0f:Ω1​→Ω0​ in Meas\mathsf{Meas}Meas. Denoting the singleton measurable space by 111, a morphism P0:1→Ω1\mathbf{P}_0 : 1 \to \Omega_1P0​:1→Ω1​ in Stoch\mathsf{Stoch}Stoch is exactly a probability measure, so that (Ω0,P0)(\Omega_0,\mathbf{P}_0)(Ω0​,P0​) is a probability space. The pushforward of this measure is the composite 1→P0Ω1→fΩ01 \stackrel{\mathbf{P}_0}{\to} \Omega_1 \stackrel{f}{\to} \Omega_01→P0​Ω1​→fΩ0​ in Stoch\mathsf{Stoch}Stoch. If we denote it by P1\mathbf{P}_1P1​, then we can consider both measures as the two components of a cone from 111 to the diagram Ω1→Ω0\Omega_1 \to \Omega_0Ω1​→Ω0​ in Stoch\mathsf{Stoch}Stoch.

Now a conditional is a section α:Ω0→Ω1\alpha : \Omega_0 \to \Omega_1α:Ω0​→Ω1​ of fff in Stoch\mathsf{Stoch}Stoch, which means that f∘α=idf \circ \alpha = \mathrm{id}f∘α=id, and in addition such that α∘P0=P1\alpha \circ \mathbf{P}_0 = \mathbf{P}_1α∘P0​=P1​. In other words, α\alphaα must be a section such that P\mathbf{P}P also forms a cone with respect to the diagram Ω0→αΩ1\Omega_0 \stackrel{\alpha}{\to} \Omega_1Ω0​→αΩ1​.

Finally, it's worth noting that in some situations, such as in the theory of stochastic processes, we have more than just two measurable spaces, but rather a whole diagram, like …→Ω2→Ω1→Ω0\ldots \to \Omega_2 \to \Omega_1 \to \Omega_0…→Ω2​→Ω1​→Ω0​. In this case, the definition of conditional can be generalized straightforwardly by using the formulation in terms of sections and cones.

Is this an accurate representation of what you have in mind?
