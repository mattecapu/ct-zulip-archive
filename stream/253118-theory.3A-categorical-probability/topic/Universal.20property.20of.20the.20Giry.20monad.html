This is similar in spirit to what we're currently working on with the law of large numbers. This is at a sufficiently advanced stage that we could possibly share a draft if you're interested.

What I imagine that you have in mind is to consider a map XN→PXX^{\mathbb{N}} \to PXXN→PX which takes every sequence to the associated "empirical measure" defined by the relative frequencies of the elements in the sequence. The first problem now is that this doesn't make sense in general, because many sequences don't have well-defined relative frequencies. Therefore the transformation can at most be partially defined. Another issue is that you can have two sequences for which the relative frequencies do converge, and even be identical so that you get the same element of PXPXPX, even if the sequences do not differ by a finite permutation. (They don't even necessarily differ by an infinite permutation, because one may contain elements with relative frequency zero!) So although a partial transformation exists, there's no simple characterization of when two elements of its domain get mapped to the same element of the codomain.

(There are further analytical subtleties that we've had to struggle with, which is part of why it's taken us a few years to get to the point where we have a draft.)

Reviewing the [[Giry monad]] page, I see that the definition is more complicated than I had naively misremembered it was; it's not just a matter of homming into an interval, for instance. The definition of probability measure on an object of a countably (co)complete Boolean lextensive category is a monotone function from its BA of subobjects to [0,1][0,1][0,1] satisfying countable constraints (these can either be expressed in terms of countable increasing chains or countable disjoint elements) and modularity (see [[probability valuation]] for that condition, which amounts to inclusion-exclusion). On the face of it, there is no reason for the collection of such maps to have the structure of an object of the same Boolean category, nor does the interval itself need to be a part of the Boolean category for this definition to make sense.

On the other hand, suppose that [0,1][0,1][0,1], rather than being a mere object of a Boolean category, were instead realised as the lattice of subobjects of some structure III in such category. Suppose further that this type of structure were such that every object comes with a trivial such structure (whose substructures are in correspondence with subobjects) and that the category of such structures is enriched over the Boolean category. Then the hom-object I→XI \to XI→X could recover the collection of monotone bla-bla-bla functions from subobjects of X to [0,1][0,1][0,1] by inverse images. Tobias Fritz (Feb 16 2025 at 17:27):

The idea here is that the Giry monad first of all does preserve countable cofiltered limits; that's one version of the Kolmogorov extension theorem. For the intuition behind the conjectured universality, think of building the free affine commutative monad inductively by repeatedly applying the generating binary operation and imposing the relevant equations. Then the preservation of cofiltered limits gives us the additional power to create generalized elements of {0,1}N\{0,1\}^{\mathbb{N}}{0,1}N by constructing a compatible family of marginals. In terms of a coin with transcendental bias, this amounts to allowing protocols with infinitely many coin flips! The point now is that if we allow infinite protocols, then we should be able to use that coin to simulate any other coin with arbitrary bias, and in particular get all real probabilities. Formally, this is by combining the preservation of cofiltered limits with functoriality with respect to measurable maps {0,1}N→{0,1}\{0,1\}^{\mathbb{N}} \to \{0,1\}{0,1}N→{0,1}, which will create new generalized elements of {0,1}\{0,1\}{0,1}. This will hopefully make the resulting set over {0,1}\{0,1\}{0,1} become exactly [0,1][0,1][0,1].

Hey everyone—I haven't really been part of the discussion here, but I've recently taken a good chunk out of this problem—the preprint is here: https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.15485

My result is that Borel→BorelStoch\mathsf{Borel} \to \mathsf{BorelStoch}Borel→BorelStoch (writing Borel\mathsf{Borel}Borel for standard Borel spaces and measurable maps) is the initial way of adding an unbiased coinflip (suitably axiomatized), among Markov functors that preserves countable (determinitsic) coproducts, countable Kolmogorov products and pullbacks along deterministic coproduct inclusions (that is, strong Markov functors so that the restriction to the deterministic subcategories is countably extensive and countably continuous).

Eigil Rischel (Jan 20 2026 at 16:17):

I wanted to comment a bit on Tobias' conjecture above about probability monads being generated by a transcendentally biased coinflip (and no equations). My result rules this out for the Giry monad on Borel: if Giry is free on a transcendental coinflip, it implies the Giry monad has many automorphisms: for every pair of transcendental numbers, there is a monad morphism carrying one to the other. But such an automorphism is determined by where it carries the fair coinflip (since this generates the Giry monad, too) and must carry the fair coinflip to itself (because it must be carried to a symmetric coinflip, of which there is only one). Hence the Giry monad has no nontrivial automorphisms. I think this also works for many other probability monads (like on CHaus and so on), at least if you believe the fair coinflip generates the whole monad (but not necessarily freely), and it seems hard to believe that a transcendental coinflip is generating but not a fair one.
