In case this is relevant: the set μ−1(dx)⊆GGX\mu^{-1}(dx)\subseteq GGXμ−1(dx)⊆GGX is naturally equipped with the structure of a partial order under partial evaluation. δ(gx)\delta(gx)δ(gx) and Gδ(dx)G\delta(dx)Gδ(dx) are the top and bottom elements in this partial order, and this order comes up indeed in Bayesian inference (as "Blackwell's order"). This is part of an active line of research, see the last paper on the topic as well as the references therein.

Paolo Perrone (May 15 2025 at 14:14):

Also something that could be relevant: one of the most natural ways to come up with elements of GGXGGXGGX is via de Finetti's theorem.
The theorem says that GGXGGXGGX is in (natural, functorial, etc) bijection with exchangeable sequences, which form the subspace of G(XN)G(X^\mathbb{N})G(XN) of those measures on XNX^\mathbb{N}XN which are invariant under finite permutations.
Now, given dx∈GXdx\in GXdx∈GX,

  • The measure dx⊗dx⊗dx⊗...dx\otimes dx\otimes dx\otimes...dx⊗dx⊗dx⊗... (that is, the iid measure) corresponds exactly to δ(dx)∈GGX\delta(dx)\in GGXδ(dx)∈GGX. (These are exactly the extreme points of the simplex GGXGGXGGX, this is related to the Hewitt-Savage zero-one law)
  • The measure GΔ(dx)G\Delta(dx)GΔ(dx), where Δ:X→XN\Delta:X\to X^\mathbb{N}Δ:X→XN is the diagonal map, corresponds exactly to Gδ(dx)G\delta(dx)Gδ(dx).

The Dirichlet process is exchangeable, and so we can apply de Finetti, obtaining once again an element of GGXGGXGGX. A way to see this explicitly is via the stick-breaking picture.

Hugo Paquet (May 15 2025 at 16:40):

Thanks Alex, we also tried to be a bit more precise in Section 8 of this paper: https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.17595.
The idea is roughly that the Dirichlet process is natural because the atom weights are sampled independenly (in the probabilistic sense) from the atom locations. In this case, the weights are generated by stick-breaking, but you will still get a natural transformation if you use any other distribution on the space of families of weights that sum to 111. (We called these "element-free distributions".)

Hugo Paquet (May 15 2025 at 16:42):

There is quite a bit of work on random discrete distributions (by Kingman and Pitman and probably many others in probability theory).

Hugo Paquet (May 15 2025 at 16:45):

I don't know much about random continuous distributions. Naturality is quite strong, we showed that the continuous part of a natural random distribution must coincide with the base measure after renormalizing. (By "base measure" I mean the input to the function GX→GGXGX \to GGXGX→GGX).
