So I think I was satisfied with the resolution here. Let me summarize (see the last bullet below for a lingering, more general question, about "solving species equations" in general):

  • Define w(z)=−W(−z)w(z) = -W(-z)w(z)=−W(−z) (to get rid of the minus signs in the Taylor series).

  • This gives us a functional equation
    (1)w(z)=zew(z)(1) \qquad w(z) = z e^{w(z)}(1)w(z)=zew(z).

  • This functional equation (1)(1)(1) categorifies to a "species equation" which exactly says that w(z)w(z)w(z) is the exponential generating function for the species of rooted trees.

I think this species equation is well-known (it even appears as an example illustrating composition of species, buried in the wikipedia page for Combinatorial Species, where the species for rooted trees is called "ArArAr" -- as that's a French abbreviation, I suspect this example appears already in Joyal's original paper). But here's a rundown: if XXX is a finite set, then a rooted tree structure on XXX (corresponding to the w(z)w(z)w(z) on the LHS of (1)(1)(1)) is given by choosing a basepoint (that's the zzz factor on the RHS of (1)(1)(1)), partitioning the rest (that's the exponential on the RHS of (1)(1)(1)), and then choosing a rooted tree structure on each member of the partition (that's the w(z)w(z)w(z) on the RHS of (1)(1)(1)).

  • I don't know a direct argument which allows us to compute that

(2)w(z)=∑n≥1nn−1n!zn (2) \qquad w(z) = \sum_{n \geq 1} \frac{n^{n-1}}{n!} z^n(2)w(z)=∑n≥1​n!nn−1​zn.

But apparently it's a famous theorem of Cayley that the number of unrooted trees on a labeled set of order nnn is nn−2n^{n-2}nn−2. You can deduce (2)(2)(2) from Cayley's formula, of course, simply by noting that there are nnn possible choices of "root" for each unrooted tree on nnn elements. And in turn, Cayley's theorem has a beautiful "species-inspired" proof given by Joyal, again in his original paper on species. Everything I know about this I learned from Tom Leinster's lovely blog post on the topic.

(Here's what I learned from Tom's blog post: Joyal's proof actually proceeds by counting bipointed trees TTT (where the two chosen points AAA and BBB are allowed to be equal). To see that there are nnn^nnn of these, Joyal gives a bijection to the set of endofunctions of XXX as follows. Observe that there is a unique path [A,B][A,B][A,B] in TTT from AAA to BBB, specified by a total ordering of the vertices of [A,B][A,B][A,B]. The rest of the tree structure TTT on XXX amounts to the choice of a partial endofunction PPP of XXX, which tells each vertex not in [A,B][A,B][A,B] how to move one step closer to [A,B][A,B][A,B]. Now Joyal observes that total orderings on [A,B][A,B][A,B] are in (noncanonical) bijection with permutations of [A,B][A,B][A,B]. Swapping the former for the latter, we now how the data to promote PPP to an endofunction of all of XXX, and this correspondence between bipointed trees and endofunctions is bijective -- though not Σn\Sigma_nΣn​-equivariant.)

  • Earlier I referred to "the non-canonical identification of the species for linear orders and the species for permutations". That was an incorrect reference -- it's worse than that. Really the species LinLinLin of linear orders and the species of ExpExpExp of permutations have the same generating function, but are not isomorphic species at all. One way to think about it is that Exp(X)Exp(X)Exp(X) is the set ΣX\Sigma_XΣX​ with its natural conjugation action, whereas Lin(X)Lin(X)Lin(X) is the set ΣX\Sigma_XΣX​ with its action by left multiplication. These ΣX\Sigma_XΣX​-sets are not isomorphic -- the latter, but not the former, is a ΣX\Sigma_XΣX​-torsor -- it has a unique orbit, which is free. The former has an orbit for every cycle type / partition of ∣X∣|X|∣X∣, and they are not free.

  • I wonder if there is another species categorifying the generating function w(z)w(z)w(z) which is a sub-species of the species of endofunctions? Something like "partial endofunctions defined at all but one point", except that's not quite right unless you can say which point... This would be a natural species with the same generating function which is not isomorphic as a species, much as Joyal / Cayley show us that the species of bipointed trees and the species of endofunctions have the same generating function but are not isomorphic.

  • I guess one thing I'd still like to understand better is the following: I'm a bit hazy on the art of "solving species equations" in general. If I have a functional equation of generating functions like (1)(1)(1), then I suppose I could say something about the set of generating functions satisfying the equations. For instance, I could prove by inducting through the coefficients of the Taylor series that (2)(2)(2) is the unique solution to (1)(1)(1). But if I interpret (1)(1)(1) as a species equation, I suddenly get a bit worried -- sitting here right now I would not assert with 100% confidence that the species of rooted trees is the unique species satisfying (1)(1)(1) (EDIT: wait -- do we even expect this??) -- I would have to really think about how to inductively nail down all the Σn\Sigma_nΣn​ actions. And of course, the sense of "unique" is more flexible -- does the species of rooted trees have any automorphisms, for instance?
