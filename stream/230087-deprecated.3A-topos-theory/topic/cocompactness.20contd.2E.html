A comorphism of sites F:(C,J)→(D,K)F: (\mathcal{C},J) \to (\mathcal{D},K)F:(C,J)→(D,K) consists of a functor F:C→DF: \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{D}F:C→D which has the cover lifting property. Any such induces a geometric morphism f:Sh(C,J)→Sh(D,K)f: \mathbf{Sh}(\mathcal{C},J) \to \mathbf{Sh}(\mathcal{D},K)f:Sh(C,J)→Sh(D,K) whose inverse image functor f∗f^*f∗ is defined by P↦aJ(P∘F)P \mapsto \mathbf{a}_J(P \circ F)P↦aJ​(P∘F). This is an extension of the definition on representables: the covering lifting property guarantees that for an object DDD of D\mathcal{D}D, f∗(ℓ(D))=aJ(HomD(F(−),D))f^*(\ell(D)) = \mathbf{a}_J(\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{D}}(F(-),D))f∗(ℓ(D))=aJ​(HomD​(F(−),D)). Have a look at @Olivia Caramello's preprint from this summer for lots of results on comorphisms of sites. As you can probably tell, the sheafification involved in this definition can be challenging to work with. If only there were a way to move that operation inside the brackets...

Given a site (C,J)(\mathcal{C},J)(C,J), recall that the plus construction is a process by which one turns presheaves on C\mathcal{C}C into JJJ-separated presheaves, and then turns these into JJJ-sheaves. Given a presheaf PPP on C\mathcal{C}C, we obtain P+P^+P+ as
P+(C)=colimR∈J(C)Match(R,P)P^+(C) = \mathrm{colim}_{R \in J(C)}\mathrm{Match}(R,P)P+(C)=colimR∈J(C)​Match(R,P), where the latter collection is the collection of matching families for PPP indexed by the morphisms in the JJJ-covering sieve RRR. Assuming the sites are small-generated, in the special case P=HomD(F(−),D)P = \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{D}}(F(-),D)P=HomD​(F(−),D), the conditions on a matching family mean that we can express Match(R,P)\mathrm{Match}(R,P)Match(R,P) as HomSh(D,K)(colimRF(Ci),ℓ(D))\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Sh}(\mathcal{D},K)}(\mathrm{colim}_{R}F(C_i),\ell(D))HomSh(D,K)​(colimR​F(Ci​),ℓ(D)), where that colimit is indexed by RRR, viewed as a subcategory of C/C\mathcal{C}/CC/C, and is taken in Sh(D,K)\mathbf{Sh}(\mathcal{D},K)Sh(D,K) in case D\mathcal{D}D lacks the required colimits. I'll write FR(C)F_R(C)FR​(C) for that colimit object. Thus P+(C)=colimR∈J(C)HomSh(D,K)(FR(C),ℓ(D))P^+(C) = \mathrm{colim}_{R \in J(C)}\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Sh}(\mathcal{D},K)}(F_R(C),\ell(D))P+(C)=colimR∈J(C)​HomSh(D,K)​(FR​(C),ℓ(D)).

Now for the link with my earlier post: if ℓ(D)\ell(D)ℓ(D) happens to be a strongly cocompact object, since the JJJ-covering sieves form a filtered diagram, we get an isomorphism HomSh(D,K)(lim⁡R∈J(C)FR(C),ℓ(D))≅colimR∈J(C)HomSh(D,K)(FR(C),ℓ(D))\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Sh}(\mathcal{D},K)}(\lim_{R \in J(C)}F_R(C),\ell(D)) \cong \mathrm{colim}_{R \in J(C)}\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Sh}(\mathcal{D},K)}(F_R(C),\ell(D))HomSh(D,K)​(limR∈J(C)​FR​(C),ℓ(D))≅colimR∈J(C)​HomSh(D,K)​(FR​(C),ℓ(D)). This looks exciting: a way to re-express the inverse image of a geometric morphism coming from a comorphism of sites in an almost-representable form!

In the examples of Grothendieck toposes I've looked at so far, strongly cocompact objects are too rare for this to be useful. But I haven't looked at a lot of examples yet!

The same kind of argument applies when you compute stalks of strongly cocompact objects. The stalk of a sheaf F\mathcal{F}F is
colimi F(Ci)\mathrm{colim}_{i }\, \mathcal{F}(C_i)colimi​F(Ci​),
if the point is given by a formal cofiltered limit limi Ci\mathrm{lim}_{i } \, C_ilimi​Ci​.

If F\mathcal{F}F is strongly cocompact, then
colimi F(Ci)=Hom(limi yCi,F)\mathrm{colim}_i \, \mathcal{F}(C_i) = \mathrm{Hom}(\mathrm{lim}_i \, \mathbf{y}C_i, \mathcal{F})colimi​F(Ci​)=Hom(limi​yCi​,F).

In a topological space XXX the points are formal intersections limi Ui\mathrm{lim}_i\, U_ilimi​Ui​ for open sets UiU_iUi​. Now suppose that XXX is Hausdorff without isolated points. The stalk of a strongly cocompact sheaf at a point like that is then:
colimi F(Ui)=Hom(limi yUi,F)=Hom(∅,F)=1\mathrm{colim}_i \, \mathcal{F}(U_i) = \mathrm{Hom}(\mathrm{lim}_i \, \mathbf{y}U_i, \mathcal{F}) = \mathrm{Hom}(\varnothing, \mathcal{F}) = 1colimi​F(Ui​)=Hom(limi​yUi​,F)=Hom(∅,F)=1.

So F\mathcal{F}F has a unique element in each stalk, which can only happen if F\mathcal{F}F is the terminal object (maybe you looked at this example already).
