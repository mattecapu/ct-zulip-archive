Morgan Rogers (he/him) (Apr 26 2020 at 10:28):

The answer to this question is always yes (and technically lots of things, but we can at least reconstruct one).
The topos of presheaves on PPP classifies flat functors with domain PPP. Since flat functors preserve any finite limits that exist, this means that all of the morphisms in PPP must get sent to monomorphisms and any meets get sent to products. Since the inverse image functor of any geometric morphism E‚Üí[Pop,Set]\mathcal{E} \to [P^{\mathrm{op}},\mathrm{Set}]E‚Üí[Pop,Set] must preserve the terminal object, we conclude that this topos classifies P-indexed collections of sub-terminal objects.

The subtopos of ¬¨¬¨\neg\neg¬¨¬¨-sheaves classifies a quotient of this theory (those PPP-indexed diagrams of subterminals satisfying some extra constraints), and I'm sure with a little more work we could work out a nice expression for that.

Jens Hemelaer (Apr 26 2020 at 19:46):

Yes, I agree. The definition of flat functor makes this formal.
In practice it is difficult to give a concrete description of what the flat functors are. It becomes easier when the poset P has meets, because then by Corollary 2.4 of https://ncatlab.org/nlab/show/flat+functor a functor F:P‚ÜíEF : P \to \mathcal{E}F:P‚ÜíE is flat if and only if it preserves finite limits.
So for example: if PPP has two elements 0 and 1, with 0 < 1, then the topos of presheaves over PPP classifies subterminal objects, i.e. flat functors P‚ÜíEP \to \mathcal{E}P‚ÜíE are the same as subobjects of the terminal object in E\mathcal{E}E. By the way, the topos of presheaves in this case is equivalent to the topos of sheaves on the Sierpinski space.
If PPP has four elements 0,1,a,b with 0 < a < 1 and 0 < b < 1, then taking a flat functors P‚ÜíEP \to \mathcal{E}P‚ÜíE is the same as taking a pair of subobjects of the terminal object of E\mathcal{E}E.
For more difficult posets, the characterization in terms of flat functors is probably the best you can do.
(At least if you want to study flat functors F:P‚ÜíEF : P \to \mathcal{E}F:P‚ÜíE for E\mathcal{E}E arbitrary.)

Mike Shulman (Apr 27 2020 at 23:06):

@Jens Hemelaer Since, as you say, different geometric theories can have the same category of models in a classical Set (the most blatant example being a theory that has no models in a classical Set but has models in nonclassical toposes), there can't really be a general procedure in that direction. But in this particular case, I think you can really just write down the explicit theory of flat cover-preserving functors on P (which is propositional, since P is a poset) and observe that its models in Set are generic filters on P.
