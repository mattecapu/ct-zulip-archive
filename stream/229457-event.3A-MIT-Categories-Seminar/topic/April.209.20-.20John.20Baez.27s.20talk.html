John Baez (Apr 06 2020 at 01:10):

My talk is on "Structured cospans and Petri nets".

Abstract. “Structured cospans” are a general way to study networks with inputs and outputs. Here we illustrate this using a type of network popular in theoretical computer science: Petri nets. An “open” Petri net is one with certain places designated as inputs and outputs. We can compose open Petri nets by gluing the outputs of one to the inputs of another. Using the formalism of structured cospans, open Petri nets can be treated as morphisms of a symmetric monoidal category — or better, a symmetric monoidal double category. We explain two forms of semantics for open Petri nets using symmetric monoidal double functors out of this double category. The first, an operational semantics, gives for each open Petri net a category whose morphisms are the processes that this net can carry out. The second, a “reachability” semantics, simply says what these processes can accomplish. This is joint work with Kenny Courser and Jade Master.

You can see the slides for my talk here.

The talk is based on these papers:

  • John Baez and Kenny Courser, Structured cospans.
  • John Baez and Jade Master, Open Petri nets.
  • Jade Master, Generalized Petri nets.

This talk is a followup to my earlier talk Structured cospans and double categories, but it should be understandable on its own.

We're looking for a map X⊗X→Y⊗YX \otimes X \to Y \otimes YX⊗X→Y⊗Y. The ingredients are maps f,g:X→Yf,g: X \to Yf,g:X→Y. The three maps are (f⊗1X)∘(f⊗1X),(f⊗1X)∘(g⊗1X)(f \otimes 1_X) \circ (f \otimes 1_X), (f \otimes 1_X) \circ (g \otimes 1_X)(f⊗1X​)∘(f⊗1X​),(f⊗1X​)∘(g⊗1X​), and (g⊗1X)∘(g⊗1X)(g \otimes 1_X) \circ (g \otimes 1_X)(g⊗1X​)∘(g⊗1X​). John Baez (Apr 14 2020 at 05:22):

@Faré is correct. An element of the free commutative monoid on SSS is a finite formal sum of elements of SSS, so we can identify it with a function f:S→Nf: S \to \mathbb{N}f:S→N that's zero except at finitely many points of SSS. The set of these functions is the same as the set of all functions f:S→Nf : S \to \mathbb{N}f:S→N iff SSS is finite.

So, N[S]\mathbb{N}[S]N[S] is naturally isomorphic to NS\mathbb{N}^SNS as a commutative monoid when SSS is finite, but not otherwise.

A subtler but extremely important point is that

S↦N[S] S \mapsto \mathbb{N}[S]S↦N[S]

defines a covariant functor Set→CommMon\mathsf{Set} \to \mathsf{CommMon}Set→CommMon, while

S↦NS S \mapsto \mathbb{N}^S S↦NS

defines a contravariant functor Set→CommMon\mathsf{Set} \to \mathsf{CommMon}Set→CommMon.

None of this depends on the special features of N\mathbb{N}N. A commutative monoid is an N\mathbb{N}N-module, but we can (and should) generalize everything I just said to modules of any commutative monoid MMM.

Here's how it goes. Let M[S]M[S]M[S] be the free MMM-module on a set SSS, and MSM^SMS be the MMM-module of functions from SSS to MMM. In the case of finite sets we can use the isomorphism M[S]≅MS\mathbb{M}[S] \cong \mathbb{M}^SM[S]≅MS to make

S↦M[S] S \mapsto \mathbb{M}[S]S↦M[S]

into both a covariant and a contravariant functor from FinSet\mathsf{FinSet}FinSet to the category of MMM-modules. But it only defines a covariant functor from Set\mathsf{Set}Set to the category of MMM-modules, and

S↦NS S \mapsto \mathbb{N}^S S↦NS

only defines a contravariant functor from Set\mathsf{Set}Set to the category of MMM-modules.

It's the covariant functor that's relevant in my paper with Jade. In my paper on reaction networks with Blake and my paper on Markov processes with Kenny it's really important that

S↦R[S] S \mapsto \mathbb{R}[S]S↦R[S]

defines both a covariant and contravariant functor from FinSet\mathsf{FinSet}FinSet to the category of real vector spaces.
