John Baez (Jan 14 2021 at 17:04):

I've often wondered about the history of this. I just learned that Abū Naṣr al-Fārābī (Iraq, c. 870–c. 950) noticed this issue when explaining Aristotle's syllogisms. Aristotle's syllogisms have funny names like "Barbara", and it turns out "Darapti" is a bad one:

All squares are rectangles.
All squares are rhombuses.
∴ Some rhombuses are rectangles.

According to the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy:

Aristotle’s justification of Darapti relies on the conversion implication: from “Every B is an A” infer “Some A is a B” (cf. [Syllogism] 30.3). This implication fails to hold if we allow “Every B is an A” to be true when there are no Bs. Accordingly al-Fārābī declares that “Every B is an A” is false unless there is at least one B ([Categories] 124.14). Though he sometimes seems to forget he said this, he is the earliest logician on record as having spelled out this implication of “Every B is an A”.

So Al-Farabi at least made it explicit that he was ruling out the empty set, though I don't like this solution to the problem!

I think logicians are still fighting about this issue, but I think ∀xP(x)⇒∃xP(x)\forall x P(x) \Rightarrow \exist x P(x) ∀xP(x)⇒∃xP(x) is losing the fight.

In

  • Harry Gensler, Introduction to Logic, 2nd edition, 2010, page 32.

there's a nice simple explanation of why Aristotle's approach is not so good.

Historically, “Aristotelian” and “modern” logicians disagree about the validity of some syllogism forms. They disagree because of differing policies about allowing empty terms (general terms that don’t refer to any existing beings).

Compare these two arguments:

  • All cats are animals. Therefore : Some animals are cats.

  • All unicorns are animals. Therefore : Some animals are unicorns.

The first seems valid while the second seems invalid. Yet both have the same form [...]. What’s going on here?

When we read the first argument, we tend to presuppose that there’s at least one cat. Given this as an assumed additional premise, it follows validly that some animals are cats. When we read the second argument, we don’t assume that there’s at least one unicorn. Without this additional assumption, it doesn’t follow that some animals are unicorns.

The Aristotelian view, which assumes that each general term in a syllogism refers to at least one existing being, calls the argument “valid.” The modern view, which allows empty terms like “unicorn” that don’t refer to existing beings, calls the argument “invalid.”

Consider this pair of arguments with the same form (a form that’s valid on the Aristotelian view but invalid on the modern view):

  • All cats are mammals. All cats are furry. Therefore : Some mammals are furry.

  • All square circles are squares. All square circles are circles. Therefore : Some squares are circles.

The first inference is sensible, because there are cats. The second inference isn’t sensible, because there are no square circles. Some logic books use the Aristotelian view, but most use the modern view. It makes a difference in very few cases.

Here's a webpage on the subject, that doesn't completely answer your question:

  • Existential import

A proposition is said to have existential import if the truth of the proposition requires a belief in the existence of members of the subject class. I and O propositions have existential import; they assert that the classes designated by their subject terms are not empty. But in Aristotelian logic, I and O propositions follow validly from A and E propositions by sub-alternation. As a result, Aristotelian logic requires A and E propositions to have existential import, because a proposition with existential import cannot be derived from a proposition without existential import. Thus, for Aristotle, if we believe it is that "All unicorns have one horn" then we are committed to believing that unicorns exist. For the modern logician or mathematician, this is an unacceptable result because modern mathematics and logic often deal with empty or null sets or with imaginary objects. A modern mathematician might, for example, wish to make a true claim about all irrational prime numbers. Since there are no irrational prime numbers, Aristotle would say that any claim about them is necessarily false.

George Boole developed an interpretation of categorical propositions solves the dilemma by denying that universal propositions have existential import. Modern logic accepts the Boolean interpretation of categorical propositions.

Todd Trimble (Jan 15 2021 at 23:00):

So here's the deal. In a traditional sequent-based presentation of deductive systems, one has a pair of rules

∀xP(x)⊢P(x),P(x)⊢∃xP(x)\forall_x P(x) \vdash P(x), \qquad P(x) \vdash \exists_x P(x)∀x​P(x)⊢P(x),P(x)⊢∃x​P(x)

where the turnstyle ⊢\vdash⊢ denotes logical entailment, and of course something like these rules occur in the categorical language as well (but let me come to that in a moment). By transitivity of entailment, we are apparently led to ∀xP(x)⊢∃xP(x)\forall_x P(x) \vdash \exists_x P(x)∀x​P(x)⊢∃x​P(x). So what could be the problem?

Todd Trimble (Jan 15 2021 at 23:00):

The problem is that the rules involves a tacit conventional shorthand which is brushing some details under the carpet. Let's back up a minute. A free variable xxx in an expression P(x)P(x)P(x) is attached to a type or domain DDD where the variable is considered as taking values. In conventional language, we associate to an expression P(x)P(x)P(x) a truth set of values vvv in DDD where P(v)P(v)P(v) is true. The interpretation of P(x)⊢Q(x)P(x) \vdash Q(x)P(x)⊢Q(x), where P,QP, QP,Q share a free variable xxx, is that we have a corresponding inclusion between truth sets, both being subsets of DDD. In other words, if P(v)P(v)P(v) is true for an element vvv in DDD, then so is Q(v)Q(v)Q(v).

Todd Trimble (Jan 15 2021 at 23:01):

Now, ∀xP(x)\forall_x P(x)∀x​P(x) is a closed expression (i.e., is closed if xxx is the only free variable of P(x)P(x)P(x)). What is its truth set? Well, for such a proposition with 0 free variables, we wouldn't normally refer to its truth set, but rather to its truth value. But we could think of it as a truth set, namely as a subset of a 1-element set 111, where it's all of 111 if true and the empty subset if false.

Todd Trimble (Jan 15 2021 at 23:01):

Armed with these understandings, we can come back and examine the sequent ∀xP(x)⊢P(x)\forall_x P(x) \vdash P(x)∀x​P(x)⊢P(x). As it stands, this doesn't have a coherent interpretation! The truth set of the left side is a subset of 111; the truth set of the right side is a subset of DDD. There's a mismatch. To speak of an entailment relation P⊢QP \vdash QP⊢Q between formulae P,QP, QP,Q, we should insist that their truth sets be subsets of the same domain DDD, so that we can compare them. In other words, that the formulas PPP and QQQ be of the same type.

Todd Trimble (Jan 15 2021 at 23:02):

So what ought we have said instead of ∀xP(x)⊢P(x)\forall_x P(x) \vdash P(x)∀x​P(x)⊢P(x)? The main thing to say is that if we have a proposition ccc of empty type 111 (for example, if ccc is a closed term), then we can "pull back" to a formula of the same type as P(x)P(x)P(x), e.g., we can consider the expression c∧[x=x]c \wedge [x = x]c∧[x=x]. (At the truth set level, this corresponds to the set-theoretic pullback of a subset of 111 along the unique function D→1D \to 1D→1.) Category theorists like to denote this pullback formula by something like D∗cD^\ast cD∗c. Anyway, we pull back the formula ∀xP(x)\forall_x P(x)∀x​P(x) to D∗∀xP(x)D^\ast \forall_x P(x)D∗∀x​P(x), and this can now be compared with P(x)P(x)P(x). Our rule becomes

D∗∀xP(x)⊢P(x)D^\ast \forall_x P(x) \vdash P(x)D∗∀x​P(x)⊢P(x)

or, if we really wanted to be hygienic about it, we should just admit that quantification is always relative to some domain DDD, and declare that domain, instead of just leaving it implicit. Thus, we should rather write

D∗∀x:DP(x)⊢P(x)(1)D^\ast \forall_{x: D} P(x) \vdash P(x) \qquad (1)D∗∀x:D​P(x)⊢P(x)(1).

Todd Trimble (Jan 15 2021 at 23:02):

Category theorists will recognize here a counit of an adjunction. In the language of Lawvere, universal quantification along a domain DDD is right adjoint to pulling back along D→1D \to 1D→1. In the semantic language of truth sets, letting [P][P][P] denote the truth set of a formula PPP, this means we ought to have [D∗c]⊆[P][D^\ast c] \subseteq [P][D∗c]⊆[P] if and only if [c]⊆[∀x:DP(x)][c] \subseteq [\forall_{x: D} P(x)][c]⊆[∀x:D​P(x)]. If we want the syntactic language to match up, we ought to arrange our deductive system so that from a sequent D∗c⊢P(x)D^\ast c \vdash P(x)D∗c⊢P(x) we can derive the sequent c⊢∀x:DP(x)c \vdash \forall_{x: D} P(x)c⊢∀x:D​P(x), and vice-versa. But that will follow from (1) above together with the unit of the adjunction

c⊢∀x:DD∗cc \vdash \forall_{x: D} D^\ast cc⊢∀x:D​D∗c

(together with other very standard rules).

Todd Trimble (Jan 15 2021 at 23:02):

(Actually, Lawvere generalizes this considerably: it doesn't have to be a function D→1D \to 1D→1 with codomain 111. In principle one could pull back a formula QQQ of type YYY along any function f:X→Yf: X \to Yf:X→Y (or a morphism in the domain of a hyperdoctrine) to get a formula f∗Qf^\ast Qf∗Q of type XXX, and likewise there is a universal quantification operator ∀f\forall_f∀f​ taking a formula PPP of type XXX to a formula ∀fP\forall_f P∀f​P of type YYY, enjoying the same type of adjunction relationship as above. But now is not the time to go into the significance of this powerful generalization of Lawvere.)

Todd Trimble (Jan 15 2021 at 23:03):

The sequent P(x)⊢∃xP(x)P(x) \vdash \exists_x P(x)P(x)⊢∃x​P(x) (I ought to write P(x)⊢∃x:DP(x)P(x) \vdash \exists_{x: D} P(x)P(x)⊢∃x:D​P(x)) undergoes a similar treatment. Here existential quantification is left adjoint to pulling back, and so we ought to write this sequent as

P(x)⊢D∗∃x:DP(x)(3)P(x) \vdash D^\ast \exists_{x: D} P(x) \qquad (3)P(x)⊢D∗∃x:D​P(x)(3)

which is a unit for an adjunction ∃x:D⊣D∗\exists_{x: D} \dashv D^\ast∃x:D​⊣D∗ (where the categories involved are, at the semantic level, posets of truth sets and inclusions between them; at the syntactic level, posets of formulae of the same type partially ordered by provable/derivable entailment).

Todd Trimble (Jan 15 2021 at 23:03):

Composing (1) and (3), we get the "corrected" form of the sequent we are debating, which takes the form

D∗∀x:DP(x)⊢D∗∃x:DP(x)D^\ast \forall_{x: D} P(x) \vdash D^\ast \exists_{x: D} P(x)D∗∀x:D​P(x)⊢D∗∃x:D​P(x)

where now all the types match up correctly.

Todd Trimble (Jan 15 2021 at 23:04):

Now, it may be felt that the notation is a bit heavy at this point. It's clear at this point that some obeisance to a typing discipline is required to make sense of entailments, but that modulo that, mathematicians are accustomed to validating ∀x:DP(x)⊢P(x)\forall_{x: D} P(x) \vdash P(x)∀x:D​P(x)⊢P(x) by mentally inserting some dummy free variables if need be to put the antecedent and succedent on the same footing. What's the expression the type theorists use? I think it's coercion: we subconsciously apply implicit coercions to get expressions to match and make sense.

Todd Trimble (Jan 15 2021 at 23:04):

But the main takeaway is not to conflate different contexts for entailments. The type discipline strongly suggests that we consider entailments in context, i.e., a separate entailment ⊢D\vdash_D⊢D​ for each domain DDD, writing for example ∀x:DP(x)⊢DP(x)\forall_{x: D} P(x) \vdash_D P(x)∀x:D​P(x)⊢D​P(x), or even just

∀xP(x)⊢DP(x)\forall_x P(x) \vdash_D P(x)∀x​P(x)⊢D​P(x).

This is the device Lambek (and Scott) use, rather than smushing all the entailments together into a single symbol ⊢\vdash⊢, which potentially conflates contexts and leads to confusion.

Todd Trimble (Jan 15 2021 at 23:05):

Thus, we can assert (modulo these coercions) that ∀xP(x)⊢D∃xP(x)\forall_x P(x) \vdash_D \exists_x P(x)∀x​P(x)⊢D​∃x​P(x) is universally valid -- just so long as we're clear what that means exactly and where we are in context!

And we can write ⊢1\vdash_1⊢1​, entailment between truth values, simply as ⊢\vdash⊢ if we want to.

Todd Trimble (Jan 15 2021 at 23:05):

So,

∀x:DP(x)⊢D∃x:DP(x)(4)\forall_{x: D} P(x) \vdash_D \exists_{x: D} P(x) \qquad (4)∀x:D​P(x)⊢D​∃x:D​P(x)(4)

is universally valid. The entailment

∀x:DP(x)⊢∃x:DP(x)(5)\forall_{x: D} P(x) \vdash \exists_{x: D} P(x) \qquad (5)∀x:D​P(x)⊢∃x:D​P(x)(5),

where ⊢=⊢1\vdash = \vdash_1⊢=⊢1​, is not universally valid.

Todd Trimble (Jan 15 2021 at 23:06):

But, if we can produce a term a:1→Da: 1 \to Da:1→D, then we can derive that last sequent by hitting (4) on both sides with the pullback operator a∗a^\asta∗. In language closer to hyperdoctrines, we have a composite

1→aD→!11 \stackrel{a}{\to} D \stackrel{!}{\to} 11→aD→!1

which is the identity on 111. Correspondingly, a∗∘D∗a^\ast \circ D^\asta∗∘D∗, by which we really mean a∗∘!∗a^\ast \circ !^\asta∗∘!∗, behaves as an identity pullback operator. Hence if we apply a∗a^\asta∗ to both sides of the correctly written entailment

D∗∀x:DP(x)⊢DD∗∃x:DP(x)D^\ast \forall_{x: D} P(x) \vdash_D D^\ast \exists_{x: D} P(x)D∗∀x:D​P(x)⊢D​D∗∃x:D​P(x),

we get

a∗D∗∀x:DP(x)⊢1a∗D∗∃x:DP(x)a^\ast D^\ast \forall_{x: D} P(x) \vdash_1 a^\ast D^\ast \exists_{x: D} P(x)a∗D∗∀x:D​P(x)⊢1​a∗D∗∃x:D​P(x)

which reduces to

∀x:DP(x)⊢1∃x:DP(x)\forall_{x: D} P(x) \vdash_1 \exists_{x: D} P(x)∀x:D​P(x)⊢1​∃x:D​P(x).

(That's if we have a term a:1→Da: 1 \to Da:1→D to work with.) And all is well.
