James Deikun (Feb 01 2026 at 07:15):

I would like to second @Kevin Carlson and say that ChatGPT is currently incapable of doing research-level reasoning or writing without constant monitoring by an actual researcher with a good understanding of what it's trying to do and the means by which it's trying to do it. You can't just have it do a big lump of work that you don't know how to check and get it checked afterwards and expect the result to be anything but worthless.

Further, asking professional researchers to check "your" research that was done by ChatGPT is looking down on them, and even asking ChatGPT to do the things you ask of it is arrogant on your part. You're trying to get credit for being a "researcher", or perhaps trying to drive research agendas, when you're only actually doing what amounts to brainstorming, which is less than 10% of the work of research when you do it correctly, and way less than that if you don't understand enough to know when the ideas you come up with are any good. If you want to be a researcher, put in the work and become capable of understanding the concepts you're trying to use. There are plenty of university programs, and plenty of free learning materials for the autodidact. If you're not capable of doing that, there are many ways to contribute to the world other than research.

James Deikun (Feb 01 2026 at 19:30):

It's already been seen that the robot can't do that. One of the biggest problems with all LLMs so far, is that they don't have an internal concept of confidence to connect to how confident the text sounds, so they can only tune how confident the text sounds to external cues, like how confident the user seems to be in their results. The LLMs themselves are "not great with math", but they don't know it. It becomes a process of the blind leading the blind, and the results are not good.

James Deikun (Feb 01 2026 at 19:35):

The reasons people want money from you to check your papers are dual: 1) they think they will be wasting their time (the papers will not be salvageable) and 2) you are not offering the usual currency in which this kind of work is paid for, namely co-credit and contribution of the result to one's own research program. You won't be able to participate in research of any kind as an insider until you fix both of these problems.

David Egolf (he/him) (Feb 01 2026 at 19:54):

I can relate to the challenge of wanting to do interesting research, but struggling to know how to do it! In my experience, LLMs are not as helpful as one might hope for this purpose. Specifically, they tend to give very "zoomed out" conceptual ideas - and struggle to give precise details and examples that would help these vague ideas really come to life! They also tend to lose track of what exactly they were talking about, contradicting themselves in somewhat subtle ways in longer conversations. That quickly becomes a roadblock if one presses an LLM for details on some big picture idea it has.

If you would like to develop your research skills, I think learning from a human is still (by far!) the best way. Personally, I have found it quite helpful to ask questions relating to specific exercises in areas of interest on this zulip. Working through a specific problem in detail is a lot of work, and is less exciting in some ways than trying to dream up new research ideas. But I think the practice it gives with "zooming in" - seeing how abstract concepts can be realized in very specific examples/theorems/definitions - can be quite valuable for the goal of doing research in the future.

This sounds interesting but very vague to me! What is an "operational configuration"? What is an "apparatus"? What is an "interface"? If you try to do this, you may find that there is some specific challenging-to-understand concept/approach/calculation/theorem used in prior work, which you would like to understand. Then, you could try asking a specific question about that thing on this zulip. That might lead to an interesting conversation helpful to your goal of doing research!

But Claude announced it was going to search that domain and I okayed it thinking that the resulting answer would be close to correct. I checked some of the results and they look reasonable. I'm also finding it helpful to use Claude to learn mathematics - right now, the mathematics of regular tuning systems as developed by Gene Ward Smith, an algebraist I knew slightly, who died of COVID in 2021, whose writings on music theory are unfortunately unpublished, and scattered in a number of chat forums and wikis. I learned about 'Tenney height' from Claude, and now I'm learning about other concepts, and it's a lot of fun.

However, in my interaction with Claude I'm constantly questioning it when I don't completely understand something, or doubt something, and comparing its calculations to my own, and comparing its definitions to those I can find elsewhere. And I am not eager to have it make large-scale decisions about what to do.

Here's a sample interaction:

JB: Is a temperament the kind of surjective homomophism you mentioned, or merely its kernel, as I first guessed?

Claude: That's a good question, and I think I was being imprecise.

Two homomorphisms with the same kernel can differ by an automorphism of the target. If φ: ℤⁿ → ℤʳ and ψ: ℤⁿ → ℤʳ have the same kernel, then ψ = A ∘ φ for some A ∈ GL(r, ℤ).

In the regular temperament paradigm, I believe the standard usage is:

  • The temperament is the kernel (or equivalently, the equivalence class of homomorphisms sharing that kernel). This is what the wedgie captures invariantly.

  • A mapping is a specific homomorphism—a choice of how to express the temperament in terms of generators. The "standard icon" [⟨1 2 4|, ⟨0 −1 −4|] on Gene Ward Smith's page is a mapping: it tells you not just which commas vanish, but which specific generators to use (period and fourth).

Different mappings for the same temperament correspond to different choices of basis for ℤʳ—i.e., different choices of generators. For meantone, you could use (octave, fifth) or (octave, fourth) or (octave, major second); these give different mappings but the same temperament.

So my earlier statement "a temperament is a surjective homomorphism" was too strong. The temperament is really just the kernel; the homomorphism (mapping) is additional structure you impose when you want to work with specific generators.

In short, I caught it in a mistake, and it fessed up.

(The term 'wedgie' sounds silly, but it's an invention of Gene Ward Smith, who used wedge products to describe submodules of ℤʳ.)

David Michael Roberts (Feb 02 2026 at 05:52):

Our current belief is that, as a general principle, mathematics papers should always be written by humans, even when the AI-generated content is (fully) mathematically correct. The reason is that authorship entails accountability for both mathematical precision and expositional integrity, such as the correctness of attributions, which is a responsibility that only humans can bear.

https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.22401
