Alexander Gietelink Oldenziel (Nov 26 2020 at 14:16):

Does anybody know the relation between the Kock-Lawvere axiom for a ring object RRR and a Topos having amazing right adjoints for atoms/tiny objects. Is the latter a categorical way of phrasing infinitesimal subspaces D⊂RD\subset RD⊂R?

Matteo Capucci (Nov 26 2020 at 14:35):

To have an amazing right adjoint is the definition of 'infinitesimal object' (for Lawvere, at least). The KC axioms uses an infinitesimal object to say that R×R→RDR \times R \to R^DR×R→RD defined by (x,y)↦x+εy(x,y) \mapsto x+\varepsilon y(x,y)↦x+εy has to be an iso

Fawzi Hreiki (Nov 26 2020 at 14:37):

I don't think this is correct actually. The K-L axiom says nothing about amazing right adjoints.

Fawzi Hreiki (Nov 26 2020 at 14:41):

The K-L axiom says that, for our ring object RRR, and our subspace D={d∈R:d2=0}D = \{d \in R : d^2 = 0\}D={d∈R:d2=0}, the map R×R→RDR \times R \rightarrow R^DR×R→RD, (x,y)↦(x+yϵ(x, y) \mapsto (x+y\epsilon(x,y)↦(x+yϵ) is an isomorphism. Basically, every map from DDD to RRR is affine and has a unique slope.

Ben MacAdam (Nov 27 2020 at 05:55):

The amazing right adjoint implies that DDD is a small projective, and it holds in a Grothedieck model of synthetic differential geometry whenever DDD is a representable functor. You can look at 5.5 in Basic Concepts of Enriched Category Theory for some background on how that argument works (I believe the small projective/representable functor thing is due to Bunge).
