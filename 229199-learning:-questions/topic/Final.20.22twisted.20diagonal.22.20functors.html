fosco (Sep 11 2020 at 09:38):

It is well known that given a category C\cal CC the following two conditions are equivalent:

  1. the diagonal functor Δ:C→C×C\Delta : {\cal C} \to {\cal C}\times {\cal C}Δ:C→C×C is final
  2. For every two objects X,YX,YX,Y of C{\cal C}C, the category of cospans X→A←YX \to A\leftarrow YX→A←Y is (nonempty and?) connected.

We say that C{\cal C}C is sifted if any of those conditions is true. It takes very little effort to verify that if C{\cal C}C is sifted, then every diagonal functor C→Cn{\cal C} \to {\cal C}^nC→Cn to a finite power of C{\cal C}C is also final.

Where I can look for an explicit proof of the above equivalence? It seems to me that it entails the following: let ∇\nabla∇ be the "twisted diagonal functor" ∇:Cop×C→Cop×Cop×C×C\nabla : {\cal C}^\text{op}\times {\cal C} \to {\cal C}^\text{op}\times {\cal C}^\text{op}\times {\cal C} \times {\cal C}∇:Cop×C→Cop×Cop×C×C, defined as ΔCop×ΔC\Delta_{{\cal C}^\text{op}}\times \Delta_{\cal C}ΔCop​×ΔC​. If C{\cal C}C is sifted and cosifted, for example if it has products and coproducts, then ∇\nabla∇ is final.

Is this correct, or I'm losing something?
