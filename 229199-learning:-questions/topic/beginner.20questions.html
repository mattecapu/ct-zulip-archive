Matteo Capucci (he/him) (Mar 01 2021 at 10:20):

Uh, there's certainly a lot of overlap among these books. I didn't read any of them from cover to cover though, so I don't know exactly how much. What's sure is that each of them covers CT from a different point of view, and this means that the set of things you'll learn from each of them is different, and even in the intersection the way you're exposed to thing is gonna be different.
For example, monads in Milewski's book are gonna be explained from a CS point of view (effects or inductive data types), which is quite different from monads in algebra (free constructions) and monads in topology (completions, I guess?).
This is a general theme in CT: the core concepts are not numerous, but they are incredibly versatile. So learning is not idempotent anymore.

John Baez (Mar 14 2021 at 07:06):

If XXX is a category with at least one object and YYY has no objects, then there is no functor f:X→Yf : X \to Yf:X→Y.

But if YYY is a category with at least one object and XXX is any category at all, there's at least one functor f:X→Yf: X \to Yf:X→Y. We can get one by choosing an object y∈Yy \in Yy∈Y, and mapping every object of XXX to yyy, and every morphism in XXX to the morphism 1y1_y1y​. You can check that this gives a functor.

John Baez (Mar 14 2021 at 07:07):

Also, if both XXX and YYY have no objects, then there is a unique functor from f:X→Yf: X \to Yf:X→Y.

John Baez (Mar 14 2021 at 07:11):

So, for any two categories CCC and DDD, there is always a functor f:C→Df: C \to Df:C→D or a functor f:D→Cf: D \to Cf:D→C (or both).

John Baez (Mar 14 2021 at 07:12):

All this is very like functions between sets: there's no function from a nonempty set to an empty set, but there's always a function from a nonempty set to any other nonempty set, and there's a unique function between empty sets.

John Baez (Apr 05 2021 at 15:20):

More bluntly, the answer to this question:

Can functors that have a natural transformation between them be considered equivalent?

is no, unless the natural transformation is an natural isomorphism. This question is like asking "should sets with a function between them be considered equivalent?" Indeed, the latter question is a special case of the former, since a set is the same as a functor F:1→SetF: 1 \to \mathsf{Set}F:1→Set, and a function between sets is the same as a natural transformation between such functors. (Here 111 is the category with just one object and its identity morphism.)

John Baez (Apr 05 2021 at 15:21):

The point is: things should not be considered equivalent unless there is an invertible map between them; an invertible natural transformation is the same as a natural isomorphism.

Mike Stay (Apr 05 2021 at 20:15):

things should not be considered equivalent unless there is an invertible map between them

@Kale Evans John's using n-categorical language when he says "equivalent" here. In the category theory literature, two objects are "isomorphic" when there's an invertible map between them. The word "equivalent" applies in a more general scenario. For example, objects C,DC, DC,D in a 2-category are equivalent if there's a pair of morphisms f:C→D,g:D→C,f:C\to D, g:D\to C,f:C→D,g:D→C, but gfgfgf isn't equal to the identity on CCC, only isomorphic to it. Similarly, fgfgfg is isomorphic to the identity on DDD. So in this case, "equivalent" means "isomorphic up to 2-isomorphism". When talking about n-categories, they'll say that two objects in an n-category are "equivalent" if they're "isomorphic up to 2-isomorphism up to ... up to n-isomorphism". So while it's technically true that two sets can be equivalent, or even two elements of a set, people usually use the terms "isomorphic" and "equal" for those cases.

Suppose I have an isomorphism f:X→Yf:X \to Yf:X→Y with inverse g:Y→Xg:Y \to Xg:Y→X. Whenever I have a morphism Z→XZ \to XZ→X, which can be thought of as a mapping, or more generally a relationship from ZZZ to XXX, then by composing with fff, I get a relationship from ZZZ to YYY for free. Similarly, by composing with ggg, any relationship from ZZZ to YYY produces a relationship from ZZZ to XXX. The fact that fff and ggg compose to give identities in both directions mean that these operations of composition are exactly inverses to one another: there's a bijective correspondence between morphisms Z→XZ \to XZ→X and Z→YZ \to YZ→Y. This is also true on the other side: there's a bijection between morphisms X→AX \to AX→A and Y→AY \to AY→A. Since morphisms into and out of XXX are the only way that other objects in the category can "see" XXX, the consequence of this is that XXX and YYY 'look identical' from the perspective of any object in the category.
