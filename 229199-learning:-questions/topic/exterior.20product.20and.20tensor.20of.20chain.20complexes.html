Altogether these make me think that the sign (−1)pq(-1)^{pq}(−1)pq in the exterior product is “the same” as the sign (−1)pq(-1)^{pq}(−1)pq in the symmetry on the tensor product of chain complexes. But I am failing to find a source that explains the connection. Can anyone help?

Simon Burton (May 05 2021 at 13:49):

Even before you get to chain complexes you can consider Z\mathbb{Z}Z-graded vector spaces with the same tensor (forget the boundary operation) and the same symmetry operation x⊗y↦(−1)pqy⊗xx \otimes y \mapsto (-1)^{pq} y \otimes xx⊗y↦(−1)pqy⊗x. Exterior product in grade zero is then the same thing as symmetric product in grade 1. So that's how I think of exterior product as a graded thing, it's the symmetrization of odd graded vector spaces...

Reid Barton (May 06 2021 at 14:15):

I think this is true: if ω≠0\omega \ne 0ω=0 then we can choose a point at which ω\omegaω does not vanish, choose local coordinates at that point and write ω\omegaω in the standard basis dxi1∧⋯∧dxindx_{i_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge dx_{i_n}dxi1​​∧⋯∧dxin​​ (i1<⋯<ini_1 < \cdots < i_ni1​<⋯<in​, for an nnn-form), and choose one of the terms that has a nonzero coefficient, say positive. Then nearby the original point, that coefficient is still positive. So a suitable small chain near the original point that varies only in the corresponding directions will pair positively with ω\omegaω.
