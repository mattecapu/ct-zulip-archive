[Mod] Morgan Rogers (Jun 04 2020 at 10:08):

A feature request mentioned in the discussion after Nina Otter's talk is to make admins/moderators more visible, so that people know who to contact if someone is displaying inappropriate behaviour.
On a related note, I would like to know what conventions we can agree upon about what should be done when:

  1. Someone is being (or coming across as) aggressive, or otherwise violating the intended spirit of this forum. Should a moderator simply make their presence felt in requesting a change of tone? If that fails or things return to ugly, should messages be deleted? Should such conversations be moved to a private stream for conflict resolution!?
  2. A small number of people are dominating a topic that would benefit from more varied contributions. This has happened a bunch of times, notably in #practice: communication. The discussions have generally been interesting to read, so I have not acted to take them down, but at a certain point the volume of text must be offputting to newcomers to the conversation. Perhaps a conciseness guideline would be valuable? I'll suggest this in #general: values too.

Todd Trimble (Jun 04 2020 at 11:46):

Depending on how big the community gets, moderators should not be expected to follow every discussion. If possible, it might be better to have a flagging mechanism for anyone to raise a (privately issued) flag to the moderator team for attention, with a brief description of the problem. That's how it works at the StackExchange sites like MathOverflow.

[Mod] Morgan Rogers (Jun 04 2020 at 11:59):

That would work if and when the scale increases, but I like the idea of people knowing individual people that they can discuss a problem/issue with. At the moment, as John Baez noted, the scale is small enough that the active users could in principle know and recognise one another personally, and I would like to foster that sense of community for as long as it remains feasible.

Todd Trimble (Jun 04 2020 at 12:30):
